# Distilling Spirits Course

## Overview

A practical guide to home-scale distillation of beverage alcohol on a pot still. The course covers what is universal across spirits, how the still works, how a wash ferments, how the cuts (foreshots, heads, hearts and tails) are taken by smell and by volume, and how barrel aging changes a clear spirit into something amber. The recipes that follow apply the same machinery and the same cuts to the major categories of distilled drink: whiskies (Scotch, Irish, bourbon, rye, Tennessee, corn), neutral spirits (vodka, grain alcohol), fruit brandies (apple, peach, plum, pear) and the un-aged variants (moonshine, white dog, flavoured infusions).

Three things this course assumes before you start:

1. **You have read the legal section below.** Distilling beverage alcohol is licensed activity in every Western country. The detail differs sharply between the US, UK and EU, and between countries inside the EU. The course is written so the technique works wherever you are; the legal frame around it does not.
2. **You hold the appropriate licence for your jurisdiction**, or you are reading the course for study only. In the US that is a Federal Distilled Spirits Permit (DSP) from the TTB plus any state permit. In the UK it is an HMRC distiller's licence under Notice 39. In the EU it is whatever national authorisation your country requires. Without that paperwork, home distilling of beverage alcohol is a criminal offence almost everywhere; the course does not relitigate the point.
3. **You will read the [safety page](safety.md) before you light a heat source for the first time.** Methanol contamination, fire and explosion are not theoretical risks; they are how home distillers get hurt. The safety page is short, blunt and essential.

## Why distilling makes a coherent course

Every distilled spirit on the planet, from a 12-year-old Speyside single malt to a clear Hungarian pálinka, is the same set of moves done at different scales and with different inputs. You make a sugar-bearing wash; you ferment it; you heat it under a still; you cool the vapour back to liquid; you discard the head and tail fractions and keep the heart. The grain you use, the wood you age in, the water you cut with and the customs you observe at each step are what give the spirit its national or regional identity. The mechanics underneath are the same.

Pot stills are the family-scale equipment of distilling, used by farmers and crofters from County Cork to Kentucky to the Caucasus for the last 400 years. The great commercial column stills that produce most of today's bourbon, vodka and white rum are industrial scalings of the same basic ideas: a fermented wash, a heat source, a condenser, a careful set of cuts. What you learn here is what a farmer in the Scottish Highlands, the Appalachian foothills, the Normandy orchards or the Czech countryside would have done in 1815 with the same simple apparatus.

The recipes in this course are drawn primarily from the American tradition because it is one of the best-documented home-scale canons and the equipment, the cooperage, the corn-rye-barley mash bills and the cut-by-smell practice all transfer directly to spirits anywhere else in the world. Once you have made bourbon you understand how to make Scotch single malt; once you have made apple brandy you understand calvados, eaux-de-vie and slivovitz. The course covers the principles first and the named regional examples second.

## Course outline

### 1. Techniques and guidance

Read these before making anything. They are the foundation the recipe pages assume.

- [Building a still](building-a-still.md): a working pot still from copper tubing and a heat source you already own.
- [Safety](safety.md): the most important page. Foreshots cuts, methanol, fire and pressure. Read every season.
- [Sour mash](sour-mash.md): the consistency trick from the American whiskey tradition, also used in some European single-malt practice. Save spent grain from the previous batch, stir into the next.
- [Cuts by smell](cuts-by-smell.md): the sensory side. Safety.md tells you the volumes; this tells you what to smell for.
- [Aging in small barrels](aging-small-barrels.md): the 5-gallon-barrel trick, the surface-area math, when to bottle. The technique applies to any aged spirit, from bourbon to apple brandy to single-malt whisky.

### 2. Recipes

Each page covers the spirit category, the defining technical features (mash bill, ferment, cut, aging), a worked 5-gallon recipe, what to expect on the palate, and variations. The current set is drawn from the American canon; the same techniques apply to spirits from any tradition (Scotch single malt, French calvados and eaux-de-vie, Caribbean rum, Czech and Hungarian fruit brandies, etc.). Alphabetical from here.

- [Applejack](applejack.md): two methods. Freeze concentration (the pre-Prohibition folk technique) and apple-brandy distillation (the proper licensed approach). The technique generalises to calvados and other apple brandies.
- [Bourbon](bourbon.md): the corn-led American whiskey. The federal definition, the cooperage, the family-farm version. A worked example of the broader whisky family.
- [Corn whiskey](corn-whiskey.md): aged corn whiskey. ≥80% corn, aged in used or uncharred oak. The bridge between moonshine and bourbon.
- [Flavoured moonshine](flavoured-moonshine.md): Apple Pie, Peach, Blackberry, Strawberry, Cherry, White Lightnin', Hunch Punch, Mountain Java. Cold-infused un-aged spirit; the technique applies to any neutral spirit base.
- [Grain alcohol](grain-alcohol.md): the 190-proof base for infusions, extracts and household spirits. The neutral spirit that underpins vodka, gin and most flavoured liqueurs.
- [Moonshine](ole-smoky-moonshine.md): un-aged corn whiskey, the un-barrelled version of bourbon. Across world spirits, this category is "white dog" (US), "new make" (Scotland), "white whisky" (anywhere).
- [Peach brandy](peach-brandy.md): the Southern American fruit brandy. Ripe peaches fermented and distilled into a clear fragrant spirit. The technique is identical to French eau-de-vie, Czech slivovitz, Italian grappa and German Obstler.
- [Rye](rye.md): the spicier, drier cousin of bourbon. Pennsylvania and Maryland traditions. The American rye canon parallels the rye-led Canadian and Northern European traditions.
- [Tennessee whiskey](tennessee-whiskey.md): bourbon plus the Lincoln County Process. Charcoal mellowing, a regional finishing step that exists in no other major tradition.
- [Vodka](vodka.md): clean, repeatable, the foundation of countless cocktails. The Eastern European and Scandinavian traditions all build on the same neutral spirit.
- [Whisky (the umbrella)](whisky.md): the umbrella category. Mash, ferment, distil, cut, barrel, age. Read first if any of the whisky-family pages above feel like they assume background. Scotch single malt, Irish triple-distilled, Japanese single malt and American bourbon all sit under this umbrella with different mash bills and different cooperage.

## What you will need

A working list before starting:

- A pot still (copper or stainless, 5-20 gallon / 20-75 litre capacity for family scale)
- A heat source (propane burner or electric immersion element; electric gives cleaner temperature control)
- A fermentation vessel (food-grade plastic or stainless, sized to your still)
- A wash hydrometer (specific gravity) and a spirit hydrometer (% ABV). Different instruments, both needed.
- A thermometer that reads to at least 100 °C (212 °F), ideally a digital probe in the column
- A parrot (a small overflow chamber where the spirit comes off, with a hydrometer in it for continuous ABV monitoring)
- Glass collection vessels (mason jars are the American convention; clear glass demijohns work equally well)
- A clean ventilated workspace. Alcohol vapour is flammable.

Most small-scale distillers buy a ready-made pot-still kit (StillDragon, Hillbilly Stills, Mile Hi Distilling in the US; Pure Distilling, Still Spirits and equivalent suppliers in Europe and Australia) rather than fabricating from scratch. The [Building a still](building-a-still.md) page covers both the bought-kit and the home-fabricated routes.

## Legal considerations

Distilling beverage alcohol is licensed activity in every Western jurisdiction. The detail differs significantly between the US, the UK and the EU, and within the EU between member states. This section is a starting point, not a substitute for current advice from your national tax or excise authority.

### United States

Federal Distilled Spirits Permits (DSP) are administered by the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB). The application requires plant security, accounting, federal bond and ongoing reporting. State licensing varies. Small-scale licensed distilling is legal, common, and increasingly so since the post-2010 wave of craft distilleries; the smallest licensed operations run on a single pot still in a converted barn. Home distillation of beverage alcohol without a federal permit is a felony regardless of state law or "personal use" framing.

This course does not cover the federal licensing paperwork itself. That is your TTB officer's job. Once the paperwork is in place, the course covers everything else.

### United Kingdom

Distilling beverage alcohol at home is illegal in the UK without a distiller's licence from HMRC. The licensing regime is administered under the Alcoholic Liquor Duties Act and the associated Notice 39, and is designed for commercial production: it requires premises approval, bonded warehousing, plant security, ongoing duty accounting, and (historically) a minimum still size that effectively rules out family-scale equipment. Owning or possessing a still capable of distilling spirits without a licence is a separate offence in its own right; HMRC has powers of seizure for unlicensed stills regardless of whether they have been used. Penalties for unlicensed distillation run to unlimited fines, equipment confiscation, and custodial sentences of up to seven years.

There is no UK equivalent of a "personal-use" or "hobby" exemption for spirits. Home brewing of beer and home making of wine for personal consumption is permitted without a licence; distillation is not, and the line is firmly drawn at the moment any wash is heated for the purpose of separating alcohol. UK readers can take this course as a historical, technical and cultural study. Producing any of the recipes practically requires a commercial HMRC distiller's licence, which is achievable but is a real commercial undertaking, not a household project.

### Continental Europe

Rules vary by country, but the underlying pattern across the EU is the same as the UK: distillation of beverage alcohol is licensed activity, administered by national tax or excise authorities, and most member states have no general hobby or personal-use exemption.

A handful of countries permit limited home production of fruit brandies (typically schnapps from the household's own-grown fruit), under strict conditions: registration with the tax authority in advance, a per-household annual quota, and a duty payment per litre of pure alcohol. The Austrian Abfindungsbrennerei system, the Czech pestitelské pálení, the Hungarian magánfőzés / bérfőzés framework for pálinka, and the German small-still tradition are the better-known examples. The detail of each scheme changes regularly; check with the national customs / tax authority for current limits and registration requirements.

Two important caveats for European readers:

- **The exemptions are for fruit brandy from own-grown fruit only.** Grain-based spirits (whisky, vodka, bourbon, rye, every recipe in this course's whisky family) fall outside these schemes and require full commercial licensing everywhere in the EU.
- **Owning or operating a still without registration is an offence even where home production is permitted in principle.** The Austrian, Czech and Hungarian schemes all require the still to be declared in advance, not after the fact.

France, Italy, Spain, Belgium, the Netherlands, the Nordic countries, and most other EU member states do not permit home distillation in any form. Penalties for unlicensed distillation range from large fines to imprisonment, depending on the country and the scale.

### Elsewhere

Canada, Australia and New Zealand all require licensing for home distillation, with some narrow exemptions for declared low-volume hobby distilling in specific Australian states. South Africa, India and most South American countries restrict distilling to licensed producers. Local rules change; check with the relevant national authority before buying or building a still.

If you are outside the US and intend to follow this course practically, the technical content (the still, the cuts, the cooperage, the recipes) is the same anywhere in the world; the legal context in which you can use it is not. The course assumes you have squared that away before you light the burner.
