# Sausages

![Fresh sausages and dry-cured salami sliced](resources/sausages.jpg)

*Three sausage families: fresh (no cure, cook and eat or freeze), semi-dry (cooked then briefly aged), and dry-cured salami (the hardest, with fermentation and weeks of hanging). The same grinder and stuffer make all three; the safety bar climbs with each.*

## Overview
Sausage-making spans the easiest charcuterie project (fresh pork-and-fennel sausages, eaten that night) and one of the hardest (dry-cured salami, requiring a curing chamber and a fermentation phase). The equipment is the same: a meat grinder and a stuffer. The choice is what you do after stuffing.

This lesson covers fresh sausages (straightforward home project), semi-dry sausages (fresh sausages cooked then dried briefly for a longer keeping time), and dry-cured salami (the long project). The food-safety bar rises with each step; dry-cured salami is the riskiest project in the course outside of full prosciutto.

## Fresh Sausages

### What You Need

- Meat grinder (manual or electric; a KitchenAid attachment is fine)
- Stuffer (a stuffer attachment for a grinder works for small batches; a dedicated vertical stuffer is faster)
- Natural hog casing (the standard for fresh; 32-36 mm diameter)
- 2 kg pork shoulder, bone-out, with a good fat-to-meat ratio (around 30% fat is the target; pork shoulder is usually 25-30% as cut)
- Aromatics and seasonings (recipe-specific)
- 18-20 g salt per kg of meat (2%); no cure #1 (fresh sausages, cooked before eating, do not need nitrite)

### The Process

1. **Cube and chill.** Cut the pork shoulder into 2-3 cm cubes. Chill in the freezer for 30-45 minutes until the surface is firm but the centre is not frozen.
2. **Grind.** Use a coarse plate (8 mm) for chunky sausages or a medium plate (4-5 mm) for smoother. Grind once.
3. **Season.** Mix the salt and other seasonings into the ground meat thoroughly. Test the seasoning by frying a small patty before stuffing.
4. **Soak casings.** Natural hog casings come in salt; rinse in cold water and soak in water with a splash of vinegar for 30 minutes to soften.
5. **Stuff.** Slide a length of casing onto the stuffer nozzle. Stuff at low speed (or by hand) so the meat fills the casing tightly without trapping air pockets. Don't overfill, the casing should be plump but not bursting.
6. **Twist into links.** Pinch the filled casing at intervals (10-12 cm for breakfast sausage, 15-20 cm for larger). Twist alternately in opposite directions to lock the links.
7. **Hang briefly.** Hang in the fridge uncovered for 1-12 hours. The casing dries slightly and the seasoning equilibrates.

### Cook Fresh, or Freeze Fresh

Fresh sausages are not preserved. Cook within 2-3 days, or vacuum-pack and freeze for 3 months.

To cook: pan-fry, grill, oven-roast at 180 C, poach gently then sear. The traditional pan-fry method: medium heat, no oil (the sausage renders its own), 10-12 minutes turning frequently, until 70 C internal.

### Three Worked Recipes

**Italian fennel pork sausage (per kg of pork shoulder).**
- 20 g salt
- 5 g cracked black pepper
- 5 g fennel seed, toasted and lightly crushed
- 2 g chilli flakes (optional, for a piccante version)
- 30 ml dry white wine
- 3 cloves garlic, minced

**British pork-and-sage sausage (per kg).**
- 20 g salt
- 5 g cracked black pepper
- 5 g dried sage
- 5 g dried thyme
- 2 g nutmeg, grated
- 50 g panko breadcrumbs (traditional British style; soaks juices)
- 30 ml ice water (to bind)

**Toulouse-style French (per kg).**
- 18 g salt
- 4 g white pepper
- 30 ml dry red wine
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 g black pepper
- 2 g nutmeg

## Semi-Dry Sausages

These are fresh sausages that are then cooked AND briefly dried, intermediate between fresh and dry-cured. They keep longer than fresh but are not shelf-stable. Examples: kielbasa, frankfurters, weisswurst when hot-smoked then briefly chilled.

The process: grind, season (with cure #1 at 0.25% by weight, since these go into the smoker before cooking and the warm phase needs nitrite protection), stuff, hot-smoke at 60-80 C until 70 C internal, then refrigerate. Keeps 2-3 weeks refrigerated, 3 months frozen.

This is the easiest entry to smoked sausages. A kielbasa-style smoked sausage:

- 1 kg pork shoulder
- 200 g lean beef (chuck or shoulder)
- 18 g salt
- 3 g cure #1
- 5 g black pepper
- 5 g garlic powder (or 6 cloves fresh, minced)
- 3 g marjoram, dried
- 2 g coriander seed, ground

Grind, mix, stuff into hog casings, hot-smoke at 65 C with hickory or oak for 3-4 hours until 70 C internal, ice-bath to stop the cook, refrigerate.

## Dry-Cured Salami

The hardest project in the charcuterie course. Salami needs a curing chamber (same as the salumi chamber), a fermentation phase at warm-and-humid for 24-72 hours, and then 4-12 weeks of aging at 12-14 C and 70-75% humidity.

### What Makes Salami Different From Salumi

In whole-muscle salumi (bresaola, lonza), the meat is one piece; the cure penetrates by diffusion from the surface. The interior stays anaerobic, but the muscle structure is intact and bacteria struggle to spread through it.

In salami, the meat is ground. Every gram of meat now has surface area exposed to the cure. Every gram is also a potential entry point for pathogens. The cure must reach equilibrium throughout the mince in hours, not weeks. And the casing creates an anaerobic environment that botulism would love if the cure failed.

The safety mechanism is fermentation. A starter culture (commercial lactic acid bacteria, often Bactoferm F-RM-52 or similar) is mixed into the salami meat at stuffing. The salami hangs at warm and humid conditions (25 C, 90% humidity) for 24-72 hours; the bacteria multiply and ferment dextrose into lactic acid; the pH drops from 6 to 4.8-5.0. The acidic environment plus the cure #2 plus the gradual drying make the salami safe.

### Ingredients (per kg of meat)

For a basic Italian-style salami:

- 700 g pork shoulder (with the fat cap)
- 300 g pork back fat, cubed small
- 28 g salt (this is higher than for fresh; 2.8% for safety in dry-cured)
- 2.5 g cure #2
- 3 g dextrose (food for the fermentation bacteria; ordinary sugar works less well)
- 5 g cracked black peppercorns
- 4 cloves garlic, finely minced
- 30 ml dry red wine
- 0.04 g starter culture (Bactoferm F-RM-52 or similar; very small amount, follow product instructions)

Beef middles (the larger casings, around 50-60 mm diameter) are the standard.

### Method

1. **Grind.** Pork shoulder and back fat through a 6 mm plate. Keep cold.
2. **Hydrate culture.** Dissolve the starter culture in 30 ml of non-chlorinated water (tap water that has been left out overnight, or bottled water). Let stand 10 minutes.
3. **Mix.** Combine ground meat, salt, cure #2, dextrose, pepper, garlic, wine and hydrated culture. Mix thoroughly by hand for 2 minutes until the mixture is sticky and tacky (this protein-extraction step is important; under-mixed salami crumbles).
4. **Stuff.** Soak casings, slide onto the stuffer, stuff slowly and firmly. Tie at both ends with butcher's twine, creating a hanging loop.
5. **Pin-prick.** Lightly prick any visible air pockets with a clean sterilised needle.
6. **Ferment.** Hang at 24-25 C and 85-90% humidity for 24-72 hours. The exact time depends on diameter and the starter culture; follow the culture manufacturer's instructions. A small fermentation chamber, a covered cooler with a controlled heat source, or a cool oven with the light on can work.
7. **Test pH.** Use a meat pH meter. Target: pH 4.8-5.0. If it has not dropped below 5.2 by 72 hours, something is wrong (culture inactive, temperature off, conditions wrong).
8. **Dry.** Move to the curing chamber at 12-14 C, 70-75% humidity. Hang for 4-12 weeks, depending on diameter. A 50 mm salami takes 4-6 weeks; a 60-80 mm salami takes 8-12 weeks. Target weight loss is 30-35%.

### Why This Is Harder Than Salumi

Two extra failure modes:
- **Fermentation can stall.** Wrong temperature, weak culture, contamination, the pH never drops, the cure does not develop the protective acidity, the salami is unsafe.
- **Texture defects.** Under-mixed mince: crumbly when sliced. Over-stuffed casing: bursts during fermentation. Wrong fat-to-meat ratio: greasy or dry.

Start with a small batch (500 g - 1 kg of meat). Use a measured culture. Buy a pH meter. Follow the recipe exactly; this is not a freelancing project.

## A Note on Starting Order

For the home charcuterist working through these courses, a sensible order is:

1. Bacon (1 week, low risk)
2. Gravlax (2 days, low risk)
3. Fresh sausages (immediate, low risk)
4. Confit and rillettes (1 day cook, low risk)
5. Bresaola (8 weeks, moderate risk, needs chamber)
6. Coppa, lonza, pancetta arrotolata (8-10 weeks, moderate risk)
7. Semi-dry smoked sausages (3 weeks, low-moderate risk)
8. Dry-cured salami (8-12 weeks, higher risk, needs chamber + fermentation + pH meter)

Each step builds skill and equipment investment.
