# Salt and Cure

![Salt, cure #1, sugar in dishes](resources/salt-and-cure.jpg)

*Salt does the preserving; nitrite stops the worst pathogen; sugar softens the flavour and feeds the curing bacteria; every gram is weighed against the meat itself. The science is small, the rigour is large.*

## Overview
Every cure in this course is built from the same handful of ingredients: salt, sodium nitrite (cure #1 / Prague powder #1 / pink curing salt), sodium nitrate (cure #2, for long-aged products), sugar, sometimes a small amount of culture starter, and the flavourings. The proportions change, but the chemistry does not.

Understanding what each ingredient does explains why every recipe in the course is expressed by weight as a percentage of the raw meat, why the cure #1 dose is precise to a tenth of a gram, why bacon does not become salami even with extra time, and why a small amount of sugar appears in nearly every cure.

## What Salt Does

Salt does three things at once:

1. **Draws water out of cells by osmosis.** The lower the water content of the meat, the harder it is for bacteria to grow. Drying via salt is the foundation of every cured product.
2. **Inhibits most bacteria directly.** Salt at the surface and at the curing concentration (3-5% of meat weight, depending on the cure) stops most spoilage and pathogen organisms from multiplying.
3. **Denatures proteins.** Salt unfolds muscle proteins, which gives cured meats their characteristic firmer, more cohesive texture compared with raw meat.

Salt does not kill bacteria directly; it stops them growing. Bacteria already present will persist if conditions are favourable. This is why the rest of the cure matters.

The salt percentage that matters: 2.5-3% by raw weight for fresh cures (bacon, gravlax), 3-4% for dry cures (bresaola, lonza). Below 2% is unsafe; above 5% is unpleasantly salty.

## What Nitrite Does (Cure #1)

Cure #1 is a mixture of sodium chloride (regular salt) and **sodium nitrite** (NaNO2). Commercial versions are dyed pink so you cannot confuse it with table salt. Different brands call it cure #1, Prague powder #1, pink curing salt, Insta Cure #1.

What it does:

- **Blocks Clostridium botulinum.** This is the dangerous pathogen of charcuterie. It grows in low-oxygen environments (under a layer of fat, inside a sausage casing, in a cured ham), at warm-ish temperatures (above 4 C), and produces a toxin that is one of the most lethal known to medicine. Nitrite blocks its growth at concentrations as low as 150 parts per million.
- **Stabilises colour.** The pink-red of cured ham (versus the brown of cooked roast pork) is the nitrite-myoglobin bond. Bacon, ham, frankfurters all owe their colour to nitrite.
- **Contributes flavour.** The characteristic "cured" flavour - the difference between a cured pork chop and a roast one - is partly the nitrite.

The standard dose is **0.25% of meat weight**. That is 2.5 g of cure #1 per kilo of meat. Cure #1 is approximately 6.25% nitrite by weight; 2.5 g cure #1 contains about 156 mg of nitrite, which lands at the 150 ppm in-meat concentration that is the food-safety target.

**This is not a knob to dial up.** Too much nitrite is toxic. Too little leaves the meat at risk. Weigh the cure to 0.1 g precision. Do not measure nitrite by volume.

Use cure #1 for any cure that is finished within 30 days and either cooked before eating (bacon, ham, frankfurters) or eaten cold without further aging (gravlax with nitrite, pastrami before cooking).

## What Nitrate Does (Cure #2)

Cure #2 is salt plus sodium nitrate (NaNO3) plus a small amount of sodium nitrite (NaNO2). Different brands: Prague powder #2, Insta Cure #2.

Nitrate is the slow-release form of nitrite. Bacteria naturally present on cured meat convert nitrate to nitrite over weeks, which means a single dose of cure #2 at the start of a long dry cure releases nitrite gradually for the entire aging period. Without that slow release, the initial nitrite would be exhausted within days and the rest of the cure would have no botulism protection.

**Use cure #2 only for products aged 30+ days at curing temperatures.** Bresaola, lonza, coppa, saucisson sec, salami. The dose is the same: 0.25% of meat weight.

**Do not use cure #2 in bacon or short cures.** It contains less nitrite than cure #1 (because the nitrate releases more over time), so the short-cure product would be under-protected.

## The Nitrate Confusion

Nitrate and nitrite are different molecules. The naming is unhelpful for new cooks.

| Name | Formula | Use | Action |
|------|---------|-----|--------|
| Sodium chloride | NaCl | Every cure | The salt itself |
| Sodium nitrite | NaNO2 | Cure #1, short cures | Fast: blocks botulism immediately |
| Sodium nitrate | NaNO3 | Cure #2, long cures | Slow: converts to nitrite over weeks |

Both nitrites and nitrates occur naturally in vegetables (especially celery, beetroot, leafy greens). "Uncured" bacon sold in supermarkets is usually cured with celery juice powder, which is a high-nitrate vegetable concentrate. The chemistry is identical; the labelling is marketing.

## What Sugar Does

Most cures contain sugar at 1-2% of meat weight. It does several things:

- **Softens the saltiness.** A cure with 3% salt and 1% sugar tastes less harshly salty than the same cure without sugar; the flavour rounds out.
- **Feeds the curing bacteria.** In long-aged dry cures (salami, dry-cured ham), specific bacteria - lactobacilli and related species - ferment the sugar, lower the pH, and add tang. The lower pH (alongside the salt and the water loss) is the third safety leg of the cure.
- **Balances the cure aesthetically.** A bit of sugar in bacon makes it taste like bacon and not like jerky.

Brown sugar, maple syrup, honey, white sugar - all work. The differences are flavour, not function.

## The Percentage-by-Weight Method

Every recipe in this course assumes you weigh the meat, then calculate the cure from a percentage:

```
Meat:       1000 g pork belly
Salt:       3.0% =  30.0 g
Cure #1:    0.25% =  2.5 g
Sugar:      2.0% =  20.0 g
Black pepper: 1.0% = 10.0 g
```

Mix all the dry cure together, coat the meat thoroughly, vacuum-seal or place in a heavy-duty resealable bag, refrigerate. Flip the bag daily. Done.

This method scales to any size of meat. The percentages stay the same; only the gram totals change. There is no margin of safety in the cure #1 ratio - it must be 0.25% to land in the safe-and-effective range.

A useful safety hack: never premix a large batch of "house cure" with cure #1 included. The cure #1 is so small that it concentrates unevenly when mixed in bulk. Mix each cure fresh, using a 0.1 g scale, for the specific batch of meat you are working with.

## Why Not Just Sea Salt and Time?

Some traditional dry-cured products use only sea salt and time (notably some prosciutto and country hams). These work because they sit at controlled temperatures and humidities for very long periods (a year or more for prosciutto), during which the meat loses 30-40% of its weight before bacteria can grow. The water activity drops below the danger zone fast enough.

This is not safely achievable at home without serious aging infrastructure. The professional curing rooms of an Italian prosciutto producer have decades of microflora colonising the walls (favourable Penicillium and Staphylococcus species) and the temperature control to back it up. Replicating that is not a beginner project.

For the home cook, cure #1 (short) and cure #2 (long) are the safety insurance that makes the project achievable without dedicated infrastructure. The flavour of nitrite-cured meat is also what most palates expect of "cured" - prosciutto-without-nitrite is a different ingredient.
