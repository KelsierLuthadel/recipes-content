# American Spirits Course

## Overview

A practical American spirits course built for a TTB-licensed family operation in Tennessee. The other [spirits tutorial](../spirits/home.md) on this site is an educational overview of how spirits are made commercially. This course is the hands-on counterpart: how to actually build a pot still, run it safely, cut the foreshots properly, and produce the American canon of distilled drinks. Whisky, rye, bourbon, Tennessee whiskey, vodka, grain alcohol, applejack and moonshine including the Ole Smoky flavoured line.

Two things this course assumes:

1. **You hold a Federal Distilled Spirits Permit from the TTB** (and any applicable Tennessee state permit). Without that, home distillation of beverage alcohol is a federal felony, regardless of state law or "personal use" framing. The course does not relitigate that point; it assumes you have the paperwork.
2. **You will read the [safety page](safety.md) before you light a heat source for the first time.** Methanol contamination, fire and explosion are not theoretical risks; they are how home distillers get hurt. The safety page is short, blunt and essential.

The course is organised around equipment first, technique second, and the eight named American spirits third. The flavoured-moonshine page sits at the end as the family-fun chapter - once you can make clean corn whiskey safely, the Apple Pie and Peach versions are an afternoon's infusion work.

## Why the American canon is a coherent course

American distilling traces a continuous tradition from the Scotch-Irish settlers who came down the Great Wagon Road into Appalachia in the 1700s, brought their pot stills with them, and worked out how to make whisky from the corn that grew where the rye and barley they were used to did not. The result is the closest thing the United States has to a national spirit tradition: corn-led whiskies (bourbon, Tennessee whiskey, moonshine) on one branch, rye whiskies on the other, neutral grain spirit and vodka as the cleaner cousins, and applejack as the apple-orchard outlier.

Everything in this course is made on a pot still. Pot stills are the family-scale equipment of American whiskey; the great commercial column stills at Jack Daniel's or Buffalo Trace are an industrial scaling-up of the same basic ideas (a fermented wash, a heat source, a condenser, a careful set of cuts). What you learn here is what a Tennessee farmer would have done in 1815 with the same equipment.

## What you will need

A working list before starting:

- A pot still (copper or stainless, 5-20 gallon capacity for family scale)
- A heat source (propane burner is standard; electric immersion element for cleaner control)
- A fermentation vessel (food-grade plastic or stainless, sized to your still)
- A wash hydrometer (specific gravity) and a spirit hydrometer (% ABV) - different instruments, both needed
- A thermometer that reads to at least 100 °C (212 °F), ideally a digital probe in the column
- A parrot (a small overflow chamber where the spirit comes off, with a hydrometer in it for continuous ABV monitoring)
- Glass collection vessels (mason jars are traditional and correct)
- A clean ventilated workspace - alcohol vapour is flammable

Most American family-scale distillers buy a ready-made pot-still kit (StillDragon, Hillbilly Stills, Mile Hi Distilling) rather than fabricating from scratch. The [Building a still](building-a-still.md) page covers both the bought-kit and the home-fabricated routes.

## A note on the law

Federal Distilled Spirits Permits (DSP) are administered by the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB). The application requires plant security, accounting, federal bond and ongoing reporting. Tennessee adds state licensing requirements administered by the Alcoholic Beverage Commission. Family-scale licensed distilling is legal, common, and increasingly so since the post-2010 wave of craft distilleries; the smallest licensed operations in Tennessee run on a single pot still in a converted barn. Your family is in good company.

The course does not cover the licensing paperwork itself - that is your TTB officer's job. Once the paperwork is in place, the course covers everything else.
