# American Spirits Course

## Overview

A practical guide to the American canon of distilled drinks: whisky, bourbon, rye, Tennessee whiskey, vodka, grain alcohol, applejack, peach brandy and moonshine including the Ole Smoky flavoured line. Everything is built on a pot still, the family-scale equipment American distillers have used since the 1700s. The course covers how to build the still, how to run it safely, how to handle the cuts (foreshots, heads, hearts and tails), and how to age the result.

Two things this course assumes:

1. **You hold a Federal Distilled Spirits Permit from the TTB** (and any applicable state permit). Without that, home distillation of beverage alcohol is a federal felony, regardless of state law or "personal use" framing. The course does not relitigate that point; it assumes you have the paperwork.
2. **You will read the [safety page](safety.md) before you light a heat source for the first time.** Methanol contamination, fire and explosion are not theoretical risks; they are how home distillers get hurt. The safety page is short, blunt and essential.

## Why the American canon is a coherent course

American distilling traces a continuous tradition from the Scotch-Irish settlers who came down the Great Wagon Road into Appalachia in the 1700s, brought their pot stills with them, and worked out how to make whisky from the corn that grew where the rye and barley they were used to did not. The result is the closest thing the United States has to a national spirit tradition: corn-led whiskies (bourbon, Tennessee whiskey, moonshine) on one branch, rye whiskies on the other, neutral grain spirit and vodka as the cleaner cousins, and applejack and peach brandy as the orchard outliers.

Everything in this course is made on a pot still. Pot stills are the family-scale equipment of American whiskey; the great commercial column stills at Jack Daniel's or Buffalo Trace are an industrial scaling-up of the same basic ideas (a fermented wash, a heat source, a condenser, a careful set of cuts). What you learn here is what a farmer in Kentucky or Tennessee would have done in 1815 with the same equipment.

## What you will need

A working list before starting:

- A pot still (copper or stainless, 5-20 gallon capacity for family scale)
- A heat source (propane burner is standard; electric immersion element for cleaner control)
- A fermentation vessel (food-grade plastic or stainless, sized to your still)
- A wash hydrometer (specific gravity) and a spirit hydrometer (% ABV). Different instruments, both needed.
- A thermometer that reads to at least 100 °C (212 °F), ideally a digital probe in the column
- A parrot (a small overflow chamber where the spirit comes off, with a hydrometer in it for continuous ABV monitoring)
- Glass collection vessels (mason jars are traditional and correct)
- A clean ventilated workspace. Alcohol vapour is flammable.

Most small-scale distillers buy a ready-made pot-still kit (StillDragon, Hillbilly Stills, Mile Hi Distilling) rather than fabricating from scratch. The [Building a still](building-a-still.md) page covers both the bought-kit and the home-fabricated routes.

## A note on the law

Federal Distilled Spirits Permits (DSP) are administered by the Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau (TTB). The application requires plant security, accounting, federal bond and ongoing reporting. State licensing varies. Small-scale licensed distilling is legal, common, and increasingly so since the post-2010 wave of craft distilleries; the smallest licensed operations run on a single pot still in a converted barn.

The course does not cover the licensing paperwork itself. That is your TTB officer's job. Once the paperwork is in place, the course covers everything else.
