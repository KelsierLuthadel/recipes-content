# BIR Curry Course

## Overview

British-Indian-Restaurant cooking (BIR for short) is the curry-house style that took root in the UK from the 1960s onwards, refined in the back kitchens of Bradford, Birmingham, Glasgow and East London. It is not how Indian food is cooked in India. It is a kitchen system that lets a single chef plate twenty different curries in twenty minutes, every one tasting distinct, all built from the same four pre-cooked components. That trick, the speed-and-consistency engineering of a Saturday-night curry house, is what this course teaches.

The popularity of BIR food is partly nostalgia (a Friday night takeaway for two generations of Britons), partly the dishes themselves (sweeter, richer and more layered than home-cooked Indian food), and mostly the fact that nothing on the menu takes longer than ten minutes to make once the prep is done. You can run the same system at home. A Saturday morning of base-gravy work, pre-cooked chicken in the fridge, a jar each of red masala and tandoori marinade, and you have curry-night-on-tap for a fortnight. The four components keep beautifully, freeze well, and turn a weeknight into a takeaway without the bag.

What you get back is a repertoire, not a single recipe. Once base gravy and pre-cooked protein are in the fridge, you can plate korma one night, vindaloo the next, jalfrezi the night after, all in the same time it takes to boil rice. The spice mixes do the heavy lifting; the base gravy is the canvas; the protein is already cooked. Assembly takes five minutes. That is the whole game.

## Why BIR food works

A few features of the system worth understanding before you cook from it:

- **The base gravy is mild.** It is not a curry on its own. It is a vegetable-and-spice broth, blended smooth, that adds body and aromatic depth to whatever you finish it with. The character of a curry comes from the spice mix and final ladle of fresh aromatics, not from the gravy.
- **Pre-cooked protein is the secret of the speed.** The chicken in your jalfrezi was poached in a spiced stock and held warm. By the time it hits the curry pan, it just needs to be coated in sauce and warmed through.
- **The masala mixes are scaled, not adjusted.** A vindaloo and a korma start with the same spoonful of pre-cooked chicken, the same ladle of base gravy. They diverge only in the masala mix (and, for korma, a finishing pour of cream). The chef does not "rebalance" the curry; he uses different mixes.
- **The oil at the start is deliberate.** Curry-house cooking begins with a generous splash of oil because the spice mixes need to bloom in hot fat for thirty seconds before liquid hits the pan. Skipping that step gives you a flat, raw-tasting curry.

Master those four ideas and you can reverse-engineer any takeaway menu.

## Popular BIR curries

The takeaway menu is more about heat level and finish than fundamentally different dishes. Once you have the four components, you can produce all of them by varying the masala mix, the chilli quantity and the final additions.

- [**Chicken tikka masala**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Tikka-Masala.md): the UK's unofficial national dish. Tandoori-marinated chicken, mild creamy tomato sauce, cream and almond finish. Sweet, smoky from the tandoori marinade, family-friendly.
- [**Korma**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Korma.md): the mildest on the menu. Coconut cream, ground almonds, a soft warm spice mix, sometimes a sprinkle of sultanas. Often the gateway curry.
- [**Madras**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Lamb-madrass.md): medium-hot, with a sharper spice mix heavy on chilli and tomato. The starting point for most "I want some heat" orders.
- [**Vindaloo**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Vindaloo.md): hot, sharp, vinegared. The masala adds extra chilli and a slug of vinegar (sometimes red wine vinegar) that cuts through the gravy. Goan in origin, naturalised in the UK as the heat-seeker's order.
- [**Phall**](../../cuisine/indian/BIR-Chicken-Phall.md): the hottest on most menus. Vindaloo with extra fresh chillies, often Scotch Bonnet or naga, sometimes a chilli-eating contest in disguise. Best approached with cold lager.
- [**Jalfrezi**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Jalfrezi.md): stir-fried with green peppers, onions and fresh green chillies. Drier than most BIR curries, with a fresh, slightly smoky finish from the high-heat pan-tossing.
- [**Dopiaza**](../../cuisine/indian/BIR-chicken-dopiaza.md): literally "two onions", finished with crisp-fried onion. Sweet, rich, moderate heat.
- [**Bhuna**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Lamb-bhuna.md): the gravy is reduced almost to nothing, the spice clings to the meat. Drier and more concentrated than the standard sauce-heavy curries.
- [**Rogan josh**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Lamb-Rogan-Josh.md): red, aromatic, Kashmiri in name though heavily anglicised in the curry house. Tomato-heavy, paprika and Kashmiri chilli for colour, mild to medium heat.
- [**Pasanda**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Pasanda.md): a milder, creamier curry built on cream and ground almonds. Often with lamb. Sweet and aromatic, similar in register to korma.
- [**Dansak**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Dhansak.md): the lentil curry. Pre-cooked toor or red lentils stirred into the gravy, with a slightly sour-sweet finish from a splash of pineapple or tamarind. Drier and more substantial than the standard.
- [**Balti**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/BIR-Chicken-Balti.md): the Birmingham invention. Cooked and served in a thin-pressed steel balti pan, with a slightly drier, more aromatic finish than a standard curry. The pan goes straight to the table.

## Rice to serve alongside

Most BIR meals get one of these. The pilau is the takeaway default; the others are upgrades.

- [**Plain basmati**](../../cuisine/indian/rice/plain-basmati.md): rinsed until the water runs clear, soaked for 20 minutes, drained, simmered with a 1:1.5 rice-to-water ratio for 12 minutes. The default.
- [**Pilau rice**](../../cuisine/indian/rice/pilau-rice.md): basmati cooked in stock with whole spices (cinnamon, cardamom, cloves, bay) and a touch of saffron or food colouring for the trademark yellow. Often finished with a knob of butter.
- [**Mushroom pilau**](../../cuisine/indian/rice/mushroom-pilau.md): pilau base with sliced mushrooms folded in during the final 5 minutes.
- [**Vegetable rice**](../../cuisine/indian/rice/vegetable-rice.md): pilau with diced carrot, peas and sometimes sweetcorn. The family-meal default.
- [**Lemon rice**](../../cuisine/indian/rice/lemon-rice.md): South Indian in origin, occasionally on a BIR menu. Plain rice tossed with mustard seed, curry leaf and lemon juice. Bright and sharp.
- [**Keema rice**](../../cuisine/indian/rice/keema-rice.md): pilau with spiced minced lamb folded through. Substantial enough to eat on its own.

## Sides and starters

The starter slot on a curry house menu is short and rarely changes:

- [**Onion bhaji**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/onion-bhaji.md): chickpea-flour batter with sliced onion, spices and fresh coriander, deep-fried in clumps until crisp. Served with mint raita.
- [**Vegetable samosa**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/samosa.md): spiced potato and pea filling folded into a thin pastry triangle and deep-fried. Mint raita and tamarind chutney alongside.
- [**Meat samosa**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/keema-samosa.md): minced lamb or beef with onion and warm spice, in the same triangle.
- [**Papadum**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/papadum.md): thin lentil-flour wafer, deep-fried until it puffs. Served with mango chutney, lime pickle, onion salad and mint raita. The opening ritual at every curry house.
- [**Sheek kebab**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/lamb-seekh-kebab.md): minced lamb on a skewer, charred under a grill. Often comes with a wedge of lemon and sliced raw onion.
- [**Tandoori chicken or tikka**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/tandoori-chicken-tikka.md): yoghurt-and-spice-marinated chicken, cooked in a tandoor or hot oven until charred at the edges. Served with salad and mint raita.
- [**Mixed grill**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/mixed-grill.md): a plate of two or three of the tandoori options together. Standard "we're hungry" opener for a group.
- [**Mint raita**](../../cuisine/indian/sauces-pickles/mint-raita.md): yoghurt with grated cucumber, chopped mint, salt, sometimes a pinch of ground cumin. The cooling balance to everything else on the table.
- [**Mango chutney**](../../cuisine/indian/sauces-pickles/mango-chutney.md), [**lime pickle**](../../cuisine/indian/sauces-pickles/lime-pickle.md), [**onion salad**](../../cuisine/indian/side-dishes/onion-salad.md): the trio of small bowls that arrive with the papadums.

## Breads

The bread basket is half the meal:

- [**Plain naan**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/naan.md): yoghurt-and-yeast leavened, slapped onto the side of a tandoor and pulled off in 90 seconds. Soft, blistered, charred on the underside.
- [**Garlic naan**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/naan.md): plain naan brushed with garlic butter and chopped coriander before serving. (See the Variations section of the naan recipe.)
- [**Peshwari naan**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/naan.md): stuffed with a sweet paste of ground almond, coconut and sultana. A dessert masquerading as a side. (Variation.)
- [**Keema naan**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/keema-naan.md): stuffed with spiced minced lamb. A meal on its own.
- [**Cheese naan**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/naan.md): a relatively recent addition, but firmly part of the canon now. Mozzarella melts into the soft interior. (Variation.)
- [**Tandoori roti**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/tandoori-roti.md): an unleavened whole-wheat round, also cooked against the tandoor wall. Drier and chewier than naan.
- [**Chapati**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/rotis.md): a softer everyday flatbread cooked on a dry tava. The home-Indian default rather than the restaurant default, but increasingly available.
- [**Paratha**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/bengali-paratha.md): a layered, ghee-brushed flatbread, sometimes plain, sometimes stuffed with potato (aloo paratha). Rich and substantial.
- [**Kulcha**](../../cuisine/indian/Breads/kulcha.md): a soft yeasted bread, often stuffed with onion or paneer. Northern Indian rather than strictly BIR, but it appears on broader menus.
