# Equipment and Ingredients

![Mead equipment](resources/equipment.jpg)

*Small kit, three essential ingredients. The whole brewery for a 5-litre starter batch costs about £40. The real spend is on better honey as you scale up.*

## Overview

This page covers the gear and the three ingredients (honey, yeast, water + nutrient). The kit is similar to homebrew-beer equipment but lighter (no boil kettle needed since mead isn't boiled).

## Equipment

### Essentials
- **5-litre glass demijohn / carboy** - the primary fermenter. Glass not plastic (mead ages for months; plastic absorbs oxygen and flavours). £8-15.
- **Bung + airlock** - fits the demijohn neck. Lets CO2 escape, keeps oxygen out. £2.
- **Auto-siphon / racking cane** - for transferring between vessels without splashing (avoiding oxidation). £8.
- **Digital hydrometer** - measures sugar concentration to track fermentation progress. £6.
- **Long brewing thermometer** - for monitoring fermentation temperature. £4.
- **Sanitiser** - Star San is the traditional brewer's no-rinse sanitiser. £8 for enough to last a year of brewing.
- **Cleaning brush** - long-handled for the demijohn. £5.
- **Funnel** - for clean transfers. £3.

### Nice-to-have
- **Refractometer** - alternative to hydrometer; uses a single drop, gives quick readings. £25.
- **Wine thief** - for pulling samples without contaminating the batch. £6.
- **Stir plate + magnetic stirrer** - for making yeast starters that ramp up fermentation. £30. Overkill for 5-litre batches.
- **pH meter** - useful for advanced brewers; not essential. £20.

### Bottling
- **8 × 500 ml flip-top bottles** (or 12 × 330 ml beer bottles + new caps + a capper). £20-25.
- **Bottle brush** - for cleaning. £4.

## The three ingredients

### 1. Honey

Honey is 80% sugar, 18% water, 2% nutrients/flavour/colour. The kind of honey you choose controls the flavour of the finished mead.

**Common honeys for mead:**

- **Clover** - pale, sweet, neutral. The "default" honey in many countries. Good for first batches; lets the yeast character show. £6-10 per kg.
- **Wildflower (multi-floral)** - medium amber, rich and floral. Gives a more complex traditional mead. £8-12 per kg.
- **Orange blossom** - gold, citrus-forward. Excellent for a citrus-themed melomel (orange peel + ginger). £10-15 per kg.
- **Buckwheat** - dark, intensely malty, almost porter-like. Makes dark complex meads; not for first-time brewers. £12-18 per kg.
- **Heather** - dark, intensely floral and slightly bitter. Scottish/Irish heather mead is iconic. £18-25 per kg.
- **Tupelo / acacia / leatherwood** - speciality honeys, distinctive regional flavours. £15-30 per kg.

**Quality tier:**
- **Supermarket "honey blend"** - mostly cheap Chinese honey, often adulterated. AVOID. £3 per kg.
- **Local honey** - from a small beekeeper in your area. £8-15 per kg. Best value.
- **Single-origin / single-flower** - from a specialty supplier. £15-30 per kg. For specific styles.
- **Raw, unfiltered honey** - preserves more pollen and enzymes; richer flavour. Often the same as local. £10-15 per kg.

**Amount:** 1-1.2 kg honey per 5 litres of finished mead for a medium-dry mead at about 11-12% ABV. Scale up for sweeter / higher-ABV meads.

### 2. Yeast

Yeast eats the honey's sugar and converts it to alcohol. Mead yeast choices:

- **D47 (Lalvin)** - the gold standard. Wine yeast. Clean, dry, good for traditional and fruit meads. Tolerates up to about 14% ABV. £2-3 per sachet.
- **K1-V1116 (Lalvin)** - fruit-forward. Best for melomels (fruit meads). about 14-16% ABV tolerance.
- **EC-1118 (Lalvin Premier Cuvée / Lalvin Premier Blanc)** - champagne yeast. Very hardy, very dry; will ferment to 18%+ ABV. Less flavour development; better for sack (high-ABV) meads.
- **71B-1122 (Lalvin)** - fruit/wine yeast that mellows malic acid. Good for cyser (apple mead). about 14% ABV.
- **English ale yeast (Wyeast 1098 / Safale S-04)** - gives rounder, more honey-forward character. Lower ABV tolerance (about 10%). Good for session meads.
- **Wild yeast (Brettanomyces, Lactobacillus)** - advanced. For sour/funky mead styles. £4-8 per blend.

For a first traditional mead: **D47**. For a first fruit mead: **K1-V1116**.

### 3. Water + nutrient

**Water:** filtered tap water is fine for most regions. If your tap water tastes heavily of chlorine, use bottled spring water or a Brita filter pitcher. Distilled water has no minerals; the yeast needs trace minerals so add a tiny pinch of yeast nutrient to compensate.

**Yeast nutrient:** essential for mead. Unlike beer wort or grape juice, honey has very little nitrogen - the yeast struggles without supplementation. Use:

- **Fermaid O** (organic; preferred) - 5 g per 5-litre batch. Add in 3 staggered doses (at the start, 24 hours in, 72 hours in).
- **Fermaid K** - same protocol, slightly different nitrogen profile.
- **DAP (diammonium phosphate)** - basic nitrogen. Cheaper but harsher. 3 g per 5-litre batch.
- **Wyeast Beer Nutrient** or **Yeastex** - generic blends; OK.

Without nutrient, the yeast will stress and produce off-flavours (fusel alcohol, hot/solventy notes). Don't skip.

## The math

For a basic 5-litre traditional mead at 11-12% ABV:

- 1.2 kg honey
- 4 litres water (top up to 5 litres after honey is mixed in)
- 1 sachet D47 yeast (5-10 g)
- 5 g Fermaid O (staggered)
- Starting gravity: about 1.090 (measured by hydrometer)
- Final gravity (after fermentation): about 1.000 (dry)
- ABV: about 12%

A "sweet" or "sack" mead would use 1.5-2 kg honey for the same 5 litres and ferment to a higher gravity (yeast can't fully ferment all the sugar).

## Cost

A first 5-litre batch costs:

- Demijohn + airlock + tools: £40 (one-time)
- Honey: £10-15
- Yeast + nutrient: £4-6
- Bottling: £20-25 for 8 reusable flip-tops

Total first batch: about £75. Subsequent batches: about £15-20 each (no kit cost).

A 500 ml bottle of decent commercial mead retails at £15-25. Three or four bottles into your own brewing and you've broken even on the gear.
