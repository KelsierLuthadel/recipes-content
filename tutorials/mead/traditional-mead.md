# Traditional Mead

![Traditional mead](resources/traditional.jpg)

*The basic mead. Honey, water, yeast, time. Make this first; everything else (fruit meads, spiced meads, cyser, pyment) is a variation on this foundation.*

## Overview

A 5-litre traditional mead, medium-dry, about 11-12% ABV. Total active time about 40 minutes spread over 16 weeks. Wait time after that: 3+ months bottle-aging.

## Recipe (5 litres)

- 1.2 kg good honey (clover or wildflower for first batch)
- 4 litres filtered water (split: 2 litres warm for dissolving honey, 2 litres cold for top-up)
- 1 sachet Lalvin D47 yeast
- 5 g Fermaid O yeast nutrient (split into 3 doses: 2 g at pitch, 2 g at 24 hours, 1 g at 72 hours)

## Day 0: Brewing day

### Sanitise everything
Mix Star San per instructions (about 2 ml per litre of water). Submerge or spray the demijohn, bung, airlock, funnel, hydrometer, racking cane. Drain (Star San is no-rinse, the residual foam is fine).

### Heat the must
1. Heat 2 litres of water to about 40°C (warm to the touch but not hot).
2. Stir in the 1.2 kg honey until fully dissolved. Don't boil, boiling drives off the honey's aromatics.
3. Pour the honey-water (the "must") into the demijohn through a funnel.
4. Top up with 2 litres of cold water (cooling and filling at the same time).
5. Add 2 g of Fermaid O (the first staggered nutrient dose).
6. Cover with a sanitised lid and shake vigorously for 1 minute to oxygenate (yeast needs oxygen at the start).

### Pitch the yeast
1. Sprinkle the D47 yeast directly on top of the must (no rehydration needed for dry yeast).
2. Cap with the bung and airlock.
3. Put in a cool dark place (ideally 16-19°C; basement, cool cupboard, north-facing room).
4. Within 12-24 hours you should see bubbles in the airlock. This is the start of active fermentation.

### Hydrometer reading
Before pitching the yeast, take a sample with the wine thief and measure the gravity. Should be around 1.085-1.095. Record this as your starting gravity.

## Days 1-4: Active fermentation

The yeast is multiplying and converting sugar to alcohol. You'll see steady bubbling in the airlock (one bubble every few seconds is good).

### Staggered nutrient additions
- **24 hours in:** add 2 g of Fermaid O. Open the airlock briefly, sprinkle the nutrient in, reseal.
- **72 hours in:** add the final 1 g of Fermaid O.

This staggered nutrient feeding is crucial for clean mead. Single-dose nutrient at the start gives the yeast everything at once and tends to produce stressed off-flavours.

## Days 4-21: Primary fermentation

Bubbling slows progressively. After 2-3 weeks, the airlock activity slows to one bubble per minute or less.

### Track gravity
Once a week, take a hydrometer reading. The gravity drops as the yeast converts sugar to alcohol.

- Day 0: about 1.090 (sugar is high)
- Day 7: about 1.030
- Day 14: about 1.005
- Day 21: about 1.000 or close

A reading of 1.000 (or below) for two consecutive weeks means primary fermentation is finished.

## Weeks 4-8: Conditioning in primary

Even after active fermentation stops, leave the mead in the primary demijohn for another 2-4 weeks. The yeast continues to work in subtle ways (consuming residual diacetyl, mellowing fusel alcohols). Don't rush this.

The yeast will start to settle to the bottom (the "lees"). The mead above will gradually clarify.

## Weeks 8-12: Racking to secondary

After 8 weeks total, rack (siphon) the mead off the lees into a fresh sanitised demijohn.

### Racking
1. Sanitise a second demijohn and the racking cane.
2. Place the primary on a higher surface than the secondary.
3. Insert the racking cane into the primary (don't disturb the lees on the bottom).
4. Siphon by sucking the racking cane or using an auto-siphon's bulb action.
5. The mead flows into the secondary. Stop when you see the lees start to come up the tube (leave about 1 cm of lees behind in the primary).
6. Top up the secondary with sanitised cold water if needed (reduces oxidation risk).
7. Fit the airlock to the secondary.

### Take a taste
At this point, taste the mead. It should be:
- Dry (most of the sugar converted)
- Honey-forward (the floral character of the honey survives)
- Slightly "hot" or "rough" (young alcohol; will mellow)
- Clear-ish (still slightly cloudy from yeast)

## Months 3-4: Secondary aging

The mead sits in the secondary for another 4-8 weeks. The flavour matures, harshness softens, the mead clarifies further. Top up with sanitised water any time you transfer or rack to minimise oxidation.

After 4-6 weeks in secondary, decide whether to:
- **Bottle now**: a younger, more honey-forward mead with some heat remaining.
- **Continue aging** in the demijohn for another 4-6 months. Smoother, more complex, more wine-like.

For a first batch: rack to a third demijohn at month 4 if the lees layer is more than 5 mm. Otherwise just leave it.

## Bottling

After at least 3-4 months from pitch (and ideally 6-12 months):

1. Sanitise 8 × 500 ml flip-top bottles.
2. Optionally back-sweeten with 50-100 g honey dissolved in 100 ml warm water (taste and adjust). If you back-sweeten, also add 1 g potassium sorbate per 5 litres to prevent re-fermentation in the bottle.
3. For still mead: bottle as-is.
4. For sparkling mead: add 5 g caster sugar per litre (priming sugar) for natural carbonation in-bottle. Don't add potassium sorbate (it'd block the carbonation).
5. Cap and label with date.
6. Store at 12-15°C (cellar temperature).

## Aging in bottle

Bottle-age at least 3 months before opening the first bottle. 6 months is much better. 12 months is excellent.

Mead's "ugly duckling" phase is roughly the 2-6 month mark, young, sharp, slightly hot. Past 6 months it transforms. The first bottle you open at 12 months will taste like a different drink from the one you bottled.

## Common mistakes (worth avoiding)

- **Boiling the honey**: drives off aromatics. Warm-dissolve only.
- **Skipping the nutrient**: gives stressed, off-flavour mead.
- **Pitching at too-hot temperature** (above 25°C): produces fusel alcohols (hot, solventy notes).
- **Racking too early** (during active fermentation): disturbs the yeast; can stall the ferment.
- **Bottling too early** (before primary is fully done): bottles can explode.
- **Insufficient sanitisation**: wild yeast / bacteria → spoiled mead.
- **Opening the first bottle too early**: you'll be disappointed. Wait at least 3 months.

## What good mead tastes like

A well-made traditional mead is:
- Honey-floral on the nose (clover or wildflower character clearly present)
- Dry-to-medium on the palate
- Slightly warming from the alcohol
- Clean finish (no harsh edges)
- The honey is the foreground; yeast notes are background

Bad signs (don't pour another):
- Solventy / nail-polish smell (fusel alcohols from hot fermentation or low nutrient)
- Vinegar / sourness (acetobacter contamination)
- "Plastic" or "band-aid" notes (Brett contamination)
- Cidery / apple-cider smell (yeast was stressed)

If you make a bad batch, learn from it and try again. The 4th or 5th batch is usually noticeably better than the first.
