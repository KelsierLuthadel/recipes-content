Christmas dinner is a feat of theatre as much as cookery. There is a moment, usually around half past two in the afternoon, when a very large piece of meat comes out of the oven, and everything else at the table has to be ready in the same fifteen-minute window. Yorkshire puddings rising, sprouts glazed, gravy hot, roast potatoes crisp, the table set. The rest of the day is the gentle decline that follows.

The centrepiece can be anything that earns the room: a beef wellington if you want pastry drama, a sauerbraten if you want something marinated for days, a duck confit if you want the smell of duck fat all afternoon. What matters is the table around it: roast potatoes that crackle, sprouts treated with respect rather than boiled into submission, red cabbage shredded with apple and cider vinegar, a stack of Yorkshires. Mince pies are not optional.

Bake one more thing than you think you need. Someone will eat the leftover cannoli at midnight and thank you for it.
