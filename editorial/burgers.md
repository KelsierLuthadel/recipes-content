The burger is the most adaptable Western food export. Every cuisine that
encountered it kept the basic structure (patty, bun, toppings) and then
rebuilt every other element around its own flavour vocabulary. The
result is fifteen national signatures held together by one shared shape.

In America the conversation runs from the lacy-edged smashburger off the
flat-top griddle to the green-chile-cheeseburger of New Mexico, where
the dish is officially the state cuisine icon. Australia layers beetroot
and a fried egg onto the Aussie Burger and rolls in lamb mince with mint
and chilli for the gastropub iteration. The Mediterranean kitchens lean
on lamb: Greek with feta and tzatziki, Lebanese kofta-spiced with
sumac-onions, Turkish with the strange and brilliant Istanbul wet burger
that steams under butter-and-tomato sauce till the bun saturates.

In Asia the burger becomes something else again. Japanese MOS Burger
glazes the patty in teriyaki and finishes with Kewpie mayo. Korean
gastropubs grind bulgogi-marinated beef and crown it with a runny fried
egg and gochujang mayo. Indian fast-food vegetarianism gives the world
the aloo tikki burger, a spiced potato patty with mint-coriander chutney
and tamarind glaze. Tibetan kitchens fold momo flavours into the patty;
Uyghur cooks spice it like a kebab. South Africa heaps the patty with
chakalaka, the country's iconic spicy vegetable relish. Mexican street
cooks rebuild it as a taco al pastor in burger form, charred pineapple
and chipotle mayo included.

Different country, different burger. Same shape, fifteen answers.
