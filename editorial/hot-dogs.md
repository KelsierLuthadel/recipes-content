The hot dog emigrated from German frankfurter stands through Ellis
Island and onto street-cart trays in every American city in the late
nineteenth century. Then it kept moving. Every country that adopted it
gave it different toppings, a different bun, and an opinion about what
should never be allowed near it.

In America the regional rules become law. Chicago bans ketchup and
demands a poppy-seed bun with pickle spear, sport peppers and celery
salt. Coney Island Detroit-style ladles a finely-textured beef chili
over the dog with raw chopped onion and yellow mustard. Carolina piles
red cabbage slaw on top of chili. Memphis wraps the dog in bacon and
drowns it in BBQ sauce. Seattle smears cream cheese on the bun, then
piles cabbage, jalapeños and sriracha on top. New York keeps it minimal:
sauerkraut, the canonical pinkish onion sauce, yellow mustard. Bagel
shops wrap the dog in everything-bagel dough and bake it golden.

Latin America loaded it up. Tijuana wraps in bacon, grills the bell
peppers on the same flat-top, finishes with three sauces. Colombian
street carts pile pineapple, crushed potato chips, mozzarella and crispy
onions on a single dog. Chile's completo gets mashed avocado, chopped
tomato, sauerkraut and mayonnaise stacked taller than the bun.

Asia rewrites it again. Japan's Japadog (born in Vancouver, perfected on
the Burrard Street cart) crowns the dog with shredded nori, dancing
bonito flakes, daikon and Kewpie mayo. Korea's gamja hot dog rolls a
mozzarella stick in batter, coats it in cubes of raw potato, deep-fries
it gold-crispy, then dusts it in sugar before the ketchup-mustard zigzag.

Thirteen recipes, one shape, every country an opinion.
