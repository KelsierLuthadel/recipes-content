# Normandy Sauce

![Normandy sauce](resources/normandy-sauce.png)

*The classic sauce for sole à la Normande, this goes well with most white fish. The addition of mussel cooking juices makes it extra special.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 35 minutes

## Overview
Sauce Normande is the building block for the classic French sole à la Normande and any refined white fish preparation: a silky white sauce that takes a velouté base, adds sliced mushrooms and thyme during the cook, then finishes with an egg yolk and cream liaison and a squeeze of lemon. The optional addition of mussel cooking juices (saved from a separate batch of moules marinière) is what makes it properly Norman; the briny depth turns a competent fish velouté into something with the sea's character running underneath. Two technique points are critical. First, the white roux. Melt 30 g of butter, pull off the heat, stir in the flour, then return to medium-low and cook 2 to 3 minutes stirring constantly to make a properly cooked white roux (raw flour taste must be cooked out, but you don't want any browning; this is a white sauce, not a brown one). Second, the egg yolk liaison. Mix the cream with the egg yolks in a separate bowl before adding to the hot sauce, then stir the mixture in slowly while whisking; cold yolks straight into hot sauce scramble immediately, while a properly slackened pre-mix slips in cleanly to thicken the sauce without curdling. In a separate pan, melt the remaining butter and sweat sliced button mushrooms with a thyme sprig for 2 minutes, then stir in the hot roux. Gradually pour in fish stock (and mussel juice if using) while whisking, bring to the boil, then drop to a gentle bubble for 20 minutes whisking every 5 minutes. Stir in the cream-and-yolk mixture with lemon juice and bubble gently another 10 minutes; do not boil after the yolks go in or you'll scramble them. Season and strain through a fine-meshed conical sieve. Serve immediately over poached sole, turbot or any delicate white fish.

## Ingredients

### Base
- 60 grams butter
- 30 grams plain flour

### Vegetables & aromatics
- 100 grams button mushrooms (thinly sliced)
- 1 sprig thyme

### Liquid
- 500 ml Fish stock
- 50 ml mussel juice (optional)

### Finishing
- 200 ml double cream
- 3 egg yolks
- ½ lemon (juice)
- salt
- pepper

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make roux
1. Melt 30 grams of butter in a heavy-bases saucepan, then take off the heat and stir in the flour. 
1. Return to a medium-low heat and cook for 2-3 minutes, stirring constantly, to make a white roux.

### Stage 2 - Sweat mushrooms
1. Meanwhile, melt the remaining 30 grams of butter in another saucepan over a low heat. 
1. Add the mushrooms and thyme and sweat them for 2 minutes, then stir in the hot roux.

### Stage 3 - Build sauce  
1. Gradually pour in the fish stock and mussel juice, if using, mixing with a small whisk.
1. Bring to the boil, still whisking, and let the sauce bubble gently for 20 minutes, stirring it with the whisk every 5 minutes.

### Stage 4 - Finish with egg yolks
1. Meanwhile, mix the cream with the egg yolks. Stir the mixture into the sauce with the lemon juice and let it continue to bubble gently for another 10 minutes.
1. Season to taste with salt and white pepper. Pass the sauce through a fine-meshed conical sieve and serve immediately.

## Notes
- **White roux:** Cook adequately to remove raw flour taste but don't let it colour; control heat carefully.
- **Egg liaison:** Mix cream and yolks well before adding; add slowly while stirring to prevent scrambling.
- **Mussel juice:** If available from mussels cooked à la mari nière, add for authentic Norman flavour; optional but recommended.

## Serving
Serve immediately with sole, other white fish fillets, or shellfish. Classic pairing for sole à la Normande.

## Storage
- Best eaten immediately after preparation.
- Keeps refrigerated for 1 day; reheat gently, stirring constantly to maintain emulsion without boiling.
- Does not freeze well due to egg yolk content and emulsion instability.
