# Mayonnaise

![Mayonnaise](resources/mayonnaise.png)

*A fundamental emulsion sauce that serves as the foundation for countless derivatives.*

**Serves:** Makes 300ml

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 0 minutes

## Overview
Mayonnaise is the building block for an entire family of cold sauces (aïoli, rouille, sauce verte, tartare, gribiche, sauce cocktail, remoulade) and a kitchen fundamental in its own right, the cold emulsion that goes alongside sandwiches, cold poached salmon, cold roast chicken, crudités and salads. Three ingredients (egg yolks, oil, an acid) and one technique (slow whisking) build a glossy stable emulsion. The technique is the whole game and three details keep the emulsion from breaking. First, every ingredient must be at room temperature. Cold yolks straight from the fridge will split as soon as the oil hits them, so let them sit on the counter for half an hour before you start. Second, the oil goes in slowly at the start, especially the first quarter. Stand a mixing bowl on a damp tea-towel on the work surface to stop it slipping while you whisk, drop in the egg yolks, mustard, salt and pepper, and whisk with a balloon whisk till smooth. Now trickle the oil in a thin steady stream while whisking constantly; this is the moment the emulsion forms, and rushing it gives you a greasy split mess. Once the mayonnaise has visibly thickened, you can speed up and pour the rest of the oil in a faster stream. Third, the acid (white wine vinegar or lemon juice) goes in at the end. Adding it too early thins the emulsion before it has the chance to build. Once all the oil is in, whisk for a final 30 seconds till the mayonnaise turns thick and glossy, then beat in the vinegar or lemon, taste and adjust. If you mess it up (the mixture goes oily and split rather than thick), don't throw it away; take a fresh yolk and a teaspoon of mustard in a clean bowl, whisk together, then slowly trickle the broken mayonnaise into the new base.

## Ingredients

### Base & emulsifier
- 2 egg yolks (at room temperature)
- 1 tablespoon Dijon mustard (strong)

### Oil & acid
- 250 ml groundnut oil
- 2 tablespoons white wine vinegar (or lemon juice)

### Seasoning
- salt
- pepper

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare base
1. Stand a mixing bowl on a tea-towel on the work surface to prevent it slipping.
1. Put the egg yolks, mustard, salt and pepper into the bowl and mix with a balloon whisk.

### Stage 2 - Initial emulsification
1. Slowly add the oil in a thin trickle to begin with, whisking continuously.
1. As the mayonnaise begins to thicken, add the oil in a steady stream, still whisking all the time.

### Stage 3 - Final incorporation
1. When the oil is completely incorporated, whisk more rapidly for 30 seconds until the mayonnaise is thick and glossy.

### Stage 4 - Season
1. Add the vinegar or lemon juice, taste and adjust the seasoning as necessary.

## Notes
- **Broken mayonnaise recovery:** If it separates, leave out at room temperature 30 minutes. Using a high-speed mixer, beat in half teaspoon fresh mustard. Put a spoonful of warm water in clean bowl and beat in a spoonful of broken mayonnaise at high speed. The mixture should foam. Add more broken mayonnaise gradually, beating constantly, until emulsion reforms.
- **Temperature:** All ingredients must be at room temperature for proper emulsification.
- **Oil addition:** Never rush; slow addition of oil is key to smooth emulsion.

## Serving
- Serve with salads, cold meats, sandwiches, crudités, or as base for flavored mayonnaises (aioli, rouille, etc.).

## Storage
- Keeps refrigerated in an airtight container for 5-7 days.
- Does not freeze; emulsion breaks upon thawing.
- Best when made fresh; flavour degrades slightly with time.
