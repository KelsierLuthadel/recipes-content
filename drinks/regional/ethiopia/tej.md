# Tej (Ethiopian Honey Wine)

![Tej](resources/tej.jpg)

*Honey, water and gesho (a hop-like Ethiopian shrub) fermented for two to three weeks into Ethiopia's golden traditional honey wine, served in round-bottomed flasks called berele.*

**Serves:** makes about 1.5 litres

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus 14 to 21 days fermenting)

**Cook Time:** 5 minutes

## Overview
Tej is Ethiopia's traditional honey wine, brewed in homes and at dedicated tej bets (tej houses) across the country: water, wild honey and gesho - the dried leaves and twigs of Rhamnus prinoides, a shrub that acts as the local equivalent of hops, contributing bitterness and helping the ferment - fermented with wild or added yeast for two to three weeks. The result is sweet, slightly sour, faintly bitter from the gesho, golden in colour, around 7 to 11% alcohol. Served in a round-bottomed glass flask (berele) you hold by the neck, sipped slowly alongside spicy stews like doro wat. Tej without gesho is just mead; tej without honey is impossible. Gesho is sold dried at Ethiopian groceries; substitutes (hops + a pinch of bitter orange peel) work but read different.

## Ingredients

- 1.5 litres cold water (filtered or spring water; chlorinated tap water kills the yeast)
- 750 g pure honey (a mild wildflower honey, not strong-flavoured like manuka)
- 30 g dried gesho leaves and twigs (from any Ethiopian grocer; OR substitute 10 g dried hops + 1 teaspoon dried bitter orange peel)
- 5 g champagne yeast or ale yeast (or 1/4 teaspoon active dry yeast)

### Equipment
- A 2-litre glass jar or fermenter with a loose-fitting lid (or an airlock)
- A muslin cloth for straining
- Glass bottles for storage

### To serve
- Berele flasks (round-bottomed glass; from Ethiopian groceries) OR any small wine glass

## Method

### Stage 1 - Mix the must
1. Warm 500 ml of the water in a saucepan over low heat (not above 40°C - hot water destroys honey's enzymes).
1. Stir in the honey until completely dissolved.
1. Pour into the fermenter; add the remaining 1 litre of cold water. The temperature should now be lukewarm to body temperature.

### Stage 2 - Add gesho
1. Lightly toast the gesho in a dry pan over low heat for 2 to 3 minutes until faintly aromatic.
1. Add to the honey water; stir.

### Stage 3 - Pitch the yeast
1. Sprinkle the yeast over the surface; stir gently to dissolve.
1. Cover loosely with a clean cloth held by an elastic band (or attach an airlock).

### Stage 4 - Ferment
1. Leave at room temperature (18 to 22°C) for 14 to 21 days.
1. The mixture will bubble actively in the first week, then slow down.
1. Taste at day 14; if still very sweet, leave another week. The finished tej should be mildly sweet with the gesho bitterness coming through.

### Stage 5 - Strain and bottle
1. Strain through a fine sieve lined with muslin into a clean jug; discard the spent gesho.
1. Decant into clean glass bottles; refrigerate.
1. Drink within 2 months; flavour deepens for the first week in the bottle then stays stable.

## Notes
- **Gesho is the defining ingredient.** Without it, you have a mead, not a tej. Ethiopian and Eritrean groceries stock it dried.
- **Wild fermentation works too.** Skip the cultivated yeast entirely; ambient yeasts will start a ferment within 48 hours. Slower (3 to 4 weeks) and more variable but traditional. Use a slightly thinner muslin cloth so airborne yeasts can fall in.
- **Cool temperature, slow ferment.** Above 25°C the yeast can produce off-flavours; below 18°C it stalls. Room temperature in a UK kitchen is usually right.
- **Berele is the traditional vessel.** A small round-bottomed flask you cradle in one hand; sold cheaply at Ethiopian groceries. Any small wine glass works.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 2 months in sealed bottles. The flavour is best in weeks 2 to 6 after bottling.
