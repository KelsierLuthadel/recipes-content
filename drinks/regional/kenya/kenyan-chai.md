# Kenyan Chai

![Kenyan Chai](resources/kenyan-chai.jpg)

*The Kenyan home version of milk tea: strong black tea leaves stewed with whole milk, cardamom and ginger over a steady simmer until rich, sweet and amber, the drink that opens and closes every day.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Kenyan chai is the everyday drink of the country, brewed in a heavy saucepan multiple times a day in most homes. The technique is distinct: equal parts water and whole milk brought to a simmer together with loose black tea (Kenyan, of course; Kenya is among the world's largest tea producers), bruised cardamom pods and a fat slice of ginger, then simmered for ten minutes until it reduces, turns a deep amber and develops a faintly creamy froth. Sugar is added at the end (Kenyans take their tea sweet, two teaspoons a cup is standard). It is poured from a height to aerate, into mugs (or for special guests, glass cups), and drunk with mandazi, mahamri or chapati. The chai is the social glue: meetings, family visits, business deals, school mornings all start and end here.

## Ingredients

- 500 ml water
- 500 ml whole milk
- 4 tbsp loose black tea (Kenyan CTC tea, or any strong black tea; bags work as a substitute)
- 6 green cardamom pods, lightly crushed
- 2 cm piece of fresh ginger, sliced or smashed
- 2 to 4 tbsp sugar (to taste; Kenyan tea is sweet)
- A small cinnamon stick (optional, less traditional but common in some homes)
- A pinch of ground black pepper (optional, for warmth)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Combine
1. In a heavy saucepan, combine the water, milk, crushed cardamom pods, sliced ginger, cinnamon (if using) and pepper (if using).
1. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat. Do not let it boil over; if it climbs the pan, lift it off the heat for a second and return.

### Stage 2 - Add the tea
1. Once simmering, add the loose tea leaves; stir once.
1. Reduce heat to medium-low; simmer 10 minutes, stirring every couple of minutes to prevent a skin forming.
1. The chai will darken, reduce slightly, and develop a creamy, slightly frothy surface.

### Stage 3 - Sweeten and finish
1. Stir in the sugar (start with 2 tbsp; taste; add more if you like).
1. Bring the heat up to high for 30 seconds; let the chai climb, just to the brim of the pan; remove instantly.
1. Pour through a fine sieve into a teapot or directly into mugs from a height of 30 cm (aerates and froths).

## Notes
- **Equal parts water and milk.** The classic ratio. Some homes go heavier on milk (60:40); fewer go heavier on water (the diluted "weak tea" is a less prestigious version).
- **CTC tea.** Kenyan CTC (crush-tear-curl) is the most common style locally; it gives the deep amber colour and strong flavour. Standard breakfast tea bags work as a substitute (2 bags per 500 ml).
- **Cardamom is essential.** Without it, the tea is just milky tea. The pods should be lightly crushed to release the seeds.
- **Sweetness.** Kenyan chai is sweet. Two teaspoons per cup is standard; underaweet chai tastes strange to a Kenyan palate. Adjust to taste but do not go fully unsweetened.
- **No milk-first vs milk-last argument.** Kenyan chai is everything-together-from-the-start. The British milk debate does not apply.

## Variations
- **Masala chai:** add 2 cloves and a pinch of nutmeg with the cardamom for the more spice-heavy Indo-Kenyan version.
- **Tangawizi chai:** double the ginger, drop the cardamom, the ginger-tea version drunk for colds.
- **Chai ya rangi:** "coloured tea", made with water only (no milk); the cheaper Indian-style street version, also common.
- **Lemongrass chai:** add a stalk of crushed lemongrass; an East African coastal variant.
- **Saturday brew:** add an extra spoon of tea and a splash of evaporated milk for the richer weekend pot.

## Serving
A steaming mug · alongside mandazi or mahamri for breakfast · with a slice of chapati for an afternoon tea-time · poured for guests as the first gesture of welcome · sweetened to taste at the table or pre-sweetened in the pot.

## Storage
- Best drunk within 30 minutes of brewing.
- Refrigerate up to 24 hours; reheat gently in a pan (not the microwave, which curdles the milk).
- Do not freeze.
- A second-brew from the spent leaves is unrewarding; throw and start fresh.
