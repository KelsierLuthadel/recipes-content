# Belavezhskaya Pushcha Bitter

![Belavezhskaya Pushcha Bitter](resources/belavezhskaya-pushcha-bitter.jpg)

*A Belarusian herbal digestif named after the ancient primeval forest on the Polish border: vodka infused with juniper berries, bison grass, pine buds, oak bark and a long list of bog herbs, sipped neat after a heavy meal.*

**Serves:** about 700 ml

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes (plus 3 weeks infusing)

**Cook Time:** none

## Overview
Belavezhskaya Pushcha is the 150,000-hectare primeval forest that straddles the Belarus-Poland border, the last remnant of the old lowland forest that once covered northern Europe, home to the European bison and a botany of medicinal herbs that no commercial farming has touched. The bitter named after the forest is a Soviet-era distillery product (the Brest brand has been made since 1947) but the home-infused version has its roots in much older monastic and Old Believer traditions: a base spirit, a careful list of bitter and aromatic plants picked at the right season, weeks of patience and a final sweetening with a touch of honey. The home recipe below is a workable approximation: juniper berries and pine buds carry the resinous forest note, oak bark and gentian carry the bitter spine, dried apple and lemon peel lift the citric edge, bison grass (or substitutes; the real thing is restricted) gives the green hay perfume. The result is dark amber, perfumed, properly bitter, served in a small glass with no ice as the final touch to a heavy table.

## Ingredients

- 700 ml good clean vodka (40 percent)
- 2 tbsp dried juniper berries, lightly cracked
- 1 tbsp dried pine or fir buds (or 1 fresh small sprig of rosemary as substitute)
- 1 tsp dried oak bark (from a herbalist; or skip if unavailable)
- 1 tsp dried gentian root (from a herbalist; or 1 tsp wormwood as a substitute)
- 1 dried bay leaf
- 4 dried apple slices
- 1 long strip of unwaxed lemon peel
- 4 black peppercorns
- 4 cloves
- 1/4 tsp ground nutmeg
- 1 small dried bison-grass blade (zubrowka; or 1 tsp dried sweet woodruff, both restricted in some countries; or skip)
- 2 tbsp dark forest honey (added at the end)

### Equipment
- 1 litre glass jar with a tight lid
- Funnel, muslin, 750 ml bottle

## Method

### Stage 1 - First infusion
1. In a clean 1 litre glass jar, combine all the dried herbs, spices, apple slices, lemon peel and bison grass (if using).
2. Pour over the vodka and seal tightly.
3. Shake well.
4. Store in a cool dark cupboard for 21 days. Shake the jar every 2 to 3 days.

### Stage 2 - Strain
1. After 3 weeks the vodka will have turned a deep amber-brown and smell of forest, resin and citrus.
2. Line a funnel with muslin and pour the infused vodka through into a clean jug. Discard the solids.

### Stage 3 - Sweeten
1. Warm the honey gently in a small pan with 2 tablespoons of hot water until just liquid.
2. Stir the honey-water into the strained vodka.
3. Pour into a clean 750 ml bottle, cap tightly, and let the bitter rest a further 5 to 7 days in the cupboard before drinking.

### Stage 4 - Serve
1. Pour 20 to 30 ml into a small thimble glass or a small heavy tumbler.
2. Serve at cellar temperature (around 14 to 16°C); not chilled, not room-temperature.
3. Sip slowly after a heavy meal; the bitter cuts the fat and signals the end of the table.

## Notes
- **The herbs are non-negotiable for character but flexible for availability.** Juniper, lemon peel, bay and honey are the must-haves; gentian/wormwood is the bitter spine. The rest can be adjusted to what your herbalist actually has.
- **Bison grass (zubrowka) is restricted in many countries** because of its coumarin content. Sweet woodruff is a close legal substitute in most jurisdictions. The drink works fine without either.
- **Pine buds give the forest perfume.** A herbalist or specialist Eastern European deli will stock dried pine buds; rosemary is a workable substitute though less resinous.
- **Sweeten late.** Honey added at the start can muddy the infusion; added once strained, it stays bright.

## Variations
- **Krupnik-leaning.** Triple the honey for a sweet, almost liqueur-like drink; the line between bitter and krupnik blurs in many country households.
- **Spruce-tip bitter.** Replace the pine buds with fresh young spruce tips picked in May; brighter, lemonier, more medicinal.
- **Beaver-stone bitter.** A historical recipe added a tiny chip of "kastoreum" (beaver-gland tincture, available in some herbalists) for a deep musk; a niche, museum-piece variant.
- **Bog-myrtle bitter.** Add 1 tsp of dried bog myrtle leaves; a Polesia-region marsh variant.

## Serving
- Serve neat in small glasses after a heavy meal · also as a digestif with strong black coffee · at cellar temperature, never iced · paired with a piece of dark chocolate or a single salted nut

## Storage
- Keeps indefinitely in a tightly capped bottle in a cool dark cupboard
- The bitter mellows over the first year and stays good for many years
- Do not refrigerate; the perfume dulls
- A sediment may settle over time; either decant once a year or live with it
