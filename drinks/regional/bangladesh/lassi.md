# Bangladeshi Salted Lassi

![Bangladeshi Salted Lassi](resources/lassi.jpg)

*Bangladeshi savoury yogurt drink: thick yogurt whisked thin with cold water, roasted cumin, fine and black salt, finely chopped green chilli and fresh mint; the summer cooler of choice in mid-July heat.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 2 minutes

## Overview
The Indian and Bangladeshi lassi traditions diverged a long time ago. The sweet mango lassi most diners abroad think of is a Punjabi confection; in Bangladesh the standard lassi is the savoury version, thinned with iced water and seasoned with toasted cumin, two kinds of salt, finely chopped green chilli and a tear of fresh mint. It is the drink reached for when the temperature hits 38 degrees C in late June, when the humidity is unforgiving and a hot tea is unthinkable. The cumin and the salts together act as a folk electrolyte; villagers drink a tall glass at midday during the heaviest fieldwork hours. The texture should be thinner than a smoothie, thicker than juice; the chilli should register clearly but not burn.

## Ingredients

- 1 teaspoon cumin seeds
- 500 g thick full-fat plain yogurt
- 500 ml cold water (or chilled milk for richer)
- 1 green chilli, very finely chopped (deseeded for milder)
- 8 to 10 fresh mint leaves, torn
- 1 teaspoon fine salt
- 1 teaspoon black salt (kala namak)
- ½ teaspoon caster sugar (optional, to balance)
- Plenty of ice cubes

### To serve
- A pinch of toasted cumin powder
- A small sprig of mint
- A thin lime wedge

## Method

1. Toast the cumin seeds in a small dry pan over medium heat for 60 seconds, shaking the pan, until they smell deeply nutty and turn a shade darker. Tip onto a plate; cool; crush coarsely in a pestle and mortar.
2. Whisk the yogurt in a wide jug or bowl until smooth and lump-free.
3. Whisk in the cold water gradually until you reach the consistency of single cream.
4. Stir in most of the crushed cumin (keep a pinch back for the top), the chopped green chilli, torn mint, fine salt, black salt and sugar if using.
5. Taste; adjust salt. The lassi should read clearly savoury, slightly hot from the chilli and rounded by the cumin.
6. Pour over plenty of ice in tall glasses; top with the reserved cumin, a mint sprig and a thin slice of lime perched on the rim.

## Notes
- **Both salts are required.** Fine salt for body, black salt (kala namak) for the slightly sulphurous mineral edge that defines a properly savoury lassi.
- **Toast the cumin fresh.** Pre-ground cumin from a jar reads flat; 60 seconds in a dry pan transforms it.
- **Thin yogurt to single-cream consistency.** Too thick and it sits like a milkshake; too thin and it watery.
- **Real chilli, finely chopped.** A whole blended chilli overpowers; finely chopped lets the heat come through cleanly without bitterness.
- **Use full-fat yogurt.** Low-fat yogurt thins to a watery base that lacks the proper lassi body.

## Variations
- **With ginger:** grate 2 cm fresh ginger into the lassi for a stronger savoury bite.
- **With coriander:** add a small handful of fresh coriander leaves blitzed in for a green-tinged version.
- **Doi-er ghol:** the Bengali cousin, thinner still, with mustard oil floated on top.
- **With curry leaves:** infuse 4 curry leaves in a teaspoon of hot oil; pour into the lassi for a fragrant top note.
- **Spicier:** add 2 green chillies and a pinch of black pepper for a fire-and-ice version popular with biryani.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 24 hours in a sealed jug
- Whisk vigorously to recombine before serving (the yogurt separates)
- Do not freeze; the yogurt texture breaks down on thaw
