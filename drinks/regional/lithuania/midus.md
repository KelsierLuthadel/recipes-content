# Midus

![Midus](resources/midus.jpg)

*Lithuania's ancient honey wine: fermented honey, water and herbs aged for months into a golden amber drink with the sweetness of nectar and the depth of old oak, poured at weddings and midsummer feasts since the time of the pagan Balts.*

**Serves:** Makes 4 litres (about 32 servings of 125 ml)

**Prep Time:** 1 hour

**Cook Time:** 30 minutes (plus 3-6 months ageing)

## Overview
Midus is the oldest known alcoholic drink of the Baltic peoples, a mead that the pagan Lithuanians were already drinking when Christianity reached them in the fourteenth century, long after most of Europe had given up mead for wine and beer. The tradition survived in pockets through the centuries and has had a strong revival; today the famous Stakliškės distillery in Aukštaitija makes some of the most respected honey wines in Europe, with examples aged ten and twenty years. The basic method is ancient and simple: pure honey is dissolved in water, herbs and spices are added (juniper, hops, caraway, lime flower, sometimes nutmeg or cloves), yeast is introduced and the must is fermented slowly for weeks or months. After the primary ferment, the mead is racked and aged in oak or glass for three to twelve months before bottling. The colour deepens from pale gold to amber to chestnut; the flavour develops from sweet honey to complex herb-and-oak. The Lithuanian style sits between sweet and semi-sweet; properly aged midus is a remarkable drink.

## Ingredients

- 1.5 kg pure raw honey (linden or wildflower for the most traditional flavour)
- 3.5 litres spring water
- 5 g wine yeast (a champagne or mead yeast)
- 1 tsp yeast nutrient (available from brew shops)
- 20 g juniper berries, lightly crushed
- 10 g whole hops (or 1 tbsp dried hop cones)
- 1 tsp caraway seeds
- 6 lime-flower (linden) buds, dried
- 2 cloves
- Zest of 1 lemon (no white pith)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Sanitise
1. Sanitise a 5-litre fermentation jar, airlock, stirring spoon and funnel with brewing sanitiser (or boil all metal/glass parts for 10 minutes).
2. Cleanliness is everything for mead; any wild bacteria spoil the brew.

### Stage 2 - Make the must
1. Pour the spring water into a large clean stockpot.
2. Heat gently to 65°C (do not boil; high heat drives off the honey aromatics).
3. Stir in the honey until completely dissolved.
4. Add the juniper, hops, caraway, lime-flower, cloves and lemon zest.
5. Hold at 65°C for 20 minutes; this kills wild yeasts without harming flavour.
6. Cool to 20°C (set the pot in a sink of cold water to speed this).

### Stage 3 - Pitch the yeast
1. Once cool, strain the must through clean muslin into the sanitised fermentation jar.
2. Sprinkle the yeast and yeast nutrient across the top.
3. Stir gently with a sanitised spoon.

### Stage 4 - Primary fermentation
1. Fit the airlock filled with sanitiser water.
2. Stand the jar at 18-22°C in a dark spot.
3. Bubbling starts within 24-48 hours and runs for 2-3 weeks.
4. When the bubble rate slows to once every 30-40 seconds, primary is done.

### Stage 5 - Rack to secondary
1. Sanitise a second clean jar.
2. Siphon the clear mead carefully, leaving the sediment behind.
3. Top up with cooled boiled water if needed to reach the neck.
4. Fit a fresh airlock.

### Stage 6 - Age
1. Store at 12-16°C (a cool cellar or unheated room) for 3-6 months.
2. Some makers age 12 months for a refined character.
3. The colour deepens; the flavour rounds and softens.

### Stage 7 - Bottle
1. Sanitise glass bottles and stoppers.
2. Siphon the clear mead into bottles.
3. Cork or cap; label with the date.
4. Lay flat in a dark cool place.

### Stage 8 - Serve
1. Open after at least 6 months from bottling for the best flavour.
2. Pour at cellar temperature (14-16°C) into small wine glasses.
3. Sip slowly; midus is a meditation drink, not a session drink.

## Notes
- **Honey quality is everything:** the cheapest blended honey gives a thin mead. Use the best raw honey you can afford; the lime (linden) honey of Aukštaitija is the most prized.
- **Sanitise obsessively:** any contamination ruins six months of patience.
- **Don't boil the honey:** above 65°C the floral aromatics evaporate; gentle warmth dissolves it without loss.
- **Patience finishes it:** a one-month-old midus is rough. A six-month midus is good. A two-year midus is remarkable.

## Variations
**Sweet midus:** use 2 kg honey to 3 litres water for a sweeter style; less fermentation, more residual sugar.
**Spiced (krupnikas-style):** add cinnamon, vanilla, nutmeg and saffron for a Christmas mead.
**Berry midus:** add 500 g crushed lingonberries or blackcurrants to the secondary; the deep red Žemaitija version.
**Hop-forward:** double the hops for a more beer-like bitter mead.
**Quick mead (1 month):** drinkable young but loses the depth that long ageing brings.

## Serving
Pour at cellar temperature into small glasses · at weddings · at midsummer · at Christmas · as an after-dinner sip · with sweet curd cheese · with šakotis · as a digestif · the most Lithuanian of all toasts.

## Storage
- Bottled midus keeps 5 years easily, often improves up to 10.
- Store flat in a cool dark place.
- Once opened, drink within 2 weeks.
- Sweet midus keeps longer than dry.
