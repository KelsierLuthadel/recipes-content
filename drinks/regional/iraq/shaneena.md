# Shaneena

![Shaneena](resources/shaneena.jpg)

*Iraqi salted yogurt drink: thick laban whisked with cold water, salt, dried mint and sometimes a crushed dried lime, served deeply chilled in tall glasses alongside heavy meals. Cooling, savoury, the antidote to a Baghdad summer afternoon.*

**Serves:** 4 tall glasses

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 0 minutes

## Overview
Shaneena (sometimes spelled shineena, شنينه) is Iraq's everyday yogurt drink and the regional sibling of Turkish ayran, Iranian doogh and Indian buttermilk. The base is full-fat plain yogurt (laban, ideally thick set yogurt from a Middle Eastern grocery) whisked smooth with cold water, salt and a generous shake of dried mint. The defining Iraqi touch is the optional crushed dried lime (loomi or limu): one whole dried lime cracked open, the seeds discarded and the dried flesh whisked in or steeped briefly, adds a sour-aromatic depth that no Turkish or Iranian version has. Served deeply chilled in tall glasses with a few extra mint flecks scattered on top, it's the standard drink alongside heavy rice-and-stew meals (like timman bagilla or quzi) where the yogurt's coolness cuts the richness. Outside of meals you'll find it in clay jugs at the souq in summer, where it's pulled cold from ice tubs and sold by the small glass.

## Ingredients

- 400 g full-fat plain yogurt (Greek-style works; Middle Eastern thick laban is better)
- 600 ml cold water (chilled)
- 1 teaspoon fine salt (start here, taste and adjust)
- 2 teaspoons dried mint, crumbled between your fingers (more for stronger mint)
- 1 dried lime (loomi/limu), cracked open and seeds discarded (optional, the Iraqi signature)
- 1 small clove garlic, finely grated (optional, for the savoury version)
- A few ice cubes (optional, but the drink should be very cold)

### To serve
- A small pinch of dried mint, plus a fresh mint sprig if you have one
- 4 tall glasses, chilled

## Method

### Stage 1 - Loosen the yogurt
1. Spoon the yogurt into a large jug or bowl.
1. Whisk with a fork or balloon whisk until completely smooth, with no lumps; about 30 seconds.

### Stage 2 - Build the drink
1. Slowly whisk in the cold water in a steady stream. Pouring it all at once gives a clumpy result; slow and steady gives a smooth, creamy texture.
1. Whisk in the salt and dried mint.
1. If using the dried lime: crack it open, remove all the seeds (they're bitter), and crumble the flesh straight into the jug. Whisk in; let it sit 5 minutes to release its sour-aromatic flavour, then strain through a fine sieve back into the jug if you want a smooth drink, or leave the flecks in for a more rustic finish.
1. If using garlic: whisk in the grated garlic last.

### Stage 3 - Chill and taste
1. Refrigerate for 15 to 30 minutes to drop the temperature, or add a few ice cubes directly to the jug.
1. Taste: it should be salty, tangy from the yogurt, and aromatic from the mint. Adjust salt if flat; add more mint if it tastes one-note.

### Stage 4 - Serve
1. Pour into chilled tall glasses.
1. Scatter a pinch more dried mint on top of each glass, plus a fresh mint sprig if you have one.
1. Serve immediately, very cold.

## Notes
- **Whisk the yogurt smooth before adding water.** This is the single technique that separates good shaneena from lumpy shaneena. Loosen first, then thin.
- **Cold ingredients.** The yogurt and water both from the fridge. Shaneena should bracingly cold; lukewarm just tastes flat and salty.
- **Dried mint over fresh.** Counterintuitive, but the right call: dried mint gives a deeper, slightly hay-like quality that fresh mint can't. If you only have fresh, use double the quantity and rub between your palms to bruise it.
- **The dried lime is what makes it Iraqi.** Without it, you've made ayran or doogh; with it, you've made shaneena. If you can't find loomi, a small squeeze of fresh lemon stands in but the flavour is different (brighter, less aromatic).

## Variations
- **With cucumber (xiyar shaneena).** Add half a finely grated cucumber for a fresher, summer version. Common at home in summer.
- **With garlic and dried mint only.** Skip the loomi for the most stripped-back home version.
- **As a marinade.** Shaneena makes a brilliant marinade for grilled chicken or lamb; the acid and salt do most of the work.
- **Doogh-style (carbonated).** Replace the water with chilled soda water for a fizzier Persian-Iraqi crossover.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 24 hours in a sealed jug. Whisk again before serving as the yogurt may settle. After a day the flavour gets sharper and the texture thinner; still drinkable, but freshest is best.
