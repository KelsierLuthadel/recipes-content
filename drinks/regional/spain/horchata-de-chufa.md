# Horchata de Chufa (Valencian Tiger-Nut Milk)

![Horchata de Chufa](resources/horchata-de-chufa.jpg)

*Valencia's signature cold drink: tiger nuts soaked overnight, ground with water, strained, sweetened with sugar, served deeply chilled in a tall glass. Looks like milk, tastes faintly nutty-sweet with an almond-vanilla edge, and is completely dairy-free. The Spanish answer to a heat wave.*

**Serves:** 4 tall glasses (makes 1 litre)

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus 12 hours tiger-nut soak)

**Cook Time:** 0 minutes

## Overview
Horchata de chufa is a Valencian specialty, completely distinct from the Mexican horchata that shares its name. Where Mexican horchata is rice-based, Valencian horchata is made from chufas (tiger nuts), the small wrinkled tubers of the cyperus esculentus sedge, grown predominantly in the Mediterranean wetlands around Valencia. Tiger nuts are soaked overnight to soften, then blitzed with cold water, sieved through fine cloth, sweetened with sugar, sometimes flavoured with a touch of cinnamon or lemon zest, and chilled until ice-cold. The drink looks like a thin milk, tastes faintly nutty and almond-like with a clean sweetness, and pairs traditionally with fartons (long sugar-glazed pastries you dip into the horchata). Every Valencian café and ice-cream parlour serves it from May to September; outside Spain it's harder to find but tiger nuts are widely sold at health food shops and South Asian / Middle Eastern groceries (where they're called "kunda"). The drink is naturally dairy-free, gluten-free and has been called the "world's healthiest milk substitute" in modern food press.

## Ingredients

- 250 g dried tiger nuts (chufas, kunda)
- 1 litre cold water (for blending)
- 100 to 150 g caster sugar, to taste
- A 5 cm strip of unwaxed lemon peel (optional, traditional)
- 1 cinnamon stick (optional, traditional)
- A pinch of fine sea salt

### To serve
- Plenty of ice cubes (crushed for the proper texture, cubes work)
- 4 tall glasses, chilled
- Optional: fartons (Valencian pastries) for dipping

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak the tiger nuts
1. Rinse the tiger nuts in cold water through a sieve to remove dust.
1. Put them in a large bowl, cover with cold water by at least 4 cm, and refrigerate at least 12 hours (overnight is ideal; 24 hours is even better). The tiger nuts will swell visibly and become noticeably softer when ready.

### Stage 2 - Blend
1. Drain the tiger nuts; rinse once more in cold water.
1. Put them into a high-powered blender with 500 ml of the 1 litre of cold water.
1. Blitz on high for 2 to 3 minutes until you have a thick milky liquid. The longer you blend, the more flavour and body you extract.
1. Add the remaining 500 ml of cold water and blitz again for 30 seconds to combine.

### Stage 3 - Strain
1. Set a fine sieve over a large jug, then lay a clean muslin cloth (or a nut milk bag) inside the sieve.
1. Pour the blended liquid through, then gather the cloth and squeeze hard to extract every drop. This step matters: under-strained horchata is gritty.
1. The pulp left behind can be dried and used as flour, or composted.

### Stage 4 - Sweeten and flavour
1. Stir 100 g of caster sugar into the strained liquid until completely dissolved.
1. Add the lemon peel and cinnamon stick (if using) and a pinch of salt.
1. Taste: the drink should be subtly sweet but not cloying, about as sweet as horchata is in a Valencian café. Add another 50 g sugar if it tastes flat.

### Stage 5 - Chill and infuse
1. Refrigerate at least 4 hours, or overnight. The cinnamon and lemon infuse slowly into the cold liquid.
1. Strain once more before serving to remove the spices.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Pour into chilled tall glasses over crushed ice (or cubes).
1. Stir well before drinking; the tiger-nut starch settles to the bottom as it sits.
1. Serve with fartons on the side if you have them; otherwise a plain wedge of lemon-iced pastry works.

## Notes
- **The soak is non-negotiable.** Tiger nuts are rock-hard when dry. Skipping the soak gives a gritty, flavourless drink. 12 hours minimum.
- **Strain through cloth.** A sieve alone leaves fine sediment. Cloth (muslin or a nut-milk bag) gives the smooth pale-milk consistency.
- **No dairy.** Horchata de chufa is properly vegan; modern Valencian cafés sometimes add a splash of cream for richness, but that's a modern twist, not traditional.
- **Granizada (slushie) version.** Pour the chilled horchata into a metal tray, freeze 2 hours, then scrape with a fork into shaved-ice texture. The Valencian summer treat.

## Variations
- **Granizada de horchata.** As above; frozen, scraped, served as a shaved-ice slushie.
- **With cinnamon dusting.** Skip the cinnamon stick in the brew and instead dust ground cinnamon on the top of each glass at serving.
- **Mediterranean almond version.** Blend 100 g blanched almonds with the tiger nuts for an almond-tiger-nut hybrid (less traditional but lovely).
- **Coconut horchata.** Half the water replaced with coconut milk. Tropical, not Valencian, but a modern café variation.

## Storage
- Horchata keeps 3 days sealed in the fridge. The starch separates; shake or stir well before pouring. After 3 days the flavour fades; better to make fresh.
- Don't freeze the liquid horchata (it separates badly on thawing); only freeze it as granizada (shaved ice).
