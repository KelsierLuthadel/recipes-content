# Slemp (Dutch Sinterklaas Saffron Milk)

![Slemp](resources/slemp.jpg)

*The Netherlands' oldest winter warming drink, dating to the 16th century and once the canonical Sinterklaas evening drink before being supplanted by the simpler anijsmelk: whole milk steeped slowly with a generous pinch of saffron, a cinnamon stick, a piece of star anise, a clove or two, a small amount of strong black tea, and brown sugar - then strained and served piping hot in small mugs. Deep gold-orange in colour from the saffron; warmly spiced; subtly tea-tinted; more complex than anijsmelk but in the same Dutch winter-warming family. Modern Dutch home cooks make slemp for Sinterklaas on 5 December and Christmas; rural Dutch regions of Drenthe and Friesland keep the older tradition alive.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Slemp is the Netherlands' oldest documented winter hot drink, recorded in Dutch cookbooks from the early 17th century. The construction reflects the Dutch East India Company era - saffron from the Persian / Spanish trade, cinnamon and cloves from the Maluku Islands, star anise from China, tea from Java - all of which arrived in the Netherlands through the Amsterdam-centred spice trade and ended up in this single drink. The construction has three Dutch-specific moves. First, the saffron: a generous pinch (about 20 strands per litre) of good saffron threads is steeped in a small amount of hot water for 10 minutes before being added to the milk - this pre-bloom releases the saffron's deep gold colour and floral-honeyed aroma. The colour is one of the drink's signatures - slemp arrives golden-orange in the mug, distinct from the white-or-cream of anijsmelk. Second, the spice mix: a cinnamon stick + a piece of star anise + 2 cloves + a thin slice of fresh ginger (some recipes include white pepper or cardamom too) - the warm-spice combination that anchors the drink. Third, the tea: a small amount of strong brewed black tea (Ceylon, Assam, or Earl Grey) goes into the milk - the tannins balance the milk's sweetness and the spice's warmth, and contribute the slight pale-amber tint underneath the saffron gold. The drink is sweetened with brown sugar (white sugar is also acceptable, but the brown sugar's molasses notes pair better with the spice). Strained, served in small mugs. The classic Dutch ritual: slemp is poured into small heavy ceramic mugs, accompanied by speculaas biscuits, pepernoten (Sinterklaas spice biscuits), or taai-taai (chewy aniseed cookies). The drink is sipped slowly over a long Sinterklaas evening or a Christmas-Eve evening; the mug is refilled at least once. Three details: BLOOM THE SAFFRON FIRST (10 minutes in hot water before adding to milk; this releases the colour and aroma fully), LONG GENTLE STEEP (15 minutes at 80-85°C; longer is better for spice depth), and BROWN SUGAR NOT WHITE (the molasses notes complement the saffron and spice in a way white sugar can't).

## Ingredients

### Per 1 litre slemp (serves 4 small mugs)
- 1 litre whole milk
- A generous pinch of good saffron threads (about 20-25 strands)
- 2 tablespoons hot water (for blooming the saffron)
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 2 whole star anise pods (lightly crushed)
- 4 whole cloves
- 1 thin slice fresh ginger (about 5 mm thick)
- 1/4 teaspoon ground white pepper (optional; classical Dutch recipes include)
- 1 cardamom pod (lightly crushed, optional)
- 60 g soft dark brown sugar (Muscovado or Demerara)
- 2 black tea bags (Ceylon, Assam, or Earl Grey) OR 2 tablespoons loose-leaf black tea

### Optional adult variant
- 2 tablespoons brandy, dark rum, or Dutch genever (added at the end, per drink)

### To finish (optional)
- A few extra saffron strands floated on top
- A grating of fresh nutmeg
- A small dollop of whipped cream (for the modern decadent variant)

### To serve
- 4 small heavy ceramic mugs (200 ml each), pre-warmed
- Small spoons for stirring
- A small plate of speculaas, pepernoten, or taai-taai biscuits
- A wool blanket (the canonical Dutch winter pairing)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Bloom the saffron
1. Place the saffron threads in a small heatproof bowl.
2. Pour over 2 tablespoons of just-boiled water.
3. Let stand 10 minutes - the water will turn a deep gold-orange and release the saffron's aroma.
4. Don't skip this step - it doubles the colour and aroma of the finished drink.

### Stage 2 - Warm the milk and spices
1. Pour the milk into a heavy saucepan.
2. Add the cinnamon stick, crushed star anise, cloves, slice of ginger, optional white pepper and cardamom.
3. Add the brown sugar.
4. Place over medium-low heat; warm 8-10 minutes, stirring once, till the milk reaches 80-85°C (small bubbles forming at the edge, steam rising; do NOT boil).

### Stage 3 - Add the saffron and tea
1. Pour the saffron-and-its-water into the warm spiced milk.
2. Add the tea bags (or loose-leaf in a strainer ball).
3. Stir to combine.

### Stage 4 - Long gentle steep
1. Reduce heat to the lowest possible.
2. Continue warming gently 8-10 more minutes (do not boil).
3. The milk will turn a deep gold-orange, fragrant with saffron and spice, with the tea's tannin balancing the sweetness.
4. Taste; the flavour should be warmly spiced, gently sweet, with the saffron the dominant aromatic note.

### Stage 5 - Strain
1. Pre-warm 4 small mugs with hot water; tip out.
2. Pour the slemp through a fine sieve into a clean jug (catches the tea bags, cinnamon stick, star anise, cloves, ginger).

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Pour into the warm small mugs - about 200 ml each.
2. (For the adult variant: add a small splash of brandy, dark rum or genever to each mug before pouring.)
3. (Optional) Float a few extra saffron strands on top; grate a tiny pinch of nutmeg over.
4. (Optional) Add a small dollop of whipped cream.

### Stage 7 - Sip slowly
1. Place a small plate of speculaas, pepernoten or taai-taai biscuits in the centre of the table.
2. Sip the slemp over the next 10-15 minutes; the aroma intensifies as it cools slightly.
3. Refill the mug at least once - one slemp is rarely enough on a cold Dutch winter evening.

## Notes
- **Saffron quality matters:** good Spanish, Iranian or Kashmiri saffron is night-and-day different from the pale supermarket stuff. A small amount of good saffron is worth more than a large amount of cheap saffron.
- **Bloom the saffron in water first:** this releases the colour and aroma far more effectively than adding the strands directly to milk. 10 minutes is the canonical bloom time.
- **Whole milk is essential:** the fat carries the saffron and spice oils. Skim or semi-skim gives a thin, one-note drink.
- **Don't boil:** scorched milk ruins the flavour and the texture. 80-85°C is the sweet spot.
- **Brown sugar vs white:** the molasses notes pair better with the saffron and spice. White sugar gives a flatter result.
- **The tea is essential:** the tannins balance the sweetness and the milk; without tea, slemp is overly sweet and one-dimensional.
- **Better the second day:** the spice flavours marry overnight if you have leftovers. Reheat gently.

## Variations
**Slemp without tea (lighter):** skip the tea; the drink is more milky-sweet and more saffron-dominant - the modern Amsterdam tea-room variant.
**Adult slemp (with brandy):** add 30 ml of brandy or dark rum per mug just before serving - the canonical adult Sinterklaas drink.
**Cardamom-heavy slemp:** add 4 lightly crushed cardamom pods to the spice mix - more Persian-influenced, more fragrant.
**Honey slemp:** swap the brown sugar for 4 tablespoons of good honey - mellower, more aromatic.
**Vanilla-bean slemp (modern):** add a split vanilla pod to the steep - the modern variant.
**Slemp with orange peel:** add the zest of one orange to the steep - brighter, more festive.
**Slemp on ice (summer variant):** cool the brewed slemp fully; serve over ice with a small wedge of orange - the modern Dutch summer-café variant.
**Quick slemp from leftovers:** if you have made anijsmelk earlier, you can add saffron, a tea bag, and a cinnamon stick to it; warm 5 minutes; strain - a faster (slightly less complex) version.
**Vegan slemp:** swap whole milk for full-fat coconut milk or oat milk - the spice and saffron dominate; the dairy isn't essential.

## Serving
At a Dutch family Sinterklaas (5 December) celebration (the canonical setting) · at a Dutch Christmas Eve · at a Dutch winter market hot-drink stall · at a Drenthe or Friesland rural Dutch farmhouse on a snowy evening · at an Amsterdam canal-house tea-room · at a Dutch winter games evening (board games, cards) · at home as the more elaborate cousin of anijsmelk · paired with speculaas, pepernoten, taai-taai, or a hand-made Dutch chocolate.

## Storage
- Refrigerates 3 days; reheats well on the stovetop (don't boil).
- Don't reheat in the microwave - uneven heating creates hot spots.
- The leftover slemp actually improves overnight as the spices fully marry - many Dutch cooks deliberately make it the day before Sinterklaas.
- The whole spices (cinnamon stick, star anise, cloves) can be re-used once for a lighter second infusion.
- Saffron threads keep indefinitely in a sealed jar in a dark dry place.
- A "slemp spice mix" (cinnamon + star anise + cloves + ginger + saffron in small portions) can be made up ahead in 4-portion sachets - useful for last-minute winter evenings.
