# Boza

![Boza](resources/boza.jpg)

*Turkey's ancient fermented grain drink: millet (or bulgur) cooked into a thick porridge, sweetened, fermented gently for two days until lightly tangy and faintly fizzy, served in small glasses topped with cinnamon and a handful of roasted chickpeas. The street drink of Istanbul winter, sold by vendors calling "Bozaaa!" through the cold evening streets.*

**Serves:** 8 small glasses (makes about 1.2 litres)

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus 48 hours fermentation)

**Cook Time:** 30 minutes

## Overview
Boza is one of the world's oldest drinks, predating beer in some accounts: a thick, lightly fermented, very faintly alcoholic (~1% ABV in traditional preparations, less in modern commercial versions) drink made from cooked millet or bulgur wheat, sweetened with sugar, and fermented at room temperature for 2-3 days. It's been brewed across the Ottoman lands for over 1,000 years, with the modern Turkish version centred in Istanbul where the famous Vefa Bozacısı shop has sold boza on the same corner since 1876. The texture is the giveaway: thick like a thin oatmeal, viscous enough to coat a spoon, slightly fizzy from the fermentation but not effervescent like soda. The taste is sweet-tangy, faintly grain-like, with a clean finish; the classic serve is a small glass topped with a generous dusting of cinnamon and a small dish of leblebi (roasted chickpeas) alongside, eaten by the spoonful between sips. Boza is a winter drink almost exclusively; in summer, the fermentation runs too quickly and the drink becomes too alcoholic.

## Ingredients

- 150 g millet (whole or coarsely ground; wholefood shops or Turkish/Middle Eastern groceries)
- 1.5 litres water
- 150 g caster sugar
- 1 teaspoon active dry yeast OR 2 tablespoons of plain whole-milk yogurt (the "starter")
- 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon (for serving)

### To serve
- 8 small heatproof glasses (about 150 ml capacity)
- 100 g leblebi (roasted chickpeas) — Turkish brand at any Turkish grocery; or substitute with roasted chickpeas
- Ground cinnamon for dusting

## Method

### Stage 1 - Cook the grain
1. Rinse the millet thoroughly under cold water in a sieve.
1. Bring the 1.5 litres of water to the boil in a large saucepan.
1. Add the millet, reduce to medium-low, and simmer for 25-30 minutes, stirring frequently. The millet will burst and the mixture will thicken to a porridge-like consistency.
1. Remove from heat.

### Stage 2 - Blend smooth
1. Using a stick blender (or transferring in batches to a regular blender), blitz the porridge until completely smooth and lump-free. The texture should be like a thick milkshake.
1. Pass through a fine sieve into a clean large jug or pot, pressing the solids hard to extract every bit of liquid. Discard the spent solids.

### Stage 3 - Sweeten and cool
1. Stir the sugar into the still-warm liquid until fully dissolved.
1. Cool to about 30°C (just slightly warm to the touch; tepid). Too hot will kill the yeast/starter; too cold won't activate it.

### Stage 4 - Add starter and ferment
1. Stir in the active dry yeast OR the 2 tablespoons of yogurt as a starter.
1. Cover the jug with a clean tea towel (allowing air exchange) and leave at warm room temperature (18-22°C) for 36 to 48 hours. Stir gently twice a day.
1. The mixture will start to bubble faintly after 12 hours; by 36-48 hours it should taste pleasantly tangy with a faint fizz, and have a slightly grain-and-yeast aroma.

### Stage 5 - Chill and bottle
1. Transfer to clean bottles or jars, sealing loosely (the fermentation continues slowly under refrigeration; tight-sealed bottles can pressure-up).
1. Refrigerate at least 12 hours before serving. The chilling slows the fermentation and lets the flavour settle.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Stir well before pouring; the grain settles as it sits.
1. Pour into small chilled glasses.
1. Dust each glass with a generous pinch of ground cinnamon.
1. Serve with a small dish of roasted chickpeas (leblebi) alongside — the tradition is to alternate sips of boza with handfuls of leblebi.

## Notes
- **Watch the fermentation.** Boza fermenting too long becomes more strongly alcoholic and quite sour. In a warm kitchen (above 25°C) fermentation can be done in 24 hours; in cool conditions it may need 60+ hours. Taste from 24 hours onward to catch the right balance.
- **Yeast vs yogurt starter.** Yeast gives a more consistent fizz and faster fermentation; yogurt gives a more complex, tangier flavour but takes longer to develop. Both are valid traditional approaches.
- **Texture should be thick.** Boza is meant to be thick — spoon-coating, not water-thin. If yours comes out too thin, you blended too much water in; next time use 1.2 litres of water in stage 1.
- **Winter drink.** Don't try to make boza in summer unless you have good temperature control; the warm room temperatures over-ferment it and push the alcohol up.

## Variations
- **Bulgur boza.** Use 150 g coarse bulgur instead of millet. Slightly different flavour, more bread-like. Less common but traditional.
- **With honey.** Substitute 100 g of the sugar with 100 g of mild honey. Subtly different sweetness profile.
- **Stronger ferment.** Let it ferment 3-4 days for a more pronounced tang and slightly higher alcohol (still very low, around 1.5% ABV).
- **Modern Vefa-style.** The famous Vefa Bozacısı in Istanbul makes a thicker, sweeter version. Reduce water to 1 litre and increase sugar to 200 g for closer approximation.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 5 days. The fermentation continues slowly even cold; after 4-5 days the drink gets noticeably more tart and slightly more alcoholic.
- Don't freeze; the texture is destroyed.
