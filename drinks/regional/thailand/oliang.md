# Oliang (Thai Iced Coffee)

![Oliang](resources/oliang.jpg)

*Thailand's iced coffee: a dark, slightly smoky brew of roasted coffee mixed with sesame, soybean and corn, sweetened heavily and poured over crushed ice. Often finished with evaporated milk for the cha-yen sibling, or drunk black with sugar for the truer street version. The morning rocket fuel of Bangkok.*

**Serves:** 2 tall glasses

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 10 minutes (plus chilling)

## Overview
Oliang (อ-เลี้ยง) is the Thai answer to iced coffee, and like cha yen it has a distinctive character that comes partly from the bean blend and partly from the technique. The traditional grounds are "oliang powder", a roasted blend of coffee, soybean, sesame and sometimes corn or cardamom; the soybean and sesame give a slightly nutty-smoky undertone that pure coffee can't replicate. Brewed strong in a tung tom kafae (a cloth bag traditionally hung over the rim of a pot), the resulting concentrate is dark, almost black, and is sweetened generously with sugar while still hot. Served over a tall glass of crushed ice. Some versions ("oliang nom") add evaporated milk for a creamier drink; the unmilked version ("oliang dam") is the truer streetside drink and tastes more of the smoky oliang blend. Easy to make at home with a Thai oliang powder bag from a Southeast Asian grocery, or substitute with a strong dark-roast coffee plus a tablespoon of toasted sesame seeds.

## Ingredients

- 30 g Thai oliang powder (sold in Asian groceries; brands like Pantai or O-Liang) OR 4 tablespoons strong dark-roast ground coffee + 1 tablespoon toasted black sesame seeds + 1 tablespoon roasted soybean flour
- 600 ml boiling water
- 4 tablespoons caster sugar (or 50 g brown sugar; adjust to taste)
- Plenty of crushed ice

### Optional, for oliang nom (milky version)
- 4 tablespoons evaporated milk
- 2 tablespoons sweetened condensed milk

### To serve
- 2 tall glasses, chilled
- Long-handled spoons or wide straws

## Method

### Stage 1 - Brew
1. Put the oliang powder (or DIY blend) into a small heatproof jug, French press, or a muslin cloth bag.
1. Pour over the boiling water and stir well.
1. Let steep for 5 minutes for a strong brew. The colour should be very dark, almost black.

### Stage 2 - Strain and sweeten
1. Strain through a fine sieve (or press the French press, or lift the muslin bag) into a jug, discarding the grounds.
1. While still hot, stir in the caster sugar until completely dissolved. Taste: oliang should be aggressively sweet to balance the smoky coffee. Add another tablespoon if too sharp.

### Stage 3 - Chill
1. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate 30 minutes minimum so it doesn't melt the ice on contact.

### Stage 4 - Serve
1. Fill the chilled glasses with crushed ice three-quarters full.
1. Pour the cold brewed oliang over the ice until the glass is almost full.
1. For oliang dam (black): serve as-is with a long spoon.
1. For oliang nom (milky): drizzle the evaporated milk over the top, then a final swirl of sweetened condensed milk; the milks slowly sink through the dark coffee in pale ribbons.

## Notes
- **The blend matters.** Pure coffee gives a sharper, more bitter iced coffee that isn't quite oliang. The sesame and soybean in proper oliang powder soften and round the bitterness. Worth getting from a Thai grocery.
- **Brew strong.** This is a concentrate that gets diluted by ice. A weak brew makes a weak finished drink. Use 30 g of powder per 600 ml; don't be shy.
- **Sweet at brewing temperature.** Stir sugar in while the brew is hot so it dissolves completely. Adding to chilled liquid leaves gritty undissolved crystals.
- **Crushed ice.** Like cha yen, oliang is built over crushed ice that dilutes as you sip. Cubes are wrong.

## Variations
- **Oliang yok lor.** Strong oliang served hot in a tall glass. The same recipe without chilling and without ice.
- **Yuan jin (Thai Vietnamese-style).** Use sweetened condensed milk only (no evaporated) for the Vietnamese-inflected version popular in southern Thailand.
- **Coconut oliang.** Replace the evaporated milk with coconut cream for a tropical version. Not traditional but common in modern cafés.
- **Soybean-heavy.** For a more pronounced toasty character, use a 2:1 coffee-to-soybean ratio in the DIY blend.

## Storage
- The brewed concentrate keeps 3 days sealed in the fridge. Build over ice and add milks at serving; pre-assembled milky oliang separates and looks unappealing within an hour.
