# Kissangua

![Kissangua](resources/kissangua.jpg)

*Angolan fermented maize-and-pineapple drink: ground maize cooked into a thin porridge, sweetened, fermented overnight with pineapple peel, strained and served cold; a low-alcohol traditional brew.*

**Serves:** Makes about 2 litres

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus 24-48 hours fermentation)

**Cook Time:** 30 minutes

## Overview
Kissangua is the traditional fermented village drink of Angola, a thin, slightly fizzy, mildly alcoholic brew made from ground maize and pineapple peel. The process is older than colonial contact and still made the same way: white or yellow maize meal is cooked into a thin porridge with water, sweetened with sugar (or honey, or sugarcane juice in the interior), the unused peel and core of a ripe pineapple is added to provide the wild yeast for fermentation, and the whole mixture is left to ferment for one to two days at warm room temperature until it goes lightly sour and slightly fizzy. The drink is strained and served cold in a clay jug or a tall glass. The alcohol level is low (1-3%) and the flavour is sour, sweet, lightly yeasty and pineappley. It is the proper village welcome drink, often offered to honoured guests on arrival.

## Ingredients

- 200 g white or yellow maize meal (cornmeal; medium grind)
- 2 litres water
- 200 g caster sugar (or 200 ml honey)
- The peel and core of 1 ripe pineapple (well washed; the flesh saved for eating)
- 2 cloves (optional, for aroma)
- A small piece of fresh ginger (optional, sliced)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Cook the maize porridge
1. Bring 1.5 litres of the water to a hard boil in a heavy pan.
2. Whisk the maize meal with the remaining 500 ml cold water in a bowl to a smooth slurry.
3. Pour the slurry slowly into the boiling water, whisking constantly to avoid lumps.
4. Lower the heat to medium; cook 15-20 minutes, stirring often, until the porridge is thin (the texture of single cream) and the raw maize taste has cooked out.

### Stage 2 - Sweeten and cool
1. Take off the heat; stir in the sugar (or honey) until dissolved.
2. Cool to lukewarm (around 30°C; warm enough to barely feel on the inside of your wrist).

### Stage 3 - Ferment
1. Pour the cooled porridge into a clean glass jar or earthenware pot.
2. Add the pineapple peel and core, the cloves and the ginger if using.
3. Cover with a clean cloth (not a tight lid; the brew needs to breathe).
4. Leave at warm room temperature (22-28°C) for 24-48 hours.
5. Taste at 24 hours; the drink should be lightly sour with a faint fizz on the tongue. If still flat and sweet, leave another 12-24 hours.

### Stage 4 - Strain and chill
1. Strain through a fine sieve into a clean jug; press the solids gently and discard the pineapple peel.
2. Refrigerate at least 2 hours; cold sharpens the flavour.

### Stage 5 - Serve
1. Pour into glasses or a clay jug.
2. Stir before pouring; the maize settles.

## Notes
- **Lukewarm before the pineapple goes in:** Hot porridge kills the wild yeast on the pineapple skin; cold porridge ferments too slowly. Skin temperature is right.
- **Cover, don't seal:** Fermentation produces gas; a tight lid risks a burst jar. A clean cloth over the top with a rubber band lets the gas out and keeps flies and dust out.
- **Taste as it goes:** Twenty-four hours in a warm kitchen is usually enough; cooler rooms need longer. Stop the ferment when the drink is pleasantly sour but still has some sweetness left.

## Variations
**Kissangua de cana:** Replace the sugar with 200 ml of fresh sugarcane juice; the interior-country version.
**Kissangua de banana:** Add a ripe mashed banana with the pineapple peel for a thicker, sweeter brew.
**Kissangua de mel:** Use honey instead of sugar; the honey gives a richer, slightly floral fermentation.

## Serving
The traditional welcome drink. Offered in a clay jug to honoured guests on arrival. Cold, in a tall glass, alongside grilled meat or a heavy palm-oil stew for the cooling sour counterpoint.

## Storage
- Keeps 5 days refrigerated; the fermentation slows in the cold but continues, so the drink gets more sour over time.
- Open the jar carefully; pressure builds.
- Don't freeze.
