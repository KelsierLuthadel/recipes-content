# Lemon Leaf Tea

![Lemon Leaf Tea](resources/lemon-leaf-tea.jpg)

*Fresh lemon leaves picked off the tree, dropped into boiling water with a knob of ginger, steeped a few minutes and sweetened with raw sugar. The Fijian bush tea drunk at any hour from a tin cup.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 10 minutes

## Overview
Lemon-leaf tea is the everyday bush tea of Fijian villages: a handful of fresh lemon leaves from the tree at the back of the house, dropped into a billy of boiling water with a knob of ginger and a stick of lemongrass if it is to hand, steeped for five minutes and sweetened from the sugar tin. The flavour is bright, herbal, slightly resinous, with the lemon character coming through the leaves rather than the fruit. It is drunk hot from tin cups at any time of day, served to guests as a welcome, made for a child with a cold, sipped at sunset on the verandah. It costs nothing, uses no imported ingredients, and is one of the most distinctive Fijian drinks the visitor will be offered.

## Ingredients

- 10 fresh lemon leaves (or 6 large kaffir lime leaves as a substitute), torn lightly
- 1 thumb of fresh ginger, sliced
- 1 stick of lemongrass, bruised (optional)
- 1 litre water
- 2-3 tbsp raw sugar (or honey), to taste
- Optional: a squeeze of fresh lemon juice to finish

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the leaves
1. Rinse the lemon leaves under cold water.
2. Tear each leaf in half lightly; the bruised edges release the oils.
3. Slice the ginger thin; bruise the lemongrass stick with the back of a knife.

### Stage 2 - Boil
1. Bring the water to a rolling boil in a kettle or open pan.
2. Add the lemon leaves, ginger and lemongrass.
3. Reduce to a gentle simmer; cover; brew 8 minutes.

### Stage 3 - Sweeten and serve
1. Stir in the raw sugar; taste and adjust.
2. Add a squeeze of lemon juice if you want a sharper finish.
3. Strain into tin cups or thick ceramic mugs.

## Notes
- **Use fresh leaves, not dried:** dried lemon leaves lose almost all of their character. The bright lemony resin is in the fresh oil.
- **Bruise the leaves:** tearing or crushing each leaf is what releases the oils into the water. Whole leaves give a much weaker tea.
- **Kaffir lime substitute:** if no lemon tree is to hand, kaffir lime leaves work but the character is more sharply citrus and less herbal.

## Variations
- **With turmeric:** add a 2 cm piece of fresh turmeric root, sliced, alongside the ginger; the tea turns yellow and gains an earthy note.
- **With a chilli:** a single split bird-chilli in the pot lifts the tea into a warming cold-and-flu cure.
- **Cold infusion:** put the same ingredients in a jug with cold water; refrigerate 8 hours; strain. The Fijian iced version.
- **With coconut milk:** finish with a splash of thick coconut milk for a creamier soothing version.
- **Mint version:** add a small handful of fresh mint at the end of the steep; common in the Indo-Fijian household.

## Serving
Serve hot in a tin cup or thick mug · with a wedge of fresh lemon on the saucer · at any hour of the day · with a slice of cassava cake or a babakau · on the verandah at sunset · as the welcome drink for guests · for a Fijian cold remedy.

## Storage
- Best drunk fresh; the bright character fades within the hour.
- Refrigerate the cold infusion 2 days.
- Do not freeze.
