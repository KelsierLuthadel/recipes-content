# Pit-Pit Ginger Tea

![Pit-Pit Ginger Tea](resources/pit-pit-ginger-tea.jpg)

*Fresh ginger root and bruised lemongrass simmered in water with a strip of pandan and sweetened with palm sugar, the warming Highlands cup that takes the chill off a cool mountain morning.*

**Serves:** 4 mugs

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 20 minutes

## Overview
Pit-pit ginger tea is the Highlands cup, the brew that village households make first thing in the morning to take the chill off the cool mountain air at 2,000 metres. The base is a thumb of fresh ginger root, smashed and sliced, simmered with bruised lemongrass and a strip of pandan in a pan of water; the sweetener is grated palm sugar; the lift is a squeeze of lime or, in the missionary tradition, a pinch of salt. The flavour is hot-sweet-aromatic, the kind of cup that opens the airways and warms the chest in 30 seconds. Most Highlands households drink several mugs a day; visitors are offered a cup the moment they sit down. The name pit-pit covers various Highlands aromatic grasses, of which lemongrass is the most useful for the substitute below.

## Ingredients
- 1 thumb of fresh ginger root (about 40 g), unpeeled, smashed and sliced
- 2 lemongrass stalks, bruised and roughly chopped
- 1 pandan leaf, knotted (optional)
- 800 ml water
- 2 tbsp palm sugar, grated (or honey)
- A pinch of salt
- Juice of 1/2 lime (optional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prep the aromatics
1. Smash the ginger root with the side of a heavy knife; slice into rough coins.
2. Trim the lemongrass stalks; bruise with the back of the knife and chop into 5 cm pieces.

### Stage 2 - Simmer
1. Place the ginger, lemongrass and pandan in a pan with the water.
2. Bring to a boil over high heat.
3. Drop to a gentle simmer; cook 15-18 minutes. The kitchen should fill with the smell of ginger and lemongrass.

### Stage 3 - Sweeten
1. Stir in the palm sugar and salt; stir until fully dissolved.
2. Taste; add more palm sugar if you want it sweeter, or more salt if you want it sharper.

### Stage 4 - Strain and serve
1. Strain through a fine sieve into a teapot or pour straight into mugs.
2. Add a squeeze of lime if using.
3. Serve hot.

## Notes
- **The ginger:** Unpeeled is fine; just wash it well. Smashing rather than slicing finely releases more of the aromatic oils.
- **The lemongrass:** Use only the lower pale portion; the upper green leaves are too tough to extract clean flavour in 15 minutes.
- **The pandan:** Optional but contributes a deep grassy-vanilla note that ties everything together.

## Variations
- **Coconut milk version:** Stir in 200 ml coconut milk at the end for a richer creamier cup; warm but do not boil.
- **Chilli ginger tea:** Add 1 small red chilli, slit lengthways, to the simmer for a serious warming brew.
- **Honey-lime:** Skip the palm sugar; sweeten with honey and finish with a heavier hand of lime.
- **Cold version:** Cool the brew and serve over ice with extra lime in hot coastal weather.

## Serving
Serve hot in mugs · a slice of fresh lime alongside · drink first thing in the morning in the Highlands cool · with a wedge of grilled sago pancake or a fresh-roasted kaukau for breakfast.

## Storage
- Refrigerate the strained tea up to 3 days in a covered jug; warm gently to serve.
- Freeze in ice-cube trays for ginger cubes to drop into other drinks.
- The aromatics (ginger, lemongrass, pandan) can be re-simmered once for a milder second brew.
