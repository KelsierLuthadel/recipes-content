# Pawpaw Juice

![Pawpaw Juice](resources/pawpaw-juice.jpg)

*Ripe pawpaw (papaya) pulped with cold water, a squeeze of lime and a pinch of salt, strained light and orange, the easy fresh juice of the coastal markets and the village kitchen.*

**Serves:** 4 tall glasses

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** None

## Overview
Pawpaw is what Papua New Guineans call the papaya, and the fruit grows in every village garden along the coast, dropping ripe orange fruit faster than the household can eat them. The standard treatment is the simplest: peel and deseed a ripe pawpaw, blitz the flesh with cold water and a squeeze of lime, strain, and pour over ice. The juice is a vivid orange, mildly sweet, with the faintly musky perfume that pawpaw carries. A pinch of salt sharpens the sweetness, and a knot of pandan added to the jug rounds the whole thing out. Drink cold on a hot afternoon; the digestive enzymes in the pawpaw settle the stomach after a heavy mumu lunch.

## Ingredients
- 1 large ripe pawpaw (papaya), about 1 kg whole
- 500 ml cold water
- Juice of 1 lime
- 1 tbsp palm sugar or honey (optional, depending on the fruit)
- A pinch of salt
- 1 pandan leaf, knotted (optional)
- Ice cubes to serve

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prep the fruit
1. Halve the pawpaw lengthways; scoop out the black seeds with a spoon.
2. Peel off the skin with a vegetable peeler or a knife.
3. Cut the flesh into rough 3 cm chunks; you should have about 600 g.

### Stage 2 - Blend
1. Place the pawpaw chunks in a blender.
2. Add the cold water, lime juice, salt and palm sugar or honey if using.
3. Blitz on high for 30-40 seconds until smooth.

### Stage 3 - Strain
1. Pour the puree through a fine sieve into a jug.
2. Press with the back of a spoon to extract the juice; discard the pulp.

### Stage 4 - Rest and serve
1. Stir in the knotted pandan leaf if using; rest 10 minutes in the fridge for the aroma to infuse.
2. Lift out the pandan.
3. Pour over ice in tall glasses; serve cold.

## Notes
- **The pawpaw:** Must be fully ripe; the skin should be yellow-orange with no green patches, and the fruit should yield when gently pressed. Under-ripe pawpaw tastes vegetal and bitter.
- **The lime and salt:** The salt lifts the sweetness and the lime brightens the musky note of the fruit; both are needed.
- **No pulp:** Straining is important; the unstrained puree is too thick to drink and the texture is grainy.

## Variations
- **Pawpaw and pineapple:** Replace 200 g of the pawpaw with fresh pineapple chunks for a sharper version.
- **Pawpaw and lime:** Double the lime juice and skip the sugar for a more astringent drink.
- **Coconut pawpaw:** Replace 200 ml of the water with coconut water for a richer juice.
- **Smoothie version:** Skip the strain; serve as a thick smoothie with extra ice.

## Serving
Serve over plenty of ice in tall glasses · a wedge of lime on the rim · drink as a long after-lunch refresher · the digestive enzymes make it the standard ending to a heavy mumu meal.

## Storage
- Best drunk the day it is made; the flavour and colour both fade by the next morning.
- Refrigerate up to 24 hours in a covered jug; stir before serving as it can separate.
- Do not freeze; the pawpaw turns grainy on thaw.
