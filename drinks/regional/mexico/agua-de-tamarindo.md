# Agua de Tamarindo

![Agua de Tamarindo](resources/agua-de-tamarindo.jpg)

*The third member of Mexico's holy aguas frescas trinity: tamarind pods boiled, the sticky pulp scraped off and pushed through a sieve, sweetened heavily, chilled, poured over ice. Deep amber-brown, sweet-tart, faintly fruity, surprisingly close to a Coca-Cola in taste profile. The taquería staple alongside horchata and jamaica.*

**Serves:** 6 to 8 tall glasses (makes 2 litres)

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes (plus chilling)

## Overview
Agua de tamarindo is made from the sticky brown pulp inside tamarind pods, a fruit indigenous to East Africa that travelled across India, Southeast Asia, the Caribbean and Mexico, where it became a foundational ingredient. The pulp is sour and sweet at once, with a slightly fruity, leather-like character. Dissolved in water and sweetened heavily, it becomes the deep amber drink served at every taqueria counter in Mexico. The colour is roughly the colour of a Coca-Cola; the taste is in the same family but cleaner, fruitier and far less artificial-tasting. Tamarindo pairs beautifully with rich Mexican food - the acid cuts through grease, the sweetness balances chilli heat. At a stand it comes in a tall glass with a wedge of lime and sometimes a chamoy-rimmed glass for an extra sour-spicy edge.

## Ingredients

- 200 g whole tamarind pods (the brittle brown pods from any Mexican / South Asian / Asian grocery) OR 100 g seedless tamarind pulp block (often labelled "tamarind paste" in supermarkets)
- 2 litres cold water
- 200 to 250 g caster sugar, to taste
- A pinch of fine salt (yes, salt - it amplifies the tamarind)
- Juice of 1 lime (optional, but recommended)

### To serve
- Plenty of ice cubes
- Lime wedges
- 6 to 8 tall glasses, chilled
- Optional: chamoy (Mexican chilli-tamarind sauce) or Tajín (chilli-lime salt) for the glass rim

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prep the tamarind
1. **If using whole pods**: crack the brittle outer shells with your hands. Pull off the shells and the long string-like fibres, leaving just the sticky brown pulp clinging to the seeds.
1. **If using a pulp block**: cut off the amount needed and chop it into smaller chunks for faster dissolving.

### Stage 2 - Simmer
1. Put the tamarind pulp (or pod contents) in a saucepan with 1 litre of the water.
1. Bring to the boil, then reduce to a simmer for 15 minutes. Press down on the pulp with the back of a spoon every few minutes to break it up. The water turns a deep amber-brown.
1. Remove from heat and let stand for 10 minutes to cool slightly.

### Stage 3 - Strain hard
1. Push the mixture through a fine sieve into a large jug, using the back of a wooden spoon to extract every bit of pulp. Take time with this - most of the flavour is in the pulp paste, not the watery liquid.
1. Discard the spent seeds and fibre left in the sieve.

### Stage 4 - Sweeten
1. Add the remaining 1 litre of cold water to dilute.
1. Stir in 200 g of sugar and the pinch of salt until dissolved. Taste - tamarindo is properly very sweet to balance the strong sourness of the fruit. Add more sugar 25 g at a time to taste.
1. Stir in the lime juice if using.

### Stage 5 - Chill and serve
1. Refrigerate at least 3 hours, ideally overnight.
1. Pour over ice in tall glasses; garnish with lime wedges.
1. For the festive version: rim each glass with chamoy or Tajín before pouring.

## Notes
- **Whole pods over block paste.** Both work; whole pods give a fresher, cleaner flavour. The block paste is faster but can have a slightly cooked-tasting character. Both produce a fine agua de tamarindo.
- **The salt matters.** A pinch of salt amplifies the tamarind's natural depth. Don't skip it.
- **Press hard through the sieve.** This step is the difference between a thin watery drink and a rich one. The good stuff is the paste that comes through with persistent pressing.

## Variations
- **Chamoyada de tamarindo.** With chamoy rim + Tajín dusting on top. Sour-sweet-spicy combination.
- **With chia.** Stir in 2 tablespoons of soaked chia seeds for textured agua.
- **Tamarindo y piña.** Half tamarindo, half fresh pineapple juice.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 5 days. Flavour stays bright for the first 3 days.
