# Sharab al-Loomi

![Sharab al-Loomi](resources/sharab-al-loomi.jpg)

*A tart amber cooler made from dried Omani limes steeped with sugar and cold water, the household summer drink of Tripoli and Benghazi when the afternoon heat settles in.*

**Serves:** 4 tall glasses

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes (plus 4 hours steeping)

**Cook Time:** 10 minutes

## Overview
Sharab al-loomi is the everyday summer cooler of Libyan homes, an amber-coloured drink built around loomi, the small hard dried limes that turn dark brown and sour-bitter after weeks of sun-drying. The limes are smashed open, simmered briefly with sugar and water to draw the flavour out, then left to steep cold for several hours until the liquid takes on a deep tea-colour and a sharp citrus depth quite unlike fresh lemon. The drink is poured over ice with a sprig of mint, and the sourness of the loomi cuts through any heavy meal that came before. Where Egypt and the Levant lean on hibiscus and tamarind for their summer drinks, Libya leans east toward the Gulf for this one; the dried lime is the same loomi that flavours Gulf rice and Iranian stews, used here as a refreshment rather than a seasoning.

## Ingredients

- 4 whole dried limes (loomi, also called black lime)
- 100 g caster sugar (or to taste)
- 1.2 litres cold water
- 1 tsp orange blossom water (optional)
- Plenty of ice
- A small handful fresh mint, to serve

## Method

### Stage 1 - Crack the limes
1. Set each dried lime on a board.
2. Press hard with the flat of a heavy knife or the base of a small pan until it splits open.
3. The inside is dark brown and powdery; that is correct.
4. Shake out and discard any seeds.

### Stage 2 - Simmer
1. Tip the broken limes into a saucepan.
2. Add the sugar and 300 ml of the water.
3. Bring to a gentle simmer over medium heat.
4. Simmer 8-10 minutes, pressing on the limes now and then with a wooden spoon; the liquid will darken and the kitchen will smell sharply citrus.
5. Take off the heat.

### Stage 3 - Steep cold
1. Pour the rest of the cold water (900 ml) into the pan.
2. Stir; let cool to room temperature.
3. Tip into a jug, cover, and refrigerate at least 4 hours, ideally overnight.

### Stage 4 - Finish and serve
1. Strain through a fine sieve into a clean jug, pressing gently on the limes.
2. Stir in the orange blossom water if using.
3. Taste; adjust with more sugar or a squeeze of fresh lemon if needed.
4. Pour over ice into tall glasses.
5. Add a sprig of fresh mint to each glass.

## Notes
- **Loomi quality matters:** good dried limes are hard, brown-black and rattle slightly when shaken (the dried pulp inside). Limp pale limes give a weak drink.
- **Crack do not pulverise:** the limes should split open so the inside is exposed to the water, but pulverising them releases too much bitterness.
- **Sugar balances bitterness:** loomi carries a real bitter edge alongside the sour. Start with the listed sugar, then adjust upward only after tasting cold.
- **Cold steep is essential:** the long cold rest is where the flavour develops; a hot infusion alone tastes thin and bitter.

## Variations
- Stir in a tablespoon of grenadine for a pink festive version.
- Add a star anise to the simmer for a warmer spiced edge.
- Replace half the water with sparkling water at serving for a fizzy version.
- Add a pinch of crushed saffron threads to the simmer for a golden touch.
- Stir in 100 ml fresh lime juice at the end for a sharper modern version.

## Serving
On a hot afternoon between lunch and the evening meal · alongside grilled fish or kebabs · at a Ramadan iftar table to break the fast · with a plate of fresh dates and watermelon · at a family lunch as the everyday table drink.

## Storage
- Keeps 4 days refrigerated in a sealed jug.
- The flavour deepens on day two and starts to fade by day four.
- Do not freeze (the citrus oils break down).
