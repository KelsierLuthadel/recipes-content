# Bērzu sula

![Bērzu sula](resources/berzu-sula.jpg)

*Latvian spring birch sap: the clear watery sap drawn from silver birch trees in March and April when the trees first wake, drunk fresh and cold, or lightly fermented with raisins and lemon for a faintly sparkling spring tonic.*

**Serves:** Makes 2 litres (fermented version)

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes (plus collection time)

**Cook Time:** 0 (fresh) or 3 to 5 days (fermented)

## Overview
Bērzu sula is the clear, faintly sweet sap that rises in silver birch trees (Betula pendula) in the few weeks between the last snow and the bud-break of leaves, roughly mid-March to mid-April depending on the year. Rural Latvians have tapped birches every spring for centuries: the sap is drunk fresh and cold as a spring tonic (Latvians say it cleanses the body after winter), bottled and refrigerated, or lightly fermented with sugar, lemon and raisins into a faintly sparkling spring drink that lasts a few weeks. Tapping is straightforward: a small bore is drilled into the trunk at chest height, a length of food-grade tubing is fitted, and the sap drips into a clean container over a few days. A mature healthy tree yields 5 to 15 litres without harm if the hole is plugged afterwards. Fresh sap tastes like cold soft water with a whisper of sweetness; the fermented version develops a citrus edge and a fine prickly fizz. Birch sap is sold commercially in Latvian shops in spring; the homemade version is a Baltic ritual.

## Ingredients

### Fresh birch sap
- 1 litre fresh birch sap (collected within 24 hours; keep chilled)
- Optional: 1 mint leaf, lemon slice

### Fermented spring tonic
- 2 litres fresh birch sap
- 100 g caster sugar (or 80 g honey)
- 1 lemon (zest in long strips, plus the juice)
- 8 raisins
- 2 sprigs of fresh mint (optional)
- 1 cardamom pod, lightly crushed (optional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Collect the sap (optional, if you can tap your own)
1. Pick a healthy birch over 25 cm trunk diameter.
2. Drill a small 8 mm hole, 3 cm deep, slightly downward, at chest height.
3. Insert a short length of food-grade tubing; lead it into a clean covered jar.
4. Collect over 1 to 3 days. Empty the jar into clean bottles every 12 hours; refrigerate.
5. Plug the hole afterwards with a wooden dowel and seal with beeswax. The tree heals over.
6. Tap only in the proper window (mid-March to mid-April in Latvia, slightly later further north).

### Stage 2 - Drink fresh
1. Strain the sap through muslin to remove any bark fragments.
2. Refrigerate; drink cold within 3 days.
3. Optional: float a mint leaf or a lemon slice in the glass.

### Stage 3 - Make the fermented tonic
1. Pour the 2 litres of birch sap into a large clean jug or jar.
2. Stir in the sugar until dissolved.
3. Add the lemon zest, lemon juice, raisins, mint and cardamom (if using).
4. Cover loosely with muslin or a loose lid.

### Stage 4 - Ferment
1. Leave at room temperature 3 to 5 days. The wild yeasts on the lemon peel and raisins start the ferment.
2. Within 24 hours the surface should start fizzing softly. The raisins will rise.
3. Taste daily. The drink should turn from sweet-flat to gently sour and faintly sparkling.
4. Stop the ferment by chilling (refrigerate) when the sweetness is to your liking; the drink will keep developing slowly cold.

### Stage 5 - Strain and bottle
1. Strain the liquid into clean glass bottles.
2. Cap loosely or use flip-top bottles vented for the first day, then sealed.
3. Refrigerate at least 6 hours before drinking.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Pour cold into glasses.
2. A sprig of mint or a slice of lemon to garnish.
3. Drink straight, or use as the base for a long spritz with sparkling water.

## Notes
- **Tap responsibly.** Only on your own land or with permission; one small hole per tree; plug it cleanly when finished; never tap a tree under 25 cm trunk diameter or in poor health.
- **Fresh sap spoils fast.** Without preservation it stays good 2 to 3 days refrigerated, less if not chilled within hours of tapping. Freezing extends to 6 months.
- **The fermented version is alive.** Like kvass, the bottle builds pressure. Vent flip-top bottles every few days if you keep them past a week.

## Variations
- **Birch-and-pine-bud tonic:** Add a small handful of young pine buds to the ferment for a resinous spring drink.
- **Honey-sweetened:** Replace the sugar with honey for a softer, more rounded version.
- **Birch sap kvass:** Use the sap in place of water in a kvass recipe; the lightest, freshest version of rye kvass.
- **Sap and elderflower:** Add an elderflower head to the ferment in May for a heavier-perfumed drink.

## Serving
Drink cold straight from the fridge as a spring tonic, with a slice of dark rye bread on the side. Some Latvians keep a glass bottle ready for guests for the entire spring; others share with neighbours fresh from the tap.

## Storage
- Fresh sap: 3 days refrigerated; freezes 6 months.
- Fermented tonic: 3 to 4 weeks refrigerated, capped loosely or vented every few days. Sour deepens with time.
- Stop using if it smells off (a healthy ferment smells faintly yeasty, fresh and lemon-bright; a spoilt batch smells vinegary or musty).
