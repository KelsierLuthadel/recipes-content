# Polish

*The kitchen of the long winter. Cabbage, potatoes, pork, sour cream, dill, and the dumpling that defined Eastern European cooking. Hearty, slow, generous, and built for cold.*

## A short history

Polish cooking sits between the German west and the Russian-Ukrainian east, with strong Jewish, Lithuanian and Tatar layers. The basic ingredient list — wheat and rye bread, cabbage (fresh and fermented), potatoes (since the 18th century, when they arrived from the New World), pork, sour cream, dill, beetroot — has been the working spine of Polish food for centuries.

The Jagiellonian-era royal kitchens (15th–16th century) imported Italian and French refinements; the Partitions (1795–1918, when Poland was carved up between Prussia, Austria and Russia) layered on regional foreign influences that survived in family kitchens. The post-WWII Communist period made meat scarce and put cabbage, potatoes and bread at the centre of daily life — the cuisine became defined by what it could make from very little.

The post-1989 recovery brought Polish food back into the international restaurant conversation. The diaspora kitchens in Chicago, London and Berlin preserve and codify the tradition; the modern scene at home draws on the regional larders that survived the lean decades.

## Regional variations

- **Małopolska (south, Kraków)**: maczanka (pork sandwich), oscypek (smoked sheep cheese), the Carpathian hill cooking.
- **Mazovia (Warsaw, central)**: the codified national cuisine. Pierogi, bigos, żurek.
- **Pomerania (north, coastal)**: smoked fish, herring, the Baltic influence.
- **Silesia (south-west)**: roladek (rolled beef), Silesian dumplings, German overlap.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cabbage**: fresh in surówka (slaws), fermented as kapusta kiszona (sauerkraut), stuffed in gołąbki.
- **Pork**: every part. Kielbasa, schab (loin), boczek (bacon), trotters.
- **Sour cream (śmietana)**: thinning sauces, dressing pierogi, in soups.
- **Dill**: the herb. Fresh, dried, in nearly every dish.
- **Beetroot**: in barszcz, in salads, in pickles.
- **Rye bread (chleb żytni)**: dense, sour. The everyday loaf.
- **Vodka**: cooking spirit and table drink.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Pierogi Ruskie](/recipes/cuisine/polish/pierogi-ruskie)**: potato-and-cheese dumplings boiled then pan-fried with onion butter. The most-loved pierogi.
2. **[Pierogi z Kapusta i Grzybami](/recipes/cuisine/polish/pierogi-z-kapusta-i-grzybami)**: sauerkraut-and-mushroom pierogi, the Christmas Eve standard.
3. **[Bigos](/recipes/cuisine/polish/bigos)**: hunter's stew with sauerkraut, fresh cabbage, several meats (pork, kielbasa, venison), slow-cooked for days. The national one-pot.
4. **[Barszcz Czerwony](/recipes/cuisine/polish/barszcz-czerwony)**: clear beetroot soup, served at Christmas with small mushroom uszka dumplings.
5. **[Latkes](/recipes/cuisine/polish/latkes)**: potato pancakes with onion, served with sour cream. The Jewish-Polish Hanukkah classic that became part of the broader Polish table.
6. **[Grilled Kielbasa](/recipes/cuisine/polish/grilled-kielbasa)**: the Polish sausage, grilled and served with mustard and rye bread.
7. **[Chopped Egg and Onion](/recipes/cuisine/polish/chopped-egg-and-onion)**: hard-boiled egg mashed with caramelised onion and chicken fat. The simplest Jewish-Polish breakfast spread.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with vodka (a shot per course is the tradition) or with rich Polish beer (Żywiec, Tyskie). Build a Christmas Eve (Wigilia) twelve-dish vegetarian table, or a Sunday family lunch around bigos and pierogi.
