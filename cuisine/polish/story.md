# Polish

*The kitchen of the long winter. Cabbage, potatoes, pork, sour cream, dill, and the dumpling that defined Eastern European cooking. Hearty, slow, generous, and built for cold.*

## A short history

Polish cooking sits between the German west and the Russian-Ukrainian east, with strong Jewish, Lithuanian and Tatar layers. The working spine (wheat and rye bread, fresh and fermented cabbage, potatoes from the 18th century onwards, pork, sour cream, dill, beetroot, foraged mushrooms from the great forests) has held the table together for centuries, and the climate explains most of it: a long cold winter, a short hot summer, and a farming year that turns on what can be preserved through the dark months.

The early Slavic settlers who occupied the Polish lands from the 6th century onwards built the foundations. They grew rye, oats, millet and barley on the heavy soils, kept pigs in the oak forests on acorns, fermented cabbage in barrels for the winter, foraged mushrooms and wild berries from the woods, and made bread the centre of life. The custom of greeting guests with bread and salt (chleb i sól) goes back to that pagan Slavic era. When Mieszko I converted the country to Christianity in 966, the kitchen absorbed the Catholic fasting calendar without giving up the older traditions; Lent and Christmas Eve became the two most important food days of the year, and they still are.

The medieval and early modern centuries opened the kitchen outward. Tatar incursions from the 13th century brought minced-meat preparations and the pierog itself (the name probably comes from a Slavic root, but the dumpling tradition was reinforced by contact with the Mongol and Tatar world). Sephardic and Ashkenazi Jews settled in Poland from the 14th century onwards, eventually forming the largest Jewish community in Europe and shaping the kitchen permanently: gefilte fish, cholent, the sweet-sour braised meats, the chopped liver tradition and much of the baking shelf came from Polish-Jewish kitchens before the 20th century devastated them. The Jagiellonian-era royal kitchens (15th and 16th century, when Poland-Lithuania was the largest country in Europe) brought in Italian and French refinements; Queen Bona Sforza, arriving from Milan in 1518, is credited with introducing the salad vegetables (the *włoszczyzna*, literally "Italian things") that Polish soups still depend on: carrots, parsley root, leek and celery root.

Potatoes arrived from the Americas in the late 17th century, took hold in the 18th, and reshaped the everyday table. By 1800 the potato was the second staple after bread. The Partitions (1795 to 1918, when Prussia, Austria and Russia carved Poland up between them and the country disappeared from the map for 123 years) layered on regional foreign influences that survived in family kitchens: German baking and pork curing in the west, Austrian pastries and dumpling traditions in the south (Kraków was Habsburg territory), and Russian-influenced tea drinking and pickling in the east. Polish identity went underground and partly took refuge in the kitchen; the Christmas Eve Wigilia twelve-dish supper, codified in this era, became a national ritual that crossed all three partitions.

The 20th century took everything apart and put a thinner version back together. The two world wars destroyed the Jewish-Polish kitchen and killed millions of cooks and eaters; the post-war Communist period (1945 to 1989) made meat scarce, put cabbage, potatoes and bread at the centre of daily life, and produced the bar mleczny (milk bar) tradition of cheap canteen food that fed the cities. The cuisine defined itself by what it could make from very little: bigos stretching scraps of several meats with cabbage for days, pierogi from flour and water and whatever was in the larder, żurek soured from rye-flour starter.

The recovery after 1989 brought Polish food back into the international restaurant conversation. Diaspora kitchens in Chicago (one of the largest Polish populations outside Poland), London, Berlin and New York keep the older tradition; the modern scene at home draws on the regional larders that pulled through the lean decades. Warsaw and Kraków now run a quietly excellent restaurant culture built around heritage Polish ingredients (smoked oscypek, wild mushrooms, sour rye, heritage pork breeds) and the renewed pride in the kitchen that always carried the country through.

## Regional variations

- **Małopolska (south, Kraków)**: maczanka (pork sandwich), oscypek (smoked sheep cheese), the Carpathian hill cooking.
- **Mazovia (Warsaw, central)**: the national mainstream. Pierogi, bigos, żurek.
- **Pomerania (north, coastal)**: smoked fish, herring, the Baltic influence.
- **Silesia (south-west)**: roladek (rolled beef), Silesian dumplings, German overlap.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cabbage**: fresh in surówka (slaws), fermented as kapusta kiszona (sauerkraut), stuffed in gołąbki.
- **Pork**: every part. Kielbasa, schab (loin), boczek (bacon), trotters.
- **Sour cream (śmietana)**: thinning sauces, dressing pierogi, finishing soups.
- **Dill**: the herb. Fresh, dried, in nearly every dish.
- **Beetroot**: in barszcz, in salads, in pickles.
- **Rye bread (chleb żytni)**: dense, sour. The everyday loaf.
- **Vodka**: cooking spirit and table drink.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Pierogi Ruskie](/recipes/cuisine/polish/pierogi-ruskie)**: potato-and-cheese dumplings boiled then pan-fried in onion butter. The most-loved pierogi.
2. **[Pierogi z Kapusta i Grzybami](/recipes/cuisine/polish/pierogi-z-kapusta-i-grzybami)**: sauerkraut-and-mushroom pierogi, the Christmas Eve standard.
3. **[Bigos](/recipes/cuisine/polish/bigos)**: hunter's stew with sauerkraut, fresh cabbage and several meats (pork, kielbasa, venison), simmered for days. The national one-pot.
4. **[Barszcz Czerwony](/recipes/cuisine/polish/barszcz-czerwony)**: clear beetroot soup, served at Christmas with small mushroom uszka dumplings.
5. **[Latkes](/recipes/cuisine/polish/latkes)**: potato pancakes with onion, served with sour cream. The Jewish-Polish Hanukkah classic that became part of the broader Polish table.
6. **[Grilled Kielbasa](/recipes/cuisine/polish/grilled-kielbasa)**: the Polish sausage, grilled and served with mustard and rye bread.
7. **[Chopped Egg and Onion](/recipes/cuisine/polish/chopped-egg-and-onion)**: hard-boiled egg mashed with caramelised onion and chicken fat. The simplest Jewish-Polish breakfast spread.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with vodka (a shot per course is the tradition) or a rich Polish beer (Żywiec, Tyskie). Build a Christmas Eve (Wigilia) twelve-dish vegetarian table, or a Sunday family lunch around bigos and pierogi.
