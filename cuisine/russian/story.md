# Russian

*The kitchen of the long winter and the long table. Beetroot soup served with a dollop of sour cream, the dumpling family (pelmeni, vareniki), the slow-stewed cabbage rolls, and a sour cream finishing pour that goes on absolutely everything.*

## A short history

Russian cooking is the food of the world's largest country, shaped by a long-winter farming-and-foraging tradition. The basic shelf — rye and wheat bread, cabbage (fresh and fermented), potato (from the 18th century), beetroot, dairy, salt-cured fish, pork, beef, dill, mushrooms from the vast forests, the slow-stewed one-pot in the русская печь (Russian masonry stove) — has been the working kitchen for centuries.

The Mongol Empire (13th-14th century) left dumplings (pelmeni — the Siberian dumpling tradition the Mongols carried). The 17th-century Tsarist court began absorbing French cuisine, and from Peter the Great (early 1700s) onwards, the aristocracy ate French-influenced food while the peasantry kept the traditional tables. The 1917 Revolution flattened the distinction; the Soviet period (1922–1991) codified the canon (borscht, beef stroganoff, pelmeni, salad olivier) into mass-canteen dishes.

The post-Soviet recovery has been complicated by international politics but has produced a robust modern Russian restaurant scene (LavkaLavka, White Rabbit) drawing on regional produce. The diaspora kitchens worldwide preserve the codified tradition.

## Regional variations

- **Moscow and central Russia**: the codified mainstream. Pelmeni, borscht, beef stroganoff.
- **Siberia**: the pelmeni heartland. Smoked fish from the rivers; permafrost-preserved game.
- **St Petersburg and north-west**: closer to Baltic cooking. More fish, more bread.
- **Caucasus borderlands**: Georgian and Armenian influences. Shashlik, lavash.
- **Far East (Vladivostok)**: more Korean and Chinese influence. Salaty (cold salads) with East Asian flavours.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sour cream (smetana)**: on borscht, on pelmeni, on pancakes, on everything.
- **Dill (укроп)**: heavy use, more than in any other European cuisine.
- **Rye bread**: dense, dark, sour.
- **Beetroot, cabbage, potato**: the vegetable trinity.
- **Pickles**: cucumber, sauerkraut, pickled mushrooms.
- **Smoked fish**: especially sturgeon, salmon, herring.
- **Vodka**: cooking spirit and the universal table drink.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Borscht](/recipes/cuisine/russian/borscht)**: beetroot soup with cabbage, potato, beef and a dollop of sour cream. The national soup.
2. **[Vegetarian Borscht](/recipes/cuisine/russian/vegetarian-borscht)**: the Lenten version — same dish, no meat, often with mushrooms in the stock.
3. **[Pelmeni](/recipes/cuisine/russian/pelmeni)**: small meat dumplings (pork-and-beef), boiled or pan-fried, served with sour cream and vinegar. The Siberian classic.
4. **[Vareniki](/recipes/cuisine/russian/vareniki)**: larger dumplings stuffed with potato, cheese or cherry. The Ukrainian-Russian shared dish.
5. **[Beef Stroganoff](/recipes/cuisine/russian/beef-stroganoff)**: beef strips in mustard-and-sour-cream sauce, served over rice or noodles. Invented in the Stroganov family kitchen (1860s).
6. **[Kotlety](/recipes/cuisine/russian/kotlety)**: pan-fried minced-meat patties (think Russian meatballs flattened into burgers), served with mashed potato and dill.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with vodka (chilled, served in shot glasses, with cold zakuski — pickles, salted fish, black bread, salo) or with strong black tea from a samovar. End with syrniki (cottage-cheese pancakes) or with medovik (the multi-layered honey cake).
