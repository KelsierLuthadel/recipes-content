# Russian

*The kitchen of the long winter and the long table. Beetroot soup with a dollop of sour cream, the dumpling family (pelmeni, vareniki), the slow-stewed cabbage rolls, and a sour cream pour that finishes absolutely everything.*

## A short history

Russian cooking is the food of the world's largest country, shaped by a long-winter farming-and-foraging tradition that runs back over a thousand years. The early East Slavic peoples who settled the river basins of what is now European Russia, Belarus and Ukraine from the 6th century onwards built a kitchen on rye, wheat, oats, millet, cabbage, beetroot, turnip, foraged mushrooms and berries, river fish, honey from forest beehives and dairy from small herds. The conversion of Kyivan Rus' to Orthodox Christianity in 988 added the long Orthodox fasting calendar to the year (the four great fasts and the weekly Wednesday-Friday observance together took up nearly two hundred days), and Russian cooking developed a deep tradition of fast-day cooking built on mushrooms, grains, pickled vegetables and honey-sweetened pastries.

The Russian masonry stove (the русская печь, pronounced russkaya pech') is the central piece of the kitchen and explains a lot of the cuisine. A massive whitewashed brick stove with a sleeping platform on top, a baking chamber inside and a slow declining heat from a single morning firing, the pech let a cook bake bread in the morning, simmer schi (cabbage soup) all day in the cooling chamber, and keep porridge warm overnight. Almost every Russian country home had one; the slow-cooked, baked-not-fried character of traditional Russian food (kasha, baked piroshki, slow stewed shchi, baked fish in pots) comes straight from the stove. The pech also kept the family warm through the winter; it's hard to overstate how much of Russian domestic life happened on or around it.

The Mongol Empire (13th and 14th centuries, when the Golden Horde ruled most of the Russian lands) left dumplings; pelmeni, the Siberian dumpling tradition, came in with the Mongols, who learnt similar dumplings from the Chinese and brought them westward. Tatar and Turkic contact also brought sour milk cultures (kefir crossed in later from the Caucasus, but smetana traditions deepened in this period), the spit-roasted lamb tradition that informs shashlik, and tea, which by the 17th century was arriving by camel caravan from China and reshaping Russian sociability around the samovar.

The Romanov period (1613 to 1917) opened the kitchen to the West. Peter the Great's reforms from 1698 onwards put Dutch, German and French chefs in the St Petersburg palace; Catherine the Great's court ate French food off French porcelain and read French menus. By the 19th century the Russian aristocracy was eating an entirely French-influenced cuisine while the rural tables kept the older ways. The collision produced the dishes that now define Russian cooking abroad: beef stroganoff was born in the Stroganov family kitchen in the 1860s; salat olivier was invented by the French-Belgian chef Lucien Olivier at his Moscow restaurant the Hermitage in the 1860s; chicken Kiev (kotleta po-kiyevski) was a late-Tsarist hotel kitchen creation; even the "Russian service" of bringing courses sequentially rather than all at once was a Petersburg innovation that conquered European dining. Underneath it all, the country table kept on with schi, kasha and pelmeni.

The 1917 Revolution flattened the distinction between palace and country food, sometimes by destroying both. The Soviet period (1922 to 1991) settled the canon (borscht, beef stroganoff, pelmeni, salad olivier) into mass-canteen dishes through state cookbooks; Anastas Mikoyan's *Kniga o vkusnoy i zdorovoy pishche* (Book of Tasty and Healthy Food), first published in 1939 and reprinted across generations, taught the Soviet Union to cook a single shared cuisine. Wartime rationing and post-war shortages kept the cooking frugal; the deficit economy of the Brezhnev era turned grocery shopping into an art and produced the famous Soviet preserving tradition (every family put up tomatoes, cucumbers, mushrooms and jam for the winter). At the same time the Soviet Union's geographic reach folded in Caucasian, Central Asian, Baltic and Ukrainian dishes that everyone learnt to cook: Georgian khinkali, Uzbek plov, Ukrainian borshch, Armenian dolma all became part of the everyday Russian repertoire.

The post-Soviet recovery has been complicated by international politics but has produced a robust modern Russian restaurant scene (LavkaLavka, White Rabbit, Twins Garden) drawing on regional produce: wild fish from the Russian North, lamb from the steppes, foraged mushrooms and berries, heritage grains, fermentation revived as fine technique. Diaspora kitchens in Israel, Germany, the United States and across the former Soviet space keep the older tradition. The home table looks much as it did in the 19th century: borscht with smetana, pelmeni from a freezer pack on a cold weeknight, herring under a fur coat at New Year, and the samovar humming away in the corner.

## Regional variations

- **Moscow and central Russia**: the mainstream. Pelmeni, borscht, beef stroganoff.
- **Siberia**: the pelmeni heartland. Smoked fish from the rivers; permafrost-preserved game.
- **St Petersburg and north-west**: closer to Baltic cooking. More fish, more bread.
- **Caucasus borderlands**: Georgian and Armenian influences. Shashlik, lavash.
- **Far East (Vladivostok)**: more Korean and Chinese influence. Salaty (cold salads) with East Asian flavours.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sour cream (smetana)**: on borscht, on pelmeni, on pancakes, on everything.
- **Dill (укроп)**: heavy use, more than in any other European cuisine.
- **Rye bread**: dense, dark, sour.
- **Beetroot, cabbage, potato**: the vegetable trinity.
- **Pickles**: cucumber, sauerkraut, pickled mushrooms.
- **Smoked fish**: especially sturgeon, salmon, herring.
- **Vodka**: cooking spirit and the universal table drink.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Borscht](/recipes/cuisine/russian/borscht)**: beetroot soup with cabbage, potato, beef and a dollop of sour cream. The national soup.
2. **[Vegetarian Borscht](/recipes/cuisine/russian/vegetarian-borscht)**: the Lenten version. Same dish, no meat, often with mushrooms in the stock.
3. **[Pelmeni](/recipes/cuisine/russian/pelmeni)**: small meat dumplings (pork and beef), boiled or pan-fried, served with sour cream and vinegar. The Siberian classic.
4. **[Vareniki](/recipes/cuisine/russian/vareniki)**: larger dumplings stuffed with potato, cheese or cherry. The Ukrainian-Russian shared dish.
5. **[Beef Stroganoff](/recipes/cuisine/russian/beef-stroganoff)**: beef strips in mustard-and-sour-cream sauce, served over rice or noodles. Born in the Stroganov family kitchen in the 1860s.
6. **[Kotlety](/recipes/cuisine/russian/kotlety)**: pan-fried minced-meat patties (think Russian meatballs flattened into burgers), served with mashed potato and dill.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with vodka (chilled, served in shot glasses, with cold zakuski: pickles, salted fish, black bread, salo) or strong black tea from a samovar. Finish with syrniki (cottage-cheese pancakes) or medovik (the multi-layered honey cake).
