# Pirozhki

![Pirozhki](resources/pirozhki.jpg)

*Russia's savoury filled bun: pillowy yeasted buns filled with seasoned minced beef (or cabbage and egg, or potato), baked or pan-fried till golden, eaten warm with a small bowl of broth. The Russian street-and-home staple that turns up at every market stall and family table.*

**Serves:** Makes 16 pirozhki

**Prep Time:** 35 minutes (plus 1.5 hours rising)

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Pirozhki (пирожки; singular pirozhok) are Russia's most beloved savoury bun and one of the country's most widely eaten snacks: small pillowy yeasted dough buns filled with one of several traditional fillings (the most common are seasoned minced beef-and-onion; cabbage-and-egg; potato-and-mushroom; or a sweet jam filling for dessert pirozhki), shaped into oval buns, baked till golden on a sheet pan, or alternatively pan-fried in oil till deep-fried golden (the deep-fried version is the "fried pirozhki" of street markets and metro-station kiosks across Russia). The dish has been the Russian everyday food for centuries; it's what babushkas (grandmothers) make on a Sunday afternoon and freeze for the family's lunches, what street kiosks sell in the morning to commuters, what is packed for picnics and train journeys (the soft buns travel well). The dish is closely related to the Ukrainian pyrohy, the Polish pierożki, and a host of other Eastern European filled-bread traditions; the Russian version distinguishes itself with the slightly enriched dough (butter and milk; sometimes egg) and the variety of fillings. Three details define proper pirozhki. First, the dough must be properly yeasted and properly risen. A 1.5-hour first rise (or longer if your kitchen is cool) gives the proper pillowy texture. Quick-bread (baking-powder) shortcuts give a denser bun. Second, the filling must be cool before shaping. Hot filling melts the dough and the pirozhki tear during baking. Third, baked or fried, both are valid. The baked version is what most home cooks make (less work, less mess); the fried version is what street stalls sell. Both are properly Russian.

## Ingredients

### Dough
- 500 g plain flour
- 7 g instant dried yeast (1 sachet)
- 2 tablespoons caster sugar
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
- 250 ml warm milk
- 60 g unsalted butter (melted, slightly cooled)
- 1 large egg
- 1 large egg yolk

### Filling (beef-and-onion; the canonical)
- 400 g minced beef
- 1 large onion (finely chopped)
- 2 tablespoons butter
- 4 garlic cloves (crushed)
- 1 large hard-boiled egg (finely chopped; for added richness)
- 2 tablespoons fresh dill (finely chopped)
- 2 tablespoons fresh parsley (finely chopped)
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
- 1 teaspoon ground black pepper
- ½ teaspoon ground paprika
- 50 ml beef stock (or water)

### Egg wash
- 1 large egg (beaten with 1 tablespoon milk)

### For frying (if making fried pirozhki)
- Vegetable oil for shallow frying (about 1 cm depth)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make the dough
1. In a wide bowl, whisk together the flour, yeast, sugar and salt.
2. In a smaller bowl, whisk together the warm milk, melted butter, egg and egg yolk.
3. Pour the wet ingredients into the flour; stir to combine.
4. Knead for 8-10 minutes till smooth and elastic.
5. Place in an oiled bowl; cover with a damp cloth; let rise 1.5 hours at room temperature till doubled.

### Stage 2 - Make the beef filling
1. Heat the butter in a wide frying pan over medium heat.
2. Add the chopped onion; cook 6-7 minutes till soft and golden.
3. Add the crushed garlic; cook 30 seconds.
4. Add the minced beef; break up with a wooden spoon and cook 8-10 minutes till the beef is browned and any released liquid has evaporated.
5. Add the beef stock, salt, pepper and paprika; cook 3-4 minutes till the liquid has reduced and the filling is moist but not wet.
6. Take off the heat; let cool slightly.
7. Stir in the chopped hard-boiled egg, dill and parsley.
8. Cool to room temperature (or refrigerate till cold).

### Stage 3 - Shape the pirozhki
1. Knock back the risen dough; divide into 16 equal pieces (about 50 g each).
2. Work one at a time (keep the rest covered with a damp cloth).
3. Roll or pat one piece into a circle about 10 cm across and 4 mm thick.
4. Place 1 heaping tablespoon of cooled filling in the centre.
5. Bring the long edges of the dough together; pinch firmly to seal along the seam.
6. Shape into an oval bun with the seam on the bottom.
7. Place seam-side-down on a baking sheet lined with parchment.
8. Repeat with the remaining dough and filling.

### Stage 4 - Second rise
1. Cover the shaped pirozhki loosely with a damp cloth.
2. Let rise 30 minutes at room temperature.

### Stage 5 - Bake (or fry)
1. **To bake:** preheat the oven to 200°C (400°F).
2. Brush each pirozhok generously with egg wash.
3. Bake 18-22 minutes till deep golden.
4. **To fry:** heat 1 cm of oil in a wide heavy pan to 175°C (350°F).
5. Fry the pirozhki in batches (don't crowd); 3-4 minutes per side till deep golden all over.
6. Drain on kitchen paper.

### Stage 6 - Cool briefly and serve
1. Transfer baked pirozhki to a wire rack; cool for 5 minutes.
2. Serve warm; the filling should be properly hot.
3. Often eaten with a small bowl of clear broth (bouillon) or beetroot soup (borscht) on the side; the pirozhok dipped briefly into the warm broth.

## Notes
- **Yeasted dough, not quick bread:** proper pirozhki use a yeasted dough that rises slowly. The 1.5-hour rise is essential for the pillowy texture. Don't try with baking powder; the texture is wrong.
- **Cool the filling completely:** hot filling melts the dough and gives torn pirozhki. The filling must be cool before shaping.
- **Seal the seam firmly:** any gap lets filling leak during baking. Pinch the seam well and place seam-side-down.
- **Egg wash for shine:** baked pirozhki without egg wash look pale and dull; the egg wash gives the proper golden glossy crust.
- **Don't overfill:** 1 heaping tablespoon of filling per 10 cm dough circle is about right. Overfilled pirozhki tear during baking.

## Variations
**Cabbage-and-egg filling (kapustnye pirozhki):** sauté 1 small head of finely chopped white cabbage in butter for 30 minutes till deeply softened; mix with 4 finely chopped hard-boiled eggs, salt, pepper and dill. Classic vegetarian filling.
**Potato-and-mushroom filling:** cook 400 g of mashed potato; sauté 300 g of finely chopped mushrooms with onion; combine with dill. Classic vegetarian winter filling.
**Sweet pirozhki (with jam or fruit):** fill with thick jam (cherry, apricot, raspberry); brush with egg wash and bake the same way. Dust with icing sugar after baking. Dessert version.
**Fish pirozhki:** fill with cooked flaked salmon or cod mixed with rice, dill, hard-boiled egg and seasoning. Common Lent-friendly variation.

## Serving
Warm, eaten with the hands. As a snack with strong tea; as a meal with a small bowl of broth or borscht. Children love them in lunchboxes; adults love them at picnics. Russian babushkas pack them for train journeys (the soft yeasted bread travels well).

## Storage
- Keeps in a sealed container at room temperature 1 day; refrigerated 4 days.
- Reheat in a 160°C / 320°F oven for 8-10 minutes till warmed through; or briefly in the microwave (15-20 seconds).
- Freezes 2 months cooked or unbaked. For unbaked: shape, freeze on a tray, transfer to a bag. Bake from frozen at 180°C (360°F) for 25-30 minutes (no need to thaw).
- Day-old pirozhki are excellent split in half and toasted with butter.
- Don't aggressively reheat; the bread dries out.
