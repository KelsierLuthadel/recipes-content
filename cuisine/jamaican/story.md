# Jamaican

*The Caribbean's most assertive island cuisine: a Taíno-Arawak foundation overlaid with West African, Spanish, English, Indian and Chinese cooking, all built around jerk, ackee, scotch bonnet and allspice.*

## A short history

Jamaican cooking starts with the Taíno-Arawak people, who reached the island in canoes from the South American mainland around 600 CE. They farmed cassava (boiled, grated into flour, baked into the flatbread *bammy* still sold at every roadside stall), sweet potato and maize on conuco plot-farms cleared from the forest, harvested allspice berries (which they called pimento) from the island's evergreen *Pimenta dioica* trees, fished the reef with weighted nets, and cooked wild boar on a slow open-pit fire over a green-wood frame. They called that frame the *barbacoa*. The word, the technique and the use of pimento all survived the next five hundred years intact, which is unusual for any cuisine.

Spanish colonisation from 1494 brought citrus, plantains, sugar cane, beef cattle and pigs, and within a generation the Taíno had been almost entirely wiped out by smallpox, forced labour and warfare. The Spanish ran the island as a minor plantation outpost for over 150 years, importing the first enslaved Africans in the 1510s and seeding the populations that would later define the cuisine. When the English seized the island from Spain in 1655, the Spanish freed their enslaved population to fight, and many escaped to the interior. These were the first Maroons.

The West Africans brought as enslaved labour from the 1500s onward, mostly from what is now Ghana, Nigeria, Benin and Senegal, shaped the modern kitchen more than any other group. They contributed yam, okra, callaloo (a green similar to amaranth or taro leaf, called *kalalu* in West Africa), ackee (which arrived from West Africa on the slave ships in the 18th century and gave the country its national fruit), kidney beans and the bean-and-rice stewing tradition that became rice and peas. The Maroons in the Cockpit Country interior fused their own West African smoke-curing methods with the Taíno pit-roast, developed what we now call jerk, and signed peace treaties with the British in 1739 and 1740 that preserved their autonomy and their cooking traditions.

British rule from 1655 to independence in 1962 ran the island as a sugar colony. Salt cod (cured Newfoundland and Nova Scotia cod, shipped down by Yankee traders in exchange for sugar and rum) became the everyday plantation protein for both enslaved labourers and free workers; it remains the soul of ackee and saltfish today. The British added the breakfast meat tradition, Sunday-roast structures, the rum-and-molasses sugar economy, and the patty (a turmeric-yellow shortcrust filled with spiced beef, descended from the Cornish pasty by way of British colonial cooks). After abolition in 1834, the sugar estates ran short of labour and imported indentured workers, mostly from India between 1845 and 1917, with a smaller wave from China. The Indians brought curry powder, goat curry and roti; the Chinese brought stir-frying technique, the rice bowl as a vehicle, and the soy sauce that now flavours many Jamaican stews.

Independence in 1962 and the rise of Rastafari (the Ital cooking tradition: no salt, no pork, no shellfish, vegetable-forward) added another layer to the kitchen. Reggae and the Bob Marley generation carried Jamaican food culture into the global imagination from the 1970s onward, and the diaspora kitchens in Brixton, Toronto, Brooklyn and Miami codified jerk chicken and curry goat as international street food. Today, the home kitchen runs on the same four-part backbone the Maroons would recognise: scotch bonnet plus allspice plus fresh thyme plus scallion, applied to slow-cooked meats, stewed beans and fried sides. That backbone is the through-line.

## Regional variations

Jamaica is small (4,200 square miles) but the regional cooking differs sharply by terrain, history and migration pattern.

- **Boston Bay, Portland**: the jerk capital. Pit-smoked over fresh pimento (allspice) wood. The roadside jerk pans of Boston Beach are the reference point every other jerk cook compares themselves to.
- **Kingston and St Andrew**: city cooking. Oxtail braised slow with butter beans, ackee and saltfish for Saturday breakfast, mannish water (goat soup) for special occasions. The capital's food is heavier, longer-cooked, often one-pot.
- **Maroon Country (Cockpit, Accompong, Moore Town)**: the original pit-roast tradition. Smoke-cured wild pig, herb-heavy seasoning, very little sugar, recipes passed orally for three centuries.
- **Trelawny and Falmouth**: yam country. The north-central interior grows the country's finest yellow yam, dasheen and other ground provisions. Boiled-food (yam, dumpling, green banana) accompanies everything.
- **St Mary and Annotto Bay**: Indian and Chinese fusion is strongest here. Curry goat with roti, stir-fried chow mein, jerk-curry crossover dishes. The 19th-century indentured-labour communities settled along this coast.

A jerk pan in Boston Beach and a curry goat in Annotto Bay sit at opposite ends of the island and tell two different parts of the same story.

## Signature ingredients

- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat. Fruity, floral, hot. Used whole or pricked rather than chopped, so the cook can pull it out when the dish is hot enough.
- **Allspice (pimento)**: whole dried berries from the *Pimenta dioica* tree, not the powdered four-spice mix sold abroad. The single most important Jamaican spice, used in jerk, in soups, in marinades.
- **Fresh thyme**: West Indian thyme on the bush, finer-leafed than European, stripped from the woody stem and used in everything savoury.
- **Scallion**: used the way European cooks use onions. Crushed into marinades, added whole to soups.
- **Ginger and garlic**: the base aromatics under jerk paste and curry alike.
- **Ackee**: the national fruit. Pale yellow, soft-textured, slightly egg-like when cooked. Mild on its own, anchored by the salt cod it almost always sits with.
- **Salt cod (saltfish)**: legacy of British plantation provisioning, soaked overnight, flaked into ackee, fritters and rundown.
- **Yam, dasheen, green banana, breadfruit**: the ground provisions. Boiled, served alongside stewed mains, soaks up sauce.
- **Coconut**: milk for rice and peas, brown stew and rundown; flesh for sweets.
- **[Jerk seasoning](../../base-ingredients/spices/jerk-seasoning.md)**: the house spice blend that combines allspice, thyme, scotch bonnet, ginger, garlic, scallion and nutmeg into the marinade backbone of the island's defining dish.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Jerk chicken](jerk-chicken.md). The cornerstone. Bone-in chicken marinated in allspice, scotch bonnet, thyme and scallion, then slow-roasted (ideally over pimento wood, oven-finished works at home). Teaches the jerk-paste backbone every other Jamaican marinade leans on.
2. [Ackee and saltfish](ackee-and-saltfish.md). The national dish, eaten for Saturday breakfast across the island. Salt cod soaked, flaked and sauteed with scotch bonnet, onion, thyme and ackee. Teaches the soak-and-flake salt-cod technique plus the gentle fold-in that keeps ackee from breaking up.
3. [Goat curry](goat-curry.md). The Indo-Jamaican backbone. Bone-in goat browned hard, then long-simmered with curry powder, scotch bonnet, thyme and potato. Teaches the toast-the-curry-powder-in-oil move that defines island curries.
4. [Rice and peas](side-dishes/rice-and-peas.md). The Sunday institution. Kidney beans (called "peas" locally) cooked with rice, coconut milk, thyme and a whole scotch bonnet. Teaches the coconut-rice technique that turns a side dish into the centre of the plate.
5. [Brown stew chicken](brown-stew-chicken.md). The weeknight workhorse. Chicken browned in burnt-sugar caramel, then stewed with onion, thyme, tomato and scotch bonnet. Teaches the browning-sugar (burnt sugar) base that gives Jamaican stews their colour and depth.
6. [Festival](snacks/festival.md). Slightly sweet fried cornmeal dumplings, crisp outside, soft inside. The standard partner to jerk and to fried fish. Teaches the cornmeal-dough plus shallow-fry technique that produces the island's defining starch side.
7. [Callaloo](callaloo.md). Steamed leafy greens (amaranth or taro leaf) with scotch bonnet, garlic, thyme and onion. Teaches the quick-steam-with-aromatics method that handles the island's many leafy greens.

After those seven you have the jerk vocabulary, the salt-cod technique, the curry framework, the rice-and-peas method, the burnt-sugar stew base, the fried-dumpling craft and the leafy-green move. That covers most of what shows up on a Jamaican plate.
