# Welsh

*The kitchen of the green hills. Mountain lamb, leeks in everything (the national vegetable, worn on St David's Day), a serious cheese tradition, and a love of breads and griddle cakes that goes back to Celtic times.*

## A short history

Welsh cooking is the food of an upland sheep-farming nation, and the story starts before the Romans got there. The Iron Age Celts who settled the hills grazed cattle and sheep on the high pasture, ground oats and barley on quernstones at home, and baked the world's first griddle cakes on flat stones in the hearth. The bakestone (planc in Welsh) is still the right way to cook a Welsh cake. Bread, butter, lamb, leeks, cheese: the daily shelf hasn't changed much in two thousand years.

The medieval centuries layered religion on top. Cistercian monks at Tintern, Strata Florida and Valle Crucis built the wool and dairy industries that put Welsh cheese on European tables by the 1200s, and the Welsh hard cheeses behind a proper Welsh rarebit trace back to that monastic dairy tradition. The leek attached itself to St David in the 6th century, the story being that he told his Welsh soldiers to wear leeks in their helmets so they could tell each other apart in battle with the Saxons. They won. The leek has been the national badge and the national vegetable ever since.

The Tudor and Stuart centuries kept Wales rural and poor, but the kitchen got rich on what the hills produced: lamb, milk, cheese, leeks, oats. The Welsh dresser, the bakestone and the iron griddle ran every farmhouse. Bara brith (speckled bread, raisin-studded and tea-soaked) and the bara planc (bakestone bread) anchored every family table. Cawl, the slow lamb-and-leek-and-root broth eaten from a wooden bowl with a spoon, kept the household fed through the long Welsh winter.

The Industrial Revolution turned south Wales upside down. Coal mining filled the Valleys with workers who needed filling, portable food, and the kitchen answered: the Welsh oggie (a lamb and leek pasty taken down the pit), the Welsh cake (sweet, raisin-studded, baked on a bakestone for the lunch tin), the cockles and laverbread breakfast that fuelled the Swansea docks. Welsh language and Welsh food preserved each other through the long decline of the heavy industries; what kept the language alive on the hearth kept the cooking alive too.

The post-industrial decades from the 1990s on rebuilt the Welsh produce shelf. Welsh black beef, Penclawdd cockles, laverbread, Pembrokeshire crab and lobster, the cheeses out of mid-Wales and the Brecon Beacons: a quietly excellent modern food scene grew up on the back of the older traditions. The kitchen reads the same today as it has for centuries. Lamb and leek and cheese and oats, cooked simply and eaten warm.

## Regional variations

- **South Wales (Cardiff, Swansea)**: the cockles-and-laverbread breakfast tradition, Glamorgan sausages, the Valleys' filling pit-village cooking.
- **North Wales (Snowdonia)**: lamb country. Sunday lamb roasts, hill-farm cooking, the Conwy mussel beds.
- **West Wales (Pembrokeshire)**: seafood. Crab, lobster, sewin (sea trout), the cawl tradition at its strongest.
- **Mid Wales**: the home of the Welsh cake, the cheese-making heartland, the Brecon Beacons hill-lamb farms.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb**: Welsh mountain lamb is the great culinary export. Spring lamb is the seasonal celebration; older hill-grazed lamb carries the deeper flavour.
- **Leeks**: the national vegetable. In cawl, in cheese-and-onion pie, in soups, sliced raw onto cheese on toast.
- **Cheese**: Welsh cellars make excellent hard cheeses and the crumbly white Caerphilly. The big four are Caerphilly, Y Fenni, Snowdonia and the Pant-ys-Gawn goat's cheese.
- **Laverbread**: seaweed boiled to a black puree, fried with oats and bacon for breakfast. The proper Welsh wake-up.
- **Cockles**: from Penclawdd on the Gower (the famous low-tide harvest), served with bacon and laverbread.
- **Oats and barley**: in cakes, in porridge, in the bara brith.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cawl](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/cawl)**: lamb, leek and root vegetable stew. The national dish. Eat in winter from a deep bowl with bread and cheese on the side.
2. **[Welsh Rarebit](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/welsh-rarebit)**: thick cheese-and-mustard-and-ale sauce on toasted bread, grilled till bubbling. The classic supper.
3. **[Glamorgan Sausages](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/glamorgan-sausages)**: vegetarian "sausages" of Caerphilly cheese, leek and breadcrumb, shallow-fried. The South Wales vegetarian classic.
4. **[Welsh Oggie](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/welsh-oggie)**: lamb, leek and potato pasty. The miner's lunch.
5. **[Welsh Leek and Bacon Pie](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/welsh-leek-and-bacon-pie)**: leek and bacon in cream sauce under shortcrust pastry. The Sunday supper.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Welsh ale (Brains, Felinfoel) or Penderyn whisky (the country's first single malt distillery since the 19th century). For St David's Day on 1st March, wear a leek, serve cawl, and finish with Welsh cakes and Glamorgan cheese.
