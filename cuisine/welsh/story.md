# Welsh

*The kitchen of the green hills. Lamb from the mountains, leeks in everything (the national vegetable, worn on St David's Day), the cheese tradition, and a love of breads and griddle cakes that goes back to Celtic times.*

## A short history

Welsh cooking is the food of an upland sheep-farming nation. The mountains gave the world's most-loved lamb (Welsh lamb is PGI-protected); the dairy industry produced cawl (Welsh hard cheese) and the famous Welsh rarebit (cheese on toast, properly made with mustard and beer); the iron pans (bakestones, planken) baked the Welsh cakes, bara brith and Glamorgan sausages that still anchor the home kitchen.

The 19th-century industrial era brought coal-mining communities and an emphasis on filling, cheap, portable food: the Welsh oggie (pasty), the Welsh cake (sweet griddle cake taken down the mine), the bowl of cawl with bread. The post-industrial recovery in the 1990s-2000s recodified Welsh produce — Welsh black beef, Penclawdd cockles, laverbread (seaweed), Tintern apples — as the basis of a quietly excellent modern restaurant scene.

## Regional variations

- **South Wales (Cardiff, Swansea)**: cockles-and-laverbread breakfast tradition, Glamorgan sausages, the Valleys' coal-era cheap-and-filling.
- **North Wales (Snowdonia)**: lamb country. Sunday lamb roasts, hill-farm cooking.
- **West Wales (Pembrokeshire)**: seafood. Crab, lobster, the cawl tradition strongest here.
- **Mid Wales**: the home of the Welsh cake; cheese-making heartland.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb**: PGI Welsh lamb is the most-loved British lamb. Spring lamb is the seasonal celebration.
- **Leeks**: the national vegetable. In cawl, in cheese-and-onion pie, in soups.
- **Cheddar**: West Country style but Welsh cellars make excellent versions. Cawl Cymreig (a hard cheese) and Caerphilly (crumbly white).
- **Laverbread**: seaweed boiled to a black puree, fried with oats and bacon for breakfast.
- **Cockles**: from Penclawdd (the famous Gower harvest), served with bacon and laverbread.
- **Oats and barley**: in cakes, in porridge, in the bara brith.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cawl](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/cawl)**: lamb, leek and root vegetable stew. The national dish, eaten in winter from a deep bowl with bread and cheese on the side.
2. **[Welsh Rarebit](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/welsh-rarebit)**: thick cheese-and-mustard-and-ale sauce on toasted bread, grilled till bubbling. The classic supper.
3. **[Glamorgan Sausages](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/glamorgan-sausages)**: vegetarian "sausages" of Caerphilly cheese, leek and breadcrumb, shallow-fried. The South Wales vegetarian classic.
4. **[Welsh Oggie](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/welsh-oggie)**: lamb, leek and potato pasty. The miner's lunch.
5. **[Welsh Leek and Bacon Pie](/recipes/cuisine/welsh/welsh-leek-and-bacon-pie)**: leek and bacon in cream sauce under shortcrust pastry. The Sunday supper.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Welsh ale (Brains, Felinfoel) or Penderyn whisky (Wales's first single malt distillery since the 19th century). For St David's Day (March 1st), wear a leek, serve cawl, finish with Welsh cakes and Glamorgan cheese.
