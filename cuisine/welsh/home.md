# Welsh Cuisine

![Welsh Cuisine](resources/hero.jpg)

Welsh food is shaped by hill-country sheep farming, leek-and-dairy smallholdings, and a long coastline. Lamb is the centrepiece (cawl, the lamb-and-vegetable broth, is the national dish), and the leek shares equal billing as a national symbol on the plate. The chapel-tea tradition gave Wales its baking culture: bara brith (the tea-soaked spiced fruit loaf), Welsh cakes (griddle-cooked spiced raisin rounds), and teisen lap (the moist fruit cake a miner could carry down the pit in his pocket). Welsh rarebit came out of the pubs and inns, a proper cheese-and-ale toast that travelled across Britain under its Welsh name. Lobsgows feeds the north as cawl feeds the south, and the seaboard adds laverbread (cooked seaweed) and Penclawdd cockles to the breakfast plate.
