# Welsh Bubble and Squeak

![Welsh Bubble and Squeak](resources/welsh-bubble-and-squeak.jpg)

*Mashed potato, soft cabbage and softened leek pressed into a hot pan with dripping or butter and fried until the bottom goes deep gold and crisp: the Welsh take on Monday's leftovers.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes (with leftovers; 25 minutes from scratch)

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Every British region has a fry-up of leftover potato and cabbage from the Sunday roast, and the Welsh version adds a sliced leek to the pan, which makes the whole thing sweeter and quieter than the English version. The name comes from the noise the mix makes in the pan as it cooks (the bubble of moisture, the squeak of the cabbage), and the rule is plain: it must be cooked in a wide pan with enough fat that the bottom layer goes deep gold and crisp, while the inside stays soft. It is traditionally Monday's breakfast or lunch from Sunday's leftovers, but it has earned its place on the side of a weekday plate of cold lamb, a fried egg, or a piece of grilled bacon. Beef dripping is the right fat; butter is the everyday substitute.

## Ingredients

- 600 g floury potatoes (or 600 g cold leftover mash)
- 300 g savoy or spring cabbage, shredded
- 1 medium leek, sliced thin
- 3 tbsp beef dripping or butter
- 1 tsp salt
- Plenty of black pepper
- Optional: 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
- Optional: 4 eggs to fry alongside

## Method

### Stage 1 - Cook the potato (skip if using leftover mash)
1. Peel and chop the potatoes; cover with cold salted water.
2. Bring to a boil; simmer 15 minutes until tender.
3. Drain very well; return to the dry pan over low heat 1 minute to dry out.
4. Mash without milk or butter; leave it on the dry side.

### Stage 2 - Soften the leek and cabbage
1. Melt 1 tbsp dripping in a pan over medium heat.
2. Add the sliced leek; cook 4 minutes until soft.
3. Add the shredded cabbage; cook 4 minutes until wilted but still bright.
4. Tip into a large bowl.

### Stage 3 - Mix
1. Add the mash to the bowl with the cabbage and leek.
2. Season with salt, pepper and Worcestershire sauce if using.
3. Mix with a fork, keep the texture rough, not a puree.

### Stage 4 - Fry the cake
1. Heat the remaining 2 tbsp dripping in a wide non-stick pan over medium-high heat.
2. Tip the mix in; press down to a thick cake.
3. Cook 6 to 7 minutes without stirring until the underside is deep gold and crisp.

### Stage 5 - Flip
1. Slide the cake onto a plate.
2. Invert another plate on top; flip; slide back into the pan, crisp side up.
3. Cook a further 5 minutes; the second side will be lighter.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Slide onto a board; cut into wedges.
2. Top with a fried egg if you like.

## Notes
- **Dry mash:** wet mash will not crisp.
- **Beef dripping is the right fat:** butter is the workable alternative, oil is a poor third.
- **Press down firmly:** the cake needs to hold together for the flip.
- **Wide pan, hot heat:** the bottom must caramelise without the inside drying out.
- **Don't stir:** stirring kills the crust.

## Variations
- **With bacon:** add 100 g chopped fried bacon to the mix.
- **With cheese:** scatter grated cheddar over the top in the last minute under a grill.
- **Sprouts version:** swap cabbage for shredded leftover Christmas sprouts after Boxing Day.
- **Individual cakes:** shape into 4 small patties and fry separately.
- **With swede:** add 200 g mashed cooked swede for a Welsh-border twist.

## Serving
- Monday breakfast with a fried egg and bacon · alongside cold roast lamb or chicken · as a side to grilled chops · with brown sauce or HP on the plate · as a quick supper with a poached egg on top.

## Storage
- Mix keeps 2 days raw in the fridge; fry on demand.
- Cooked cake keeps 2 days; reheat in a hot pan to refresh the crust.
- Freezes 1 month as a raw shaped patty; fry from frozen, add 4 minutes.
