# Belgian

*The kitchen of beer, frites and mussels. A French-influenced cuisine with the world's most-loved chocolate, the world's most-loved fries (insistently Belgian, not French), and 1,500+ beer varieties from a country smaller than Switzerland.*

## A short history

Belgian cooking sits between French and Dutch-German traditions, on a stretch of low country that has been a crossroads for as long as Europe has had crossroads. The Gauls and Belgae (the Celtic tribes Caesar described as "the bravest of all Gauls") farmed grain on the Flemish plain and raised cattle on the wetter pasture; the Romans brought viticulture, the orchard, and the first hard cheeses. By the early Middle Ages, the abbeys of the Low Countries were already brewing beer for travellers and the poor, a tradition that would shape the country's drink for the next thousand years.

The medieval centuries turned Flanders and Brabant into the wealthiest urban region of northern Europe. Bruges, Ghent and Ypres ran the wool and cloth trade with England from the 1200s, and that wealth bought spices through Venice (pepper, cinnamon, ginger, saffron, sugar) that Flemish cookbooks of the 1300s were already using more freely than anyone west of the Italian city-states. The Burgundian dukes of the 1400s, the richest court in Europe, codified the formal banquet style and turned Brussels into a kitchen capital. Beer brewing meanwhile diversified into a thicket of regional styles: lambic (spontaneously-fermented in the open air of the Pajottenland west of Brussels, possibly the oldest beer style still being made), Flemish red and brown ales, the abbey tripels and dubbels.

The Habsburg Netherlands and then Spanish rule in the 16th and 17th centuries layered Mediterranean and Iberian influence onto the kitchen and pushed the Counter-Reformation Catholic identity that kept the Trappist brewing tradition alive in monasteries. The Columbian Exchange brought the potato in the late 1600s; Belgians took to it so thoroughly that the country's claim to have invented the frite (twice-fried in beef fat, served from a paper cone with mayonnaise) is plausible, if disputed. Belgian independence from the Netherlands in 1830 created the modern country and a particular Brussels bourgeois cuisine that fused French haute technique with the older Flemish home cooking.

The 19th century codified the dishes that now define the country: moules-frites (Brussels mussels in white wine and celery, with chips), carbonnade flamande (beef braised in dark beer with onion, mustard and a bread cap), waterzooi (the creamy chicken or fish stew from Ghent), chicon au gratin (endive wrapped in ham and baked under cheese sauce), stoemp (mashed potato with vegetables). The chocolate tradition turned Brussels and Antwerp into world chocolate capitals: Jean Neuhaus invented the filled praline in 1912 at his shop on the Galeries Royales Saint-Hubert, and Godiva opened in 1926. Belgian waffles (the Brussels waffle is rectangular with deep pockets; the Liège waffle is round, denser, studded with pearl sugar) had already been Belgian fair food for centuries and travelled to the 1964 New York World's Fair to become American mall food.

The two world wars devastated Belgium twice over (the country sat in the path of both German invasions), and rationing forced the kitchen back to its frugal Flemish roots: stoemp, the long beer stew, the cabbage-and-bacon plate. The post-war recovery, the rise of EU institutions in Brussels from the 1950s, and a new generation of chefs (Pierre Wynants at Comme Chez Soi, Peter Goossens at Hof van Cleve) rebuilt Belgian fine dining into a quietly world-class scene. Today the brewing tradition remains unmatched: just six Trappist monasteries in Belgium make beer under the appellation (Westvleteren, Chimay, Orval, Rochefort, Westmalle, Achel), and the country supports more genuinely distinct beer styles than anywhere else on earth.

## Regional variations

- **Flanders (Dutch-speaking, north)**: carbonnade flamande, waterzooi, Flemish red ale, witbier.
- **Wallonia (French-speaking, south)**: more French influence. Trappist beer territory.
- **Brussels (officially bilingual)**: codified mainstream. Moules-frites, stoemp, the chocolate capital.
- **Ardennes (south-east hills)**: cured meat country. Ardennes ham, pâtés, game.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beer**: as a cooking ingredient (carbonnade, Flemish stew) and as the universal table drink.
- **Frites (chips)**: twice-fried, served in a paper cone with mayonnaise (the proper Belgian condiment, not ketchup).
- **Mussels**: from the Zeeland banks (across the Dutch border, but eaten as a Belgian institution). Steamed, served with chips.
- **Endive (witloof / chicon)**: braised with ham and cheese (chicon au gratin).
- **Speculoos**: the spiced shortbread biscuit, used as a cookie and a spread.
- **Chocolate**: dark, milk, in pralines.
- **Butter and cream**: heavy use.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Moules-Frites](/recipes/cuisine/belgian/moules-frites)**: mussels steamed in white wine, garlic and celery; served with chips and mayo. The Brussels institution.
2. **[Carbonnade Flamande](/recipes/cuisine/belgian/carbonnade-flamande)**: beef slow-braised in dark beer (typically a Belgian dubbel or brown ale) with onions, sugar and mustard-spread bread. The Flemish stew.
3. **[Waterzooi](/recipes/cuisine/belgian/waterzooi)**: Ghent's creamy fish (originally) or chicken stew with leek, potato and cream, finished with egg yolk.
4. **[Stoemp](/recipes/cuisine/belgian/stoemp)**: mashed potato with vegetables (carrot, leek, spinach), served with sausage or bacon. The Brussels comfort food.
5. **[Vol-au-Vent à la Belge](/recipes/cuisine/belgian/vol-au-vent-a-la-belge)**: puff-pastry shells filled with chicken-and-mushroom in cream sauce. The Sunday family dish.

## Where to go next

Pour a Trappist beer (Chimay Blue, Orval, Westvleteren 12 if you can get one) or a lambic gueuze (sour, complex, beer-meets-champagne). Finish with a few good chocolate pralines or a hot Liège waffle from a street cart.
