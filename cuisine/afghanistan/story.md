# Afghan

*The kitchen at the crossroads of Central Asia, Persia and the subcontinent. Long-grain rice with caramelised carrot and raisin, lamb cooked slow, the kebab tradition, and a love of yoghurt that runs through nearly every plate.*

## A short history

Afghan cooking begins on the high plateau between the Hindu Kush and the Helmand, where the land forces the menu. The earliest farmers of the Bactrian plain were grinding wheat and barley by 7000 BCE, and Aï Khanoum's archaeological layers show vineyards, almond orchards and the first ovens for the round flatbread that's still called nan-e Afghani. Pastoralist Pashtun and Tajik families moved sheep and goats up and down the valleys for the summer pasture, and the lamb-on-an-open-fire kebab and the slow-simmered korma trace back to that nomad shelf. Yoghurt and dried mulberry kept through the long winters when nothing else would.

Afghan cooking sits where the Silk Road crossed itself. From the first millennium BCE onwards the caravan trade ran east-west through Balkh, Herat and Kandahar, and every passing kitchen left something. Persia handed over the rice tradition (palaw), the saffron-and-dried-fruit profile, the dolma and the rosewater-scented sweets. The Indian subcontinent brought the spice rack: cumin, coriander, cardamom, black pepper, and the habit of finishing things with yoghurt. Central Asia carried in the dumpling (mantu, cousin to the Uzbek manti and the Chinese mantou) and the lamb-on-the-fire kebab. Buddhist Bamiyan, Zoroastrian Balkh and the Islamic conquest of the 7th and 8th centuries each layered their own food rules onto the same pot.

The medieval centuries saw Herat under the Timurids (14th and 15th centuries) become one of the great culinary capitals of the Islamic world. Court cooks codified the long-grain rice pulao, the meat-stuffed dumpling, the ash (thick herb-and-bean soup) and the dried-fruit pilaf that still defines a wedding feast. Persian poetry written in Herat sets the table: saffron rice, lamb, pomegranate, walnut, the quince paste at the end of the meal. The Mughal dynasty (founded by Babur, who set out from Kabul in 1526) carried that codified Afghan-Persian cuisine south into India, and the biryani, the korma and the kebab on every Delhi menu trace back to a Kabul kitchen.

The signature dish, Kabuli pulao (long-grain rice with lamb, caramelised carrots and raisins), got codified in the 19th-century Kabul royal kitchens under the Durrani and Barakzai amirs. It's now Afghanistan's unofficial national dish and the gateway to the rest of the cuisine. The same period saw the chaikhana (teahouse) culture spread along the trade routes, where green tea, naan and a pot of lamb stew would feed a traveller all day.

The Soviet invasion (1979 to 1989), the civil war, and the long years of Taliban rule and Western intervention scattered the country. The kitchen, however, travelled with the people. Diaspora cooks across Iran, Pakistan, Germany, the US, the UK and Canada built one of the most quietly successful refugee restaurant traditions on earth. The Helmand brothers' chain in California, the Hamburg and London Afghan rooms, and a growing Toronto and Sydney scene have carried the cooking worldwide while access at home stayed difficult. Inside Afghanistan, the home table still reads as it has for centuries. Pulao, kebab, naan, chai, yoghurt, fresh herb-and-radish platter on the side.

## Regional variations

- **Kabul and the centre**: the codified mainstream. Kabuli pulao, mantu, the urban refinement.
- **Pashtun belt (south and east)**: closer to Pakistani Pashtun. Chapli kebab, lamb karahi, more chilli.
- **Hazara (central highlands)**: distinct dumpling tradition (mantu, ashak), more turnip and squash.
- **North (Mazar-e-Sharif)**: closer to Central Asian. Plov-style rice, more lamb.

## Signature ingredients

- **Long-grain basmati**: the rice of choice. Aged, fragrant.
- **Lamb**: the meat.
- **Yoghurt (mast)**: dressing for many dishes; the base of chaka (strained yoghurt sauce).
- **Coriander and dill**: the herb pair.
- **Cumin and cardamom**: the warm spices.
- **Dried fruit (raisins, apricots)**: in pulao, in stews.
- **Pomegranate molasses**: souring agent in some stews.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Kabuli Pulao](/recipes/cuisine/afghanistan/kabuli-pulao)**: long-grain rice with chunks of slow-cooked lamb, topped with caramelised carrots, raisins and pistachio slivers. The national dish.
2. **[Mantu](/recipes/cuisine/afghanistan/mantu)**: small steamed dumplings of spiced minced lamb and onion, served with tomato-lentil sauce and garlic yoghurt.
3. **[Chapli Kebab](/recipes/cuisine/afghanistan/chapli-kebab)**: flat patties of spiced minced lamb with pomegranate seeds, shallow-fried.
4. **[Lamb Karahi (Afghan)](/recipes/cuisine/afghanistan/lamb-karahi-afghani)**: lamb wok-fried with tomato, ginger, green chilli and coriander. The Afghan answer to the Pakistani karahi.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with chai (black tea, sometimes cardamom-spiced) and naan-e-Afghani (long oval flatbread). Build an Afghan table around the pulao centrepiece with mantu or chapli on the side, a yoghurt sauce, and a fresh herb-and-radish platter.
