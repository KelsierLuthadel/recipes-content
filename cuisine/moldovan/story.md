# Moldovan

*The kitchen between Romania and Ukraine. Mămăligă cornmeal mash, sarmale cabbage rolls, the placintă cheese pies that go with the famous Moldovan red wine, and the country's signature zeamă — a tangy chicken-and-noodle soup that's a hangover cure as much as a meal.*

## A short history

Moldovan cooking is closely related to Romanian (Moldova was historically the Principality of Moldavia, and modern Moldova was the Romanian Bessarabia before WWII), with stronger Ukrainian, Russian and Turkish layers from the country's eastern position. Romanian-Moldovan sarmale, mămăligă and placintă are essentially shared dishes; the zeamă (the soup) and the country's distinctive wine industry are more particularly Moldovan.

The Soviet period (1940–1991) made Moldova one of the USSR's main agricultural and wine-producing republics; the country still has the highest density of vineyards per capita in the world. Post-independence the cuisine is reasserting itself alongside continued Russian, Romanian and Ukrainian overlaps.

## Regional variations

- **Chișinău and the centre**: codified mainstream.
- **Transnistria (east, breakaway region)**: closer to Russian-Ukrainian.
- **Gagauz region (south)**: Turkic minority, distinct dishes.
- **Codrii (forested centre)**: foraging traditions strong.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cornmeal (mămăligă)**: the universal starch. Coarser than Italian polenta.
- **Brânză de oi**: sheep's milk cheese.
- **Cabbage**: fresh and fermented.
- **Pork**: every part. Cured, smoked, fresh.
- **Sour cream (smântână)**: dressing everything.
- **Dill and parsley**: the herb pair.
- **Wine**: the country's signature product. Used in cooking and at the table.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Sarmale (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/sarmale-moldovan)**: cabbage rolls stuffed with rice and pork, slow-cooked in sour cream and dill broth. The Moldovan variant is creamier than the Romanian.
2. **[Mămăligă (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/mamaliga-moldovan)**: cornmeal mash with brânză de oi (sheep cheese) and sour cream.
3. **[Plăcintă (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/placinta-moldovan)**: pan-fried pastry filled with cheese, cabbage, pumpkin or apple. The everyday Moldovan snack.
4. **[Zeamă](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/zeama)**: tangy chicken-and-noodle soup with borș (fermented wheat-bran liquid), parsley and homemade noodles. The signature Moldovan bowl.
5. **[Tochitură (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/tochitura-moldovan)**: pork stew with garlic, onion and wine, served with mămăligă and a fried egg on top.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Moldovan red wine (the country's Negru de Purcari is world-class) or with rachiu (the grape spirit). The autumn wine harvest and the spring Easter table are the two great food events of the Moldovan year.
