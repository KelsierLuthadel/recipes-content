# Moldovan

*The kitchen between Romania and Ukraine. Mămăligă cornmeal mash, sarmale cabbage rolls, the placintă cheese pies that go with the famous Moldovan red wine, and the country's signature zeamă, a tangy chicken-and-noodle soup that's a hangover cure as much as a meal.*

## A short history

The fertile black-earth strip between the Prut and the Dniester has been farmed and cooked on for at least seven thousand years. The Cucuteni-Trypillia culture, which flourished here from around 5500 to 2750 BCE, raised some of the largest Neolithic settlements in Europe, fired clay pots that look uncannily like the modern Moldovan ceramic tava, and grew the wheat-and-barley-and-pulse triad still on the table today. Geto-Dacian peoples followed, then Roman colonisation of Dacia in the 2nd century CE added vines, olive oil (imported), and the basic Mediterranean instinct for slow stewing in clay. Modern Moldovan corn-and-cheese cooking sits on a base that is genuinely millennia deep.

Moldovan cooking is closely related to Romanian (Moldova was historically the Principality of Moldavia, founded in 1359 by Dragoș, and modern Moldova was the Romanian Bessarabia before WWII), with stronger Ukrainian, Russian and Turkish layers thanks to the country's eastern position. The medieval Principality of Moldavia, at its height under Stephen the Great (Ștefan cel Mare, 1457 to 1504), fought off Ottoman, Polish and Hungarian armies and developed a rural kitchen of mămăligă, salt-cured pork, sheep's cheese, and the slow-fermented vegetables that carry a household through winter. Orthodox Christianity arrived with the early Slavs and shaped the food calendar around forty-day fasts (no meat, dairy, or eggs during Lent), which is why the Moldovan repertoire is unexpectedly rich in vegetable, mushroom and bean dishes.

Ottoman tribute status from the 16th century onwards layered the Turkish kitchen onto the Moldovan base. Sarmale (the cabbage roll, from the Turkish *sarma*, "to wrap") arrived in this era, along with the stuffed pepper, the use of dill and parsley as everyday herbs, baklava and gogoși (fried dough), and the everyday Turkish coffee culture that ran in Chișinău cafes until the Soviet era. The Columbian Exchange came in slowly through trade with the Habsburgs and the Ottomans: maize arrived in the 17th century and within two generations had replaced millet as the staple grain of the Moldovan countryside, with mămăligă becoming the daily bread. Tomatoes, peppers, beans and potatoes followed, all settling into the rural kitchen by the early 19th century.

The Russian Empire annexed Bessarabia in 1812 and held it for over a century, layering Russian dishes, Orthodox monastic cooking, and the samovar tea tradition onto the existing base. Romanian rule from 1918 to 1940 returned the territory to Bucharest and codified a Romanian-Moldovan national cuisine in print. The Soviet period (1940 to 1991) then made Moldova one of the USSR's main agricultural and wine-producing republics; the country still has the highest density of vineyards per capita in the world. Soviet collectivisation pushed mass production of canned vegetables, sour cream and bread, and brought dishes like the Olivier salad and the Kiev cutlet into every Moldovan household. The Soviet decades also industrialised the famous Cricova and Mileștii Mici wine cellars, which now hold the largest underground wine collection on earth (over 1.5 million bottles in tunnels stretching more than 200 kilometres).

Since independence in 1991 the cuisine has been reasserting itself alongside continued Russian, Romanian and Ukrainian overlaps. The traditional wedding feast, the Easter table with cozonac and painted eggs, and the autumn wine harvest (vinul de casă, the homemade wine that every village family makes from their own grapes) anchor the rural calendar. A new generation of Moldovan chefs is putting brânză de oi, wild boar from the Codrii forests, and the local indigenous grape varieties (Fetească Albă, Fetească Neagră, Rara Neagră) on European tables, building a quietly serious gastronomy on the country's deep agricultural roots.

## Regional variations

- **Chișinău and the centre**: codified mainstream.
- **Transnistria (east, breakaway region)**: closer to Russian-Ukrainian.
- **Gagauz region (south)**: Turkic minority, distinct dishes.
- **Codrii (forested centre)**: strong foraging traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cornmeal (mămăligă)**: the universal starch. Coarser than Italian polenta.
- **Brânză de oi**: sheep's milk cheese.
- **Cabbage**: fresh and fermented.
- **Pork**: every part. Cured, smoked, fresh.
- **Sour cream (smântână)**: dressing everything.
- **Dill and parsley**: the herb pair.
- **Wine**: the country's signature product. Used in cooking and at the table.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Sarmale (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/sarmale-moldovan)**: cabbage rolls stuffed with rice and pork, slow-cooked in sour cream and dill broth. The Moldovan variant is creamier than the Romanian.
2. **[Mămăligă (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/mamaliga-moldovan)**: cornmeal mash with brânză de oi (sheep cheese) and sour cream.
3. **[Plăcintă (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/placinta-moldovan)**: pan-fried pastry filled with cheese, cabbage, pumpkin or apple. The everyday Moldovan snack.
4. **[Zeamă](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/zeama)**: tangy chicken-and-noodle soup with borș (fermented wheat-bran liquid), parsley and homemade noodles. The signature Moldovan bowl.
5. **[Tochitură (Moldovan)](/recipes/cuisine/moldovan/tochitura-moldovan)**: pork stew with garlic, onion and wine, served with mămăligă and a fried egg on top.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Moldovan red wine (the country's Negru de Purcari is world-class) or with rachiu (the grape spirit). The autumn wine harvest and the spring Easter table are the two great food events of the Moldovan year.
