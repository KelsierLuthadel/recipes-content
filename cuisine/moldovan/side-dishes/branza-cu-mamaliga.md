# Brânză cu mămăligă

![Brânză cu mămăligă](resources/branza-cu-mamaliga.jpg)

*The classic Moldovan side: crumbled sheep's cheese melted into a slab of hot mămăligă with a knob of butter, the simplest possible plate, eaten alongside any stew or as a snack on its own.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 5 minutes

## Overview
Brânză cu mămăligă is the simplest, oldest pairing in Moldovan cooking: a slab of hot cornmeal porridge, a fistful of sharp sheep cheese crumbled over the top, and a knob of butter melting through it all. The dish is a side, a snack, and a meal depending on the hour. The cheese is brânză de oi, the salty white sheep cheese made in the Codru hills and the Bessarabian uplands, sharper and saltier than a feta. The brânză is broken into crumbs over the steaming polenta and the residual heat does the work, the cheese softening and the butter pooling. Eat with a wooden spoon and a glass of cold buttermilk; eat alongside a stew; eat for breakfast with strong tea.

## Ingredients

- 1 batch hot stiff mămăligă (see Mămăligă Moldovan), freshly cooked
- 30 g unsalted butter
- 250 g brânză de oi (sheep cheese), crumbled (feta is the closest substitute)
- A small grind of black pepper
- Optional: 2 tbsp sour cream (smântână) for finishing

## Method

### Stage 1 - Cook the mămăligă
1. Cook a stiff mămăligă as in the separate recipe; the polenta must be very hot and pulling cleanly from the pot.
2. Turn out onto a warm wooden board, or spoon into a wide warm bowl.

### Stage 2 - Layer
1. While the mămăligă is steaming hot, drop the butter on top.
2. As the butter starts to melt, scatter the crumbled cheese in a thick even layer.
3. Press the cheese lightly into the surface with the back of a spoon so it touches the heat.
4. Cover loosely with a tea towel; rest 2 minutes for the cheese to soften and the butter to pool.

### Stage 3 - Finish
1. Add a small grind of black pepper.
2. If using sour cream, spoon a generous pillow on top.
3. Serve at once with a wooden spoon, scooping straight from the board or bowl.

## Notes
- **Heat is the cook:** there is no separate cheese melt; the residual heat from the polenta softens the cheese in two minutes.
- **The cheese:** brânză de oi is the right choice; feta is a fine substitute; Bulgarian sirene is closer in saltiness. Cow's milk cheeses are wrong.
- **The butter:** unsalted is correct; the cheese carries enough salt.
- **Stiff polenta is essential:** loose polenta turns the cheese to a slurry.
- **No oven:** this is a stove-top dish; the oven version is bulz, a different recipe.

## Variations
- **Cu smântână:** with a generous spoon of sour cream on top, the everyday country version.
- **Cu jumări:** with crisp pork cracklings scattered over.
- **Cu ouă:** with two soft-fried eggs on top, the lunchtime version.
- **Cu mămăligă rece prăjită:** with day-old mămăligă slices pan-fried gold and cheese melted on top.
- **Cu urdă:** with sweet whey-cheese (urdă) instead of brânză, the lighter springtime version.

## Serving
Hot from the board, alongside sarmale or tochitură, with a glass of cold buttermilk or cold Fetească Albă. For breakfast with strong black tea. As a snack on its own with a slice of country bread.

## Storage
- Best fresh; the cheese and butter do not refrigerate well together.
- Leftover slabs: pan-fry in butter the next day until gold and crisp.
- Do not freeze.
