# Argentinian

*The kitchen of the cattle country and the European immigrant city. Asado over open coals as the universal celebration, the empanada as the universal snack, dulce de leche on everything, and an Italian-Spanish hybrid pasta-and-pizza culture that's distinctly Argentine.*

## A short history

Argentinian cooking rests on two roots: the gaucho cattle-herding tradition of the pampas (slow-grilled beef over open coals — asado — is the national passion; Argentina has the highest beef consumption per capita in the world) and the massive late-19th-and-early-20th-century European immigration (Italian, Spanish, German, Welsh, Eastern European Jewish) that gave Buenos Aires its cosmopolitan food culture.

The Italian influence is exceptionally deep. Pasta is on most Argentine home tables weekly; pizza (especially the Buenos Aires-style with a thick, focaccia-like crust and a heavy hand of cheese) is its own institution; gelato shops are everywhere. The German and Eastern European influence shows in the strudel-like postres and in the kosher-and-Yiddish-deli tradition of Buenos Aires's Once district.

The empanada (Spanish-origin) was perfected in Argentina with regional variations — Salteñas (from Salta in the north-west) are the benchmark. Locro (the pre-Columbian indigenous corn-and-bean-and-meat stew) is the official national dish of festival days. Milanesa (Italian-style breaded cutlet) is the everyday lunch.

## Regional variations

- **Buenos Aires and the Pampas**: the asado heartland. Steak, empanadas, the cosmopolitan European-influenced kitchen.
- **North-West (Salta, Jujuy)**: Andean-Quechua heritage. Locro, empanadas salteñas, humita (corn tamales).
- **Patagonia (south)**: lamb instead of beef. Welsh tea tradition (Welsh immigrants settled here in 1865).
- **Cuyo (Mendoza, San Juan)**: wine country. Bife de chorizo with Malbec is the regional pairing.
- **Mesopotamia (north-east)**: river fish. Surubí, dorado, the chipa (cheese bread) tradition shared with Paraguay.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beef**: every cut, all grass-fed. Bife de chorizo, lomo, asado de tira, vacio.
- **Chimichurri**: parsley, garlic, olive oil, vinegar, oregano, chilli. The universal asado sauce.
- **Yerba mate**: the herb. Drunk from a hollowed gourd through a metal straw — a daily social ritual.
- **Empanadas**: dozens of regional fillings.
- **Dulce de leche**: caramelised milk-and-sugar, on toast, in alfajores, eaten by the spoon.
- **Provolone**: melted over the parrilla (grill) as provoleta.
- **Malbec**: the national grape, perfected in Mendoza.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Asado](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/asado)**: beef ribs (asado de tira), chorizo and morcilla cooked low and slow over a wood-fired parrilla. The weekend social institution.
2. **[Empanadas Salteñas](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/empanadas-saltenas)**: beef empanadas with cumin, olives, hard-boiled egg and a touch of caldo (broth) to make them juicy. The Salta benchmark.
3. **[Milanesa Napolitana](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/milanesa-napolitana)**: breaded beef cutlet topped with tomato sauce, ham, melted mozzarella and oregano. The Italian-Argentine fusion at its finest.
4. **[Locro](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/locro)**: thick stew of white corn, beans, pork, sausage and pumpkin. The official patriotic-day (25 May, 9 July) dish.
5. **[Provoleta](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/provoleta)**: provolone disc grilled on the parrilla till bubbling and crusty on top, sprinkled with oregano. The asado starter.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Malbec (Argentina's defining grape — Catena Zapata, Bodega Norton from Mendoza) or with Fernet con coca (Fernet-Branca and Coca-Cola — the Argentine national mixed drink). End with alfajores (dulce de leche-filled biscuits with chocolate or icing-sugar coating).
