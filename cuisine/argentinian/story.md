# Argentinian

*The kitchen of the cattle country and the European immigrant city. Asado over open coals as the universal celebration, the empanada as the universal snack, dulce de leche on everything, and an Italian-Spanish hybrid pasta-and-pizza culture that's distinctly Argentine.*

## A short history

Argentinian cooking begins on the high north-west and along the great rivers, long before any conquistador rode south. The Diaguita people of the Andean foothills (in what's now Salta, Tucumán and Catamarca) farmed maize, beans, squash and quinoa on terraced fields and built sophisticated pre-Inca agricultural systems. The Quechua-speaking inhabitants of the Inca Empire's southern reaches added the potato shelf, the dried llama jerky (charqui, the word that became "jerky" in English), and the slow-stewed corn-and-bean pot that became locro, today the country's official patriotic-day dish. Further south on the pampas, the Mapuche, Tehuelche and Guaraní peoples hunted guanaco, gathered the mate herb (Ilex paraguariensis), and developed the open-fire roasting of meat over wood that the gaucho tradition would later inherit.

The Spanish conquest from 1536 onwards rewrote the larder. Pedro de Mendoza founded Buenos Aires; the city was abandoned and re-founded in 1580. The conquistadors brought cattle, horses, sheep, wheat, wine grapes (Mendoza was already planting vineyards by the 1550s) and the Old World pantry. The cattle, turned loose on the empty pampas, ran wild and multiplied into the vast feral herds that gaucho cowboys would harvest from the late 17th century onwards. The gaucho's working diet of asado al asador (whole cuts grilled vertical over open coals), mate poured from a hollowed gourd through a silver bombilla straw, and the empanada salteña (the Andean meat-and-egg pasty from the north-west) was effectively the colonial-rural diet that became the national myth.

The 19th-century independence wars (San Martín's 1817 crossing of the Andes is the founding image) and the consolidation of the pampas as the world's great beef-export region set up the next chapter. By the 1870s, refrigerated steamships were carrying Argentine beef to London and Liverpool, and Buenos Aires was rich. The country welcomed massive late-19th and early-20th-century European immigration, three million Italians, two million Spaniards, plus large German, Welsh, Eastern European Jewish, Polish and Levantine arrivals, and Buenos Aires turned into one of the most cosmopolitan cities in the world. The new arrivals each rewrote the menu. Italians (most of all) brought pasta, pizza, gelato, the Sunday family lunch around a long table, and the espresso culture that still defines the city's cafés.

The Italian influence runs exceptionally deep. Pasta lands on most Argentine home tables weekly, especially on the 29th of each month (the ñoquis del 29 tradition, gnocchi eaten when the wallet is light, with a banknote slipped under the plate for luck). Pizza, especially the Buenos Aires version with a thick focaccia-like crust, a heavy hand of mozzarella, and the optional fugazzeta double-cheese-and-onion variant, is its own institution. The milanesa (Italian-style breaded cutlet, often topped Napolitana-style with ham, tomato and mozzarella) is the everyday lunch. German and Eastern European influence shows in the strudel-like postres and in the kosher-and-Yiddish deli tradition of Buenos Aires's Once district. The empanada (Spanish in origin, with Moorish roots) hit its peak in Argentina with regional variations; salteñas from Salta in the north-west are the benchmark, with cumin, olives, hard-boiled egg and a slug of caldo broth to make them juicy.

The 20th century made Argentine beef the world standard. Peronism in the 1940s and 50s embedded the asado in working-class identity (Sunday family asado, factory-gate parrilla after work). The post-war years built Mendoza into one of the world's great Malbec regions, the grape having been brought from France in the mid-19th century and finding its perfect home at altitude in the Andean foothills. Even through the dictatorship years (1976 to 1983) and the recurring economic crises that defined the late 20th and early 21st centuries, the parrilla and the empanada and the dulce de leche stayed sacred. Today's Argentine table reads as a layered immigration history written over a cattle ranch. Beef at the centre, Italian-Spanish-Andean influences braided everywhere, mate at every hour of the day, and a glass of Malbec or a Fernet con coca in the evening.

## Regional variations

- **Buenos Aires and the Pampas**: the asado heartland. Steak, empanadas, the cosmopolitan European-influenced kitchen.
- **North-West (Salta, Jujuy)**: Andean-Quechua heritage. Locro, empanadas salteñas, humita (corn tamales).
- **Patagonia (south)**: lamb instead of beef. Welsh tea tradition (Welsh immigrants settled here in 1865).
- **Cuyo (Mendoza, San Juan)**: wine country. Bife de chorizo with Malbec is the regional pairing.
- **Mesopotamia (north-east)**: river fish. Surubí, dorado, the chipa (cheese bread) tradition shared with Paraguay.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beef**: every cut, all grass-fed. Bife de chorizo, lomo, asado de tira, vacio.
- **Chimichurri**: parsley, garlic, olive oil, vinegar, oregano, chilli. The universal asado sauce.
- **Yerba mate**: the herb. Drunk from a hollowed gourd through a metal straw, a daily social ritual.
- **Empanadas**: dozens of regional fillings.
- **Dulce de leche**: caramelised milk-and-sugar, on toast, in alfajores, eaten by the spoon.
- **Provolone**: melted over the parrilla (grill) as provoleta.
- **Malbec**: the national grape, perfected in Mendoza.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Asado](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/asado)**: beef ribs (asado de tira), chorizo and morcilla cooked low and slow over a wood-fired parrilla. The weekend social institution.
2. **[Empanadas Salteñas](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/empanadas-saltenas)**: beef empanadas with cumin, olives, hard-boiled egg and a touch of caldo (broth) to make them juicy. The Salta benchmark.
3. **[Milanesa Napolitana](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/milanesa-napolitana)**: breaded beef cutlet topped with tomato sauce, ham, melted mozzarella and oregano. The Italian-Argentine fusion at its finest.
4. **[Locro](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/locro)**: thick stew of white corn, beans, pork, sausage and pumpkin. The official patriotic-day (25 May, 9 July) dish.
5. **[Provoleta](/recipes/cuisine/argentinian/provoleta)**: provolone disc grilled on the parrilla till bubbling and crusty on top, sprinkled with oregano. The asado starter.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Malbec (Argentina's defining grape, with Catena Zapata and Bodega Norton from Mendoza) or with Fernet con coca (Fernet-Branca and Coca-Cola, the Argentine national mixed drink). End with alfajores (dulce de leche-filled biscuits with chocolate or icing-sugar coating).
