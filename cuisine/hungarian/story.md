# Hungarian

*The kitchen of paprika. Sweet, hot, smoked: the spice arrived in the 16th century and ended up in nearly every Hungarian dish. Slow-braised goulash, sour cream finishing everything, and the Carpathian Basin's distinctive fusion of Slavic, Turkish, German and Magyar traditions.*

## A short history

Hungarian cooking grew out of the Magyar migration story. The Magyars, a Finno-Ugric tribe whose closest linguistic cousins are the Finns and the Estonians (not the surrounding Slavs or Germans), came down the steppe from somewhere east of the Ural mountains, and arrived in the Carpathian Basin in 895 CE under the chieftain Árpád. They came in seven tribes on horseback, herding cattle and sheep ahead of them, and they carried the slow-cooked meat-and-onion stew that would become goulash. The original Magyar cookpot was the bogrács, the kettle hung over an open fire on a tripod, and the original goulash was what the cattle-herders (gulyás means "herdsman" in Hungarian) cooked in the bogrács on the puszta plains: chunks of beef, onions, water, salt, slow simmer. The bogrács cook is still the proper way to make goulash in the field at a Hungarian summer gathering.

The medieval kingdom of Hungary, founded by Saint Stephen I in 1000 CE and Christianised under his reign, anchored a Central European kitchen on wheat, rye, cabbage, pork, freshwater fish from the Danube and the Tisza, and the slow-fermented wines of Tokaj (already exported by the 14th century). The Hungarian royal court drew French and Italian cooks (Beatrice of Naples married King Matthias Corvinus in 1476 and brought Italian Renaissance cooking to Buda) and the medieval Hungarian table was sophisticated, spiced, and not yet built around paprika at all. The first golden age ended in 1526 at the catastrophic Battle of Mohács, where the Ottoman army under Suleiman the Magnificent destroyed the Hungarian army in a single afternoon and opened the country to occupation.

The 150-year Ottoman occupation (1541-1699) brought paprika, the spice that defines modern Hungarian food. Capsicum peppers had reached the Ottoman Empire from the New World by the early 1500s; the Turks grew them across the Balkans and brought them to Hungary, where the southern plain around Szeged and Kalocsa turned out to grow extraordinary peppers and the technique of drying and grinding them into paprika became a Hungarian art form. Coffee arrived with the Turks (Budapest's coffee-house tradition runs straight back to Ottoman times), as did stuffed peppers and stuffed cabbage rolls (töltött káposzta), phyllo pastry (rétes, or strudel, is a Hungarian-Turkish-Austrian synthesis), and the love of yogurt-based sauces. The Habsburg liberation in 1699 brought Austrian, Bohemian and German cooks into Hungarian aristocratic kitchens, and the dumpling-and-cake tradition (galuska, palacsinta, the apple strudel) took hold.

The Austro-Hungarian Empire (1867-1918) codified the cuisine. The puszta cattle-herding tradition refined into restaurant gulyás. The bourgeois Budapest coffee-house culture took over the city centre (Gerbeaud, Centrál, the New York Café), serving the cake tradition that turned Hungarian baking into a global export (Dobos torta, the seven-layer caramel-topped cake, invented 1884 by József Dobos; the Esterházy torta; the rigó jancsi). József Marchal, who had cooked for the Rothschilds, ran the kitchen at Gundel in Pest from the 1860s onward and codified the Hungarian restaurant menu that the world still recognises. The same decades produced the major paprika-cooking writers (Lajos Boros, Károly Gundel) whose books set the modern standard for goulash, paprikash, pörkölt and tokány, the four pillars of the paprika-stew tradition.

The 20th century was brutal: the Treaty of Trianon (1920) stripped Hungary of two-thirds of its territory and over half its population, the Holocaust destroyed the Jewish community that had run much of Budapest's coffee-house and baking trade, and Soviet occupation followed the Second World War. The Communist years after 1945 kept the cuisine traditional, almost frozen in its pre-war shape, and the post-1989 recovery put Budapest back on the map as a Central European food capital. The current scene runs from the everyday family table (paprikás krumpli, lecsó, rakott káposzta) through the classic restaurant menu (gulyás, halászlé, töltött káposzta) to a growing modern Hungarian fine-dining scene with multiple Michelin stars in Budapest.

## Regional variations

- **Budapest and the Great Plain**: the mainstream. Gulyás, pörkölt, halászlé, the coffee-house tradition.
- **Transdanubia (west, near Austria)**: more Austrian influence. Schnitzel-style dishes, dumplings.
- **Hortobágy and the puszta (east-central)**: cattle country. The slow-braised beef tradition strongest here.
- **Tokaj region (north-east)**: wine country. Tokaji Aszú is the world's most famous sweet wine.

## Signature ingredients

- **Paprika**: sweet (édes), hot (csípős), smoked (füstölt). The defining spice, added at the start of every dish.
- **Lard (zsír)**: the traditional fat. The base of every fry-up.
- **Sour cream (tejföl)**: finishing every dish that isn't a soup.
- **Onion**: heavy use. Slowly sweated in lard with paprika is the start of nearly everything.
- **Beef and pork**: the main meats. Goose at celebration.
- **Caraway seeds**: in soups and breads.
- **Cottage cheese (túró)**: in dumplings and pastries.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Goulash](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/goulash)**: thick beef stew with paprika, onion, tomato, potato and caraway. Originally a herdsman's open-fire dish, now the national dish.
2. **[Goulash Soup](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/goulash-soup)**: the lighter, brothier version. Same flavour, served as a starter.
3. **[Chicken Paprikash](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/chicken-paprikash)**: chicken slow-cooked in a paprika-onion-sour cream sauce. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Hungarian Mushroom Soup](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/hungarian-mushroom-soup)**: cremini-and-shiitake soup with paprika, sour cream and dill.
5. **[Lecsó](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/lecso)**: bell pepper, onion and tomato stewed in lard with paprika and sausage. The summer-vegetable one-pan.
6. **[Tojásos Nokedli](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/tojasos-nokedli)**: small fresh egg dumplings tossed with scrambled egg. The simplest comfort food.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a glass of Egri Bikavér ("Bull's Blood" of Eger, Hungary's signature red blend) or a Tokaji Aszú with dessert. Finish with Dobos torta or rétes (apple, sour-cherry or túró-stuffed strudel) and a strong black espresso at a Budapest café.
