# Hungarian

*The kitchen of paprika. Sweet, hot, smoked — the spice that arrived in the 16th century and ended up in nearly every Hungarian dish. Slow-braised goulash, sour cream finishing everything, and the Carpathian Basin's distinctive fusion of Slavic, Turkish, German and Magyar traditions.*

## A short history

Hungarian cooking is built on the migration history of the Magyar people, a Finno-Ugric tribe that arrived in the Carpathian Basin in the 9th century and brought with them the slow-cooked meat-and-onion stew that would become goulash. The 150-year Ottoman occupation (1541–1699) brought paprika — the spice that defined modern Hungarian food — along with coffee, stuffed peppers, and the use of phyllo pastry (rétes — strudel — is a Hungarian-Turkish-Austrian synthesis).

The Austro-Hungarian Empire (1867–1918) gave Hungarian food its imperial codification: the puszta (great plain) cattle-herding tradition refined into restaurant gulyás; the bourgeois Budapest coffee-house culture (Café Gerbeaud) and the cake tradition (Dobos torta, the seven-layer caramel-topped cake invented 1884). The post-1945 Communist period kept the cuisine traditional; the post-1989 recovery has restored Budapest as a Central European food capital.

## Regional variations

- **Budapest and the Great Plain**: the codified mainstream. Gulyás, pörkölt, halászlé, the coffee-house tradition.
- **Transdanubia (west, near Austria)**: more Austrian influence. Schnitzel-style dishes, dumplings.
- **Hortobágy and the puszta (east-central)**: cattle country. The slow-braised beef tradition strongest here.
- **Tokaj region (north-east)**: wine country (Tokaji Aszú — the world's most famous sweet wine).

## Signature ingredients

- **Paprika**: sweet (édes), hot (csípős), smoked (füstölt). The defining spice — added at the start of every dish.
- **Lard (zsír)**: the traditional fat. The base of every fry-up.
- **Sour cream (tejföl)**: finishing every dish that isn't a soup.
- **Onion**: heavy use. Slowly sweated in lard with paprika is the start of nearly everything.
- **Beef and pork**: the main meats. Goose at celebration.
- **Caraway seeds**: in soups and breads.
- **Cottage cheese (túró)**: in dumplings and pastries.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Goulash](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/goulash)**: thick beef stew with paprika, onion, tomato, potato and caraway. Originally a herdsman's open-fire dish; now the national dish.
2. **[Goulash Soup](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/goulash-soup)**: the lighter, more brothy version — same flavour, served as a starter.
3. **[Chicken Paprikash](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/chicken-paprikash)**: chicken slow-cooked in a paprika-onion-sour cream sauce. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Hungarian Mushroom Soup](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/hungarian-mushroom-soup)**: cremini-and-shiitake soup with paprika, sour cream and dill.
5. **[Lecsó](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/lecso)**: bell pepper, onion and tomato stewed in lard with paprika and sausage. The summer-vegetable one-pan.
6. **[Tojásos Nokedli](/recipes/cuisine/hungarian/tojasos-nokedli)**: small fresh egg dumplings tossed with scrambled egg. The simplest comfort food.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a glass of Egri Bikavér ("Bull's Blood" of Eger — Hungary's signature red blend) or a Tokaji Aszú with dessert. End with Dobos torta or rétes (apple, sour-cherry or túró-stuffed strudel) and strong black espresso at a Budapest café.
