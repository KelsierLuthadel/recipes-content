# Iranian

*The Persian-state-name cuisine, same kitchen as the broader Persian tradition (see cuisine/persian for the full story). Saffron rice, slow khoresh stews, the dried-lime profile, and the world's most refined rice cookery.*

## A short history

The cuisine of Iran is the cuisine of Persia: a 2,500-year continuously documented tradition built on rice, saffron, lamb, dried fruit and a sour-sweet balance. (See cuisine/persian for the deeper history; this folder gathers a subset of the same tradition under the modern country name.)

The roots run to the Achaemenid Empire (550-330 BCE), when Persian court cooks built the world's first systematic banquet kitchen. Greek historians described Darius's table running to a hundred dishes a sitting, and the techniques they wrote about (slow-cooked meat with fruit, herb-thick stews, the use of saffron and rosewater) are still visible in modern Iranian cooking. The Achaemenids ran trade routes from Egypt to India, and the cuisine picked up rice from the east, sour citrus from China, and the sweet-savoury balance that has anchored Persian food ever since.

The Islamic conquest of the 7th century reshaped the rules but kept the techniques. Sasanid Persian cooks were absorbed into the new caliphate kitchens at Baghdad, and their methods (rice cooked with meat, slow stews thickened with nuts, dried-fruit garnishes) spread across the Islamic world from Andalusia to Mughal India. The 10th-century Baghdad cookbook *Kitab al-Tabikh* records dozens of Persian dishes that are still on Iranian tables: khoresh stews, sweet-savoury duck, rosewater sweets. The Safavid dynasty (1501-1736) standardised Persian Shia identity and codified the modern rice-and-khoresh tradition at the Isfahan court; that period is when the polo (layered rice) and chelo (plain steamed rice) techniques crystallised.

The basic shape that emerged: long-grain basmati cooked twice (steamed then crisped into tahdig, the golden crust at the bottom of the pot); slow-cooked khoresh stews (the "spoon-food" family) flavoured with dried lime, pomegranate molasses or walnut-and-pomegranate; charcoal-grilled kebabs (koobideh, minced lamb on flat skewers); and the fresh-herb plate (sabzi khordan) on every table. The Qajar period (1789-1925) brought European silverware and the formal restaurant, but the home kitchen stayed on its centuries-old footing.

The 20th century pulled Iran through rapid modernisation under the Pahlavi shahs (1925-1979), with Western kitchens, cookbooks and supermarkets arriving alongside the petrodollar economy. After the 1979 Revolution, cultural isolation kept Iranian food largely in the diaspora kitchen for a generation. The diaspora led the recodification (Los Angeles "Tehrangeles", London's Iranian restaurants) and pulled the cuisine back into global view. Inside the country the food has barely changed; it's been refining itself the same way for centuries.

## Regional variations

(See cuisine/persian for the fuller breakdown. Briefly:)
- **Tehran and central**: the mainstream.
- **North (Caspian)**: fish-heavy, sour-leaning.
- **South (Bushehr, Hormozgan)**: Gulf-Indian fusion, more chilli.
- **Tabriz and Azerbaijan-Iran**: distinct dolma tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Saffron**: bloomed in hot water, poured over rice.
- **Dried lime (limu omani)**: in stews.
- **Barberries (zereshk)**: small sour red berries, on saffron rice.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry, the table seasoning.
- **Rose water and orange-blossom water**: in sweets.
- **Advieh**: warm spice blend (cinnamon, cardamom, cumin, rose petals).
- **Pomegranate molasses**: in fesenjan.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Tahdig](/recipes/cuisine/iran/tahdig)**: the golden crispy rice crust from the bottom of the pot, the most-prized part of every Persian rice dish. The technique that makes Iranian rice singular.
2. **[Fesenjan](/recipes/cuisine/iran/fesenjan)**: chicken or duck slow-cooked in a thick walnut-and-pomegranate-molasses sauce. The wedding-feast plate of Gilan.
3. **[Ghormeh Sabzi](/recipes/cuisine/iran/ghormeh-sabzi)**: green-herb stew with lamb, kidney beans and dried lime. The most-loved Iranian dish; sometimes called the national dish.
4. **[Joojeh Kabab](/recipes/cuisine/iran/joojeh-kabab)**: saffron-and-lemon-marinated chicken pieces grilled on skewers.
5. **[Gheymeh](/recipes/cuisine/iran/gheymeh)**: yellow split-pea stew with lamb, dried lime and tomato, topped with crispy potato matchsticks.
