# Backhendl

![Backhendl](resources/backhendl.jpg)

*Vienna's bone-in fried chicken: a whole spatchcocked bird jointed into eight pieces, coated like a schnitzel and shallow-fried in clarified butter till the breadcrumbs puff into deep-gold folds. Eaten warm with lemon and potato salad at every Heuriger garden in the city.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Backhendl is Vienna's bone-in answer to the wiener schnitzel: a whole spatchcocked chicken jointed into pieces, breaded in seasoned flour, egg and soft fresh breadcrumbs, shallow-fried in clarified butter till the crust puffs into the same loose ripples that mark a properly fried schnitzel. It turns up at every Heuriger through summer and early autumn with a bowl of vinegared potato salad and a wedge of lemon. Eating it warm in a vine-shaded courtyard with a glass of cool grüner veltliner is one of Vienna's great food memories.

## Ingredients

### Chicken
- 1 whole free-range chicken (about 1.5 kg, spatchcocked and jointed into 8 pieces)
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
- ½ teaspoon white pepper

### Breading
- 100 g plain flour
- 3 eggs (beaten with 1 tablespoon milk)
- 200 g fresh white breadcrumbs (Semmelbrösel, made by blitzing stale white bread)

### For frying
- 250 g clarified butter (or 200 g lard plus 50 g butter for the rustic version)

### To serve
- 2 lemons (cut into wedges)
- Flaky sea salt
- [Erdäpfelsalat](side-dishes/erdaepfelsalat.md) (Vienna-style potato salad)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the bird
1. Pat every joint piece dry with kitchen paper; moisture on the skin makes the breading slip away later.
2. Season all sides with the sea salt and white pepper. Rest at room temperature for 15 minutes so the seasoning penetrates and the meat takes the chill off (chicken straight from the fridge will undercook in the middle while the breadcrumbs scorch).

### Stage 2 - Breading station
1. Tip the flour into one shallow tray, the beaten egg and milk into a second, and the fresh breadcrumbs into a third.
2. Work in order: flour, egg, crumb, clean tray.

### Stage 3 - Bread the pieces
1. Take a piece of chicken, roll it in the flour, shake off the excess.
2. Dip into the egg, letting the excess drip back into the tray.
3. Drop into the breadcrumbs and press the crumbs on with your fingers, getting them into every crevice around the joints. Don't compact them; the looser the coat, the more it puffs in the fat.
4. Lift onto a clean tray. Repeat with each piece.

### Stage 4 - Shallow-fry
1. Melt the clarified butter in a wide deep heavy skillet (cast-iron is ideal) to about 1 cm depth. Heat over medium till a breadcrumb dropped in sizzles gently rather than violently.
2. Lay the chicken in skin-side down, working in two batches if needed; crowd the pan and the temperature drops and the crumb goes greasy.
3. Cook 5-6 minutes till deep gold and puffed underneath, then turn with tongs and cook the other side another 5-6 minutes. Move pieces around occasionally so every side catches the heat.
4. Drumsticks and thighs need 18-20 minutes total; breasts and wings 12-15. Test the thickest part of a thigh with a small knife; the juices should run clear and the meat should read 75 C at the bone.

### Stage 5 - Drain and serve
1. Lift onto a rack lined with kitchen paper to drain.
2. Scatter a pinch of flaky sea salt over the hot crust, pile onto a platter and serve straight away with the lemon wedges and potato salad.

## Notes
- **Spatchcock first:** removing the backbone and flattening the bird before jointing gives evenly sized pieces. Ask your butcher to do this for you if you don't want to wrestle with it.
- **Fresh breadcrumbs only:** Austrian Semmelbrösel are soft fine breadcrumbs from stale white bread. Panko gives the wrong crisp-shard texture; you want the soft puffy fold that only soft crumbs produce.
- **Moderate heat:** crank it too high and the crumbs scorch before the meat cooks through; too low and they go greasy. Aim for steady cheerful bubbling around the pieces.
- **Clarified butter not whole:** whole butter burns at this temperature and freckles the crumbs. Clarified butter handles the heat; the half-lard-half-butter ratio is the older Vienna rustic version.

## Variations
**Pollo alla Milanese con l'osso:** the Italian cousin uses pounded bone-in cutlets and butter rather than lard, a Habsburg-era cross-pollination.
**Cold Backhendlsalat:** lay cooled jointed pieces on dressed bitter leaves with vinegared potato salad on the side; the picnic version traditional at Heurigers when the weather is hot.
**Wiener Backhendlsalat:** strips of cold chicken folded through dressed mixed leaves with walnuts and pumpkin seeds, a 1970s revival in Vienna's wine bars.

## Serving
Eat hot from the pan with lemon wedges, [Erdäpfelsalat](side-dishes/erdaepfelsalat.md), or buttered parsley potatoes. A simple green salad with pumpkin seed oil dressing rounds it out. Drink: grüner veltliner or a cold lager. A Heuriger garden in late afternoon if you can find one.

## Storage
- Best eaten the moment it comes out of the pan; cold backhendl turns into a different dish (see the Salat variation).
- Keeps refrigerated 2 days; reheat uncovered in a 180 C oven for 8 to 10 minutes to crisp the crumbs back up. Never microwave; the crumbs go soggy.
- Doesn't freeze well; the crust collapses on thawing.
