# Austrian

*The kitchen of empire's leftovers. Wiener schnitzel, sachertorte, the Sunday Tafelspitz; a coffee-house culture that codified the Vienna-style café and the apple strudel; a Habsburg patchwork of Hungarian, Czech, Italian and Balkan influences.*

## A short history

Austrian cooking begins in the alpine valleys long before there was an Austria. Celtic and Roman settlers worked the Danube basin and the eastern Alps from the Iron Age onwards, raising cattle and pigs on the high pasture, ripening hard cheeses in cellars cut into the hillside, and grinding the spelt and rye that still go into the country bread. The Roman province of Noricum exported salted pork and alpine cheese to the empire, and the salt mines around Hallstatt (the source of the word "salary") cured the meats that became speck, kaiserfleisch and the long Austrian sausage tradition. Dumplings made from stale bread, the Knödel that anchors every alpine plate today, started life as a way to use the rye loaf to its last crumb.

The medieval centuries layered religion and trade on top. Benedictine and Cistercian monasteries in Melk, Kremsmünster and Heiligenkreuz farmed orchards and vineyards, brewed beer, baked the slow rye breads, and codified the Lenten cookbook around freshwater fish from the alpine lakes. The Babenberg and later Habsburg dukes pulled Vienna into the central European trade routes; sugar, almonds, citrus and the first oriental spices arrived through Venice from the 13th century onwards, and the pastry tradition that would become the world's most famous started in those monastery and court kitchens.

The Habsburg ascendancy from 1500 turned Vienna into the kitchen of an empire that reached from the Adriatic to Galicia. The Imperial court borrowed from everywhere it ruled: paprika and goulash from Hungary, knedlíky dumplings and beer technique from Bohemia, strudel from the Ottoman Turks (the 1683 siege of Vienna left the paper-thin pastry behind, along with the coffee that the retreating army abandoned in sacks at the gates), polenta and tomato sauces from northern Italy, plum dumplings and poppy-seed pastries from Galicia. The Columbian Exchange added the potato (slow to catch on, dominant by the 1800s), maize for polenta in the south, and the chocolate that the Habsburgs took to seriously enough to invent the modern hot-chocolate cup. The result is layered, distinctly Austrian by synthesis, even when individual dishes started somewhere else.

The 19th century codified the cuisine into something a Vienna housewife with a Hausmannskost cookbook could put on the table every night. The Wiener schnitzel (veal cutlet pounded thin, breaded, shallow-fried in butter until the crust bubbles off the meat) became the country's most-loved plate, refined at Vienna's bourgeois Bürgerstube restaurants in the mid-1800s. The coffee houses (Café Central, Café Sacher, Café Hawelka, Café Demel) gave the world a model of leisurely café culture and the Sachertorte: chocolate cake with apricot jam, invented in 1832 by 16-year-old Franz Sacher and still under franchise protection between the Hotel Sacher and Demel.

The 1918 collapse of the Habsburg Empire amputated the country but didn't change the kitchen much; Vienna had already absorbed everything it was going to absorb, and the recipes carried on under a smaller flag. Two world wars, the Anschluss, and post-war reconstruction kept the kitchen frugal. The modern Vienna scene swings between the classical Beisl tradition (schnitzel, Tafelspitz, Beuschel, served on white tablecloths with grüner veltliner by the carafe) and a smaller modernist wing under chefs like Heinz Reitbauer at Steirereck. The Sunday lunch reads as it did in 1900, and that continuity is the cuisine's real signature.

## Regional variations

- **Vienna**: the codified mainstream. Schnitzel, Tafelspitz, Sachertorte, the coffee-house tradition.
- **Salzburg and the alps**: Knödel (dumplings), Kasspatzln (cheese spaetzle), heavy alpine cooking.
- **Styria**: pumpkin-seed oil (Steirisches Kürbiskernöl, PDO), apple-cider tradition.
- **Tyrol**: Tiroler Gröstl (potato-and-bacon hash), Knödel-led winter food.
- **Burgenland (east)**: Hungarian-leaning. Paprika, goulash, more chilli.

## Signature ingredients

- **Butter**: the cooking fat for everything. Brown butter on Käsespätzle is the alpine standard.
- **Pork and veal**: the meats. Veal for schnitzel, pork for roasts and sausages.
- **Sauerkraut**: with Schweinebraten, with Bratwurst.
- **Dumplings (Knödel)**: bread, potato, semmel, liver, fruit. A whole family of pillows.
- **Paprika**: in Hungarian-influenced eastern dishes.
- **Apricot (Marille)**: in Sachertorte, in Marillenknödel (sweet apricot dumplings), in schnapps.
- **Pumpkin-seed oil**: in Styrian salad dressings; intensely dark green.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Wiener Schnitzel](/recipes/cuisine/austrian/wiener-schnitzel)** *(see German cuisine, shared dish)*. Veal pounded thin, breaded, shallow-fried in butter until the crust bubbles off the meat.
2. **[Tafelspitz](/recipes/cuisine/austrian/tafelspitz)**: beef tri-tip slow-poached in stock with root vegetables, served with apple-horseradish sauce and chive sauce. The Habsburg lunch.
3. **[Backhendl](/recipes/cuisine/austrian/backhendl)**: deep-fried chicken pieces breaded like schnitzel. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Tiroler Gröstl](/recipes/cuisine/austrian/tiroler-groestl)**: pan-fried potato and onion with bacon and beef, topped with a fried egg. The Tyrolean hearty plate.
5. **[Austrian Goulash](/recipes/cuisine/austrian/austrian-goulash)**: beef-and-paprika stew, the Hungarian inheritance refined into a Viennese signature.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Vienna lager or with grüner veltliner (Austria's dry white). Finish with Sachertorte and a Wiener Melange (espresso with milk foam) at a coffee house. Read a newspaper. Stay for two hours. That's the Austrian Sunday afternoon.
