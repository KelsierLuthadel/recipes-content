# Honduran

*The Central American kitchen that runs from Caribbean coast (Garifuna seafood, coconut, sopa de caracol) to Pacific Highland (beans, plantain, the plato típico). Less internationally known than its neighbours but quietly excellent.*

## A short history

Honduran cooking starts with the Lenca, the Maya and the Pech, the indigenous peoples who farmed the Honduran highlands and the Caribbean lowlands long before any European reached the coast. The Maya built the city of Copán in the western highlands around 400 CE; it grew into one of the great Classic Mayan capitals and held its power until the population collapse of the 9th century. The Mayan kitchen of Copán fed itself on the Three Sisters of Mesoamerica: corn nixtamalised into tortillas, beans simmered in clay pots, and squash. The Lenca, who held central and western Honduras for over two thousand years, contributed cacao (Honduras is one of the oldest cacao-cultivation regions in the Americas, with chemical evidence of chocolate drinks going back to 1100 BCE in the Ulúa valley), avocado, the loroco flower, and the tamale tradition. The Pech and Tawahka in the eastern jungle kept a different shelf: cassava, plantain, river fish, hunted game.

The Spanish arrived in 1502 (Columbus on his fourth voyage made landfall at Trujillo on the north coast and named the country Honduras, "depths", for the deep waters offshore). Spanish colonisation proper followed in the 1520s under Hernán Cortés and his lieutenants, and the country became a backwater silver- and cattle-mining colony for three centuries. The Spanish brought beef, pork, chicken, dairy, rice, citrus, garlic and onion, and the slow Iberian stew. The crucial Spanish contributions to the kitchen are the cattle ranching tradition that made beef central to the modern plate, and the cooking fats (lard, refined olive oil) that changed how Honduran food gets prepared. Wheat never really took hold; corn tortillas stayed the bread of the country.

The third great tradition arrived on the Caribbean coast in 1797. The Garifuna, the Afro-Indigenous people descended from shipwrecked enslaved Africans and the Carib and Arawak peoples of St Vincent, were deported by the British after a long colonial war and dumped on the Honduran island of Roatán with virtually no supplies. They survived, spread to the mainland north coast, and built a distinct regional cuisine on coconut, cassava, conch, the slow-fish stew, the hudut (pounded plantain dumpling) and a deep grilling-and-smoking tradition. Garifuna cooking is genuinely African in its rhythms (the use of coconut milk, the long simmer, the cassava bread baked on a clay disc) and genuinely Caribbean in its ingredients. Sopa de caracol, the conch soup with coconut milk that the Garifuna invented, became a regional pop-song hit in the 1990s.

The 19th century brought independence (1821 from Spain, 1838 from the Federal Republic of Central America), then a long string of political instabilities and the rise of the banana economy. United Fruit Company and Standard Fruit built enormous plantations on the north coast from the 1890s onward, the term "banana republic" was coined to describe the resulting political arrangements (specifically about Honduras, in O. Henry's 1904 book *Cabbages and Kings*), and the banana itself became as central to the working-day pantry as the plantain. Lebanese and Palestinian immigration in the same decades layered baleadas (the flour tortilla folded around beans and crema, possibly inspired by Arab flatbreads) and other Levantine influences onto the urban kitchen.

The 20th century carried the country through hurricanes, civil wars in neighbouring states, and a large diaspora migration to the US (Honduras has the largest US-bound migration share of any Central American country). The food isn't as internationally famous as Guatemalan or Salvadoran, partly because tourism is smaller. But the plato típico (the everyday plate of beans, rice, plantain, meat, cream and tortilla) is the universal lunch, the baleada is the national handheld, and the Garifuna kitchen on the north coast remains one of the most distinctive Afro-indigenous food traditions in the Americas. Honduran cooking pulls three traditions together: the indigenous Lenca, Maya and Pech (corn, beans, cassava, the tortilla-and-bean spine), the Spanish (beef, dairy, rice, the slow stew), and the Garifuna (coconut, conch, coastal seafood). Each kitchen still cooks in its own region, and the modern Honduran plate carries pieces of all three.

## Regional variations

- **Tegucigalpa and the centre**: the mainstream. Plato típico, baleadas.
- **San Pedro Sula and the north (Caribbean coast)**: Garifuna country. Sopa de caracol, machuca (mashed plantain with coconut fish), coconut-based seafood.
- **Western (Copán)**: stronger Mayan influence. More corn-based dishes.
- **Bay Islands (Roatán)**: English-Creole-speaking Caribbean. British colonial-era fish-and-chips meets Garifuna seafood.

## Signature ingredients

- **Tortillas**: hand-pressed corn tortillas at every meal.
- **Black beans (and red)**: in plato típico, in baleadas.
- **Plantain**: fried as tajadas (chips), boiled and mashed, ripened and caramelised (maduros).
- **Crema**: cultured cream, like sour cream but lighter. Drizzled on beans and plantain.
- **Coconut milk**: on the Caribbean coast.
- **Conch**: in sopa de caracol.
- **Loroco**: an edible flower used in cooking and tortilla fillings.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Plato Típico](/recipes/cuisine/honduras/plato-tipico)**: the typical plate. Grilled meat, beans, rice, fried plantain, avocado, cheese, crema, tortillas. The everyday Honduran lunch.
2. **[Sopa de Caracol](/recipes/cuisine/honduras/sopa-de-caracol)**: conch soup with coconut milk, yuca, plantain and cilantro. The Garifuna signature.
3. **[Pollo con Tajadas](/recipes/cuisine/honduras/pollo-con-tajadas)**: fried chicken with fried plantain slices, cabbage slaw and pickled red onion. The street-food favourite.
4. **[Carne Asada Hondureña](/recipes/cuisine/honduras/carne-asada-hondurena)**: grilled marinated beef with chimichurri-style chimol and the plato típico sides.
5. **[Pescado Frito](/recipes/cuisine/honduras/pescado-frito)**: whole-fried red snapper with green plantain chips, eaten on the Bay Islands beaches.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Salva Vida or Imperial beer, or with horchata (rice-and-cinnamon drink). The Garifuna kitchen on the north coast is the most distinctive regional shelf and worth a dedicated visit.
