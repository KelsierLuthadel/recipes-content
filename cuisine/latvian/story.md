# Latvian

*The Baltic kitchen of dark rye bread, smoked fish, the pearl-barley-and-mushroom one-pot, and the fermented sour-cabbage soup that runs through every Latvian winter.*

## A short history

Latvian cooking is the kitchen of a forest-and-coast country whose oldest cooks were the Baltic-speaking tribes (Latgalians, Selonians, Semigallians, Couronians) who settled the eastern shore of the Baltic Sea in the second millennium BCE. They were farmers and beekeepers as much as fishers: rye, barley, peas, turnip, hemp, honey and the wild forest harvest of berries, mushrooms and game ran the household. Iron Age cooks fermented rye dough in clay vessels (the same sour leaven that gives modern rupjmaize its character), pickled cabbage and root vegetables in wooden tubs to last the winter, and smoked Baltic herring and eel over alder wood. None of that has fundamentally changed.

The medieval transformation came from the north. The Livonian Brothers of the Sword and then the Teutonic Order conquered the region in the 13th-century Northern Crusades, and the German-speaking Hanseatic merchants who followed turned Riga (founded 1201) into one of the great trading cities of the eastern Baltic. The Baltic Germans ran the manor estates and the city guilds for the next seven centuries, and the kitchen took on a Germanic accent: heavy pork curing, sauerkraut, the bread-and-beer culture, the smoked-sausage tradition. The Latvian countryside stayed on the rye-and-pork-and-cabbage shelf the Germans demanded as rent in kind, and that involuntary diet became the codified national cooking.

The early modern period brought brutal back-and-forth between Sweden, Poland-Lithuania and Russia. The Columbian Exchange seeped in slowly through Hanseatic ports: potato arrived in the 18th century and within fifty years had become the second staple after rye, displacing turnip and slotting into every soup and stew. Catherine the Great's annexation of the region into the Russian Empire in 1795 brought the samovar, the pelmeni-style dumpling and the tea-drinking habit that the eastern Latgale region still carries strongest.

Latvian independence (1918 to 1940) saw the first proper codification of national cooking under cookbook authors like Minna Krauze, whose collections preserved the rural recipes that the Baltic German manor kitchens had pushed to the margins. Then the Soviet annexation in 1940, the Nazi occupation, and a second Soviet period from 1944 to 1991 flattened the cuisine into bulk-feeding staples and rationed ingredients. The Rasols mayonnaise potato salad that now appears at every Latvian celebration is a direct Soviet-era invention, the Russian Olivier salad adapted to local pantries.

The post-1991 recovery has restored regional dishes alongside the modern Riga restaurant scene, which is now one of the most ambitious in the Baltics (the Vincents-and-Bibliotēka generation of chefs leaning on foraging, fermentation and the older rye-and-game tradition). The diaspora kitchens in the US (Boston, Cleveland) and the UK keep the codified tradition alive. The kitchen reads on a modern table much as it did in a 19th-century farmhouse: rye bread at every meal, smoked sprats from a tin, sour cabbage soup in winter, grey peas with bacon at midwinter, and a glass of Black Balsam to chase it.

## Regional variations

- **Riga and central**: codified mainstream. Karbonāde, pelēkie zirņi.
- **Kurzeme (west, coastal)**: more fish. Smoked sprats are the regional speciality.
- **Vidzeme (central north)**: forest cooking. Mushrooms, berries, game.
- **Latgale (east)**: closer to Russian and Belarusian. More dumpling traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rye bread (rupjmaize)**: dense, dark, sour. The cultural icon.
- **Grey peas (pelēkie zirņi)**: a large dried pea, soaked and cooked with bacon. The Christmas Eve dish.
- **Smoked sprats**: tiny Baltic fish, smoked and tinned, exported worldwide.
- **Pork**: cured, smoked, fresh.
- **Sour cabbage**: in soup, with pork.
- **Sour cream**: dressing soups and stews.
- **Caraway**: in bread, in cheese (Jāņu siers, the summer-solstice cheese).

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Pelēkie Zirņi ar Speķi](/recipes/cuisine/latvian/pelekie-zirni-ar-speki)**: grey peas with bacon and onion. The Christmas Eve and Lutheran tradition.
2. **[Karbonāde](/recipes/cuisine/latvian/karbonade)**: pork chop pounded thin, breaded, pan-fried. The Sunday-lunch standard.
3. **[Skābu Kāpostu Zupa](/recipes/cuisine/latvian/skabu-kapostu-zupa)**: sour cabbage soup with pork and barley. The winter weeknight bowl.
4. **[Rasols](/recipes/cuisine/latvian/rasols)**: potato salad with diced ham, peas, gherkin and mayonnaise. The Soviet-era inheritance, now everywhere at every celebration.
5. **[Sklandrausis](/recipes/cuisine/latvian/sklandrausis)**: round rye-pastry tart filled with mashed carrot and potato. The Kurzeme regional speciality, the only Latvian dish with EU protected designation.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Riga Black Balsam (the country's signature bitter liqueur: herbs, roots, oak, 45% ABV) or with kvass (fermented rye drink). The summer solstice festival of Jāņi (June 23 to 24) features caraway cheese, beer, and grilled meats around bonfires.
