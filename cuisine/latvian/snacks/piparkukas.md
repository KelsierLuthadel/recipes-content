# Piparkūkas

![Piparkūkas](resources/piparkukas.jpg)

*Latvian thin gingerbread biscuits: rolled wafer-thin, cut into hearts, stars, hens and angels, baked dark and crisp. The Christmas-market staple that sits in tins on every Latvian sideboard from late November through New Year.*

**Serves:** Makes about 80 to 100 biscuits

**Prep Time:** 1 hour (plus 2 days resting the dough)

**Cook Time:** 8 to 10 minutes per tray

## Overview
Piparkūkas (literally "pepper cakes") are the dark thin gingerbread biscuits that define the Latvian Christmas. The dough is built around dark syrup or molasses, brown sugar, plenty of butter and the proper spice mix (cinnamon, ginger, clove, cardamom, allspice, black pepper, sometimes a touch of orange peel), bound with egg, lifted with bicarbonate of soda. It is mixed warm so the syrup loosens, then chilled for at least two days (some Latvian families chill a week or more) so the spices marry and the dough firms up enough to roll wafer-thin. Rolled out to 2 to 3 mm, cut with traditional shapes (hearts and stars are common, hens and angels and Christmas trees too), baked in a hot oven 8 minutes until dark gold. The biscuits are crisp through, faintly chewy at the centre on the day, fully crisp the next. Some are iced with white glaze; the plain version is the more traditional.

## Ingredients

### Wet base
- 150 g dark molasses or black treacle
- 150 g golden syrup (or honey)
- 200 g soft dark brown sugar
- 200 g unsalted butter
- 1 tablespoon water

### Spices
- 1 tablespoon ground cinnamon
- 2 teaspoons ground ginger
- 1 teaspoon ground cloves
- 1 teaspoon ground cardamom
- 1 teaspoon ground allspice
- ½ teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
- Zest of 1 orange
- ¼ teaspoon ground nutmeg

### Dry
- 600 g plain flour
- 2 teaspoons bicarbonate of soda
- ½ teaspoon fine salt

### To bind
- 2 large eggs, beaten

### Icing (optional)
- 100 g icing sugar
- 1 to 2 teaspoons lemon juice
- 1 to 2 teaspoons water

## Method

### Stage 1 - Melt the wet base
1. In a heavy saucepan, combine molasses, golden syrup, brown sugar, butter and water.
2. Heat gently, stirring, until the sugar dissolves and the mixture is smooth (do not boil). Pull off the heat.
3. Stir in all the ground spices and the orange zest.
4. Cool to lukewarm (about 35°C).

### Stage 2 - Mix the dough
1. Sift the flour, bicarbonate of soda and salt into a large bowl.
2. Pour the cooled spiced syrup into the flour. Add the beaten eggs.
3. Mix with a spoon, then knead briefly on a floured surface until smooth and elastic. The dough will be soft but workable.
4. Shape into a flat slab; wrap in clingfilm.

### Stage 3 - Rest
1. Refrigerate at least 48 hours. Up to a week is better still. The dough darkens, firms up, and the spice flavour intensifies.

### Stage 4 - Roll and cut
1. Heat the oven to 200°C (180°C fan). Line baking sheets with parchment.
2. Take a quarter of the dough out (leave the rest in the fridge).
3. Roll on a lightly floured surface to 2 to 3 mm thick. Wafer-thin is right; thick biscuits go cakey rather than crisp.
4. Cut with traditional cutters: hearts, stars, hens, angels, Christmas trees, moons. Lift to the sheets with a thin palette knife, set 1.5 cm apart (they spread very little).

### Stage 5 - Bake
1. Bake 8 to 10 minutes (longer for thicker biscuits) until the biscuits look set, slightly puffed and the edges are darker.
2. Lift onto a wire rack to cool; they crisp as they cool.
3. Repeat with the rest of the dough in batches.

### Stage 6 - Ice (optional)
1. Whisk icing sugar with lemon juice and just enough water to make a thick pipeable icing.
2. Pipe simple white outlines on the cooled biscuits with a piping bag (or a freezer bag with the tip snipped).
3. Let the icing set 30 minutes before stacking.

## Notes
- **Rest the dough.** Forty-eight hours minimum, longer is better. The spices need time. Same-day piparkūkas taste flat and sharp.
- **Roll wafer-thin.** Two to three millimetres. Thicker biscuits taste cakey rather than the crisp snap that defines the bake.
- **Watch the oven.** Eight to ten minutes is enough; another minute and they burn. The biscuits crisp as they cool.

## Variations
- **With ground almonds in the dough:** Replace 100 g of the flour with ground almonds for a richer, slightly chewier biscuit.
- **Filled (piparkūku sirsniņas):** Sandwich two heart-shaped biscuits with thick lingonberry or cranberry jam; served at name-days and weddings.
- **With cocoa:** Replace 30 g of the flour with cocoa powder for a darker version; not traditional but popular in modern bakeries.

## Serving
- Serve in a tin or wooden bowl at Christmas. Eat with strong coffee, mulled wine, Riga black balsam, or cold milk for children. Latvian markets at Christmas pile them in towers on every stand.

## Storage
- Keeps 4 weeks in an airtight tin; the spice deepens.
- Freezes 3 months baked.
- The unbaked dough keeps 2 weeks refrigerated and 3 months frozen (defrost in the fridge before rolling).
