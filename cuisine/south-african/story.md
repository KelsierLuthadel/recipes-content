# South African

*The kitchen of three nations on one plate. Cape Malay aromatic curries from the Indonesian-Bengali diaspora, the braai (open-fire grilling) that's basically a national pastime, and chakalaka, pap and boerewors from the country's African and Afrikaner traditions.*

## A short history

South African cooking starts with the oldest cooking fires on the continent. The San and Khoikhoi peoples, whose ancestors have lived in southern Africa for tens of thousands of years, perfected the slow-roasted game (eland, springbok, ostrich) over wood coals and the long-stewed pot of wild roots, tubers and leaves. The Bantu migrations from around 300 CE brought Xhosa, Zulu and Sotho farming peoples south, with cattle, sorghum, millet and the umngqusho (samp-and-beans) tradition that's now central across the eastern half of the country. The maize that defines modern South African home cooking arrived later (a Columbian Exchange crop carried up the coast by Portuguese traders), but it slotted into the existing porridge-and-stew shelf so completely that the mielie pap is now the universal starch.

The Dutch East India Company set up the Cape Town victualling station in 1652 to feed ships rounding the Cape on the Indies run, and the Afrikaner cooking tradition starts here. Dutch settlers brought wheat, dairy, beef and the slow-stewed pot; the long sea voyage meant fresh produce was scarce, so cured and preserved meat became central. Biltong (air-dried, spice-cured beef or game, descended from the Dutch droë wors tradition) and boerewors (the spiced farmer's sausage with coriander, cloves, allspice and vinegar) both date from the 17th-century Cape farm kitchen. French Huguenot refugees arrived in 1688, escaping Catholic persecution, and laid down the country's wine industry around Franschhoek and Stellenbosch.

The Cape Malay community is the cuisine's most influential single layer. The Dutch shipped enslaved people from Java, Bengal, Sumatra and the Malay archipelago to the Cape from the 1650s onward, including political exiles, Muslim religious teachers and skilled artisans. The cooks among them created the aromatic curry tradition (bobotie, the spiced minced-lamb bake with its custard cap; denningvleis, the sweet-sour tamarind stew; the rainbow shelf of sambals; the koeksisters; the chilli-and-vinegar atjar pickles) that's South Africa's most distinctive cuisine. The Bo-Kaap neighbourhood in Cape Town, with its candy-coloured houses, is still the heart of Cape Malay cooking.

British colonisation (1806-1910) added tea, scones, the meat pie, a milk-tart tradition and the standard British colonial breakfast. The discovery of diamonds at Kimberley in 1867 and gold on the Witwatersrand in 1886 pulled labour from every corner of the subcontinent and the Indian Ocean basin. Indian indentured-labour migration to Natal (from 1860 onward) brought curry, roti, the south Indian breakfast canon and ultimately bunny chow, a Durban Indian invention from the 1940s where a half-loaf of white bread is hollowed out and filled with curry: street food perfected in a city that needed portable lunches for migrant workers. Hot Durban-style curries became the third major curry tradition on the South African shelf, distinct from both Cape Malay and Indian.

The 20th century carried South African food through apartheid, with a deeply segregated kitchen running parallel cuisines in parallel restaurants. The transition to democracy in 1994 reopened the conversation. Township shisa nyama (grilled meat) joints, Cape Malay home cooks, Durban Indian curry houses and Stellenbosch fine-dining wineries now all share a recognisably national cuisine. The chef-led revival under names like Margot Janse and Reuben Riffel pulled indigenous ingredients (marula, dassie tea, kapokbos) into the restaurant scene; the home kitchen meanwhile carried on cooking the same braai it had always cooked. National Braai Day (24 September) is an actual public holiday: family and friends gather around an open-fire grill, the braai master tends it, everyone else cooks side dishes, and the country eats together.

## Regional variations

- **Western Cape (Cape Town)**: Cape Malay curries, the seafood traditions, the wine country (Stellenbosch, Franschhoek).
- **Gauteng (Johannesburg)**: cosmopolitan, the modern restaurant scene.
- **KwaZulu-Natal (Durban)**: Indian-South African. Bunny chow, the strongest curry tradition.
- **Eastern Cape and Free State**: Afrikaner and Xhosa heartlands. Pap-and-vleis (porridge-and-meat) tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Mielie meal**: maize meal for pap (porridge). The universal starch.
- **Boerewors**: spiced farmer's sausage with coriander, cloves, allspice and vinegar.
- **Biltong**: air-dried, spiced strips of beef or game.
- **Rooibos tea**: red bush tea, caffeine-free, endemic to the Cederberg region.
- **Apricot jam**: in bobotie, in milk tart.
- **Curry powders**: Durban-style (hotter) and Cape Malay (more aromatic).
- **Chakalaka**: spicy tomato-and-bean-and-pepper relish.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Bobotie](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/bobotie)**: spiced minced lamb baked under a savoury egg-and-milk custard, with raisins, almonds and bay leaves. The Cape Malay national dish.
2. **[Bunny Chow](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/bunny-chow)**: half-loaf of white bread hollowed out and filled with curry (lamb, mutton or chicken). The Durban Indian invention.
3. **[Denningvleis](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/denningvleis)**: Cape Malay sweet-sour lamb stew with tamarind, allspice and bay.
4. **[Boerewors](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/boerewors)**: thick coiled farmer's sausage grilled over coals at every braai.
5. **[Sosaties](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/sosaties)**: marinated lamb skewers with dried apricot, curry powder and onion. The Cape Malay-Afrikaner braai standard.
6. **[Chakalaka](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/chakalaka)**: spicy tomato-pepper-onion-bean relish. The braai side that goes with everything.
7. **[Melktert](/recipes/cuisine/south-african/melktert)**: milk tart, sweet milky custard in a shortcrust shell with cinnamon dusted on top. The Afrikaner Sunday dessert.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with South African wine (Pinotage from Stellenbosch is the national grape; Chenin Blanc is the world-class white) or with Castle Lager. Finish with malva pudding (sticky apricot sponge with vanilla sauce) or koeksisters (deep-fried syrup-soaked plaited dough).
