# Grenadian Provision

![Grenadian Provision](resources/grenadian-provision.jpg)

*A platter of boiled root crops and breadfruit: dasheen, yam and green plantain dropped into salted water and cooked plain to soak up gravy from whatever stew lands next to it.*

**Serves:** 4 to 6

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes

**Cook Time:** 30 minutes

## Overview
Provision is the Caribbean word for boiled ground food: the starchy roots, tubers and breadfruit that come out of the provision ground (the small garden plot every Grenadian household keeps for growing its own carbohydrate). A plate of provision is dasheen, yam, breadfruit and green plantain (sometimes sweet potato, sometimes tania) boiled in salted water until tender and served plain alongside a stewed fish, callaloo or curried meat. It is the spoon of the meal: each piece soaks up gravy, balances heat, and fills the plate without competing with the main. Boil it plain, salt the water heavily, peel just before cooking, and serve very hot. The most everyday side dish on the island.

## Ingredients

- 500 g dasheen (taro), peeled, cut into 5 cm pieces
- 500 g yellow yam, peeled, cut into 5 cm pieces
- 1 small breadfruit (or half a large one), peeled, cored, cut into wedges
- 2 green plantains, peeled, cut into 4 cm chunks
- 2 tbsp salt
- Water to cover
- 1 tbsp butter (optional, to finish)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Peel
1. Wear gloves for the dasheen and yam (raw dasheen can irritate the skin).
2. Peel each root thickly with a sharp knife; the skin is thick and woody.
3. Cut into even 5 cm pieces so they cook at the same rate.
4. Drop into a bowl of cold water as you go (stops them browning).

### Stage 2 - Boil the slow ones first
1. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil.
2. Add 2 tablespoons of salt.
3. Add the yam and dasheen first; boil 10 minutes.

### Stage 3 - Add the breadfruit and plantain
1. Add the breadfruit wedges and green plantain.
2. Continue boiling another 20 minutes.
3. Test each piece with a knife: it should slide in with mild resistance.
4. The dasheen and yam should be soft, the breadfruit floury, the plantain tender but not falling apart.

### Stage 4 - Drain and serve
1. Drain the water carefully.
2. Return to the warm pot; toss with the butter if using.
3. Serve hot on a wide platter, grouped by type.

## Notes
- **Wear gloves:** raw dasheen contains calcium oxalate; the irritation goes once cooked but the raw skin scratches.
- **Salt the water heavily:** the inside of each piece only gets seasoning through the boiling water.
- **Even pieces, even cooking:** uneven cuts give you raw centres and mushy edges.
- **Drain quickly:** sitting in the hot water turns the texture mushy.

## Variations
**Sweet potato provision:** add 500 g cubed sweet potato in stage 3.
**Tania (eddoes):** swap or supplement the dasheen with smaller tania roots.
**Cassava:** add 300 g peeled cassava in stage 2 (boil the same time as yam).
**Pumpkin in the mix:** add 300 g cubed pumpkin in the last 10 minutes.
**With salt-cooked spices:** boil with 2 bay leaves and a sprig of thyme for a more savoury pot.

## Serving
With stewed saltfish (the classic pairing) · with callaloo and dumplings · with curried chicken · with brown stew fish · with green seasoning butter on top.

## Storage
- Best eaten the day it's cooked.
- Keeps 2 days refrigerated; reheat by steaming, not microwaving.
- Leftover boiled provision is the base for the next day's bakes or fritters.
