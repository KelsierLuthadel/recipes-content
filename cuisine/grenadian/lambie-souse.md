# Lambie Souse

![Lambie Souse](resources/lambie-souse.jpg)

*Tenderised conch in a sharp lime-cucumber-onion brine: the Grenadian Sunday-afternoon dish, eaten cold from a bowl with a beer and a sea view.*

**Serves:** 4 to 6 as a starter

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes (plus 4 hours chilling)

**Cook Time:** 1 hour

## Overview
Lambie (the Grenadian name for conch, the big sea snail pulled from the reefs around Carriacou and the south coast) is the seafood of the Saturday market. Tenderising it is the work: the muscle is pounded flat, boiled long, and then sliced thin. Once cooked, it goes cold into a bright sharp dressing of lime juice, cucumber, onion, scotch bonnet and a lot of chadon beni, and the whole bowl sits in the fridge to mingle for a few hours. The result is somewhere between a ceviche and a salad: tender chewy conch in a clear sour brine, eaten cold with a spoon at the bar, on the beach, or under a sea-grape tree. It is a Sunday dish, an afternoon-lime dish, a beer dish. A Grenadian classic.

## Ingredients

### For the conch
- 600 g cleaned conch meat (lambie), fresh or frozen
- 1 lime, halved
- 1 tsp salt
- 1 small onion, halved
- 2 cloves garlic
- 1 bay leaf
- Water to cover

### For the souse dressing
- 1 large cucumber, peeled, deseeded, diced small
- 1 large onion, very finely sliced
- 6 limes, juiced (about 180 ml)
- 1 scotch bonnet, deseeded, very finely chopped
- 4 spring onions, chopped
- 1 small bunch chadon beni (or coriander), finely chopped
- 2 garlic cloves, grated
- 1 tsp salt
- 0.5 tsp black pepper
- 1 tbsp olive oil

## Method

### Stage 1 - Tenderise the conch
1. Lay each conch piece between two sheets of cling film.
2. Pound flat with a meat mallet or rolling pin until the muscle is half its original thickness and visibly broken down.
3. Rub all over with the cut lime halves and 1 tsp salt; rinse.

### Stage 2 - Boil
1. Place the conch in a pot with the onion, garlic, bay leaf and enough water to cover.
2. Bring to a boil; drop to a simmer.
3. Cook 45 minutes to 1 hour, until a knife slides through with only mild resistance.
4. Drain; let cool 10 minutes.

### Stage 3 - Slice
1. With a sharp knife, slice the cooked conch into thin strips about 5 mm thick.
2. The strips should be tender but still have a slight chew.

### Stage 4 - Make the souse
1. Combine the cucumber, onion, lime juice, scotch bonnet, spring onion, chadon beni, grated garlic, salt, pepper and olive oil in a bowl.
2. Whisk together; taste for sharpness and salt.

### Stage 5 - Marinate
1. Add the sliced conch to the dressing.
2. Toss to coat.
3. Cover; refrigerate at least 4 hours, ideally overnight.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Stir once before serving.
2. Spoon into small bowls with plenty of the lime brine.
3. Eat very cold.

## Notes
- **The pounding is the secret:** under-pounded conch stays rubbery no matter how long you boil it.
- **Don't overcook:** more than an hour and the conch turns to pencil-eraser.
- **Use real limes:** bottled lime juice is too sharp and one-note.
- **Make ahead:** the souse is better on day 2 than day 1; the flavours need time to mingle.

## Notes (cont)
- **Whelks substitute:** if conch is unavailable, large whelks work the same way (shorter boil, 30 minutes).

## Variations
**Pig souse:** the same dressing over boiled pig trotter, snout and ear (the original souse of the Caribbean).
**Chicken-foot souse:** boiled chicken feet in the same brine.
**Octopus souse:** boiled octopus tentacles, sliced thin.
**With grated carrot:** add 1 grated carrot for extra crunch.
**Hotter version:** keep the seeds in the scotch bonnet and add a second one.

## Serving
With cold Carib beer · with crackers on the side · at a Sunday afternoon lime · at a beach bar · as a starter before pelau or oildown.

## Storage
- Keeps 3 days refrigerated in the brine.
- The conch becomes a touch chewier after day 2 but the flavour deepens.
- Do not freeze.
