# Grenadian

*The Caribbean Spice Isle. Nutmeg, mace, cinnamon and clove grown on the island, the curry tradition layered onto an Afro-Caribbean base, and oildown (the national one-pot of breadfruit, salt meat and turmeric-coconut broth) as the country's defining dish.*

## A short history

Grenadian cooking starts with the island's first peoples, the Arawak and the Kalinago (the Caribs), who fished the surf, grew cassava on the volcanic hillsides, hunted iguana and agouti in the bush, and built a kitchen on the staples that still anchor the plate today: cassava, sweet potato, corn, peppers, fish from the reef, callaloo greens. They invented the pepperpot, the long-simmered spiced meat stew kept alive in a single pot for months on end, and the technique of grating bitter cassava and pressing out the toxic juice before baking it into bread. The Kalinago name for the island, Camahogne, persisted until Columbus sighted it in 1498 and renamed it Concepción; the Spanish never effectively colonised it, and the Kalinago held out against European settlement for another 150 years.

The French took the island in 1649 after a brutal war that ended with the last of the Kalinago leaping to their deaths off a north-coast cliff still called Le Morne des Sauteurs. The French ran sugar plantations on the island for over a century and brought enslaved African labourers from West and Central Africa to work them. The African contribution is the deepest single influence on the modern Grenadian kitchen: the okra and callaloo stew, the cornmeal coucou, the slow one-pot cooking with salt meat, the love of pepper, the rice-and-peas Sunday lunch, the rhythm of pounding and grinding in a wooden mortar. The French also planted cocoa, which became the island's first major export crop and is still grown on small farms above the south-east coast.

The British took Grenada in 1763 after the Seven Years' War and held it until independence in 1974. The colonial period brought two cuisine-changing arrivals. First, in 1843, British botanists planted nutmeg trees from the East Indies in the Belmont estate. The volcanic soil produced extraordinary yields, the trees thrived, and by the late 19th century Grenada was second only to Indonesia in nutmeg production. The country still grows about 20% of the world's nutmeg today, and mace, cinnamon, clove, ginger and allspice all flourish on the same hillsides. Second, after the abolition of slavery in 1834, Indian indentured workers arrived from 1857 onwards to work the sugar and cocoa plantations. They brought curry, roti, the dhalpouri-style flatbread, dhal and the chickpea tradition. The Indian-Grenadian community is small but the curry influence on the food is huge.

The 20th century saw the cocoa and nutmeg economies rise and fall, the rise of small-island independence movements after the Second World War, and the 1979 Grenadian Revolution under Maurice Bishop. The 1983 US invasion ended the revolution and didn't really change the home kitchen. Through all of it, oildown stayed the universal Sunday-and-celebration plate, the dish that everyone on the island recognises as their own regardless of politics: breadfruit, salt meat, dumplings and callaloo simmered in turmeric-spiked coconut milk until the liquid reduces to oil. Breadfruit itself was brought to the Caribbean in 1793 by Captain Bligh of HMS Providence (his second attempt after the Bounty mutiny), specifically to feed enslaved plantation workers, and it became the staple it remains today.

The post-independence decades and the recovery from Hurricane Ivan (2004, which flattened the nutmeg crop and forced a generation-long replanting) put Grenadian food back on its feet. The cuisine reflects Caribbean labour migration: the enslaved African population (the slow-stewed one-pot, breadfruit, callaloo), Indian indentured workers (curry, roti), the Carib indigenous shelf (cassava, sweet potato), and the British colonial layer (formal tea, sweet bread). Garifuna influence is lighter here than on Trinidad. Diaspora kitchens in Brooklyn, Toronto and London keep the home recipes codified, and oildown, the national dish, anchors every gathering.

## Regional variations

The country is small (344 km²) and the cuisine is broadly consistent:

- **St George's (capital, southwest)**: the codified mainstream. Oildown, callaloo, the rum-distillery tradition (River Antoine, the oldest functioning water-mill rum distillery in the Caribbean).
- **Carriacou (sister island)**: more bush food. Smoked herring, oildown variants.
- **Petite Martinique (smallest of the three islands)**: the fishing tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Nutmeg**: in cooking, in drinks (rum punch dusted with nutmeg), in sweets.
- **Other spices**: cinnamon, clove, allspice, mace. All grown on-island.
- **Breadfruit**: roasted whole or boiled.
- **Callaloo**: dasheen leaves.
- **Salt meat (salt pork, salt beef)**: in oildown, in stews.
- **Coconut milk**: in oildown, in rice and peas.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.
- **Curry powder**: Caribbean blend (turmeric, cumin, coriander, fenugreek).

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Oildown](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/oildown)**: breadfruit, salt meat, callaloo, turmeric and coconut milk slow-simmered until the milk reduces to oil. The national dish.
2. **[Grenada Curried Goat](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-curried-goat)**: goat in Caribbean curry powder with potato, served with rice. The Sunday celebration plate.
3. **[Grenada Roti](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-roti)**: dhalpouri-style flatbread wrapped around curried meat (chicken, goat or beef) with potato and chickpea.
4. **[Grenada Pelau](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-pelau)**: rice cooked with brown-sugar-caramelised chicken, coconut milk and pigeon peas. The Caribbean rice classic.
5. **[Grenada Nutmeg Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-nutmeg-chicken)**: chicken seasoned with fresh-grated nutmeg and other Grenadian spices, slow-roasted. The Spice Isle showpiece.

## Where to go next

Pour a rum punch (heavy on the Westerhall or River Antoine rum, dusted with fresh nutmeg) or a Carib lager. Finish with nutmeg ice cream, Grenada's signature dessert, the spice infused straight into the custard.
