# Grenadian

*The Caribbean Spice Isle. Nutmeg, mace, cinnamon and clove grown on the island; the curry tradition layered onto an Afro-Caribbean base; oildown (the national one-pot of breadfruit, salt meat and turmeric-coconut broth) as the country's defining dish.*

## A short history

Grenadian cooking sits on the Eastern Caribbean tradition shared with Trinidad, Tobago and St Vincent, but the spice tradition is unique. The British brought nutmeg trees from the East Indies in 1843; the volcanic soil produced extraordinary spice yields, and by the late 19th century Grenada was second only to Indonesia in nutmeg production. The country grows about 20% of the world's nutmeg today; mace, cinnamon, clove, ginger and allspice all flourish.

The cuisine reflects the Caribbean labour migration: African enslaved population (the slow-stewed one-pot, breadfruit, callaloo), Indian indentured workers (curry, roti), Carib indigenous (cassava, sweet potato), British colonial (formal tea, sweet bread). The Garifuna influence is lighter than on Trinidad.

The 1979 independence and the 1983 US invasion didn't change the home kitchen. The national dish — oildown — is the universal Sunday-and-celebration plate: breadfruit, salt meat, dumplings and callaloo simmered in turmeric-spiked coconut milk until the liquid reduces to oil.

## Regional variations

The country is small (344 km²) and the cuisine is broadly consistent:

- **St George's (capital, southwest)**: codified mainstream. Oildown, callaloo, the rum-distillery tradition (River Antoine, the oldest functioning water-mill rum distillery in the Caribbean).
- **Carriacou (sister island)**: more bush food. Smoked herring, oildown variants.
- **Petite Martinique (smallest of the three islands)**: fishing tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Nutmeg**: in cooking, in drinks (rum punch dusted with nutmeg), in sweets.
- **Other spices**: cinnamon, clove, allspice, mace — all grown on-island.
- **Breadfruit**: roasted whole or boiled.
- **Callaloo**: dasheen leaves.
- **Salt meat (salt pork, salt beef)**: in oildown, in stews.
- **Coconut milk**: in oildown, in rice and peas.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.
- **Curry powder**: Caribbean blend (turmeric-cumin-coriander-fenugreek).

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Oildown](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/oildown)**: breadfruit, salt meat, callaloo, turmeric and coconut milk slow-simmered until the milk reduces to oil. The national dish.
2. **[Grenada Curried Goat](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-curried-goat)**: goat in Caribbean curry powder with potato, served with rice. The Sunday celebration plate.
3. **[Grenada Roti](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-roti)**: dhalpouri-style flatbread wrapped around curried meat (chicken, goat or beef) with potato and chickpea.
4. **[Grenada Pelau](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-pelau)**: rice cooked with brown-sugar-caramelised chicken, coconut milk and pigeon peas. The Caribbean rice classic.
5. **[Grenada Nutmeg Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/grenadian/grenada-nutmeg-chicken)**: chicken seasoned with fresh-grated nutmeg and other Grenadian spices, slow-roasted. The Spice Isle showpiece.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a rum punch (heavy on the Westerhall or River Antoine rum, dusted with fresh nutmeg) or with Carib lager. End with nutmeg ice cream — Grenada's signature dessert, the spice infused into the custard.
