# Vietnamese

*Fresh herbs piled at the table, clear aromatic broths, rice paper softened in warm water, and the French baguette reinvented with pâté and pickle. Vietnamese cooking is built on lightness, contrast, and the cook leaving the final seasoning to the eater.*

## A short history

Vietnamese food grew out of a thousand-year conversation with its neighbours, layered onto a deep native foundation of rice cultivation in the Red River and Mekong deltas. The earliest and most lasting influence came from China, whose thousand-year occupation (roughly 111 BCE to 939 CE) left chopsticks, the wok, soy sauce, wheat noodles, and the technique of simmering bones for clear broth. The Vietnamese took these tools and bent them toward their own palate: lighter, brighter, herb-forward, with fish sauce replacing soy as the primary salty backbone.

From the south and west came Indian influence, carried up through the Champa kingdoms and along trade routes from the Mekong delta. Turmeric, coconut milk, and the idea of a slow-braised curry took root, giving rise to cà ri gà and the southern habit of finishing dishes with coconut.

The French arrived in the nineteenth century and stayed almost a hundred years. They brought wheat bread, coffee, pâté, mayonnaise, butter, and beef as a routine ingredient rather than a celebration meat. Vietnamese cooks absorbed all of it without losing themselves: the baguette became bánh mì, condensed-milk coffee became cà phê sữa đá, and the beef bone broth became phở.

The twentieth century scattered Vietnamese cooking worldwide. Refugees from the war in the 1970s carried phở and bánh mì into California, Paris, Sydney, and Toronto, and the cuisine quietly became one of the most recognised in the world. Through all of it, the anchoring principle holds steady: a clean broth or a clear dressing, fresh herbs at the table, the eater finishing the seasoning.

## Regional variations

Vietnam is a long, narrow country, and the food shifts noticeably as you travel south.

- **North (Hà Nội)**: Phở's birthplace. Northern cooking is the most restrained on the map: savoury rather than sweet, with black pepper standing in for chilli, and broths kept clear and aromatic rather than rich. Herbs are used as accents rather than handfuls.
- **Central (Huế, Đà Nẵng, Hội An)**: The spiciest and most ceremonial region, shaped by the old imperial court at Huế. Bún bò Huế is the standard-bearer: a beef broth heavy with lemongrass and chilli oil, layered with shrimp paste. Hội An contributes the herb-and-rice-paper salads that have become emblematic of central Vietnam.
- **South (Hồ Chí Minh City, Mekong delta)**: Sweeter, richer, more tropical. Coconut milk turns up in savoury dishes, palm sugar finds its way into nearly every sauce, and the herb plate at the table grows wider. The French inflection also lingers strongest here: bánh mì culture, café sữa đá culture, and the most pâté-forward sandwiches all come from Saigon.

The thread that runs the length of the country is the table itself. North, centre, or south, the cook puts out the components and the diner finishes the dish.

## Signature ingredients

- **Fish sauce (nước mắm)**: The savoury foundation of Vietnamese cooking, used the way Western cooks use salt. Fermented from anchovies and salt, it carries depth without heaviness.
- **Rice paper (bánh tráng)**: Thin, brittle discs of dried rice batter that soften in warm water and become a wrapper. The structural genius behind gỏi cuốn and a hundred other rolled snacks.
- **Rice noodles**: Three main shapes do most of the work. Bánh phở (flat, for soup), bún (round vermicelli, for cold bowls and grilled-meat plates), and bánh hỏi (woven mats, for special-occasion meals).
- **Vietnamese coriander (rau răm)**: Peppery, citrusy, slightly soapy if you are new to it. The dominant herb in northern soups and a non-negotiable companion to balut and grilled meats.
- **Thai basil (húng quế)**: Anise-scented, bigger-leaved than European basil. It goes on the side plate with phở and in nearly every fresh roll.
- **Perilla (tía tô)**: A two-tone purple-and-green leaf with a faintly cinnamon-mint flavour. Used in summer rolls and grilled-meat plates.
- **Lemongrass (sả)**: Bashed and finely chopped, used in marinades, broths, and curries. Provides the citrus-floral edge that defines southern and central Vietnamese cooking.
- **Lime (chanh)**: Squeezed over almost everything at the table. The acid lift that makes the herbs read clearly.
- **Bird's-eye chilli (ớt hiểm)**: Small, ferocious, used fresh and sliced raw rather than cooked into a paste.
- **Hoisin sauce**: Borrowed from Chinese cooking and adopted as the dipping companion to gỏi cuốn, usually loosened with peanut butter and warm water into a thick, glossy sauce.

## Learn it in seven recipes

A short course through the techniques that define the cuisine, ordered from foundation to celebration.

1. **[Beef Phở](beef-pho.md)**. Start with the broth. Phở bò is the dish all Vietnamese cooking is judged against: a beef bone broth scented with star anise, cinnamon, charred ginger, and clove-studded onion, ladled boiling-hot over flat rice noodles and raw beef that poaches on contact. Learn to toast the spices properly, slice the beef paper-thin from the freezer, and bring the broth to a rolling boil before serving. Once you can do this, you understand Vietnamese cooking.

2. **[Bánh Mì](snacks/banh-mi.md)**. The French legacy in sandwich form. A light, thin-shelled baguette warmed until it crackles, smeared thickly with pâté and Kewpie mayonnaise, then layered with cold cuts, cucumber, pickled carrot and daikon (dưa chua), coriander sprigs, sliced chilli, and a few drops of Maggi seasoning. Mostly assembly, almost no cooking, but the proportions and the bread are everything.

3. **[Gỏi Cuốn](snacks/goi-cuon.md)**. Fresh summer rolls. Pork belly simmered tender, prawns poached briefly, vermicelli cooked al dente, all rolled in rice paper with lettuce and herbs so the pink of the prawns shows through the wrapper. Served with a thick peanut-hoisin dipping sauce. This is where you learn to handle rice paper: a short dip in warm water, a firm tuck under the filling, a tight roll. Once it clicks it is faster than making a salad.

4. **[Bún Chả](bun-cha.md)**. Hà Nội's grilled-pork-and-vermicelli plate. Two cuts of pork (sliced belly and seasoned mince patties) char-grilled over high heat, served with cold rice vermicelli, a warm sweet-sour fish-sauce dressing, pickled green papaya, lettuce, mint, and Thai basil. The dressing here is the northern cousin of nước chấm, and the table assembly is the cuisine's defining ritual: each diner builds every spoonful from the components.

5. **[Cà Ri Gà (Vietnamese Chicken Curry)](vietnamese-curry.md)**. The southern coconut-and-turmeric braise that sits at the meeting point of French colonial cooking and Southeast Asian spicing. Chicken simmered in coconut milk with sweet potato, carrot, lemongrass, star anise, cardamom, and cinnamon, finished with palm sugar and fish sauce. The whole spices want a brief bloom in oil before the aromatics go in. Lighter than a Thai curry, less sour than an Indian one.

6. **[Gỏi Bò Hội An](go-bo-hoi-an.md)**. A central-Vietnam beef salad from the lantern-lit town of Hội An. Paper-thin slices of grilled steak, marinated in tamarind and palm sugar, tossed warm through green papaya, herbs, cucumber, and shattered rice paper crisps, all dressed with lime and fish sauce. This is the dish that teaches you the central-region rhythm: warm meat against cold vegetables, sour against sweet, soft against crunchy, all finished at the table.

7. **[Chè Ba Màu](desserts/che-ba-mau.md)**. The three-colour dessert that closes the meal. Red beans, yellow mung beans, and emerald pandan agar jelly layered in a tall glass over crushed ice and drowned in salted-sweet coconut cream. Three components prepared separately, then assembled at the last moment. The first spoon must reach the bottom of the glass so every layer comes up together. Cool, sweet, with the salt cutting through the cream so it never reads as cloying.

Seven recipes that give you broth, sandwich, fresh roll, grilled-meat plate, coconut curry, herb-driven salad, and a chilled dessert. The grammar of the cuisine in a single sitting.
