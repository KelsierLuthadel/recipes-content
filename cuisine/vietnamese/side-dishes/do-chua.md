# Dưa Chua (Vietnamese Pickled Carrot and Daikon)

![Dưa Chua](resources/do-chua.jpg)

*Vietnam's bright everyday pickle: julienned carrot and daikon salted to soften, then steeped in a sharp sweet-sour vinegar brine; ready in an hour, keeps in the fridge for weeks, stuffed into every bánh mì and piled next to grilled meats.*

**Serves:** 1 jar (about 600 g, enough for 12 bánh mì)

**Prep Time:** 25 minutes (plus 1 hour brine)

**Cook Time:** None

## Overview
Dưa chua (literally "sour vegetables", and often anglicised as đồ chua or do-chua) is the workhorse pickle of Vietnamese cooking, the sharp orange-and-white tangle that lifts a bánh mì into something more than a sandwich, sits beside grilled pork on a vermicelli bowl, and brightens any plate that needs acid. The construction is straightforward: equal weights of carrot and daikon (Vietnamese white radish) are julienned matchstick-thin, tossed with salt and a small amount of sugar so they sweat and soften, then drained and packed into a jar with a quick brine of rice vinegar, sugar, water and salt. The pickle is ready in an hour and gets better over the first 3 days. The flavour: sweet-sour-bright, faintly peppery from the daikon, with the carrot keeping a slight crunch. The jar lives in the door of the fridge in every Vietnamese kitchen.

## Ingredients

### Vegetables
- 300 g daikon (white radish; peeled)
- 300 g carrots (peeled)
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
- 1 tablespoon caster sugar

### Brine
- 250 ml rice vinegar (or white wine vinegar)
- 250 ml warm water
- 100 g caster sugar
- 2 teaspoons fine sea salt

### Optional adds
- 1 small red bird's-eye chilli (split lengthwise; for a faint heat)
- 2 thin slices fresh ginger
- 1/2 teaspoon black peppercorns

### Equipment
- 1 sterilised 1-litre glass jar with lid

## Method

### Stage 1 - Julienne
1. Cut the daikon and carrots into matchsticks (about 5 cm long, 3 mm thick).
2. The traditional cut is hand-julienned; a mandoline julienne attachment or a coarse box grater works too.
3. Aim for an even matchstick size so the brine penetrates uniformly.

### Stage 2 - Sweat
1. Tip the matchsticks into a large bowl.
2. Toss with the 1 teaspoon salt and 1 tablespoon sugar.
3. Massage with your hands for 1 minute till the vegetables start to soften and release water.
4. Leave 15 minutes; the pile slumps and a pool of liquid collects at the bottom.

### Stage 3 - Drain and rinse
1. Tip the vegetables into a colander.
2. Rinse briefly under cold running water to wash off the surface salt.
3. Squeeze gently in handfuls to expel excess water (the pickle should be moist but not dripping).

### Stage 4 - Brine
1. In a measuring jug, combine the rice vinegar, warm water, 100 g sugar and 2 teaspoons salt.
2. Stir till the sugar and salt fully dissolve.
3. Add the optional chilli, ginger, peppercorns if using.

### Stage 5 - Pack
1. Pack the drained vegetables tightly into the sterilised jar.
2. Pour the brine over till the vegetables are fully submerged.
3. Tap the jar to release air pockets.
4. Seal with the lid.

### Stage 6 - Steep
1. Leave at room temperature 1 hour for a quick eat-now batch.
2. For best flavour, refrigerate at least 24 hours; the pickle peaks at 3 days and stays excellent for 3 weeks.

### Stage 7 - Use
1. Lift portions out with a clean fork (no double-dipping).
2. Drain briefly before stuffing into a bánh mì; the sandwich gets soggy if too wet.
3. Serve alongside grilled pork, vermicelli bowls, or fried fish.

## Notes
- **Daikon is the key:** the white peppery radish gives the pickle its character. Substitutes (in order of nearness): mooli, kohlrabi, turnip. Carrot alone makes a less interesting pickle.
- **Salt-and-sugar pre-soak:** the brief sweat draws out water that would otherwise dilute the brine; skipping it gives a soft, watery pickle.
- **Rice vinegar is right:** unseasoned (not "seasoned" / "sushi" rice vinegar) is the cleanest. White wine vinegar works. Apple cider vinegar is too fruity. Malt is wrong.
- **Sugar-to-vinegar ratio:** 100 g sugar to 250 ml vinegar is the standard sweet-sour Vietnamese balance. Reduce sugar to 60 g for a sharper bánh mì pickle; increase to 130 g for a more dessert-balanced Southern style.
- **Keep submerged:** any vegetables above the brine line go mushy first. Top up with extra brine if the level drops.

## Variations
- **Daikon-only:** for a sharper, more peppery pickle (good with rich grilled meats).
- **Carrot-only:** sweeter, brighter; the simpler home version.
- **With chilli:** add 1-2 split bird's-eye chillies for a spicy version, traditional in some southern Vietnamese homes.
- **With ginger:** add 4 thin slices fresh ginger to the brine for a warmer, more aromatic pickle.
- **Quick eat-now version:** halve the sugar and double the vinegar; ready in 30 minutes; sharper but doesn't keep as long.

## Serving
- Stuffed into a bánh mì alongside pâté, cold cuts, coriander and chilli (the canonical use) · piled beside grilled pork chops on a bún thịt nướng vermicelli bowl · alongside fried fish or shrimp · forked over a Vietnamese summer salad · with a bowl of phở on the side · with a fried egg and rice for a quick lunch.

## Storage
- Refrigerates 3 weeks in a sealed jar with the vegetables fully submerged.
- Best from day 2 to day 14; after that the texture softens but stays edible.
- Don't freeze (the vegetables collapse).
- The brine can be reused once: strain after the first batch, top up with fresh sugar and a splash of vinegar, then steep a new batch.
- A fresh jar at all times is the Vietnamese standard; cooks restart every fortnight.
