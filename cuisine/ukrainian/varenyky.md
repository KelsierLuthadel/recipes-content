# Varenyky

![Varenyky](resources/varenyky.jpg)

*Ukraine's filled dumplings: thin half-moon pasta pockets folded around fillings of mashed potato with caramelised onion, twarog cheese, sauerkraut and mushroom, or sweet cherries in summer. Boiled in salted water and finished with melted butter, soured cream and fried onion.*

**Serves:** 6 (makes about 36 dumplings)

**Prep Time:** 1 hour 15 minutes

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Varenyky are Ukraine's beloved filled dumplings: half-moon pasta pockets that turn up at every family table from the Carpathians to Kyiv. A thin tender dough rolled into small discs, each filled with one of the traditional fillings (mashed potato with caramelised onion, fresh twarog cheese, sweet stewed cherries in summer, sauerkraut and mushroom in winter, berries for special-occasion sweet versions), folded in half and crimped along the curved edge, boiled in salted water till they float to the surface, served with melted butter, smetana (soured cream) and crispy fried onion. Every region has its preferred fillings and every family has its dough recipe, but the form and the finish are universal. The dough wants to be softer and stretchier than Italian pasta dough and needs at least a 30-minute rest for the gluten to relax. The seal must be tight, pinched from the middle outwards along the curved edge; any gap and the dumpling bursts open in the water. Cook in batches in plenty of salted water; crowded pots drop temperature and the dumplings stick.

## Ingredients

### Dough
- 400 g plain flour (plus extra for dusting)
- 1 large egg (beaten)
- 200 ml warm water (or milk for a richer dough)
- 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt

### Potato-and-onion filling (the most classic, this recipe makes the full batch with this filling)
- 600 g floury potatoes (peeled and cubed; Maris Piper or King Edward)
- 50 g butter
- 2 large onions (finely chopped)
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
- ½ teaspoon ground black pepper

### For boiling
- 3 litres water
- 1 ½ tablespoons fine sea salt

### To finish (the proper trio)
- 80 g butter
- 2 large onions (additional, finely sliced; for the fried-onion garnish)
- 2 tablespoons vegetable oil (for frying the onions)
- 250 ml smetana (Ukrainian soured cream; or thick crème fraîche)
- 2 tablespoons fresh dill (chopped)
- Salt and pepper

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make the dough
1. Tip the flour into a wide bowl. Make a well in the centre.
2. Crack the egg into the well; add the oil and salt.
3. Pour the warm water into the well in stages, mixing with one hand as you go to gradually pull the flour into the wet ingredients.
4. Mix till everything comes together as a soft slightly sticky dough.
5. Tip onto a lightly floured surface and knead for 6-8 minutes till smooth and elastic. The dough should be soft but not sticky; add a touch more flour if it sticks to your hands.
6. Wrap in cling film and rest at room temperature for 30 minutes (longer is fine; up to 2 hours).

### Stage 2 - Make the filling
1. While the dough rests, place the cubed potatoes in a saucepan of salted water; bring to the boil and simmer 12-15 minutes till properly tender.
2. Meanwhile, melt the 50 g of butter in a wide frying pan over medium heat.
3. Add the 2 chopped onions and sweat 12-15 minutes till soft and deeply golden (almost caramelised). The deep colour is part of the flavour.
4. Drain the cooked potatoes and tip into a wide bowl. Mash with a fork or potato masher till smooth.
5. Add about three-quarters of the caramelised onions (reserve a quarter for the garnish later), the salt and pepper.
6. Mix till uniform; taste and adjust seasoning. The filling should be properly seasoned and rich-tasting; a bland filling makes bland dumplings.
7. Let cool to room temperature before filling the dumplings; hot filling makes the dough soggy and impossible to seal.

### Stage 3 - Fry the onion garnish
1. Heat 2 tablespoons of vegetable oil in a small frying pan over medium heat.
2. Add the 2 additional sliced onions.
3. Cook for 12-15 minutes, stirring frequently, till deep golden brown and slightly crisp at the edges.
4. Watch the last few minutes; the line between properly browned and burnt is narrow.
5. Lift onto kitchen paper to drain. Set aside.

### Stage 4 - Roll and fill the varenyky
1. Divide the dough into 4 portions for easier handling. Cover the portions you're not working with so they don't dry out.
2. Roll one portion out on a lightly floured surface to about 2-3 mm thick (thin but not see-through; thinner gives tender dumplings, but too thin and they tear).
3. Cut out rounds with a glass or cutter (7-8 cm diameter).
4. Place a heaped teaspoon of cooled filling in the centre of each round.
5. Fold the dough over to form a half-moon shape; pinch the edges together firmly, working from the middle outwards, sealing tightly. If the dough won't stick, brush the edges very lightly with water before sealing.
6. Lay the finished varenyky on a lightly floured tray; cover with a clean cloth.
7. Repeat with the remaining dough portions. Reroll any scraps.

### Stage 5 - Boil the varenyky
1. Bring the 3 litres of water and 1.5 tablespoons of salt to a rolling boil in a wide deep pan.
2. Cook the varenyky in batches of 8-10 (don't crowd; they need space to float freely).
3. Lower the dumplings in gently with a slotted spoon.
4. They'll sink at first; after 2-3 minutes they float to the surface.
5. Once floating, cook another 90 seconds (about 4-5 minutes total cook time).
6. Lift out with a slotted spoon onto a warmed plate.
7. Brush each batch with a knob of melted butter as it comes out of the water; this stops the dumplings sticking together and adds richness.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Pile the warm varenyky into shallow bowls.
2. Drizzle with the remaining melted butter.
3. Top with a generous spoonful of smetana (soured cream).
4. Scatter the fried crisp onion over each portion.
5. Scatter chopped fresh dill.
6. Add a small grind of black pepper.
7. Serve immediately while hot.

## Notes
- **Cool filling before assembling:** hot filling makes the dough soggy and the dumplings impossible to seal. Let the potato cool to room temperature first.
- **Seal tightly:** the most common failure is leaky varenyky bursting in the water. Pinch the edges firmly with pressure, working from the middle outwards. Wet the edges with water for extra grip if needed.
- **Don't crowd the pan:** 8-10 dumplings per batch in a wide pan with plenty of boiling water. Crowding drops the temperature, makes the dumplings stick together, and gives uneven cooking.
- **Butter immediately after boiling:** brushing each batch with melted butter as it comes out of the water stops them sticking together and adds richness. Don't skip this step.
- **The trio of finish: butter, smetana, fried onion:** the three together are the traditional Ukrainian finish. Skip any one and you've made an incomplete dish. Smetana proper (the Ukrainian thick soured cream) is best; thick crème fraîche works.
- **The dough must rest:** the 30-minute rest is what gives the dumplings their proper tender bite. Skipping it gives tough chewy varenyky.

## Variations
**Cheese (twarog) filling:** 400 g of twarog (Ukrainian fresh white cheese; substitute with farmer cheese or well-drained cottage cheese), 1 beaten egg, 2 tablespoons sugar, 1 teaspoon vanilla. The classic savoury-sweet filling; serve with smetana and a drizzle of honey.
**Cherry filling (sweet summer varenyky):** 500 g fresh pitted sour cherries tossed with 4 tablespoons sugar and 2 tablespoons cornflour. Fill the dough rounds, seal tightly (cherries are juicy and prone to leaking), boil. Serve with smetana and a sprinkle of sugar. The summer-celebration version.
**Sauerkraut and mushroom filling:** 200 g sauerkraut (squeezed dry), 200 g mushrooms (chopped and sautéed in butter with onion), salt and pepper. The winter and lent-season filling; serve with melted butter and crispy fried onion (no smetana on lent days).
**Blueberry filling:** 400 g fresh or frozen blueberries with 4 tablespoons sugar and 2 tablespoons cornflour. The deep-blue summer dessert version.
**Lazy varenyky (lazy cheese dumplings):** the no-roll version; mix twarog with egg, flour and sugar into a soft dough, shape into small dumplings or roll into a log and slice. Boil; serve as breakfast or dessert. Much quicker.

## Serving
In shallow bowls with the trio of butter, smetana and fried onion as the traditional finish. A glass of horilka (Ukrainian vodka) traditionally, or kompot (the fruit drink) for non-drinkers. Borscht alongside for the proper Ukrainian feast.

## Storage
- The shaped uncooked varenyky freeze 2 months on a tray (lay flat in a single layer to freeze; then transfer to a bag once solid). Cook from frozen; add 1-2 minutes to the boiling time.
- Cooked varenyky keep refrigerated 2 days; reheat by frying gently in butter (the panfry treatment gives crispy edges; some Ukrainians prefer leftover varenyky this way).
- Don't microwave; the dough goes rubbery.
- The fillings freeze 2 months on their own; useful for making varenyky on demand.
