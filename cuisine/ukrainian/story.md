# Ukrainian

*The kitchen of Europe's breadbasket. Borscht as the national soup (UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage), the varenyky dumplings, holubtsi cabbage rolls, syrnyky cottage-cheese pancakes, and the rich black-earth tradition of grain, dairy and root vegetable cookery.*

## A short history

Ukrainian cooking sits on the world's most fertile black soil (chornozem). The country has been Europe's breadbasket since classical times — wheat, rye, barley, buckwheat, sunflower seeds (Ukraine produces ~30% of the world's sunflower oil), potatoes (since the 18th century), beets, cabbage, dairy from the steppe herds. The shelf is essentially Eastern Slavic but with distinct techniques and traditions that have developed over centuries.

Borscht — the beetroot soup — is the country's defining dish. UNESCO inscribed it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2022 (under "emergency safeguarding" because of the war), recognising Ukrainian borscht specifically (against Russian claims of shared ownership). The Ukrainian version is typically thicker than the Russian, with more beetroot, served with pampushky (garlic-bread rolls), sour cream, and rye bread.

Varenyky (the boiled dumplings — Russian-Ukrainian pierogi/pelmeni cousins) are the second pillar. Holubtsi (stuffed cabbage rolls), syrnyky (cottage-cheese pancakes), salo (cured pork fat eaten on rye bread with garlic — both peasant food and national pride) round out the home table.

The post-2022 war has driven significant emigration to Western Europe, Canada and the US, where the diaspora cooking is becoming more widely known.

## Regional variations

- **Kyiv and central**: codified mainstream. Chicken Kyiv (the dish's actual origin is contested — possibly imperial Russian, but Ukrainian by codification), borscht, varenyky.
- **Western (Lviv, Halychyna)**: Polish-Austro-Hungarian influence. Sweeter borscht, the kanapky (open sandwich) tradition.
- **Odesa and the south (Black Sea)**: more Jewish-Greek-Turkish influence. The Odesan port-city cuisine is distinctive.
- **Carpathian (Hutsul, Boyko)**: hill cooking. Banosh (cornmeal porridge), the brynza sheep cheese.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beetroot**: in borscht. Roasted, raw-grated, or boiled.
- **Sour cream (smetana)**: dressing soups and dumplings.
- **Dill**: heavy use.
- **Pork (and salo — cured pork fat)**: the meat.
- **Buckwheat (hrechka)**: in porridge, in kasha.
- **Potatoes**: in deruny, in varenyky filling.
- **Sunflower oil**: cooking and dressing.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Borscht](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/borscht)**: thick beetroot soup with cabbage, potato, carrots, beef, garlic and dill, finished with sour cream. The national soup.
2. **[Varenyky](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/varenyky)**: boiled dumplings stuffed with potato-and-cheese, cabbage-and-mushroom, or sour-cherry (the dessert version). Served with sour cream and fried onion.
3. **[Holubtsi](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/holubtsi)**: cabbage rolls stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-baked in tomato-and-sour-cream sauce. The Sunday family plate.
4. **[Chicken Kyiv](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/chicken-kyiv)**: chicken breast rolled around garlic-and-herb butter, breaded and pan-fried. The dish opens with a knife and floods butter.
5. **[Deruny](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/deruny)**: grated-potato pancakes pan-fried in oil, served with sour cream. The everyday breakfast.
6. **[Syrnyky](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/syrnyky)**: cottage-cheese pancakes with sugar and raisins. The Sunday brunch.
7. **[Pampushky](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/pampushky)**: small soft garlic-bread rolls served with borscht — fluffy, glistening with garlic-oil glaze.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with horilka (Ukrainian vodka) or with Lvivske beer (the country's oldest brewery, since 1715). End with kvas (fermented rye drink), or with kyivskyi tort (the Kyiv cake — hazelnut meringue and buttercream).
