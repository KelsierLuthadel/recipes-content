# Ukrainian

*The kitchen of Europe's breadbasket. Borscht as the national soup (UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage), varenyky dumplings, holubtsi cabbage rolls, syrnyky cottage-cheese pancakes, and the rich black-earth tradition of grain, dairy and root vegetable cookery.*

## A short history

Ukrainian cooking sits on the world's most fertile black soil (chornozem), and the kitchen has been shaped by that soil for at least three thousand years. The Scythian and Sarmatian horse peoples who held the steppe in classical times were already growing wheat and trading grain to the Greek colonies on the Black Sea coast, and the Greeks at Olbia and Chersonesos in turn passed wine, olive oil and fish sauce north. The early Slavic peoples who settled the forest-steppe from the 6th century onward built the everyday kitchen on rye, buckwheat, cabbage, mushrooms foraged from the birch forests, river fish from the Dnipro, and the famous fermented preserves (sauerkraut, pickled cucumber, fermented apple) that carry a household through the long continental winter.

The medieval centuries belonged to Kyivan Rus. Prince Volodymyr's conversion to Eastern Christianity in 988 brought Byzantine influence: leavened bread baked in clay ovens, the long fasting calendar of Orthodox Lent that drove the development of mushroom, fish, grain and fermented-vegetable cookery (almost half the year was meatless), and the honey-and-yeast drinks that became medovukha and kvas. The honey trade was so important that medieval Kyiv was one of the great honey markets of eastern Europe. The Mongol invasion of 1240 sacked Kyiv and turned the western lands toward Poland-Lithuania, while the eastern steppe remained open to Tatar and Ottoman contact. From Tatar cooking came the meat-stuffed dumpling that became varenyky's sibling and the *manty* tradition that pushed west.

The 16th and 17th centuries saw two transformations. The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth ruled most of Ukraine and brought central European tastes into the Lviv and Halychyna kitchens: heavier breads, sweet-sour cabbage stews, beetroot soups, and the dumpling-and-pierogi tradition that became varenyky. The Cossack Hetmanate that emerged in the mid-1600s on the central steppe developed its own field kitchen of *kulish* (millet-and-fatback porridge cooked in a pot over a campfire), *salo* (cured pork fat eaten on rye bread with garlic, the Cossack ration that became a point of national pride), and the slow-cooked one-pot stews fed to whole regiments. The Columbian Exchange arrived in this period: tomatoes, peppers, sunflowers and (transformatively) potatoes. The sunflower took to Ukrainian soil so well that the country now produces around 30% of the world's sunflower oil, and the potato, arriving in the 18th century, slid into every household within a generation.

Borscht itself codified in the 18th and 19th centuries as the beet-and-cabbage soup the world knows, although the name and a thinner version go back to a medieval fermented-hogweed broth. The 19th-century Ukrainian country kitchen, codified in the great cookbook of Olena Pchilka and her contemporaries, locked in the dish list still cooked today: borscht with sour cream, varenyky in all their fillings, holubtsi (stuffed cabbage rolls), syrnyky (cottage-cheese pancakes), deruny (potato pancakes), and the rich rye-and-buckwheat baking tradition. The Soviet period (1922 to 1991) imposed an awkward shared identity with Russian cuisine, and the Holodomor famine of 1932 to 1933 killed millions of Ukrainian farmers and left scars on the food culture that have never fully healed. Wartime rationing and Soviet shortages kept the everyday kitchen frugal, but the home cooking traditions survived in village kitchens and grandmothers' notebooks.

Independence in 1991 began the slow reclamation of Ukrainian food as a distinct national cuisine. UNESCO inscribed Ukrainian borscht on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2022 under "emergency safeguarding" because of the war, recognising it specifically against Russian claims of shared ownership. The Ukrainian version runs thicker than the Russian, with more beetroot, served with pampushky (garlic-bread rolls), sour cream, and rye bread. Varenyky are the second pillar: boiled dumplings, cousin to pierogi and pelmeni, filled with potato-and-cheese, sauerkraut-and-mushroom, or sour cherry in summer. Holubtsi, syrnyky, and salo round out the home table. The post-2022 war has driven heavy emigration to Western Europe, Canada and the US, and the diaspora cooking is becoming more widely known. Modern Kyiv chefs (Yevhen Klopotenko among them) have led a "new Ukrainian cuisine" movement that revives forgotten regional dishes and stakes a clear culinary identity separate from anything Russian.

## Regional variations

- **Kyiv and central**: the codified mainstream. Chicken Kyiv (origin contested, possibly imperial Russian but Ukrainian by codification), borscht, varenyky.
- **Western (Lviv, Halychyna)**: Polish and Austro-Hungarian influence. Sweeter borscht, the kanapky (open sandwich) tradition.
- **Odesa and the south (Black Sea)**: more Jewish, Greek and Turkish influence. The Odesan port-city cuisine is distinctive.
- **Carpathian (Hutsul, Boyko)**: hill cooking. Banosh (cornmeal porridge), brynza sheep cheese.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beetroot**: in borscht. Roasted, raw-grated, or boiled.
- **Sour cream (smetana)**: dressing soups and dumplings.
- **Dill**: heavy use.
- **Pork (and salo, cured pork fat)**: the meat.
- **Buckwheat (hrechka)**: in porridge, in kasha.
- **Potatoes**: in deruny, in varenyky filling.
- **Sunflower oil**: cooking and dressing.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Borscht](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/borscht)**: thick beetroot soup with cabbage, potato, carrots, beef, garlic and dill, finished with sour cream. The national soup.
2. **[Varenyky](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/varenyky)**: boiled dumplings stuffed with potato-and-cheese, cabbage-and-mushroom, or sour cherry (the dessert version). Served with sour cream and fried onion.
3. **[Holubtsi](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/holubtsi)**: cabbage rolls stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-baked in tomato-and-sour-cream sauce. The Sunday family plate.
4. **[Chicken Kyiv](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/chicken-kyiv)**: chicken breast rolled around garlic-and-herb butter, breaded and pan-fried. The knife opens it and butter floods out.
5. **[Deruny](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/deruny)**: grated-potato pancakes pan-fried in oil, served with sour cream. The everyday breakfast.
6. **[Syrnyky](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/syrnyky)**: cottage-cheese pancakes with sugar and raisins. The Sunday brunch.
7. **[Pampushky](/recipes/cuisine/ukrainian/pampushky)**: small soft garlic-bread rolls served with borscht. Fluffy, glistening with a garlic-oil glaze.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with horilka (Ukrainian vodka) or with Lvivske beer (the country's oldest brewery, since 1715). End with kvas (fermented rye drink) or with kyivskyi tort (the Kyiv cake, hazelnut meringue and buttercream).
