# Kvashena Kapusta

![Kvashena Kapusta](resources/kvashena-kapusta.jpg)

*Ukraine's sauerkraut: white cabbage and grated carrot salted in a wooden tub and left to ferment for two weeks till the cabbage turns tangy, properly sour and faintly fizzy. Eaten cold with a drizzle of oil and fresh dill alongside any meat, or warmed into bigos and cabbage stews through winter.*

**Serves:** Makes 1 large jar (about 1.5 kg of fermented cabbage)

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes (plus 10-14 days fermenting)

**Cook Time:** 0 minutes

## Overview
Kvashena kapusta is the Ukrainian (and broader Eastern European) sauerkraut, the live-fermented cabbage that turns up at every cold-weather Ukrainian meal as a side, gets warmed into bigos and cabbage stews through winter, and provides the sharp tangy counter to the heavy meat-and-potato cooking of the country: white cabbage finely shredded, salted (and only salted; no vinegar, this is real lacto-fermentation), packed tightly into a wooden barrel or large jar, weighted down so it stays submerged in its own released brine, and left at cool room temperature for 10-14 days to ferment naturally with wild lactobacillus from the cabbage leaves themselves. The result is properly sour, slightly fizzy, full of beneficial lactobacillus bacteria, and a completely different food from the supermarket "sauerkraut" that's just pickled in vinegar. Three details define proper kvashena kapusta. First, salt-only fermentation, no vinegar. Real sauerkraut is fermented by wild lactobacillus bacteria that's naturally present on the cabbage leaves. The salt creates the conditions (it kills competing bacteria but lets the lactobacillus thrive) but doesn't acidify the cabbage; the lactobacillus does that as it converts the cabbage sugars to lactic acid over 10-14 days. Adding vinegar at the start prevents the fermentation from happening and gives you pickled raw cabbage rather than sauerkraut. Second, the salt ratio. The proper proportion is 2% salt by weight of cabbage; that's 20 g of salt per 1 kg of cabbage. Less salt and competing bacteria take over and the cabbage spoils; more salt and the fermentation goes sluggish. 2% is the sweet spot. Third, the submersion under brine. As the cabbage ferments, it releases liquid that mixes with the salt to form a brine. The cabbage must stay completely submerged in this brine throughout the fermentation; any cabbage exposed to air will mould. Weight it down with a plate, a sealed jar of water, or a fermentation weight; check daily to ensure full submersion.

## Ingredients

### The cabbage and salt (exact ratio matters)
- 2 kg white cabbage (one large or two medium heads; the firmer and denser, the better)
- 200 g carrots (about 2 large)
- 40 g fine sea salt (or non-iodised salt; this is exactly 2% of the combined cabbage-and-carrot weight; weigh it out)

### Optional spices (traditional)
- 1 tablespoon caraway seeds (or fennel seeds; or both)
- 1 teaspoon black peppercorns
- 4-5 bay leaves
- 1 teaspoon juniper berries (optional, gives a slightly more Eastern European flavour)

### Equipment
- A wide ceramic or glass mixing bowl (don't use metal or plastic)
- A 2-litre wide-mouthed glass jar, or a ceramic crock, or a food-safe plastic bucket
- A small plate that fits inside the jar
- A weight (small jar filled with water, or a fermentation weight)
- Clean fine cloth or muslin and a rubber band (for covering the jar mouth while it ferments)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the cabbage
1. Remove the outer leaves of the cabbage (keep a few clean undamaged ones; they'll be useful as a top layer).
2. Cut the cabbage in half through the core; cut out the hard inner core from each half.
3. Slice the cabbage very thinly (2-3 mm wide ribbons) by hand or using a mandoline. The finer the cut, the more surface area for the fermentation to work and the better the texture.
4. Tip the shredded cabbage into a wide ceramic or glass bowl. Don't use metal; the salt-and-acid combination can react with metal.

### Stage 2 - Prepare the carrots and add salt
1. Peel the carrots and grate them coarsely.
2. Add the grated carrot to the cabbage in the bowl.
3. Weigh the combined cabbage-and-carrot mixture; calculate 2% of that weight in salt. (If you have exactly 2.2 kg of cabbage-plus-carrot, you need 44 g of salt.) The recipe above assumes the standard 2 kg cabbage + 200 g carrot = 2.2 kg total, requiring 44 g salt; I've called it 40 g for simplicity but adjust if your weights differ.
4. Sprinkle the salt over the cabbage and carrot.
5. If using the optional spices (caraway, peppercorns, bay, juniper), sprinkle them in now.

### Stage 3 - Massage and bruise
1. With clean hands, massage the salt into the cabbage and carrot vigorously for 8-10 minutes. The cabbage will release its water as you work it; you'll see liquid collecting at the bottom of the bowl.
2. After 10 minutes, the cabbage should have noticeably wilted and should be swimming in its own released brine. If it still looks dry, keep massaging another 5 minutes.

### Stage 4 - Pack the jar
1. Transfer the cabbage and all its released brine into the clean glass jar or ceramic crock, pressing down firmly with your fist or a clean wooden spoon as you fill. The packing must be tight; you want to remove any air pockets.
2. The cabbage should be tightly packed, with the brine rising to cover the top of the cabbage. If the brine isn't quite covering everything, press the cabbage down with your fist to push more brine to the surface.
3. If after pressing the brine still doesn't fully cover, add a topping brine: dissolve 1 teaspoon of salt in 200 ml of water and pour it over to submerge the cabbage.

### Stage 5 - Weight and cover
1. Place one of the reserved outer cabbage leaves on top of the shredded cabbage (this protects the surface from any small floating particles).
2. Place a small plate that fits inside the jar mouth on top of the cabbage leaf, pressing down to keep the cabbage submerged.
3. Place a weight on top of the plate (a small clean jar filled with water works; or a proper fermentation weight from a homebrew shop).
4. Cover the top of the jar with a clean fine cloth or muslin, secured with a rubber band. This keeps flies and dust out but lets the gas released during fermentation escape (don't use a sealed lid; the gas needs to vent or the jar explodes).

### Stage 6 - Ferment
1. Place the jar somewhere at cool room temperature (16-20 C is ideal; warmer ferments faster but less cleanly; colder ferments slowly).
2. Leave for 10-14 days. Check daily:
   - The cabbage should remain submerged in the brine; press down if any pokes above the surface.
   - You'll see bubbles forming in the brine after day 2-3; this is active fermentation.
   - A whitish film may form on the surface (called "kahm yeast"); skim it off if it appears; it's harmless but unpleasant tasting.
   - The brine becomes increasingly cloudy as the fermentation progresses; this is normal.
3. After 10 days, taste a piece. It should be properly sour, slightly fizzy, tangy. If it's still mild, give it another 3-4 days.

### Stage 7 - Transfer to long-term storage
1. Once the kvashena kapusta tastes properly fermented (sour, tangy, slightly fizzy), transfer to clean jars with proper sealable lids.
2. Pack tightly; ensure the cabbage stays covered by brine in the jar.
3. Refrigerate. The cold slows the fermentation almost to a stop; the sauerkraut keeps for 4-6 months in the fridge.

### Stage 8 - Serve
1. Tip a generous portion of the cold kvashena kapusta into a small bowl.
2. Drizzle with sunflower oil (or vegetable oil) and a small splash of the brine.
3. Scatter chopped fresh dill and a little raw onion if you like.
4. Eat cold as a side with grilled or roasted meats, with sausages, or with bread and dripping for an everyday Ukrainian breakfast.

## Notes
- **Real fermentation, no vinegar:** real Ukrainian sauerkraut is fermented by wild lactobacillus over 10-14 days. The salt creates conditions but doesn't acidify the cabbage; the bacteria do that. Adding vinegar at the start prevents fermentation and gives you raw pickled cabbage (a different dish, no probiotic benefit, no proper flavour).
- **2% salt is the magic number:** less salt and bad bacteria take over and the cabbage spoils; more salt and fermentation goes sluggish. Weigh the cabbage and calculate 2% of the weight in salt; this isn't a guess.
- **Submerge under brine:** the most common failure is cabbage sticking out of the brine and going mouldy. A weight on top is non-negotiable; check daily and press down if needed.
- **Ferment cool, not warm:** 16-20 C is the sweet spot. Hot kitchens (above 25 C) ferment too fast and produce off-flavours; cold rooms (below 14 C) ferment too slowly and the cabbage just sits around. A cool pantry or basement is ideal.
- **Skim kahm yeast if it appears:** the whitish film that sometimes forms on the surface during fermentation. Harmless but unpleasant; skim with a clean spoon and continue. Different from mould; mould is fuzzy and coloured (green, black, pink) and means you need to discard the batch.
- **Take taste tests:** the fermentation timing depends on temperature and the specific cabbage. After 10 days, start tasting a piece daily. You're looking for properly sour, tangy and a slight effervescence on the tongue. Stop when it tastes right.

## Variations
**Cabbage with apple:** add 1 tart eating apple (peeled and grated coarsely) to the cabbage and carrot; gives a slightly sweeter sauerkraut. Common in northern Ukraine.
**Cabbage with beetroot:** add 200 g of grated raw beetroot to the cabbage; the sauerkraut goes brilliant pink and slightly sweeter. Traditional in the Carpathian regions.
**Cabbage with cranberries:** add 100 g of fresh cranberries to the cabbage; gives a tart fruity counter to the sourness. A celebration variant.
**Spicy cabbage:** add 2 finely chopped fresh hot chillies to the cabbage; a non-traditional but popular spicy variant.

## Serving
Cold from the jar, drained slightly, dressed with a drizzle of sunflower oil and chopped fresh dill, alongside grilled meats, fried sausages, baked fish, or pelmeni. Warmed with bacon and onion as a side for roasted pork. Folded into bigos (the long-simmered hunter's stew) or kapuśniak (Polish-Ukrainian sauerkraut soup) through winter.

## Storage
- Keeps refrigerated 4-6 months in a sealed jar; the fermentation slows almost to a stop in the cold but continues very slowly. The sauerkraut gets progressively sourer over time.
- Don't freeze the live sauerkraut; freezing kills the lactobacillus and changes the texture.
- The brine itself is excellent in soups, salad dressings, or even drunk neat as a hangover cure (a Ukrainian tradition).
- Use a clean dry spoon every time you take some out (don't cross-contaminate; that shortens the shelf life).
