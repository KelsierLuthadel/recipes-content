# Indian

*A subcontinent in a spice tin: the world's deepest cuisine, layered over four thousand years and twenty-eight states. Start here.*

## A short history

Indian cooking didn't begin with curry. The Harappan kitchens of 2500 BCE along the Indus river already used cumin, coriander, ginger and turmeric, four of the five spices that still anchor the cuisine today. Archaeologists scraping the inside of Harappan cooking pots have found traces of all four, along with mustard seed and aubergine. The neighbouring Vedic tradition layered in ghee, yoghurt and the slow-cooked lentil pot, and by the time the Mauryans ruled from Pataliputra in the 3rd century BCE, the daily template of rice or flatbread, a dal, a vegetable and a chutney was already in place.

The medieval centuries opened the kitchen to outside hands. Persian and Central Asian traders moved up and down the Silk Road, bringing the long-grain rice tradition, dried fruit, nuts and the saffron that would later define Kashmiri cooking. From the 8th century onward, Arab spice merchants ran the pepper trade out of Kerala. Buddhist and Jain influence in central India deepened the vegetarian shelf, and the south Indian temple kitchens of Tamil Nadu and Karnataka codified the rice-and-sambar-and-rasam meal that still anchors a banana-leaf thali.

The Mughals arrived from Central Asia in the early 16th century and rebuilt the northern kitchen around courtly Persian technique. They brought the dum (sealed-pot) method that produces biryani, the almond-and-cream gravies behind korma and pasanda, the tandoor (clay oven) that defines naan and tandoori chicken, and the kebab tradition that runs from Lucknow to Hyderabad. Around the same time, Portuguese ships landing in Goa from 1498 emptied their cargo holds of New World produce: chillies, tomatoes, potatoes, peanuts, cashews and corn all arrived through Goa and rewrote the cuisine within a century. The vindaloo, with its vinegar-and-chilli marinade, is the most-cited Portuguese-Indian cross.

The British Raj (1858-1947) industrialised the kitchen in places and ignored it in others. Army cooks codified the pre-mixed curry powders that would later become the international shorthand for Indian food, the Anglo-Indian Madras Club kitchens invented mulligatawny, kedgeree and the chutney-on-everything trend, and the railway dining cars carried regional dishes across the subcontinent for the first time. The Bengal Famine of 1943 (three million dead under wartime British policy) and the violence of Partition in 1947 displaced tens of millions, and refugee cooks from West Punjab carried the tandoor and the butter-and-cream gravy of Lahore into Delhi, where Moti Mahal codified what the world now thinks of as North Indian restaurant food.

After Independence, the cuisine globalised through migration. The Indian diaspora in Britain (especially Bangladeshi cooks in the curry houses of Brick Lane and Birmingham) built the British-Indian Restaurant tradition with its base-gravy system and Madras / Vindaloo / Korma / Tikka Masala heat tiers. The Gulf, East Africa, South-East Asia and North America each grew their own diaspora kitchens. Inside India, the post-1991 liberalisation brought supermarkets, packaged spice blends and a chef-led restaurant scene that now reads the regional traditions back to the country as fine-dining.

What stayed constant across millennia is the principle of *masala*: a spice mix bloomed in hot fat at the start of cooking, used as the structural flavour-bed every dish is built on. Get the masala right and the rest of the recipe is just sequencing.

## Regional variations

India isn't one cuisine, it's at least six.

- **North Indian**, the cooking most non-Indians meet first. Wheat breads (naan, paratha, roti), dairy-rich gravies (butter chicken, paneer dishes), tandoor-grilled meats, garam masala as the closing flourish. Punjabi and Mughlai traditions sit here.
- **South Indian**, rice replaces wheat. Coconut, curry leaves, mustard seed, tamarind define the palate. Dosa, idli, sambar, rasam. Vegetarian by default in many homes. Quicker, lighter, brighter than the north.
- **Bengali**, eastern India and Bangladesh. Fish and mustard oil at the centre. Sweets reach a virtuoso peak: rasgulla, sandesh, mishti doi. Panch phoran (five-spice) is the signature temper.
- **Gujarati**, north-western, predominantly vegetarian. A slightly-sweet edge to most savoury dishes. Thalis are the everyday format. Dhokla, undhiyu, kadhi.
- **Goan**, Portuguese-influenced. Vinegar plays where elsewhere it doesn't. Vindaloo, sorpotel, fish caldine.
- **Hyderabadi / Andhra**, the Deccan plateau. Famously fiery; biryani is the signature. The mutton biryani of Hyderabad is one of the cuisine's defining dishes.

A dish that means one thing in Delhi can mean another in Chennai. Treat each regional tradition as its own cuisine and the variety stops feeling overwhelming.

## Signature ingredients

The kitchen runs on a small, repeating set of building blocks:

- **[Garam Masala](../../base-ingredients/curry-powder/garam-masala.md)**, the closing-stage warm-spice blend
- **[Panch Poran (Indian Five Spice)](../../base-ingredients/spices/panch-poran.md)**, Bengali tempering mix
- **[Madras Mix](../../base-ingredients/curry-powder/madras-mix.md)**, **[Vindaloo Mix](../../base-ingredients/curry-powder/vindaloo-mix.md)**, **[Korma Mix](../../base-ingredients/curry-powder/korma-mix.md)**, restaurant-style heat tiers
- **Cumin, coriander, turmeric, mustard seed**, the daily four
- **Ginger-garlic paste**, the aromatic base every curry starts with
- **Ghee**, clarified butter; the classic cooking fat for richer dishes
- **Coconut milk**, south Indian gravies
- **Tamarind, kokum, dried mango (amchur)**, the sourness toolkit
- **Curry leaves**, fragrance; fried in oil at the start of southern dishes

Stock these and most of the catalogue's recipes open up.

## Learn it in seven recipes

If you want to build the muscle for Indian cooking without trying to absorb the whole thing at once, work through these in order. Each one teaches one technique that compounds into the next.

1. [Tarka Dal](dal-makhani.md), a lentil stew with a tempered-spice finish. Teaches the *tarka* (spice-blooming) move every other recipe leans on.
2. [Garam Masala](../../base-ingredients/curry-powder/garam-masala.md), make the mix once and you stop reaching for a jar; reading the catalogue gets easier.
3. [Butter Chicken](butter-chicken.md), the famous one. Teaches yoghurt marination, the two-pan method (sauce and protein cooked separately, combined late), and how a tomato-cream gravy is built.
4. [Lamb Karahi](lamb-karahi.md), a fast braise in a wok-style pan. Teaches gravy reduction over high heat, and what happens when you actually let the oil split out.
5. [Saag Aloo](side-dishes/saag-aloo.md), a spinach-and-potato side. Teaches the southern-style mustard-seed-and-curry-leaf temper, plus how to wilt greens without making them grey.
6. [Bhindi Masala](side-dishes/bhindi-masala.md), okra braised in onion-tomato gravy. Teaches the slime-management technique okra needs (high heat, dry, no stirring until set).
7. [Hyderabadi Mutton Biryani](hyderabadi-mutton-biryani.md), the boss level. Layered rice, marinated mutton, dum cooking. Once you've made this you can make any rice dish.

After those seven, the rest of the catalogue is variations on themes you already know.
