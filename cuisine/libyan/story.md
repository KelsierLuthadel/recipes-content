# Libyan

*The North African couscous kitchen with a Mediterranean fish habit, and the most distinct soup tradition in the Maghreb: sharba libiya, lamb broth with orzo. Italian and Ottoman layers sit on a Berber-Arab base.*

## A short history

Libyan cooking sits between its Maghrebi neighbours (Tunisia, Algeria) and the Egyptian east, and the oldest layer in the pot is the Berber (Amazigh) one. The indigenous Amazigh peoples have farmed the coastal strip and herded across the Saharan interior for thousands of years; their cooking gave the country couscous (steamed semolina pearls, an Amazigh invention that travelled with them across all of North Africa), the slow tagine, the buried-fire bread of the Sahara baked directly in hot sand, and the date-and-barley shelf of the oases. Bazin, the dense barley-and-wheat dough cooked into a half-bread half-dumpling in lamb broth, is one of the oldest Amazigh dishes still cooked on a modern table, and its name and form predate every wave of conqueror that followed.

The classical Mediterranean reshaped the coast. Phoenicians from Tyre founded Tripoli (Oea, Sabratha, Leptis Magna) in the first millennium BCE and brought olive trees, vines, wheat and the salt-and-fish trade. The Romans turned Leptis Magna into one of the wealthiest cities in the empire under Emperor Septimius Severus, who was himself Libyan-born; Roman cooking left an olive-oil-and-bread tradition that lingers in modern Libyan home baking. The Arab Islamic conquest of the 7th century brought the spice cabinet, the meze table, preserved lemons, and the slow-stew-with-aromatics technique that runs through every modern Libyan main dish. The Banu Hilal migrations of the 11th century pushed Bedouin Arab tribes across Cyrenaica and Tripolitania and added the lamb-roasting, milk-curdling, dates-and-flour Bedouin shelf that still defines inland cooking.

The Columbian Exchange reached Libya through Ottoman ports in the 16th and 17th centuries. Tomato, chilli, potato and peppers arrived from the Americas via Istanbul, and by the 19th century tomato paste had become the base of nearly every Libyan stew. The Ottomans themselves (1551 to 1911) brought the meatball (kafta), the orzo soup (sharba libiya), the lahma bil ajeen meat-flatbread tradition, and a sweets shelf of syrup-soaked baklava, basbousa and maakroun that still defines a Libyan dessert table. Coffee, brought via Yemen and Egypt, became the universal hospitality drink in this period.

Italian colonisation (1911 to 1947) was a brutal political chapter but left a deep culinary footprint. Italian colonists planted pasta wheat across the coast, built pasta factories in Tripoli, and pushed rishta (like fettuccine), macarona imbakbaka (oven-baked pasta) and the Italian-style espresso onto the everyday table. The Tripoli café culture (small glass cups of bitter coffee, sweet pastries on the side) is a direct Italian inheritance. The expulsion of the Italian colonist community after independence in 1951 left the cooking behind on Libyan kitchen shelves.

The Gaddafi years (1969 to 2011) ran a state-controlled, often-restrictive economy that limited imported ingredients and kept the home cooking firmly within the traditional shelf. The pre-Gaddafi years had a busy restaurant culture in Tripoli and Benghazi; the long political turbulence since the 2011 uprising has kept the cuisine quiet abroad. Diaspora kitchens in Egypt, Tunisia, the UK and across Europe carry the tradition forward, and a quiet recovery of the Tripoli restaurant scene is underway when conditions allow. The kitchen reads as it has for centuries: lamb, couscous or bazin or pasta as the base, tomato-and-spice broth, mint tea and a date or three to close.

## Regional variations

- **Tripoli and the west**: closer to Tunisian cooking. More fish, more harissa-like chilli pastes.
- **Benghazi and the east (Cyrenaica)**: closer to Egyptian. More lamb, the bedouin tradition.
- **Fezzan (south, Sahara)**: Touareg cooking. Bread baked in sand, dates and millet.
- **Coast (Misrata, Sabratha)**: Mediterranean fish, Italian-influenced pasta dishes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Couscous**: steamed semolina pearls, the festive starch.
- **Rishta**: hand-cut pasta similar to fettuccine.
- **Bazin**: dense barley-and-wheat dough cooked into a half-bread half-dumpling in lamb broth.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Whole-roasted at celebration.
- **Orzo (or similar)**: in sharba libiya.
- **Tomato paste**: the base of most stews.
- **Mint**: fresh in tea, in salads, in some stews.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Bazin](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/bazin)**: dense barley-and-wheat dough cooked in lamb-and-tomato broth, served in the centre of a communal platter and eaten with the right hand. The national dish.
2. **[Sharba Libiya](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/sharba-libiya)**: lamb soup with orzo, tomato paste, chickpeas, mint and parsley. The Ramadan iftar soup, served year-round.
3. **[Couscous bil Osban](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/couscous-bil-osban)**: couscous with osban, a sausage of lamb organs, rice and spices stuffed into lamb intestine. The Eid al-Adha dish.
4. **[Mbattan](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/mubattan)**: potato slices stuffed with spiced minced lamb, dipped in egg-and-flour batter, deep-fried.
5. **[Rishta bil Hummus](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/rishta-bil-hummus)**: hand-cut pasta with chickpeas in a tomato-mint broth. The everyday comfort bowl.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with shahi (sweet black tea, often with mint and sometimes peanuts at the bottom of the small glass) or with strong Italian-style espresso (the legacy of Italian rule). End with maakroun (date-stuffed semolina cookies) or basbousa (semolina syrup cake).
