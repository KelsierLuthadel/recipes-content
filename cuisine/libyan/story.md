# Libyan

*The kitchen of North African couscous and Mediterranean fish, with the most distinct soup tradition in the Maghreb (sharba libiya — lamb soup with orzo). Italian and Ottoman layers on a Berber-Arab base.*

## A short history

Libyan cooking sits between its Maghrebi neighbours (Tunisia, Algeria) and the Egyptian east. The Berber (Amazigh) tradition is the oldest layer — couscous and slow-cooked tagines belong to it. The Arab Islamic conquest (7th century) brought the spice cabinet, the meze tradition and the use of preserved lemons. The Ottoman period (1551–1911) added meatballs (kafta), the orzo-based soups and certain pastries. The Italian colonisation (1911–1947) layered on pasta — rishta (similar to fettuccine), bazin (a dense bread-like dough cooked in broth — pre-Italian but the modern home cook often pairs Libyan dishes with Italian pasta), and a coffee tradition.

The pre-Gaddafi years saw a vibrant restaurant culture especially in Tripoli and Benghazi; the long political turbulence has limited the cuisine's international visibility. The Libyan diaspora kitchens in Egypt, Tunisia and across Europe carry the codified tradition.

## Regional variations

- **Tripoli and the west**: closer to Tunisian cooking. More fish, more harissa-like chilli pastes.
- **Benghazi and the east (Cyrenaica)**: closer to Egyptian. More lamb, the bedouin tradition.
- **Fezzan (south, Sahara)**: Touareg cooking. Bread baked in sand, dates and millet.
- **Coast (Misrata, Sabratha)**: Mediterranean fish, Italian-influenced pasta dishes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Couscous**: steamed semolina pearls, the festive starch.
- **Rishta**: hand-cut pasta similar to fettuccine.
- **Bazin**: dense barley-and-wheat dough cooked into a half-bread half-dumpling in lamb broth.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Whole-roasted at celebration.
- **Orzo (or similar)**: in sharba libiya.
- **Tomato paste**: the base of most stews.
- **Mint**: fresh in tea, in salads, in some stews.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Bazin](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/bazin)**: dense barley-and-wheat dough cooked in lamb-and-tomato broth, served in the centre of a communal platter, eaten with the right hand. The national dish.
2. **[Sharba Libiya](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/sharba-libiya)**: lamb soup with orzo, tomato paste, chickpeas, mint and parsley. The Ramadan iftar soup, served year-round.
3. **[Couscous bil Osban](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/couscous-bil-osban)**: couscous with osban (stuffed lamb intestine — a sausage made from lamb organs, rice and spices). The Eid al-Adha dish.
4. **[Mbattan](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/mubattan)**: potato slices stuffed with spiced minced lamb, dipped in egg-and-flour batter, deep-fried.
5. **[Rishta bil Hummus](/recipes/cuisine/libyan/rishta-bil-hummus)**: hand-cut pasta with chickpeas in a tomato-mint broth. The peasant comfort food.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with shahi (sweet black tea, often with mint and sometimes peanuts at the bottom of the small glass) or with strong Italian-style espresso (the legacy of Italian rule). End with maakroun (date-stuffed semolina cookies) or basbousa (semolina syrup cake).
