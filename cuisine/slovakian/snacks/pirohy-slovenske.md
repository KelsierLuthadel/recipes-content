# Pirohy slovenské

![Pirohy slovenské](resources/pirohy-slovenske.jpg)

*Slovak half-moon dumplings filled with mashed potato and bryndza, boiled, then finished with fried bacon and sour cream, the Saturday-lunch dumpling of the Tatra villages.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 45 minutes

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Slovak pirohy (the same family as Polish pierogi) are folded dumplings of soft wheat dough, sealed around a filling, boiled, then crowned with hot fat and sour cream. The signature Slovak filling is mashed potato and bryndza (the soft sheep's-milk cheese of the Liptov region); other regions stuff them with sauerkraut, mushrooms, plum jam or quark. The dough is rolled thin, cut into rounds, filled with a small spoon, and crimped along the edge into a half-moon. They are dropped into salted water like ravioli, lifted out, then finished in a pan with fried bacon, melted butter or browned onion. Sour cream goes through at the table.

## Ingredients

### Dough
- 400 g plain flour
- 1 large egg
- 1 tsp salt
- 150-180 ml warm water

### Filling
- 400 g floury potatoes, peeled and chopped
- 150 g bryndza (or soft feta mashed with 1 tbsp sour cream)
- 1/2 tsp salt
- A grind of black pepper

### To finish
- 200 g smoked streaky bacon, cut into 1 cm cubes
- 1 small onion, finely chopped
- 200 g sour cream
- Chopped chives, to serve

## Method

### Stage 1 - The dough
1. Tip the flour onto a board with the salt; make a well.
2. Crack the egg in; add warm water gradually, working the flour in with your hand until you have a soft pliable dough.
3. Knead 8 minutes until smooth and elastic. Wrap and rest 30 minutes.

### Stage 2 - The filling
1. Boil the potatoes in salted water until tender, 15 minutes; drain and mash smooth.
2. Mix in the bryndza while still warm, plus salt and pepper.
3. Cool to room temperature.

### Stage 3 - Shape
1. Roll the dough out 2 mm thick on a floured surface, in 2 batches.
2. Cut rounds with a 7 cm cutter or a glass.
3. Drop a teaspoon of filling onto each round; fold over into a half-moon, press the edges firmly, then crimp with a fork or your fingers.
4. Set on a lightly floured tray, not touching.

### Stage 4 - Boil
1. Bring a wide pan of salted water to a gentle boil (a rolling boil tears dumplings).
2. Drop pirohy in batches of 8-10; they sink, then rise to the surface in 3-4 minutes.
3. Lift out with a slotted spoon onto a warm plate.

### Stage 5 - Finish
1. While the pirohy boil, fry the bacon and chopped onion in a wide pan until the bacon is crisp and the onion gold.
2. Tip the boiled pirohy into the bacon pan; toss gently to coat in the hot fat.
3. Spoon onto plates; top with sour cream and chopped chives.

## Notes
- **Seal the edges hard.** A loose seal opens in the pot and you lose the filling.
- **A gentle boil, not a rolling one.** Hard-boiling tears the dough; the water should just shiver.
- **Bryndza is the Slovak signature.** Polish pierogi use farmer's cheese; the Slovak version uses sheep-milk bryndza, which gives a tangier, saltier note.
- **Make ahead.** Shape and freeze on a tray; cook from frozen, adding 2 minutes to the boil.

## Variations
- **Sauerkraut filling.** Mix sauerkraut with fried onion and a spoon of breadcrumbs for the sour-cabbage version.
- **Mushroom filling.** Dried wild mushrooms, soaked, chopped and fried with onion.
- **Plum jam filling.** Spoon a small ball of plum jam in each; a sweet dessert version with butter and ground walnuts.
- **Quark filling.** Sweetened tvaroh with raisins, eaten with sour cream and sugar.
- **Sheep-shepherd style.** Skip the bacon; melt butter, brown an onion, pour over with bryndza on top.

## Serving
Serve hot · sour cream and chives at the table · alongside a glass of cold milk for the kids, or a pilsner for the adults · or as a sit-down lunch with a vinegar cucumber salad.

## Storage
- Eat fresh, straight from the pan.
- Cooked pirohy keep 2 days refrigerated; reheat by pan-frying in butter (do not microwave; the dough turns rubbery).
- Raw shaped pirohy freeze 2 months on a tray; cook from frozen.
