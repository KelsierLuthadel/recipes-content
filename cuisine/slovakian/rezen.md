# Rezeň

![Rezeň](resources/rezen.jpg)

*Slovakia's everyday Wiener-schnitzel cousin: pounded-thin pork or chicken dredged in flour, egg and breadcrumbs, fried in lard until golden and crisp at the edges.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Rezeň is the Slovak schnitzel, eaten at school cafeterias, family Sunday lunches and roadside hostincas across the country. The Austrian Wiener schnitzel uses veal; the Slovak workaday version uses pork loin (bravčový rezeň) or chicken breast (kurací rezeň), pounded thin, then dredged in flour, beaten egg, and dry breadcrumbs before frying in shallow lard or oil. The breadcrumbs must puff and crisp into a textured shell that lifts away from the meat in pockets, which means generous fat and high heat. The plate is finished with a lemon wedge, boiled new potatoes with parsley butter, and a vinegar-dressed cucumber salad alongside.

## Ingredients

- 4 pork loin steaks (about 150 g each), or 4 chicken breasts butterflied
- 100 g plain flour
- 2 large eggs, beaten with 1 tbsp milk
- 150 g dry breadcrumbs (panko or fresh white)
- 1 tsp salt
- 1/2 tsp white pepper
- 200 g lard, or 150 ml sunflower oil for frying
- 1 lemon, cut into wedges, to serve

## Method

### Stage 1 - Pound the meat
1. Lay each pork steak (or butterflied chicken breast) between two sheets of cling film or baking parchment.
2. Pound with a meat mallet or the flat of a heavy pan until 5 mm thick and roughly the size of your hand.
3. Season both sides with salt and white pepper.

### Stage 2 - The breading line
1. Set up three shallow plates: one with flour, one with the beaten egg, one with breadcrumbs.
2. Dredge each piece first in flour (tap off the excess), then in egg (let drip), then in breadcrumbs (press lightly so they stick but do not pack tight).
3. Set on a tray; do not stack.

### Stage 3 - Fry
1. Heat the lard or oil in a wide heavy pan to 170 C; the fat should be 1 cm deep and shimmer.
2. Lay a schnitzel in carefully; the breadcrumbs should bubble vigorously the second they hit the fat.
3. Fry 2-3 minutes per side, basting now and then by tilting the pan and spooning hot fat over the top. The crust should puff and turn deep gold.
4. Lift onto kitchen paper to drain; salt lightly.
5. Repeat with the remaining pieces, keeping the pan at temperature.

### Stage 4 - Serve
1. Serve at once, while crisp, with a lemon wedge on the plate.

## Notes
- **Generous fat is the trick.** Schnitzel is shallow-fried, not pan-fried; the fat needs to come halfway up the side of the meat so the crust puffs and lifts away from the surface.
- **Lard tastes more like the Slovak original.** Sunflower oil is a fine substitute; do not use olive oil (the flavour is wrong and the smoke point too low).
- **Do not press the breadcrumbs.** A loose breadcrumb coat puffs into pockets and shatters under the knife; a tightly packed one goes flat and dense.
- **Eat at once.** Schnitzel goes soggy fast; this is a cook-and-serve plate.

## Variations
- **Sýrový rezeň.** Tuck a thin slice of Edam or Emmental between two pounded pieces of pork before breading for a Slovak cordon-bleu-style version.
- **Kurací rezeň.** Use chicken breast butterflied open and pounded thin; cooks slightly faster.
- **Smažený sýr.** Use a thick slab of Edam in place of meat; the snack-bar classic (see vyprážaný syr).
- **With ham.** A ham-and-cheese stuffed version (cordon bleu) sits at every Slovak family lunch.
- **Wild mushroom sauce.** Skip the lemon and serve with a creamy mushroom sauce, the autumn version.

## Serving
Serve hot · lemon wedge · boiled new potatoes with parsley butter · uhorkový šalát (cucumber-vinegar salad) · or a spoon of tartar sauce on the side.

## Storage
- Eat the day cooked; the crust softens quickly.
- Reheat in a hot oven (200 C) for 8 minutes to re-crisp; do not microwave.
- Pre-breaded raw schnitzels keep 24 hours in the fridge before frying.
