# Uhorkový šalát

![Uhorkový šalát](resources/uhorkovy-salat.jpg)

*The bright sharp Slovak cucumber salad: thinly sliced cucumber, white-wine vinegar, sugar, salt, dill and a finishing pour of sour cream, the side that lives on every schnitzel plate.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes

**Cook Time:** 0 minutes (plus 30 minutes resting)

## Overview
Uhorkový šalát is the salad of Slovak schnitzel lunches, summer cookouts and the everyday family dinner. Cucumber is sliced as thin as a coin, salted to draw out the water, then dressed with white-wine vinegar, sugar and dill. A spoon of sour cream goes through at the end for the creamy version (or skip the cream for the sharper one). The dressing should be balanced (slightly tart, slightly sweet, briny), and the cucumber should stay crunchy in the bowl. Eat cold straight from the fridge alongside rezeň or roast pork. It works as a relish, a side and a refreshing course all at once.

## Ingredients

- 2 large cucumbers (about 800 g total)
- 1 tsp fine salt, plus more to taste
- 3 tbsp white-wine vinegar
- 2 tsp caster sugar
- 1 small garlic clove, crushed to a paste (optional)
- 1/4 tsp black pepper
- 3 tbsp finely chopped fresh dill
- 100 g sour cream (optional, for the creamy version)
- A pinch of sweet paprika, to finish

## Method

### Stage 1 - Salt the cucumber
1. Wash the cucumbers; peel if the skin is bitter, otherwise leave on for colour.
2. Slice as thin as possible on a mandoline (1-2 mm), or with a sharp knife if you must.
3. Layer in a bowl with the fine salt; toss and leave 20-30 minutes.

### Stage 2 - Drain
1. Squeeze the salted cucumber gently in handfuls over the sink (do not pulverise; you want them limp but still crunchy).
2. Transfer to a dry bowl.

### Stage 3 - Dress
1. Whisk the vinegar, sugar, garlic if using, and pepper in a small jug until the sugar dissolves.
2. Pour over the cucumber; add the dill and toss.
3. For the creamy version, stir in the sour cream now.
4. Taste; adjust vinegar, sugar and salt.

### Stage 4 - Rest and serve
1. Refrigerate 20 minutes for the flavours to settle.
2. Spoon into a serving bowl; dust the top lightly with sweet paprika.

## Notes
- **Salt first, drain hard.** Salting draws the water out; skip this and the dressing turns to soup in the bowl.
- **Slice thin.** A mandoline gives you the right see-through slice; a thick knife cut will not absorb the dressing the same way.
- **Sweet-sour balance.** Slovak vinegar salads are slightly sweeter than the Polish or Czech equivalents; the sugar is not a mistake.
- **Dill, not parsley.** Dill is the herb that defines this salad; parsley does not work in its place.

## Variations
- **Sharp version.** Skip the sour cream entirely for a thin vinegar-and-dill brine; reduce sugar slightly.
- **With red onion.** Add 1/2 small red onion sliced paper-thin for a sharper bite.
- **Cucumber and tomato.** Add a sliced ripe tomato; the summer plate.
- **Yoghurt instead of sour cream.** Lighter, fresher, the modern home cook's version.
- **With horseradish.** A small grating of fresh horseradish in the dressing for the eastern Slovak version.

## Serving
Serve cold · alongside rezeň, slovak goulash or a roast · with hot boiled new potatoes · or as part of a cold cuts platter with rye bread.

## Storage
- The creamed version eats best within a few hours; the cream loosens overnight.
- The sharp version (no cream) keeps 3 days refrigerated and improves on day two.
- Do not freeze; the texture collapses.
