# Lebanese

*The most refined Levantine table. Mezze, lamb-and-bulgur, charcoal grills, olive-oil pickles and pomegranate; a cuisine that has been feeding mountain villages and Beirut restaurants from the same shelf for two thousand years.*

## A short history

Lebanese cooking is the Levantine tradition at its most codified. The basic ingredient list — wheat (as bulgur, freekeh, bread), olive oil, lamb, lemon, garlic, sumac, mint, parsley, chickpeas, sesame (tahini), tomato — has been in continuous use since Phoenician times. The Roman, Byzantine, Crusader, Mamluk and Ottoman periods each added a layer (the Ottoman especially — much of what we call Lebanese cuisine is a Levantine variant of imperial-Ottoman cooking).

The modern codification happened in the late 19th and early 20th centuries when Lebanese émigrés to the Gulf, West Africa and the Americas needed to make their food legible to outsiders. Restaurants in Beirut, Aleppo and Damascus standardised the mezze format — the dozen small plates that open every Lebanese meal. The civil war (1975–1990) scattered the Lebanese diaspora further; Beirut's food scene has been rebuilding through bombing campaigns and economic collapse ever since but remains one of the world's most ambitious culinary cities.

The cuisine sits between a household tradition (lamb, kibbeh, tabbouleh, rice and the matrouche bread) and the restaurant codification of mezze with hummus, baba ghanoush and tabbouleh in every Western Lebanese restaurant. The diaspora carried Lebanese food worldwide and it remains the most-recognisable Middle Eastern cuisine in Europe and the Americas.

## Regional variations

- **Beirut and the coast**: the cosmopolitan shelf. Seafood, the most refined mezze tradition, French and Ottoman pastry influence.
- **The mountains (Chouf, Metn, Keserwan)**: hearty cold-weather food. Kibbeh nayyeh (raw kibbeh), lamb stews, the maamoul (date or pistachio shortbread) tradition.
- **Bekaa Valley**: agriculture country. Freekeh wheat, lamb, the Chtaura yoghurt and kashk.
- **Tripoli and the north**: closer to Syrian cooking. The sweet tradition is the strongest in the country (knefe, halewet el-jibn).
- **South (Tyre, Sidon)**: Shia traditions, more fish, more bitter green vegetables (hindbeh).

## Signature ingredients

- **Olive oil**: heavy, used liberally. The fat of Lebanese cooking.
- **Lemon**: the acid of choice. In every salad, on every grill.
- **Sumac**: dried sour red berry, powdered. The signature Lebanese spice, on every fattoush and every grilled chicken.
- **Za'atar**: thyme, sumac, sesame seed, salt. The morning bread topping and the spice-rub for grilled meats.
- **Tahini**: ground sesame paste. The base of hummus, baba ghanoush, the tahini sauce on shawarma.
- **Mint and parsley**: heavy use, fresh. Parsley is the herb in tabbouleh (which is a parsley salad, not a bulgur salad — the bulgur is the supporting cast).
- **Pomegranate molasses**: thick, sour-sweet. The finishing flourish on muhammara and fattoush.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the mezze table, the kibbeh tradition, the grilled-skewer shelf and the stuffed-vine-leaf canon. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Kibbeh](/recipes/cuisine/lebanese/kibbeh)**: bulgur-and-lamb torpedo croquettes stuffed with spiced ground lamb and pine nuts, deep-fried. The national dish and the most complex piece of work in the Lebanese kitchen.
2. **[Kafta Mishweeyeh](/recipes/cuisine/lebanese/kafta-mishweeyeh)**: spiced minced lamb on flat skewers, grilled over charcoal. The benchmark Lebanese grill.
3. **[Warak Enab](/recipes/cuisine/lebanese/warak-enab)**: vine leaves stuffed with rice, parsley, mint and (sometimes) lamb, rolled tight, simmered with lemon and olive oil. The signature dolma.
4. **[Riz a Djej](/recipes/cuisine/lebanese/riz-a-djej)**: chicken-and-rice with spiced minced lamb and toasted nuts. The Sunday family dish in every Lebanese household.
5. **[Kofta Burger](/recipes/cuisine/lebanese/kofta-burger)**: spiced lamb kofta shaped as a patty in a bun with tahini sauce, pickled turnip and tomato. The Lebanese take on the burger.
6. **[Mahalabia](/recipes/cuisine/lebanese/mahalabia)**: milk pudding scented with rose water and orange blossom, topped with pistachios. The lightest of Levantine desserts.
7. **A grain-and-pulse mezze**: build a hummus, a tabbouleh and a baba ghanoush plate — these three from the broader catalogue are the proper opener to any Lebanese meal.

Cook the kibbeh and the kafta and you have the lamb shelf. The warak enab and the riz a djej are the home table. The kofta burger is the casual; the mahalabia is the dessert; the mezze plate is how every meal starts.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the wider mezze world (hummus, baba ghanoush, muhammara, labneh, fattoush, tabbouleh — many in shared base-ingredients folders), the shawarma tradition, the stews (mloukhieh, fasolia), and the dessert canon (knefe, baklava, maamoul). Lebanese food rewards a long table; build the mezze first and the rest follows.
