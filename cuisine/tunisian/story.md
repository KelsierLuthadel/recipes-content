# Tunisian

*The North African kitchen with the hottest hand on chilli in the Maghreb. Harissa as the table condiment, the brik (fried egg-and-tuna pastry), the ojja shakshuka-style egg-and-merguez breakfast, and the country's signature couscous variations.*

## A short history

Tunisian cooking sits between Algerian, Libyan, Sicilian Italian and Levantine traditions. The Berber (Amazigh) base — couscous, bread, slow-cooked tagines — is the oldest layer. The Phoenician (Carthaginian) period left olive oil and fish-curing traditions. The Roman period embedded the wheat-and-olive culture. The Arab Islamic conquest (7th century) layered on spices and the lamb-rice tradition. The Ottoman period (1574–1881) added the meze table and shakshuka. The French colonisation (1881–1956) brought baguettes, croissants, and a lasting Mediterranean refinement.

Harissa — the smoky red chilli paste with garlic, caraway, coriander and olive oil — is Tunisia's national condiment. UNESCO inscribed it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2022. Every Tunisian household has a jar; it goes onto every bread, soup and stew. The country eats more chilli than its Maghrebi neighbours by a significant margin.

The Sicilian-Italian influence is deep too — Tunisia's south is the closest African landmass to Sicily, and the centuries of Sicilian-Tunisian crossover gave the country its pasta tradition, certain seafood techniques, and the famous Sicilian-Tunisian harissa pasta.

## Regional variations

- **Tunis and the north**: codified mainstream. Brik, ojja, the urban dining tradition.
- **Sfax (east coast)**: famous for seafood, harissa production, the Jewish-Tunisian baking tradition.
- **Djerba (south-eastern island)**: distinct cuisine. The Jewish-Tunisian community's preserved kitchen.
- **South (Tatouine, Douz)**: Bedouin and Berber. More slow-cooked lamb, more bread baked in sand.

## Signature ingredients

- **Harissa**: smoky red chilli paste. The national condiment.
- **Tabil**: Tunisian spice blend (caraway, coriander, chilli, garlic).
- **Olive oil**: heavy use; Tunisian olive oil exports are among the world's largest.
- **Tomato paste (kunserva)**: in nearly every dish.
- **Preserved lemons**: in tagines.
- **Tuna (often canned)**: in brik, in ojja.
- **Merguez**: spicy lamb sausage.
- **Mint**: in tea, in salads.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Brik à l'Œuf](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/brik-a-l-oeuf)**: thin malsouka pastry folded around a whole egg, tuna, capers and parsley, deep-fried — the egg yolk should still run when cut.
2. **[Ojja Merguez](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/ojja-merguez)**: spicy tomato-and-pepper stew with merguez sausage and eggs poached on top. The Tunisian shakshuka.
3. **[Lablabi](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/lablabi)**: chickpea soup with stale bread, garlic, cumin, harissa, lemon and a soft-boiled egg on top. The Tunisian working-breakfast classic.
4. **[Slata Mechouia](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/slata-mechouia)**: grilled-vegetable salad (peppers, tomato, garlic, onion) chopped fine, dressed with olive oil and lemon. The meze starter.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with mint tea (Tunisian version, poured high from the pot for foam) or with Celtia (the national lager) or with boukha (the Tunisian-Jewish fig spirit). End with makroud (date-stuffed semolina) or with bambalouni (deep-fried syrup-glazed doughnuts).
