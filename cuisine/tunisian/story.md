# Tunisian

*The North African kitchen with the hottest hand on chilli in the Maghreb. Harissa as the table condiment, the brik (fried egg-and-tuna pastry), the ojja shakshuka-style egg-and-merguez breakfast, and the country's signature couscous variations.*

## A short history

Tunisian cooking sits between Algerian, Libyan, Sicilian Italian and Levantine traditions, and the layering shows up in almost every dish on the table. The oldest layer is Berber (Amazigh): the indigenous North African farmers who have been milling hard wheat into semolina, steaming it into couscous over a perforated clay pot, baking flatbreads in stone ovens, and slow-cooking lamb and root vegetables in earthenware tagines for at least three thousand years. The Berber kitchen lives on in every couscous, in the dried-meat *gueddid*, in the harissa-spiced soup that breaks the Ramadan fast, and in the bread-baked-in-sand traditions of the south.

The Phoenicians founded Carthage in 814 BCE and turned Tunisia into the western Mediterranean's commercial hub. They planted the first olive groves, introduced the salt-curing techniques that turned the Gulf of Tunis tuna run into a major industry, and brought wine cultivation along with the alphabet. Roman Carthage, after the third Punic War in 146 BCE, embedded the wheat-and-olive culture so deeply that Tunisia became the breadbasket of imperial Rome, with whole shipping fleets dedicated to moving grain to the capital. The mosaics of Roman villas at Bardo and El Jem still show the dining habits of the period: olives, fish, fruit, game, the same shopping list a Tunis market puts together today.

The 7th-century Arab Islamic conquest layered on spices and the lamb-rice tradition, and Kairouan (founded 670 CE) became one of the great cities of the medieval Islamic Mediterranean, with sophisticated cookery manuscripts circulating through its libraries. Andalusi refugees fleeing the Spanish Reconquista in the 15th and 17th centuries brought citrus, rosewater, almond pastries and the sweet-savoury combinations of Moorish kitchens. The Ottomans (1574 to 1881) added the meze table, shakshuka in its original form, layered savoury pastries, and the spiced ground-meat stuffings that turn up in dolma. The Columbian Exchange landed in this period too: tomatoes, peppers, potatoes and (critically) chillies all arrived through Mediterranean trade in the 16th and 17th centuries. The chilli was the transformative one. Tunisian cooks took to it more than any of their Maghrebi neighbours, and harissa, the country's defining condiment, exists because of it.

Harissa is Tunisia's national condiment: smoky red chilli paste with garlic, caraway, coriander and olive oil. UNESCO inscribed it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2022. Every Tunisian household keeps a jar; it goes onto every bread, soup and stew. The country eats more chilli than its Maghrebi neighbours by a wide margin, a habit that took root in the 19th century and has only intensified since.

The French protectorate (1881 to 1956) brought baguettes, croissants, formal sit-down restaurants and the European pastry tradition, and Tunisian bakeries still produce some of the best French-style breads in North Africa. The Sicilian-Italian thread runs deep too: Tunisia's south is the closest African landmass to Sicily, and centuries of crossover gave the country its pasta tradition, certain seafood techniques, and the famous Sicilian-Tunisian harissa pasta. Independence in 1956, the slow secularisation under Bourguiba, and the post-2011 democratic transition have all kept the kitchen recognisably the same. The everyday Tunisian table still runs on bread, harissa, olive oil, tomato, tuna, eggs and lamb, with mint tea poured high from the pot to finish. The Jewish-Tunisian community, large until the post-1948 emigration to France and Israel, left behind dishes like brik, the lamb-and-egg pastilla, and the famous Sabbath stew *t'fina pkaila*.

## Regional variations

- **Tunis and the north**: the codified mainstream. Brik, ojja, the urban dining tradition.
- **Sfax (east coast)**: famous for seafood, harissa production, the Jewish-Tunisian baking tradition.
- **Djerba (south-eastern island)**: distinct cuisine. The Jewish-Tunisian community's preserved kitchen.
- **South (Tatouine, Douz)**: Bedouin and Berber. More slow-cooked lamb, more bread baked in sand.

## Signature ingredients

- **Harissa**: smoky red chilli paste. The national condiment.
- **Tabil**: Tunisian spice blend (caraway, coriander, chilli, garlic).
- **Olive oil**: heavy use; Tunisian olive oil exports are among the world's largest.
- **Tomato paste (kunserva)**: in nearly every dish.
- **Preserved lemons**: in tagines.
- **Tuna (often canned)**: in brik, in ojja.
- **Merguez**: spicy lamb sausage.
- **Mint**: in tea, in salads.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Brik à l'Œuf](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/brik-a-l-oeuf)**: thin malsouka pastry folded around a whole egg, tuna, capers and parsley, deep-fried. The yolk should still run when you cut it.
2. **[Ojja Merguez](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/ojja-merguez)**: spicy tomato-and-pepper stew with merguez sausage and eggs poached on top. The Tunisian shakshuka.
3. **[Lablabi](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/lablabi)**: chickpea soup with stale bread, garlic, cumin, harissa, lemon and a soft-boiled egg on top. The working-breakfast classic.
4. **[Slata Mechouia](/recipes/cuisine/tunisian/slata-mechouia)**: grilled-vegetable salad (peppers, tomato, garlic, onion) chopped fine, dressed with olive oil and lemon. The meze starter.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with mint tea (Tunisian version, poured high from the pot for foam), with Celtia (the national lager) or with boukha (the Tunisian-Jewish fig spirit). End with makroud (date-stuffed semolina) or with bambalouni (deep-fried syrup-glazed doughnuts).
