# Uyghur

*The Turkic-Muslim kitchen of East Turkestan (Xinjiang). Hand-pulled noodles (laghman), the giant communal plate of chicken-and-potato (dapanji), pilaf (polo), lamb kebabs over open coals — the food of the old Silk Road oases, halfway between Central Asia and China.*

## A short history

Uyghur cooking is the cuisine of a Turkic-Muslim people whose homeland in East Turkestan (now governed as the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region of China) sat at the crossroads of the Silk Road. The food reflects this — pilaf (polo) shared with Central Asian neighbours (Uzbek, Kazakh), hand-pulled noodles shared with Han Chinese, the kebab tradition shared with the broader Turkic world, the lamb-and-cumin profile distinctly Uyghur.

The Hui Muslim and Han Chinese populations layered on stir-fry and dumpling techniques; the Uyghur kitchen absorbed some Chinese influences but kept its identity firmly Turkic and Islamic. Halal pork-free cooking, the central role of lamb, the bread (nan, similar to South Asian naan but cooked in tandoor-style ovens), and the kebab grills at every street corner mark a cuisine distinct from Han Chinese cooking despite geographic overlap.

The cuisine has spread internationally through the Uyghur diaspora — restaurants in Istanbul, Munich, Toronto and Washington carry the codified tradition. Inside Xinjiang, the cultural-political situation is fraught; outside it, Uyghur food is one of the most exciting Central Asian cuisines available.

## Regional variations

- **Kashgar and southern oases**: the codified mainstream. Polo, laghman, dapanji.
- **Turpan and the Tarim Basin**: lamb-and-fruit traditions. Apricot and raisin in cooking.
- **Urumchi and the north (more Han-influenced)**: dapanji originated here; more crossover with Chinese cuisine.
- **Hotan (south-east)**: distinct sweet-bread traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb**: every part. Grilled, stewed, in polo, in laghman.
- **Hand-pulled wheat noodles**: laghman, the universal noodle.
- **Cumin**: heavy use. The signature spice on lamb.
- **Chilli**: dried red, fresh green.
- **Nan bread**: cooked in tandoor-style ovens; with every meal.
- **Raisins and dried apricots**: in polo, in some sweets.
- **Onion, garlic, tomato, pepper**: the everyday vegetable base.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Laghman](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/laghman)**: hand-pulled wheat noodles with lamb, peppers, tomato, onion and garlic. Served as a stir-fry (sai laghman) or with broth.
2. **[Lamb Pilaf (Polo)](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/lamb-pilaf)**: rice cooked with lamb, carrots, onion, raisins and cumin. The Friday-and-celebration plate.
3. **[Big Plate Chicken (Dapanji)](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/big-plate-chicken)**: massive communal plate of chicken slow-cooked with potato, pepper, onion and chilli, served on top of hand-pulled noodles (the diners share off the same dish).
4. **[Pan Kabap](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/pan-kabap)**: skewered lamb pieces marinated in cumin, salt and chilli, charcoal-grilled. The street-food standard.
5. **[Lamb Ribs](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/lamb-ribs)**: lamb ribs marinated and grilled over coals, dusted with cumin and chilli flakes.
6. **[Kebab Burger](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/kebab-burger)**: the diaspora invention — kebab spiced lamb on a Uyghur-style bun.
7. **[Suykash](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/suykash)**: hand-cut noodle soup with lamb and vegetables.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with strong black tea (chai) and nan bread (no Uyghur meal goes without it). Build a Uyghur table around the polo or dapanji at the centre, lamb kebabs as starter, nan in a basket, fresh herbs and pickled radish on the side.
