# Uyghur

*The Turkic-Muslim kitchen of East Turkestan (Xinjiang). Hand-pulled noodles (laghman), the giant communal plate of chicken-and-potato (dapanji), pilaf (polo), and lamb kebabs over open coals. The food of the old Silk Road oases, halfway between Central Asia and China.*

## A short history

Uyghur cooking belongs to a Turkic-Muslim people whose homeland in East Turkestan (now governed as the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region of China) sat at the crossroads of the Silk Road for nearly two thousand years. The earliest layer is pre-Turkic. The Indo-European Tocharian peoples who farmed the Tarim Basin oases from at least the 2nd millennium BCE grew wheat, barley and millet on glacier-fed irrigation channels, raised sheep on the surrounding grasslands, and baked flatbreads in clay tandoor-style ovens (*tonur* in modern Uyghur) that are still the bread oven of every Uyghur household. The astonishingly preserved mummies of Loulan and Cherchen, buried with wheat cakes and braids of garlic, show the basic kitchen already in place. Buddhist trade through the same oases in the first millennium added Indian and Persian flavours, especially the saffron, cumin and dried fruits that still define the cuisine.

The Turkic Uyghurs arrived in the Tarim Basin from the Mongolian steppe in the 9th century, after the collapse of their first khaganate, and gradually absorbed the older oasis kitchen while bringing their own herding tradition: lamb at the centre, fermented mare's milk, the dumpling and meat-pie technology of the steppe. Conversion to Islam beginning in the 10th century under the Kara-Khanid Khanate locked in the halal pork-free framework that still defines Uyghur cooking against Han Chinese tradition. The Karakhanid scholar Mahmud al-Kashgari's 11th-century dictionary records dozens of food terms that still survive in modern Uyghur kitchens.

The Mongol invasions of the 13th century and the Timurid centuries that followed strengthened the connection west to Samarkand, Bukhara and the wider Persianate Central Asian world. Polo (pilaf, *plov* in Uzbek, *osh* in Tajik) is essentially the same dish across all three cuisines, refined slightly differently in each. Uyghur polo leans heavier on raisins, dried apricots, and the carrot-and-lamb-fat combination. Hand-pulled wheat noodles (laghman) arrived through the long contact with Chinese cooks along the Hexi Corridor: the noodle technique is recognisably Chinese, but Uyghur cooks pair it with a lamb-and-tomato sauce closer to Persian than to anything in Han cooking.

The Qing Empire conquered Xinjiang in the 1750s and ran the region as a frontier province until the 1860s Dungan revolt. The Hui Muslim and Han Chinese cooks who moved in alongside Qing administration layered on stir-fry and dumpling technique, and the Uyghur kitchen took what it wanted while keeping its identity firmly Turkic and Islamic. The Columbian Exchange landed here too, mostly through Russian and Central Asian trade routes: tomatoes, peppers, potatoes and chillies all entered the Uyghur kitchen between the 17th and 19th centuries and became fundamental. Dapanji, the giant communal plate of chicken slow-cooked with potato, pepper, onion and chilli served on top of hand-pulled noodles, is actually a 20th-century invention from Urumchi (often dated to the 1980s) that combined Sichuan-style chilli cooking with Uyghur noodles and is now the celebration dish across the region.

The cuisine has spread through the Uyghur diaspora since the 1950s: restaurants in Istanbul, Munich, Toronto, Sydney and Washington carry the codified tradition. Inside Xinjiang the cultural and political situation is fraught, with mass detention and forced cultural assimilation since 2017 disrupting the everyday food landscape and silencing many of the cooks who held the traditions. Outside it, Uyghur food has become one of the most exciting and politically charged Central Asian cuisines available, and the diaspora has worked hard to keep the lamb-and-cumin-and-tandoor-bread signature alive on plates from Almaty to Vienna to Virginia.

## Regional variations

- **Kashgar and the southern oases**: the codified mainstream. Polo, laghman, dapanji.
- **Turpan and the Tarim Basin**: lamb-and-fruit traditions. Apricot and raisin in the cooking.
- **Urumchi and the north**: more Han-influenced; dapanji originated here.
- **Hotan (south-east)**: distinct sweet-bread traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb**: every part. Grilled, stewed, in polo, in laghman.
- **Hand-pulled wheat noodles**: laghman, the universal noodle.
- **Cumin**: heavy use. The signature spice on lamb.
- **Chilli**: dried red, fresh green.
- **Nan bread**: baked in tandoor-style ovens; on the table at every meal.
- **Raisins and dried apricots**: in polo, in some sweets.
- **Onion, garlic, tomato, pepper**: the everyday vegetable base.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Laghman](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/laghman)**: hand-pulled wheat noodles with lamb, peppers, tomato, onion and garlic. Served as a stir-fry (sai laghman) or with broth.
2. **[Lamb Pilaf (Polo)](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/lamb-pilaf)**: rice cooked with lamb, carrots, onion, raisins and cumin. The Friday and celebration plate.
3. **[Big Plate Chicken (Dapanji)](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/big-plate-chicken)**: massive communal plate of chicken slow-cooked with potato, pepper, onion and chilli, served on top of hand-pulled noodles. Everyone shares off the same dish.
4. **[Pan Kabap](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/pan-kabap)**: skewered lamb pieces marinated in cumin, salt and chilli, grilled over charcoal. The street-food standard.
5. **[Lamb Ribs](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/lamb-ribs)**: lamb ribs marinated and grilled over coals, dusted with cumin and chilli flakes.
6. **[Kebab Burger](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/kebab-burger)**: the diaspora invention. Kebab-spiced lamb on a Uyghur-style bun.
7. **[Suykash](/recipes/cuisine/uyghur/suykash)**: hand-cut noodle soup with lamb and vegetables.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with strong black tea (chai) and nan bread (no Uyghur meal goes without it). Build a Uyghur table around the polo or dapanji at the centre, lamb kebabs as a starter, nan in a basket, fresh herbs and pickled radish on the side.
