# Bosnian

*The kitchen of the western Balkans. Ćevapi capital (the country claims the dish's origin against Serbian counter-claims), the slow-baked clay-pot stews, the Bosnian pita (the layered savoury phyllo pie), and an Ottoman-Slavic synthesis that produced the most distinct Balkan kitchen.*

## A short history

Bosnian cooking sits at the intersection of Ottoman and Slavic traditions, with a depth of Ottoman influence stronger than its Croatian and Serbian neighbours (Bosnia was under Ottoman rule from 1463 to 1878, longer than the surrounding countries). The cuisine reflects this — the meze tradition, the use of yoghurt as a finishing pour, the kebab grills, the layered phyllo pastry pies (pita is the Bosnian word for what Serbians call burek), and the cloud-soft kajmak.

Sarajevo's old town (Baščaršija) is one of the great Balkan food districts, with hundreds of small ćevap-grills (ćevabdžinica) and burek-shops (buregdžinica). The country's cuisine is the most Muslim-coded in the former Yugoslavia (about half of Bosnians are Muslim by tradition), with halal practices respected at most restaurants.

The Bosnian Sarajevo and the slow-baked Bosanski lonac (Bosnian pot — meat-and-vegetable layered stew in a tall clay pot) are the country's signature one-pots. Begova čorba (the "bey's soup" — okra and chicken in a creamy broth) is the celebration soup.

## Regional variations

- **Sarajevo and central**: codified mainstream. Ćevapi, bosanski lonac, begova čorba.
- **Mostar and Herzegovina (south)**: closer to Croatian and Mediterranean cooking. More fish, more wine.
- **Banja Luka and the north**: distinct ćevapi style (larger, served on a flatbread). Closer to Serbian.
- **Tuzla (east)**: more rural cooking, the salt-mining-era traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Minced meat (beef-and-lamb mix)**: for ćevapi and pljeskavica.
- **Kajmak**: rich clotted cream-cheese. On ćevapi.
- **Phyllo (jufka)**: in burek, in pita.
- **Yoghurt**: drunk with burek, dressing soups.
- **Onion**: heavy use. Raw chopped onion with ćevapi.
- **Bay leaf, paprika, salt**: the universal seasoning.
- **Plums and apples**: in pita slatka (sweet pies).

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Bosnian Ćevapi](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/bosnian-cevapi)**: small skinless minced-meat sausages charcoal-grilled, served in somun flatbread with chopped raw onion and kajmak. Sarajevo claims the dish's origin (against Serbian counter-claims).
2. **[Bosanski Lonac](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/bosanski-lonac)**: "Bosnian pot" — layers of meat (beef, lamb, sometimes chicken), cabbage, potato, carrot, onion and bay leaf slow-baked in a tall clay pot for hours.
3. **[Bosnian Pita](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/bosnian-pita)**: layered phyllo pie filled with meat, cheese, spinach or potato. The everyday breakfast and street food.
4. **[Begova Čorba](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/begova-corba)**: chicken-and-okra soup with parsley, finished with a roux. The "bey's soup" — historically the dish of the Ottoman governing class.
5. **[Ali Paša Pilav](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/ali-pasa-pilav)**: rice pilaf with diced lamb, raisins and pine nuts. The Ottoman-influenced celebration plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Bosnian coffee (Turkish-style, served in a small djezva with a sugar cube on the side — sip espresso black, never with sugar in it) or with rakija (the universal Balkan plum brandy). End with tufahija (poached apples stuffed with walnuts and topped with whipped cream) or with kadaif (shredded-pastry syrup dessert).
