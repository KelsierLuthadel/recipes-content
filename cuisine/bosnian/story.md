# Bosnian

*The kitchen of the western Balkans. Ćevapi capital (the country claims the dish against Serbian counter-claims), slow-baked clay-pot stews, the layered savoury phyllo pie they call pita, and an Ottoman-Slavic synthesis that produced the most distinct Balkan kitchen.*

## A short history

Bosnian cooking begins with the South Slavs who settled the Sava and Drina valleys in the 6th and 7th centuries, joining and gradually displacing earlier Illyrian and Roman populations. The land set the early kitchen: oak forests for pork and acorns, river valleys for wheat and rye, upland pastures for sheep and cattle, and the long cold winters that made dairy preservation a necessity. Sour milk, smoked meat, dried beans, sauerkraut and the slow-baked clay-pot stew (lonac) all trace to this pre-medieval Slavic shelf, and the lonac is still the right way to cook the country's signature dish.

The medieval Bosnian kingdom (12th to 15th centuries) sat on the trade route between Ragusa (Dubrovnik) on the Adriatic and the Hungarian plain, and the kitchen picked up both. Mediterranean olive oil, figs and almonds came up from the coast; paprika, sour cherry and goose fat came down from the north. The Bogomil heretical church, strong in medieval Bosnia, kept a quietly distinct food culture with its own fast and feast days. When the Ottomans arrived in 1463, they found a country already used to absorbing influence from several directions.

Bosnia then stayed under Ottoman rule for 415 years, from 1463 to 1878, longer than its neighbours, and the cuisine carries the proof. The meze table arrived with its small plates of cheese, pickled peppers, cured meats and ajvar (the roasted-pepper relish). Yoghurt got poured at the finish of every meal. The kebab grills set up in every market town, and the jufka (the hand-stretched phyllo) became the universal pastry, layered into burek (the meat version), sirnica (cheese), zeljanica (spinach) and the sweet pita with apple or cherry. Coffee arrived in tiny copper pots and a coffee house culture grew up that is still the social spine of Sarajevo. Roughly half the country converted to Islam over those centuries, and the cuisine reads as the most Muslim-coded in the former Yugoslavia, with halal kept in most restaurants and pork sitting alongside (not replacing) lamb and beef on the everyday plate.

Austro-Hungarian rule from 1878 added another layer: Mitteleuropean pastries, schnitzel, the strudel tradition (which the local pita absorbed and ran with), beer halls in the cities, and a more formal restaurant culture. The 20th century brought the Yugoslav years, when Sarajevo's restaurants became some of the most adventurous in the federation, mixing all six republics' dishes on one menu. The 1992 to 1995 war devastated the country, but the kitchen survived in family hands; the recipes for begova čorba, dolma and bosanski lonac came back almost untouched.

Today Sarajevo's old town, Baščaršija, is one of the great Balkan food districts. Hundreds of small ćevap grills (ćevabdžinica) and burek shops (buregdžinica) crowd the lanes. The Sarajevo ćevap and the slow-baked bosanski lonac (a tall clay pot layered with meat and vegetables) are the country's signature one-pots. Begova čorba, the "bey's soup" of okra and chicken in a creamy broth, is the celebration bowl. The food still reads exactly as the meeting of Ottoman technique and Slavic ingredient that history made it.

## Regional variations

- **Sarajevo and central**: the codified mainstream. Ćevapi, bosanski lonac, begova čorba.
- **Mostar and Herzegovina (south)**: closer to Croatian and Mediterranean cooking. More fish, more wine.
- **Banja Luka and the north**: distinct ćevapi style (larger, served on a flatbread). Closer to Serbian.
- **Tuzla (east)**: more rural cooking, the salt-mining-era traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Minced meat (beef-and-lamb mix)**: for ćevapi and pljeskavica.
- **Kajmak**: rich clotted cream-cheese. On ćevapi.
- **Phyllo (jufka)**: in burek, in pita.
- **Yoghurt**: drunk with burek, dressing soups.
- **Onion**: heavy use. Raw chopped onion with ćevapi.
- **Bay leaf, paprika, salt**: the universal seasoning.
- **Plums and apples**: in pita slatka (sweet pies).

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Bosnian Ćevapi](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/bosnian-cevapi)**: small skinless minced-meat sausages off the charcoal, served in somun flatbread with chopped raw onion and kajmak. Sarajevo claims the origin (against Serbian counter-claims).
2. **[Bosanski Lonac](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/bosanski-lonac)**: "Bosnian pot": layers of meat (beef, lamb, sometimes chicken), cabbage, potato, carrot, onion and bay leaf slow-baked in a tall clay pot for hours.
3. **[Bosnian Pita](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/bosnian-pita)**: layered phyllo pie filled with meat, cheese, spinach or potato. The everyday breakfast and street food.
4. **[Begova Čorba](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/begova-corba)**: chicken-and-okra soup with parsley, finished with a roux. The "bey's soup", historically the dish of the Ottoman governing class.
5. **[Ali Paša Pilav](/recipes/cuisine/bosnian/ali-pasa-pilav)**: rice pilaf with diced lamb, raisins and pine nuts. The Ottoman-influenced celebration plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Bosnian coffee (Turkish-style, served in a small djezva with a sugar cube on the side; sip the espresso black, never with sugar stirred in) or with rakija, the universal Balkan plum brandy. Finish with tufahija (poached apples stuffed with walnuts and topped with whipped cream) or kadaif, the shredded-pastry syrup dessert.
