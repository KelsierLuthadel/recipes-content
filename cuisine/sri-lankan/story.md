# Sri Lankan

*The most distinctive curry tradition in the Indian Ocean. Roasted spice powder turned almost black with toasting, curry leaves and Maldive fish flakes as bottom notes, and a coconut-rich sweetness that no other South Asian cuisine quite matches.*

## A short history

Sri Lankan cooking sits on the island's role as a trading-post in the spice routes, but the foundation runs deeper than that. The Sinhalese kingdoms of Anuradhapura and Polonnaruwa, from the 4th century BCE through the 13th century CE, built one of the most sophisticated irrigation systems in the ancient world: vast tanks and canals that turned the dry zone into rice paddy. Rice, coconut, jackfruit and the indigenous wild greens (the mallung tradition) anchored the daily plate for nearly two thousand years. Buddhist monasteries codified a vegetarian shelf of dal, vegetable curries and the milk-rice (kiribath) that still marks every Sri Lankan new year.

The island's place on the Indian Ocean trade routes brought a steady drift of outside influence well before the European powers arrived. Arab traders settled the coasts from the 8th century onward and founded the Moor community, bringing biryani technique, samosas and a heavier use of spices into the local kitchen. Tamil migrations from South India over many centuries built the northern Jaffna kitchen with its mustard seeds and tamarind. Cinnamon is endemic here; the world cinnamon trade was Sri Lankan for centuries, and Arab merchants sold it on through Cairo and Venice for prices that funded fleets.

The Portuguese arrived in 1505 and brought chillies, tomatoes, cashews, pineapple, breadfruit and a fondness for spice that completely transformed what had been a milder pre-colonial kitchen. Chilli took to the island so completely that within a century Sri Lankan curries were among the hottest in Asia. The Portuguese also left love cake (a cashew-and-semolina cake still served at Christmas) and the foundation of the Burgher community. The Dutch took over in 1658 and added their own creole tradition: lamprais (rice with curry baked in banana leaf), breudher (the Dutch yeast Christmas cake), frikkadel and the codified rijsttafel-style spread.

The British (1796 to 1948) commercialised tea, coffee and rubber on plantation estates carved out of the central highlands, and brought South Indian Tamil labourers in massive numbers to work them; that migration deepened the Tamil cuisine of the hill country and added the milk-tea-and-cake tradition that still runs every Sri Lankan afternoon. The British also codified the rice-and-curry plate format that now defines the daily meal: a mound of rice surrounded by five or six small curries, a dal, a sambol, a mallung, all eaten with the right hand. The Sinhalese and Tamil traditions diverged through the colonial centuries but kept borrowing from each other; the famous black curry powder (spices dry-roasted till almost burnt) is mostly a Sinhalese signature, while the Jaffna crab curry and kool stew are Tamil glories.

The civil war (1983 to 2009) limited Sri Lanka's culinary outreach for a generation; the post-war years opened the country to tourism, and the international restaurant scene started discovering Sri Lankan cooking properly only from the 2010s onward. The diaspora kitchens in London, Toronto, Sydney and Melbourne carried the recipes through the war years, and now Sri Lankan food is widely recognised as one of South Asia's most distinct cuisines, finally getting the global attention the cooking deserves.

## Regional variations

- **Sinhalese south (Colombo, Galle)**: the codified mainstream. Rice and curry, hoppers, kottu roti, the black-curry-powder roast.
- **Tamil north (Jaffna)**: closer to South India. Jaffna crab curry, kool (mixed seafood stew), more mustard seed, the Hindu vegetarian shelf.
- **Eastern coast (Batticaloa, Trincomalee)**: Tamil seafood country. Fish curries with tamarind and dried Maldive fish.
- **Hill country (Kandy, Nuwara Eliya)**: tea country and the British-influenced shelf. Beef curries (rare elsewhere on the island), the tea-and-cake tradition.
- **Burgher communities (everywhere, especially Colombo)**: lamprais (rice with curry baked in banana leaf, a Dutch-era dish), breudher (Christmas cake), seeni sambol (caramelised onion relish).

## Signature ingredients

- **Black curry powder**: cumin, coriander, fennel, fenugreek, mustard seed, peppercorns and dried chillies dry-roasted in a pan till almost burnt, then ground. The defining Sri Lankan flavour.
- **Coconut**: coconut milk in nearly every curry, fresh grated coconut in sambols, coconut oil as the fat.
- **Pandan (rampe) and curry leaves**: the herb pair under most curries. Pandan in particular sets the island apart; almost no Indian curries use it, Sri Lankan ones often do.
- **Maldive fish flakes**: dried, smoked tuna, an umami bomb. Used in sambols and as a curry base.
- **Goraka**: a small sour fruit (close to garcinia or kokum), used in fish curries.
- **Tamarind**: the alternative sour, used widely.
- **Rice (red, white)**: kekulu (white) and rathu kekulu (red) are the two everyday rices.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the curry tradition, the hopper canon, the kottu roti street food and the sambol shelf. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Sri Lankan Chicken Curry](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/sri-lankan-chicken-curry)**: chicken cooked with black curry powder, coconut milk, pandan and curry leaves. The benchmark Sri Lankan curry and the starting point for the rest of the shelf.
2. **[Polos Curry](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/polos-curry)**: young (unripe) jackfruit slow-cooked with black curry powder and coconut milk till the texture is shredded meat. The signature vegetarian curry.
3. **[Hoppers](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/hoppers)**: bowl-shaped fermented rice-flour pancakes, crisp at the edges and soft in the middle. Eat them with curry or with an egg cracked in the centre.
4. **[String Hoppers](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/string-hoppers)**: tangled nests of rice-flour vermicelli, steamed. The breakfast staple, eaten with curry and pol sambol.
5. **[Kottu Roti](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/kottu-roti)**: chopped godhamba roti stir-fried on a flat-top with curry, egg, vegetables and meat. The street-food signature, identified by the rhythmic clang of two metal blades on a hot plate.
6. **[Pol Sambol](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/pol-sambol)**: fresh grated coconut blended with chilli, onion, lime and Maldive fish flakes. The condiment on every Sri Lankan table.
7. **[Ambul Thiyal](/recipes/cuisine/sri-lankan/ambul-thiyal)**: fish cooked dry with goraka, black pepper and curry leaves till the gravy nearly disappears. The southern coast's signature sour fish curry.

Cook the chicken curry and the polos and you have the curry tradition. The hoppers and string hoppers are the breakfast canon. The kottu is the street. The pol sambol is the condiment shelf. The ambul thiyal is the coast.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the watalappam (jaggery-coconut custard) dessert tradition, more sambols (seeni sambol, lunu miris), the Jaffna kitchen (crab curry, kool), and the kiri hodi (mild coconut curry) shelf. Build a full rice-and-curry plate: one main protein curry, two vegetable curries, one dal, one sambol, mallung. That's the proper Sri Lankan meal.
