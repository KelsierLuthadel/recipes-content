# French

*The cuisine that taught the world to write recipes down. Codified technique, regional larders that read like a geography textbook, and the longest love affair with butter, cream and wine in culinary history.*

## A short history

French cooking begins long before the cookbook. Gaul under Rome (from Caesar's conquest in 52 BCE) was already a serious wine and cheese country, with vineyards in the Rhone valley and cheese-making in the Alpine pastures that Pliny the Elder name-checked in the 1st century CE. The Gallo-Roman kitchen ran on bread, wine, pork, garum (the fermented fish sauce), olive oil in the south and lard in the north. Roquefort cheese was being aged in its limestone caves by 79 CE according to Pliny, which makes it one of the oldest continuous cheese traditions on earth. After Rome collapsed, the medieval centuries layered in monastic vineyard work (the Cistercians and the Benedictines spent the 10th to 15th centuries mapping the climats of Burgundy that still define the region's wine), the elaborate spiced cookery of the royal courts (Taillevent's Le Viandier of around 1300 is the first surviving French cookbook), and the regional cheese, charcuterie and bread traditions that grew around the abbeys.

Catherine de Medici's marriage to the future Henri II in 1533 used to be credited with bringing Italian cooking to France (an overstated story), but the 16th century did open the French court to Italian Renaissance dining, the fork, and a more sophisticated table. The 17th century was the codification decade. La Varenne's Le Cuisinier Francois (1651) was the first true French cookbook in the modern sense, breaking with medieval spice-heavy cookery and pioneering the technique of building sauces from reduced stocks and roux. The court kitchens at Versailles under Louis XIV turned the dining table into theatre, and the bourgeois Paris of Louis XV and XVI refined the brigade and the service. The Columbian Exchange landed during these centuries too: tomatoes, peppers, beans, potatoes, maize and chocolate arrived from the Americas. The potato took until the 1770s to be accepted (Antoine Parmentier had to throw fashionable Parisian dinners to convince anyone) but the haricot bean fed Languedoc cassoulet within a generation.

The Revolution of 1789 unlocked French cooking in a way no court could have. When the aristocracy fled or lost their heads, their cooks went into business. The first true restaurants opened in Paris in the 1780s and 1790s, and by 1800 the city had hundreds of them. Antonin Careme, who started as an abandoned child and became chef to Talleyrand, the Prince Regent and Tsar Alexander I, turned cookery into architecture in the early 1800s with his elaborate pieces montees, his foundation sauces and his published manuals. Auguste Escoffier then modernised the brigade kitchen at the Savoy and the Ritz around 1900 and wrote Le Guide Culinaire (1903), the spine of professional French cookery for the next hundred years.

Underneath the codification sits the regional larder. France is shaped by its climates: Norman butter and cream country, Atlantic seafood, Burgundian wine and beef, Provencal olive oil and herbs, Alsatian sauerkraut and pork, Basque red pepper. Each region kept its own kitchen and its own grandmother, and the Paris cuisine that got codified was always a synthesis of those traditions polished for service. The 1900 Michelin Guide (originally a tyre-selling promotion handed out free to motorists) codified the regional restaurant scene and turned the country lunch into a national obsession.

Two revolutions followed. Nouvelle cuisine in the 1970s, led by Paul Bocuse, the Troisgros brothers, Michel Guerard and Alain Chapel, brought lighter sauces, shorter cooking times, plated portions and a respect for the produce that broke with the heavy butter-cream tradition of Escoffier. Then the bistronomy movement of the 2000s, started by Yves Camdeborde at La Regalade in Paris, put Michelin-trained chefs in small neighbourhood places running market-driven menus, with the regional producer turned into a celebrity. Today's French kitchen sits somewhere between the codified discipline of Escoffier and the bistronomy ethic of the corner bouchon, with the grandmother's regional shelf still running underneath both.

## Regional variations

- **Île-de-France (Paris)**: the codified national cuisine. Butter sauces, brigade-trained technique, the bistro classic menu.
- **Normandy**: butter, cream, apples (cider, calvados), Camembert. Sauces enrichis with cream. The most dairy-forward region.
- **Burgundy and Lyon**: the heartland. Beef bourguignon, coq au vin, the long red-wine braise, charcuterie, the bouchon table.
- **Provence and the South**: olive oil, garlic, tomato, herbes de Provence, Mediterranean seafood. Bouillabaisse, ratatouille, tapenade. The closest French shelf to the Italian and Spanish kitchens.
- **Alsace and the East**: German-leaning. Choucroute (sauerkraut with cured pork), tarte flambée, riesling-led cooking, sausage and pork everywhere.
- **Brittany and the Atlantic**: salt-water seafood, salted butter, buckwheat galettes. The densest seafood larder in the country.
- **Gascony and the South-West**: duck and goose country. Confit, cassoulet, foie gras, Armagnac. Heavy, slow, magnificent.

## Signature ingredients

- **Butter**: salted in Brittany, unsalted everywhere else. The fat of French cookery.
- **Crème fraîche**: cultured cream, tangy. The finishing flourish on a sauce.
- **Dijon mustard**: Burgundy's contribution to the world's pantry.
- **Tarragon, chervil, parsley, chives**: les fines herbes, the herb quartet of classic sauces.
- **Shallots**: the onion of choice for refined French cooking. Sharper, sweeter, smaller than the yellow onion.
- **Wine**: red Burgundy for beef and chicken braises, white Bordeaux for cream sauces, Calvados for flambéing.
- **Bay, thyme, peppercorns**: the bouquet garni base.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the long braise, the soup tradition, the duck shelf and the codified cream sauce. Cook them in order and the cuisine opens up.

1. **[Beef Bourguignon](/recipes/cuisine/french/beef-bourguignon)**: chuck slow-braised in red Burgundy with bacon, pearl onions and mushrooms. The single most famous French dish and the gateway to every wine-braised stew in the canon.
2. **[Coq au Vin](/recipes/cuisine/french/coq-au-vin)**: the chicken sibling of bourguignon. Same red-wine braise, same lardons-mushrooms-pearl-onions garnish, lighter protein.
3. **[French Onion Soup](/recipes/cuisine/french/french-onion-soup)**: onions caramelised for an hour, beef stock, gruyère-crusted bread. A patience exercise that teaches you what caramelisation does to a stock.
4. **[Bouillabaisse](/recipes/cuisine/french/bouillabaisse)**: Provençal seafood stew with saffron and fennel, served with rouille on grilled bread. The South's signature one-pot.
5. **[Duck Confit](/recipes/cuisine/french/duck-confit)**: duck legs slow-cooked submerged in their own rendered fat. Preserved for weeks, crisped to order. The Gascon trick that turned tough birds into delicacy.
6. **[Cassoulet](/recipes/cuisine/french/cassoulet)**: white beans baked with duck confit, sausage and pork in a deep clay pot under a breadcrumb crust. The grand winter dish of the South-West.
7. **[Quiche Lorraine](/recipes/cuisine/french/quiche-lorraine)**: shortcrust pastry, custard of egg and cream, bacon. The home cook's entry to French pastry and the technique behind every savoury tart.

Cook the bourguignon and the coq au vin and you have the braise. The onion soup and the bouillabaisse give you the soup tradition. The confit and the cassoulet are the duck shelf. The quiche is the pastry door. Together they map the cuisine end to end.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue's French shelf carries the cream-and-Calvados Norman tradition (blanquette de veau, sole meunière), the Provençal vegetable canon (ratatouille, pissaladière), the bistro classics (steak frites, croque monsieur), and the patisserie work (tarte tatin, crème brûlée, soufflé). Pick a region, work through it, then cross-pollinate.
