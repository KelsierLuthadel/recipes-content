# Mongolian

*The kitchen of the steppe nomad. Mutton in dozens of forms, the dumpling (buuz, bansh, khuushuur) that has been the meal-on-the-go since Genghis Khan, milk tea with salt, and a kitchen built for a life on horseback.*

## A short history

Mongolian cooking is the cuisine of nomadic pastoralism, the daily diet of the herders of the Eurasian steppe for thousands of years. The basic ingredients are essentially what a nomad can carry or what walks alongside the household: mutton, beef, yak (in the highlands), milk (cow, mare, camel, turned into airag, the fermented mare's milk drink, and aaruul, dried curds), flour (carried as a staple), and very few vegetables (foraged wild greens; cabbage and potato arrived in the 20th century).

Archaeology pushes the cooking back to at least 3000 BCE, when bronze-age herders on the Mongolian plateau were already managing the same five animals (the *tavan khoshuu mal*, the five snouts: horse, cattle, sheep, goat, camel) that anchor the modern household. The Xiongnu confederation in the 3rd century BCE through the 1st century CE codified the steppe lifestyle, fermenting mare's milk into koumiss and developing the felt-walled ger (yurt) with its central hearth. The Türkic and Uighur khanates that followed traded with Tang dynasty China, picking up tea, silk and the first iron cookware that turned slow-stewing into a workable steppe technique.

The Mongol Empire (1206 to 1368) was the decisive moment. Genghis Khan and his successors built the largest contiguous land empire in history, and the Pax Mongolica that followed opened the Silk Road as never before. The empire carried Mongolian dumplings westward: buuz are the direct ancestor of Russian pelmeni, Chinese baozi, Korean mandu, Turkish manti and (via Silk Road trade through Venice) almost certainly Italian ravioli. The dumpling diaspora across Eurasia is essentially a Mongolian export. The Yuan dynasty kitchen in Khanbaliq (modern Beijing) produced the *Yinshan Zhengyao*, a 1330 cookbook of imperial Mongol cuisine that mixes steppe meats with Chinese vegetables and Persian spices; it is the oldest detailed record of Mongolian cookery and reveals a kitchen far more spice-rich than the modern Mongolian table.

The Manchu Qing dynasty absorbed Mongolia from the 1690s and ruled it for nearly two and a half centuries, layering in Chinese tea (which became the universal Mongolian drink), the iron wok, soy sauce, and a degree of Buddhist vegetarianism that ran in the larger monasteries. Tibetan Buddhism, which had taken hold under Altan Khan in the 16th century, brought the monastic dairy traditions and the tsampa-style barley-flour mixes still seen in some western Mongolian dishes. Independence in 1911 came with Russian backing, and revolutionary Mongolia became the world's second communist state after the Soviet Union itself in 1924.

The Soviet period (1924 to 1990) introduced Russian dishes that absorbed into the everyday city table: borscht-style soups, beef stroganoff, the Olivier salad (now called niislel salat, "capital salad"), Russian black bread, pickled cabbage, and the supermarket goods that finally put cabbage, potato and onion on the Mongolian kitchen shelf permanently. Soviet-style cafeterias in Ulaanbaatar served the urban workforce, and Mongolian children grew up on a hybrid of grandmother's mutton dumplings and Russian school-canteen cooking. The democratic transition of 1990 and the opening to the wider world hasn't dramatically changed the cuisine; the herding economy continues, roughly a third of Mongolians still live as semi-nomadic herders, and the home table remains resolutely mutton-centric. Korean barbecue, Chinese hot pot and a small but growing fine-dining scene in Ulaanbaatar now sit alongside the traditional Naadam festival khuushuur and the family Tsagaan Sar buuz feast that still defines the Mongolian year.

## Regional variations

- **Ulaanbaatar (capital, central)**: codified mainstream. Buuz, khuushuur, tsuivan.
- **Western (Bayan-Ölgii, Kazakh-influenced)**: closer to Central Asian cooking. Beshbarmak, more horse meat.
- **Gobi (south desert)**: camel-meat dishes.
- **Northern (Khövsgöl, Buryat-influenced)**: yak, more dairy, the Buryat dumpling tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Mutton**: the primary meat. Lamb is fine but properly should be mutton.
- **Yak and beef**: secondary meats. Yak in the highlands.
- **Milk products (tsagaan idee, "white food")**: aaruul (dried curds), airag (fermented mare's milk), tarag (yoghurt).
- **Flour**: in dumpling wrappers, in fried bread.
- **Onions, carrots, cabbage, potato**: the introduced vegetables, now standard.
- **Salt**: in milk tea (suutei tsai, salty milk tea).
- **Cumin and black pepper**: minimal seasoning; the cuisine respects the meat.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Buuz](/recipes/cuisine/mongolian/buuz)**: steamed mutton dumplings. The Lunar New Year (Tsagaan Sar) celebration food; a Mongolian family makes thousands.
2. **[Khuushuur](/recipes/cuisine/mongolian/khuushuur)**: deep-fried mutton dumplings. The Naadam festival food (mid-July), the most-eaten Mongolian dish overall.
3. **[Tsuivan](/recipes/cuisine/mongolian/tsuivan)**: hand-cut wheat noodles stir-fried with mutton, onion, carrot and cabbage. The everyday meal.
4. **[Bansh](/recipes/cuisine/mongolian/bansh)**: small boiled mutton dumplings in broth.
5. **[Niislel Salat](/recipes/cuisine/mongolian/niislel-salat)**: "capital salad", potato, carrot, peas, gherkin, ham and mayonnaise. The Soviet-era inheritance, now beloved.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with suutei tsai (salty milk tea, served from a thermos) or with vodka (locally brewed). For Naadam (July 11 to 13, the national festival), khuushuur is the universal food eaten on the wrestling, archery and horse-racing grounds.
