# Portuguese

*The Atlantic kitchen that taught the world to love salt cod. A maritime cuisine built on long voyages, slow stews, and a quiet, almost stubborn loyalty to a small set of ingredients done very well.*

## A short history

Portuguese cooking starts where the land meets the Atlantic, and most of what's on the plate today traces back to that geography. The pre-Roman Lusitanian and Celtiberian peoples who occupied the Iberian west grazed pigs on the cork-oak forests, fished sardines and shellfish off the shore, and grew wheat, barley and olives on the inland plains. The Romans, arriving in the 2nd century BCE, codified the olive groves and the vineyards (Portugal has been making wine continuously for over 2,000 years), built the salt pans of Setúbal and the Algarve that still produce flor de sal today, and left the language that would become Portuguese.

The Moorish period (711 to 1249) layered a sophisticated southern kitchen onto that base. Arab-Berber rulers brought rice, sugar, citrus, almonds, figs, the cumin and coriander shelf, and a love of sweet pastries built on egg yolk, sugar and almond that the Portuguese later transferred wholesale into their convent baking tradition. The Algarve and Alentejo still cook in ways that read more Moorish than Iberian: the açorda bread soups, the cataplana clamshell pot (a cousin to the tagine), and the marriage of pork and clams in carne de porco à alentejana, which some food historians read as a defiant Reconquista dish (combining the two ingredients the Moors forbade and the new Christian Portugal celebrated).

The 15th and 16th centuries turned Portugal into the cuisine of seafarers. From Prince Henry the Navigator's school at Sagres onwards the country ran the longest sustained programme of ocean exploration in history; Portuguese ships rounded the Cape of Good Hope in 1488, reached India in 1498, opened Brazil in 1500, and held trading posts from Macau to Goa to Malacca to Mozambique. The kitchen picked up every stop on the route. India gave it tamarind, the curry idea (which crossed back to Goa as vindalho, from vinho de alhos, the Portuguese wine-and-garlic marinade) and a fondness for fierce chilli; the Portuguese then planted that chilli across their African and Asian colonies, kick-starting global chilli cooking. Africa gave back piri-piri; Brazil sent corn, peanut, cassava, cashew, pineapple and the tropical fruits; Japan kept tempura (Portuguese missionaries' Lenten battered vegetables, originally cooked during Ember Days, the quattuor tempora that gave the dish its name) when Portugal lost interest.

Salt cod is the unlikely centre of the Portuguese kitchen, and it landed in a deeper way than any other ingredient. Portuguese fishermen reached the Grand Banks of Newfoundland in the early 1500s, hauled in cod by the boatload, and salted it on board for the long voyage home. The dried, salted cod (bacalhau) became a staple in a country with limited refrigeration and many fasting days; the cooks say there are 365 recipes, one for every day of the year. The kitchen rides on olive oil, garlic, bay leaf, coriander leaf (the only Iberian country that uses it heavily), pork, rice and the long slow stew. The Moorish influence shows up as saffron and cumin in certain dishes; the African return-trip shows up as piri-piri at every corner.

The 18th and 19th centuries layered on the convent baking tradition and the wine trade. Portuguese nuns developed an extraordinary repertoire of sweets built on egg yolks (the whites went to clarifying wine and starching habits, leaving the kitchens with mountains of yolks): pastéis de Tentúgal, ovos moles de Aveiro, papos de anjo, and the pastel de nata that Lisbon's Pastéis de Belém has been baking from the original Jerónimos Monastery recipe since 1837. The port wine trade, codified in 1756 under the Marquês de Pombal who drew the world's first protected wine region around the Douro, sent fortified wine to England and built the cellars of Vila Nova de Gaia.

The Salazar dictatorship (1933 to 1974) kept the country poor and the kitchen frugal, and in some ways preserved the old recipes by accident; the home cooking that pulled families through the Estado Novo decades is the same home cooking that defines Portuguese food today. The Carnation Revolution and EU membership (1986) brought prosperity and the modern restaurant scene. Today Portuguese food sits between the rustic adega (wine cellar) tradition, the pastelaria on every corner, and a quietly ambitious modern restaurant scene led by Lisbon and Porto. José Avillez, Henrique Sá Pessoa and the new Algarve seafood chefs are pushing Portuguese cooking onto the world stage without disturbing the country's deep loyalty to its small set of ingredients done very well.

## Regional variations

- **Minho (north-west)**: caldo verde country. Vinho verde (lightly fizzy young wine), broa (corn bread), salt cod boiled with potatoes.
- **Douro and Trás-os-Montes**: the wine valley. Port wine, posta de vitela, alheira (the bread-and-meat sausage Jews developed as a pork-substitute disguise).
- **Beira (central)**: hill cooking. Chanfana (goat slow-braised in wine), bola de carne (meat-stuffed bread).
- **Estremadura and Lisbon**: the bairros (working-class neighbourhood) cooking. Bifana, ameijoas à bulhão pato (clams with garlic and coriander), prego no pão (steak sandwich).
- **Alentejo (south-central)**: slow-cook country. Açorda alentejana (bread soup), porco preto (black-pig pork), carne de porco à alentejana (pork and clams).
- **Algarve (south)**: seafood and citrus. Cataplana stews, fig and almond sweets, octopus and sardines off the coast.

## Signature ingredients

- **Bacalhau (salt cod)**: the national fish. Dried, salted, soaked, used in 365 ways.
- **Olive oil**: heavy and golden; the fat of Portuguese cooking.
- **Garlic, bay leaf, coriander leaf**: the herb backbone. Coriander leaf, in particular, sets Portugal apart from the rest of Iberia.
- **Piri-piri**: small African chillis brought back from the colonies. The Portuguese hot sauce.
- **Port wine and Madeira**: fortified wines, in the cooking and at the table.
- **Pork**: black-pig pork (porco preto) is the prized version. The everyday meat.
- **Custard, eggs, almonds**: the pastry backbone, much of it adapted from medieval convent kitchens.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the salt cod tradition, the rice shelf, the slow braise and the bread soup. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Bacalhau à Brás](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/bacalhau-a-bras)**: shredded salt cod scrambled with onion, matchstick potatoes and egg. The most-loved bacalhau preparation and the easiest entry to the salt cod shelf.
2. **[Bacalhau à Gomes de Sá](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/bacalhau-a-gomes-de-sa)**: layered bacalhau with potato, onion, egg and black olives, baked. The Porto-born showpiece bacalhau.
3. **[Caldo Verde](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/caldo-verde)**: pureed potato soup with finely shredded collard greens (couve galega) and a slice of chouriço. The Minho's national dish, sold in every café.
4. **[Arroz de Marisco](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/arroz-de-marisco)**: loose, soupy seafood rice with prawn, clam, mussel and white fish. Not paella; the rice is wetter, the broth is the point.
5. **[Carne de Porco à Alentejana](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/carne-de-porco-a-alentejana)**: pork cubes marinated in massa de pimentão, fried with clams, served with potatoes. The Alentejo's signature surf-and-turf.
6. **[Bifana](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/bifana)**: thin pork steaks simmered in garlic-and-wine sauce, slotted into a crusty roll. The classic Portuguese sandwich and the most reliable bar food in the country.
7. **[Cataplana de Marisco](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/cataplana-de-marisco)**: clams, prawns, white fish and chorizo steamed in a sealed copper clamshell with tomato and white wine. The Algarve's most photogenic dish.

Cook the two bacalhaus and you have the salt cod shelf. The caldo verde and the arroz are the soup and the rice. The pork dish and the bifana are the everyday meat work. The cataplana is the celebration plate.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven the catalogue carries more bacalhau (à Lagareiro, com natas), the slow-braised meat tradition (chanfana, cozido à portuguesa), the rice family (arroz de pato, arroz de tomate), and the pastelaria icons (pastéis de nata, queijadas). Portuguese food rewards patience; cook everything slowly and the flavour comes out.
