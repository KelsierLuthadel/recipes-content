# Portuguese

*The Atlantic kitchen that taught the world to love salt cod. A maritime cuisine built on long voyages, slow stews, and a quiet, almost stubborn loyalty to a small set of ingredients done very well.*

## A short history

Portuguese cooking is the cuisine of seafarers. From the early 15th century the country sponsored the longest sustained programme of ocean exploration in history; Portuguese ships rounded Africa, reached India, opened Brazil and held trading posts from Macau to Goa to Mozambique. The kitchen absorbed every stop on the route. From India came tamarind and a fondness for fierce chilli (which the Portuguese then planted across Africa); from Africa came piri-piri; from Brazil came corn, peanut, cassava and the tropical fruits; from Japan came tempura (Portuguese missionaries' Lenten battered vegetables, which Japan kept and Portugal lost).

Underneath the global reach sits a small, conservative home table. Bacalhau (dried, salted cod from the Newfoundland fisheries) is the national fish — said to have 365 recipes, one for every day of the year. The kitchen sits on olive oil, garlic, bay leaf, coriander leaf (the only Iberian country that uses it heavily), pork, rice and the long slow stew. The Moorish influence shows in saffron and cumin in certain dishes, and the African return-trip shows in piri-piri at every corner.

The 20th century Salazar dictatorship (1933–1974) kept the country poor and the kitchen frugal; the Carnation Revolution and EU membership brought prosperity and the modern restaurant scene. Today Portuguese food sits between the rustic adega (wine cellar) tradition, the pastelaria (pastry shop) on every corner, and a quietly ambitious modern restaurant scene led by Lisbon and Porto.

## Regional variations

- **Minho (north-west)**: caldo verde country. Vinho verde (lightly fizzy young wine), broa (corn bread), salt cod boiled with potatoes.
- **Douro and Trás-os-Montes**: the wine valley. Port wine, posta de vitela, alheira (the bread-and-meat sausage Jews developed as a pork-substitute disguise).
- **Beira (central)**: hill cooking. Chanfana (goat slow-braised in wine), bola de carne (meat-stuffed bread).
- **Estremadura and Lisbon**: the bairros (working-class neighbourhood) cooking. Bifana, ameijoas à bulhão pato (clams with garlic and coriander), prego no pão (steak sandwich).
- **Alentejo (south-central)**: the slow-cook country. Açorda alentejana (bread soup), porco preto (black-pig pork), carne de porco à alentejana (pork and clams).
- **Algarve (south)**: seafood and citrus. Cataplana stews, fig and almond sweets, octopus and sardines off the coast.

## Signature ingredients

- **Bacalhau (salt cod)**: the national fish. Dried, salted, soaked, used in 365 ways.
- **Olive oil**: heavy and golden; the fat of Portuguese cooking.
- **Garlic, bay leaf, coriander leaf**: the herb backbone. Coriander leaf in particular distinguishes Portugal from the rest of Iberia.
- **Piri-piri**: small African chillis brought back from the colonies. The Portuguese hot sauce.
- **Port wine and Madeira**: fortified wines used in cooking and at the table.
- **Pork**: black-pig pork (porco preto) is the prized version. The everyday meat.
- **Custard, eggs, almonds**: the pastry backbone, much of it adapted from medieval convent kitchens.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the salt cod tradition, the rice shelf, the slow braise and the bread soup. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Bacalhau à Brás](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/bacalhau-a-bras)**: shredded salt cod scrambled with onion, matchstick potatoes and egg. The most-loved bacalhau preparation and the easiest entry to the salt cod shelf.
2. **[Bacalhau à Gomes de Sá](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/bacalhau-a-gomes-de-sa)**: layered bacalhau with potato, onion, egg and black olives, baked. The Porto-born showpiece bacalhau.
3. **[Caldo Verde](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/caldo-verde)**: pureed potato soup with finely shredded collard greens (couve galega) and a slice of chouriço. The Minho's national dish, sold in every café.
4. **[Arroz de Marisco](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/arroz-de-marisco)**: loose, soupy seafood rice with prawn, clam, mussel, white fish. Not paella; the rice is wetter, the broth is the point.
5. **[Carne de Porco à Alentejana](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/carne-de-porco-a-alentejana)**: pork cubes marinated with massa de pimentão, fried with clams, served with potatoes. The Alentejo's signature surf-and-turf.
6. **[Bifana](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/bifana)**: thin pork steaks simmered in garlic-and-wine sauce, slotted into a crusty roll. The classic Portuguese sandwich and the most reliable bar food in the country.
7. **[Cataplana de Marisco](/recipes/cuisine/portuguese/cataplana-de-marisco)**: clams, prawns, white fish and chorizo steamed in a sealed copper clamshell with tomato and white wine. The Algarve's most photogenic dish.

Cook the two bacalhaus and you have the salt cod shelf. The caldo verde and the arroz are the soup and the rice. The pork dish and the bifana are the everyday meat work. The cataplana is the celebration plate.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries more bacalhau (à Lagareiro, com natas), the slow-braised meat tradition (chanfana, cozido à portuguesa), the rice family (arroz de pato, arroz de tomate), and the pastelaria icons (pastéis de nata, queijadas). Portuguese food rewards patience; cook everything slowly and the flavour comes out.
