# Louisiana

*The umbrella state cuisine that holds both country Cajun and city Creole, the rice paddies of the prairie, the Gulf shrimp boats, the Sicilian sandwich shops and the Vietnamese fishing villages, all sharing one holy trinity.*

## A short history

Long before any European flag flew over the delta, the Choctaw, Houma, Chitimacha and Atakapa peoples lived along the lower Mississippi and the Gulf marshes, fishing the bayous, harvesting sassafras leaves and pounding the dried leaves into a thickener now called filé, growing the corn-beans-squash triad on the higher ground, and cooking everything in earthen pots over open fires. The filé in your gumbo, the corn under your maque choux, and the deep knowledge of which alligator gar is worth eating all trace back to those kitchens. The French who arrived in the 1690s ate what the Native cooks were already eating, then sat down to learn.

Louisiana is a crossroads, and the cooking shows it. The French founded New Orleans in 1718 as the port at the bottom of the Mississippi, the last stop before the Gulf and the first stop for everything moving up the river. Spain governed the colony from 1763 to 1803, the United States bought it in the Louisiana Purchase of 1803, and through every flag change the kitchen kept absorbing new arrivals rather than replacing the cooks already at the stove. The Spanish decades layered in jambalaya's paella ancestry, a love of saffron and tomato in the rice pot, and the chorizo-style smoked sausages that became andouille in the River Parishes.

The single largest contribution came from West and Central African cooks, enslaved and later free, who ran the daily kitchen of the colony from the early 1700s on. They brought okra (the word "gumbo" comes from Bantu *ki ngombo*, the okra plant itself), the long simmer, the rice-and-beans logic, the spice instinct, and the technique that became gumbo. Rice itself arrived from the Senegambian coast in the holds of slave ships, along with the cooks who knew how to plant it; the prairie paddies of southwest Louisiana grew directly from that knowledge. The Acadians, French Catholic exiles from Nova Scotia, drifted into the bayou country from the 1760s after the British expelled them from what is now Canada, and they built the country French Louisiana cuisine now called Cajun on whatever the swamp and prairie gave them.

The 19th century deepened the layers. German immigrants settled the German Coast above New Orleans in the 1720s and stayed to build the butchery and the smokehouses; the Andouille of LaPlace and the cracklin tradition of pork-fat economy both run through that German hand. The Haitian Revolution of 1791 to 1804 sent waves of refugees (free people of colour, white planters, enslaved people) to New Orleans, doubling the city's population and bringing Caribbean spice instincts, the love of plantain and tropical fruit, and the voodoo-Catholic culinary calendar that gave the city its festivals. Sicilian immigrants arrived in huge numbers from the 1880s, set up groceries on Decatur Street, planted the muffuletta sandwich around the olive salad from home, and brought the red gravy and the stuffed-artichoke tradition still on the city's tables. Reconstruction-era restaurants like Antoine's (1840) and Galatoire's (1905) codified the city Creole tradition in printed menus and silver service.

The 20th century industrialised what had been a country economy. The oil boom of the 1920s through 1970s funded huge cooking on offshore rigs (boudin and rice for breakfast, gumbo for lunch) and underwrote the rise of Cajun music and the dance hall culture that fed the cooking. Paul Prudhomme opened K-Paul's in 1979 and put blackened redfish and the country Cajun roux on the global map, almost single-handedly elevating the rural tradition into restaurant cuisine. Vietnamese refugees settled along the Gulf coast after 1975, took up shrimping and fishing in the same boats their American hosts had been working, and folded fish sauce, lemongrass and ginger into the Cajun crawfish boil. Hurricane Katrina in 2005 scattered the city kitchens across the country and pulled them back; the recovery decades brought a new generation of chefs cooking the old food with new sourcing.

The state holds two distinct traditions that share a holy trinity (onion, celery, green bell pepper) and diverge from there. Country Cajun is one-pot, dark roux, pork fat, country French gone Louisiana. City Creole is tomato, butter, French technique, the old restaurant kitchens of the French Quarter. The wider Louisiana pantry includes Gulf shrimp, oysters, crawfish, alligator, soft-shell crab and blue crab, plus rice grown in industrial volume in the southwest prairie. The anchor principle across all of it: the trinity, a rice plate, a slow simmer.

## Regional variations

Louisiana is small on the map but layered on the plate. The cooking changes by parish and by who is doing the cooking.

- **New Orleans (city Creole)**: tomato-rich, French technique, butter and cream, the restaurant kitchens of the French Quarter and the Garden District. The home of shrimp Creole, oysters Rockefeller, bananas Foster and a dozen sauces with French names.
- **Acadiana and Cajun country**: rural one-pot cooking, dark roux, étouffée, jambalaya in big black pots, boudin links sold from gas-station counters across Lafayette parish.
- **North Louisiana (Shreveport region)**: more typically Southern American, cornbread and fried catfish and pulled pork, less holy trinity, more in common with east Texas and Arkansas than with the bayou.
- **The Gulf coast**: oyster bars, shrimp boats, dockside seafood, and the Vietnamese-Cajun crawfish boil culture of Buras, Empire and Versailles where lemongrass, fish sauce and ginger sit beside the cayenne and the lemons.
- **The River Parishes (between Baton Rouge and New Orleans)**: the old plantation kitchens, brown sauces, andouille smokehouses (LaPlace is the andouille capital), sweet potato pone, a cooking that bridges the city and the country.

The most important pairing to understand is Cajun versus Creole, and this catalogue tracks them as separate sub-cuisines for that reason. The pages here on Louisiana cover the dishes that sit between them, span them, or belong to the wider state rather than to one camp.

## Signature ingredients

- **The holy trinity** (onion, celery, green bell pepper): the aromatic base under almost every savoury Louisiana dish, the bayou answer to the French mirepoix with green bell pepper standing in for the carrot.
- **Dark roux**: flour and oil cooked patiently to the colour of pecan, milk chocolate or even darker for gumbo. The single technique that defines [Cajun cooking](../cajun/home.md) and underwrites the city's gumbos too.
- **Andouille sausage**: heavily smoked pork sausage from the River Parishes, sliced thick into gumbo and jambalaya, the smoke that signals Louisiana before any other ingredient.
- **Tasso**: heavily spiced, hard-smoked pork shoulder used as a seasoning meat; a few cubes flavour a pot the way pancetta does in Italy.
- **Long-grain rice**: grown in industrial volume in the southwest prairie, the starch under almost every plate. A Louisiana meal without rice is the exception.
- **Crawfish**: the heartland shellfish, boiled by the sack in spring, picked into étouffée, baked into pies, blended into bisques. The state goes through 150 million pounds a year.
- **Filé powder**: ground sassafras leaf, a Choctaw contribution, stirred off the heat into gumbo at the table when okra is not in season.
- **Louisiana hot sauce**: Crystal, Tabasco or Louisiana brand, a thin vinegar-and-cayenne sauce that sits on every table the way salt and pepper do elsewhere.

The detailed regional treatments live in the sub-cuisine pages. [Cajun](../cajun/home.md) covers the country roux-and-trinity tradition in depth, and [Creole](../creole/home.md) covers the city restaurant tradition.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Seafood gumbo](seafood-gumbo.md). The teaching dish. A dark roux, the holy trinity, seafood stock, shrimp and crab and oysters added in the last few minutes so they stay tender, ladled over rice. Teaches the roux-and-trinity foundation that underwrites the whole cuisine and the patience the technique demands.
2. [Creole jambalaya](jambalaya-creole.md). The city version of the rice pot, red with tomato where the Cajun version is brown. Trinity, andouille, chicken, shrimp and rice cooked in the same pot until the rice absorbs every bit of the seasoning. Teaches the one-pot rice technique and one half of the Cajun-Creole split in a single dish.
3. [Crawfish étouffée](crawfish-etouffee.md). A blond-to-medium roux (lighter than gumbo), trinity, stock, crawfish tails and butter, finished gently and served over rice. Teaches the lighter-roux side of Louisiana cooking and the finish-fast move that keeps shellfish tender.
4. [Red beans and rice](red-beans-and-rice.md). The traditional Monday dish, built on the Sunday ham bone, simmered for hours with andouille and trinity until the beans go creamy. Teaches the slow-stew one-pot, the bone-stock economy, and the rice-and-beans foundation that runs from Senegal through the Caribbean into the Gulf.
5. [Shrimp po-boy](shrimp-po-boy.md). Fried Gulf shrimp on light, shattery New Orleans French bread, dressed with shredded lettuce, tomato, pickles, mayonnaise and a shake of hot sauce. Teaches the city sandwich tradition and the importance of New Orleans bread, a uniquely light French loaf with no real analogue elsewhere.
6. [Muffuletta](muffuletta.md). The Sicilian-Italian contribution: a round sesame loaf split and filled with mortadella, salami, capicola, provolone and a generous spoon of marinated olive salad, pressed until the bread soaks up the olive oil. Teaches the immigrant strand of the city's cooking and how a sandwich can sit overnight and only get better.
7. [Bread pudding with whiskey sauce](desserts/bread-pudding-whiskey-sauce.md). Stale French bread, eggs, milk, sugar and cinnamon baked until set, served warm with a butter-and-bourbon sauce poured over the top. Teaches the use-it-up economy of the home kitchen and the dessert tradition that runs from the French restaurants of the Quarter back to every Louisiana grandmother.

After those seven recipes you have the dark roux, the holy trinity, the one-pot rice technique, the lighter étouffée finish, the Monday bean stew, the city sandwich on French bread, the immigrant olive-salad strand, and the Sunday dessert. That is a working spine of the state cuisine, and it sits across both Cajun and Creole without belonging entirely to either.
