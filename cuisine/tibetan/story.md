# Tibetan

*The high-altitude kitchen of the roof of the world. Momos (the dumpling family the diaspora made famous), thukpa noodle soup, the staple tsampa (roasted barley flour), and butter-tea by the bowl. A cuisine adapted to thin air and short growing seasons.*

## A short history

Tibetan cooking is the cuisine of the Tibetan Plateau, an average altitude of 4,500m where almost nothing grows except barley, the buckwheat-relative qingke. The earliest settlers, sheep-and-yak pastoralists who arrived from the Qiang highlands more than four thousand years ago, learned to live off animals that could thrive on thin grass and frozen air. The yak became the keystone: yak meat for protein, yak butter for fat (still the universal fat and the tea-thickener), and the dri (the female yak) for the milk that the kitchen turns into curd, cheese and yoghurt. Goats and sheep filled in around the edges, and barley, planted at the lowest viable altitudes in the river valleys, became the only reliable grain.

The arrival of Buddhism from India in the 7th century, under Songtsen Gampo and later Padmasambhava, slowly shifted attitudes toward killing animals without removing meat from the diet. Monastic communities developed their own kitchen, leaning on dairy, barley and root vegetables. Trade with the Chinese east opened in the same period along the Tea Horse Road, the high mountain route that ran Yunnan and Sichuan tea bricks west into Tibet in exchange for warhorses. The tea changed everything. Brewed strong, churned with yak butter and salt, *po cha* became the daily drink and the carbohydrate vehicle, slipping into tsampa to make the small dough balls that have been the staple meal for a thousand years.

Mongol contact in the 13th century, when the Yuan dynasty extended its reach across the plateau, brought the steamed-dumpling tradition that became *momo*, cousin to Chinese baozi and Mongolian buuz. Newari merchants from Kathmandu carried the dumpling techniques back and forth across the Himalayas, and the spice cabinet expanded: dried red chilli, garlic, ginger, Sichuan pepper from the eastern marches, and a small kit of warming spices from the Indian south. Hand-pulled noodles arrived from the same Chinese trade routes, and *thukpa* (noodle soup) settled in as the everyday warming bowl. Sha balep (meat-stuffed bread) and the closely related khabse (festival fried breads) carry the same dough tradition into other shapes.

Tibet stayed largely closed to the outside world until the 20th century, and the kitchen reflects that isolation. There is no Columbian Exchange cuisine here in any meaningful sense: chillies arrived late, the potato later still, and tomatoes barely registered until the 1980s. Tsampa, butter tea, momo, thukpa and dried yak meat (*sha kampo*) are recognisably the same foods Tibetan travellers ate when Younghusband's British expedition marched into Lhasa in 1904.

The Chinese annexation of 1950 and the Dalai Lama's flight into exile in 1959 created the modern split. Dharamsala in northern India became the seat of the Tibetan government-in-exile, and from there the diaspora spread to Nepal, Bhutan, the United States, Canada, Switzerland and beyond. Diaspora cooks codified the restaurant version of Tibetan food, leaning into momo and thukpa as the signature dishes and adapting to local ingredients (chicken in place of yak, refined wheat flour, ketchup-based hot sauces). Inside Tibet, the cultural-political situation remains fraught and Han migration has changed the everyday food landscape of Lhasa. Outside it, momos and butter tea have entered the global food vocabulary, and a Tibetan restaurant in Queens or Toronto can serve a meal that would be recognisable in a Lhasa teahouse of a century ago.

## Regional variations

- **U-Tsang (central Tibet, Lhasa)**: codified mainstream. Momo, thukpa, tsampa.
- **Amdo (north-east, Qinghai)**: closer to Chinese influence. More mantou-style steamed breads.
- **Kham (east, Sichuan border)**: more vegetables, more spicy seasonings (Sichuanese influence).
- **Diaspora (Dharamsala, Nepal, NYC)**: the codified restaurant tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Tsampa**: roasted barley flour. The staple grain.
- **Yak butter and yak meat**: the celebration foods.
- **Dri cheese (chura)**: dried, sometimes very dried (chura kampo, the rock-hard kind).
- **Tea (po cha)**: churned with yak butter and salt, drunk by the bowl.
- **Chilli powder**: dried red, used as a condiment.
- **Garlic and ginger**: in momo fillings and soups.
- **Soy sauce**: from Chinese trade.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Thukpa](/recipes/cuisine/tibetan/thukpa)**: noodle soup with yak meat (or chicken in the diaspora), vegetables and Tibetan spices. The most-eaten Tibetan dish.
2. **[Thenthuk](/recipes/cuisine/tibetan/thenthuk)**: hand-pulled noodle soup, pieces of dough torn directly into the broth. Distinct from thukpa.
3. **[Tingmo](/recipes/cuisine/tibetan/tingmo)**: steamed knotted bread, similar to Chinese mantou. The carbohydrate accompaniment.
4. **[Sha Balep](/recipes/cuisine/tibetan/sha-balep)**: fried bread stuffed with spiced minced beef or yak. The street-food classic.
5. **[Shapta](/recipes/cuisine/tibetan/shapta)**: stir-fried beef with onion, pepper and Tibetan spices, served over tingmo or rice.
6. **[Momo Burger](/recipes/cuisine/tibetan/momo-burger)**: the diaspora invention, momo-spiced filling in burger format.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with po cha (yak-butter-and-salt tea, drunk by the bowl, refilled constantly) or with chang (Tibetan barley beer). For a proper Tibetan meal: thukpa as starter, momos as main, sha balep as snack, finish with sweet butter tea.
