# Texas

*Big country, big cuisine: Mexican-American Tex-Mex, German-Czech-Polish smoked-meat barbecue, Gulf seafood, Hill Country game, and the all-state religion of beef cooked slow over post-oak.*

## A short history

Texas cooking starts on Indigenous ground that runs back thousands of years. The Caddo of east Texas farmed corn, beans and squash along the Red River and pit-cooked deer and bear in earth ovens; the Karankawa of the Gulf coast smoked oysters and fish on green-wood racks; the Comanche, Apache and Wichita of the plains followed the buffalo and dried strips of meat into pemmican that travelled. The earth-oven barbacoa technique that survives in south Texas pit-cooking of cow's heads on weekends comes straight out of that older cooking; the word barbacoa itself is a Taino borrowing carried north by the Spanish.

Texas was Mexico until 1836, and the Spanish-Mexican foundation runs underneath everything that came after. Spanish missions and presidios from the 1690s onward (San Antonio de Valero, the Alamo, was founded in 1718) brought cattle, sheep, horses, wheat, garlic and the Catholic feast calendar; Spanish ranchers crossed Andalusian cattle with the longhorns that became the Texas breed. Cattle ranching, the corn tortilla, the dried chile, mesquite over fire, beans and rice on a tin plate: these were here before the Republic, before statehood, before anyone drew a county line. The vaquero became the cowboy, his saddle and his food crossed with him, and the chuck-wagon cooking that ran the cattle drives north up the Chisholm Trail after 1867 was a direct descendant of Mexican rancho food cooked over open fire.

After independence and annexation in 1845, the 19th century brought waves of German, Czech, and Polish immigrants into Central Texas. They settled New Braunfels and Fredericksburg, Castroville, West, and Schulenburg, and they brought sausage-making, smokehouse craft, and a meat-market culture that knew how to handle a whole hog or a side of beef. Those immigrant butchers married their technique to African-American barbecue tradition carried west from the Deep South (enslaved cooks had been pit-roasting whole hogs in east Texas since plantation days, and the post-Emancipation freedmen took those techniques into the meat-market trade), and the union produced the Central Texas style: brisket rubbed with salt and pepper, smoked low over post-oak for a dozen hours, sliced thick on butcher paper, eaten without sauce.

The Mexican border, meanwhile, gave the state its second mother cuisine. Tex-Mex grew up in San Antonio and the Rio Grande Valley, fed by ranch cooks and the famous Chili Queens who served bowls of red on the San Antonio plaza from the 1880s onward, and it produced fajitas (born in the Valley ranches in the 1960s when cooks claimed the skirt steak the butchers were throwing out), breakfast tacos, chili con carne, queso, and the enchilada under chili gravy. The 20th century saw oil money, the rise of Houston as a megacity, and another wave of immigration: Vietnamese refugees settled the Gulf Coast after 1975 and folded lemongrass and fish sauce into the Cajun crawfish boil, producing the Viet-Cajun hybrid that defines a Houston spring weekend. The anchor principles hold: beef, corn tortilla, smoke, and chile, cooked with cumin and oregano and an aggressive sense of regional pride.

The modern Texas barbecue revival, from roughly 2005 onward, made the Central Texas style internationally famous. Franklin Barbecue (Aaron Franklin, opened 2009) put four-hour queues onto the morning news; the line of Lockhart and Taylor temples (Kreuz Market, Smitty's, Black's, Louie Mueller's) became pilgrimage sites; and a generation of younger Texas pitmasters started exporting the technique worldwide. Tex-Mex went global through the chain restaurant boom of the 1980s and 1990s, and the breakfast taco crossed every state line. The everyday table in Texas now reads exactly as it always has, but louder: brisket on butcher paper, a basket of tortillas, a bowl of red, a cold longneck.

## Regional variations

- **Central Texas (Austin, Lockhart, Taylor)**: the barbecue heartland. Brisket rubbed with salt and pepper, post-oak fire, sliced thick, no sauce on the meat. Kreuz Market, Snow's, and Franklin's are the institutions, and the meat-market lineage runs straight back to the German and Czech butchers of the 1880s.
- **East Texas (the Piney Woods)**: a wetter, South-leaning barbecue style. Chopped beef sandwiches piled on white bread, hickory rather than post-oak, more sauce, sometimes pork shoulder and pork ribs in the mix.
- **South Texas and the Rio Grande Valley**: Mexican-Texan border food. Carne asada over mesquite, fajitas (born in the Valley ranches in the 1960s when cooks claimed the skirt steak the butchers were throwing out), barbacoa pit-cooked from a cow's head on weekends, and the breakfast taco eaten standing up at six in the morning.
- **Hill Country (New Braunfels, Fredericksburg)**: the German-Czech smokehouse legacy. Kolaches in every gas station bakery, dry sausage links coiled in the meat case, schnitzel-and-fries diners along Main Street.
- **Gulf Coast (Galveston, Corpus Christi)**: Gulf shrimp, oysters from the bays, redfish on the half-shell, and the Vietnamese-Cajun crawfish boil that defines a Houston spring weekend.
- **West Texas and Big Bend**: cowboy chuck-wagon cooking. Cabrito (kid goat) over mesquite, dry-rubbed steaks, beans cooked in a Dutch oven, the food of long distances and longer days.
- **Tex-Mex (statewide)**: fajitas on a sizzling cast-iron platter, queso with a spoon, breakfast tacos in foil, chili con carne with no beans (this is a hill to die on), and enchiladas drowned in chili gravy under a layer of yellow cheese.

Across all of it, one rule: the meat is the point, and the meat is usually beef.

## Signature ingredients

Brisket is the state meat, the long pectoral muscle of the steer, full of connective tissue that breaks down only after ten or twelve hours over wood. Post-oak is the state firewood, mild and clean, the smoke that defines Central Texas barbecue. Texas chili powder leans ancho and cumin, sweeter and less smoky than New Mexican blends, and it carries chili con carne and the chili gravy under enchiladas. Cumin is the second pillar of the Tex-Mex spice cabinet, browned in oil before anything else hits the pot. Masa makes the corn tortilla, hand-pressed in border kitchens and machine-rolled in city ones. Jalapeño is the everyday chile, pickled on every taqueria counter and sliced into cornbread batter. Queso fresco and shredded Monterey Jack handle the dairy, the first crumbled cold and the second melted hot. Refried beans (frijoles refritos, mashed pinto beans cooked in lard or oil) are the table starch on a Tex-Mex plate. And on the Gulf, the shrimp is the ingredient that ties Texas to Louisiana and to Vietnam at the same time.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Smoked brisket](./smoked-brisket.md)**: the central act of Texas cooking. Salt, pepper, post-oak smoke, twelve hours, a long rest. Get this right and you understand Central Texas.
2. **[Fajitas](./fajitas.md)**: skirt steak grilled hot and fast, sliced across the grain, piled into flour tortillas with peppers and onions. The Rio Grande Valley invention that conquered the country in the 1980s.
3. **[Texas chili](./texas-chili.md)**: a bowl of red. Cubed beef, dried chiles, cumin, no beans, no tomato in the strict version. The state dish since 1977.
4. **[Migas](./migas.md)**: the Tex-Mex breakfast scramble: eggs, torn tortilla, jalapeño, tomato, cheese, all folded together in a skillet. Diner food at its most honest.
5. **[Queso](./snacks/queso.md)**: chile con queso, the molten dip that anchors every Tex-Mex appetizer round. Yellow cheese, green chile, tomato, eaten with tortilla chips while you wait for the entrees.
6. **[Texas cornbread](./side-dishes/texas-cornbread.md)**: skillet-baked, leaner and less sweet than the Southern version, often shot through with jalapeño and sharp cheddar. The bread that rides next to the chili.
7. **[Pecan pralines](./desserts/pecan-pralines.md)**: brown sugar, cream, butter, native Texas pecans, dropped in patties on parchment to set. The candy of San Antonio's River Walk and every Hill Country bakery.

Cook these seven and the state opens up: smoke and beef in the first half, the Mexican border in the middle, and the German-Czech sweet-tooth at the end.
