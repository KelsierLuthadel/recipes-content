# Laotian

*The kitchen of sticky rice. Eaten with the hands, dipped into a thousand dipping sauces, and served alongside grilled meats, raw-vegetable platters and the fiery papaya-and-fish-sauce salad that gave the world som tam.*

## A short history

Laotian cooking is the lesser-known sibling of Thai, sharing many dishes but with deeper roots in the indigenous Tai-Kadai tradition that predates the Thai kingdoms by centuries. The sticky rice (khao niao) is the defining starch — eaten in central Thailand only in the north-east (which is culturally Lao), but in Laos it's the rice everyone eats, every meal, formed into balls in the hand and dipped into the side dishes.

The cuisine sits on the Mekong River — fish from the Mekong (especially the giant pa beuk catfish), grilled meats over open fires, fermented fish sauce (padaek, similar to but stronger than Thai nam pla), wild herbs and bitter greens foraged from the forest. The mortar and pestle is the central tool; pounded laap (chopped meat salad with toasted rice powder and chilli) and tam mak hung (green papaya salad — Laos's original version of som tam) are the two signature plates.

French colonisation (1893–1953) left a baguette tradition (the Lao sandwich, khao jee pâté, is the local banh mi). The post-1975 civil war drove a significant diaspora to France, Australia, the US and Canada, where the cuisine is finally getting its own restaurant identity (distinct from Thai).

## Regional variations

- **Vientiane (capital)**: codified mainstream. Laap, tam mak hung, kao soi (the Lao version, different from the Thai-Burmese kao soi).
- **Luang Prabang (north)**: more elaborate. Or lam (jeow-thickened stew), sai oua (lemongrass sausage), the Royal Lao cuisine tradition.
- **Champasak (south)**: closer to Thai. The padaek tradition strongest here.
- **Isan (north-east Thailand, culturally Lao)**: same kitchen across the border. Som tam, laap, gai yang.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sticky rice (khao niao)**: glutinous rice, steamed in conical bamboo baskets. The starch of every meal.
- **Padaek**: fermented fish sauce, thicker and funkier than Thai nam pla.
- **Galangal, lemongrass, kaffir lime**: the aromatic trinity.
- **Bird's eye chillies**: very hot, used fresh or dried.
- **Lime**: the acid.
- **Toasted rice powder (khao kua)**: ground from dry-fried sticky rice; the textural anchor in laap.
- **Wild herbs**: pak phai (sawtooth coriander), holy basil, lemongrass leaves.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Laap](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/laap)**: chopped meat (beef, chicken, fish or duck) tossed with toasted rice powder, chilli, lime, fish sauce, mint, coriander and shallot. Eaten with sticky rice and a raw-vegetable platter. The national dish.
2. **[Tam Mak Hung](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/tam-mak-hung)**: green papaya pounded in a mortar with chilli, lime, fish sauce, tomato, long bean, garlic. The Lao papaya salad — funkier than Thai som tam (more padaek).
3. **[Khao Soi Lao](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/khao-soi-lao)**: Lao soup noodles with minced pork and fermented soybean. Different from the Burmese-Thai khao soi (no coconut curry here).
4. **[Or Lam](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/or-lam)**: Luang Prabang stew with buffalo or beef, wood-ear mushroom, sakhan (a peppery vine) and jeow bong chilli paste.
5. **[Sai Oua](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/sai-oua)**: pork sausage with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf and chilli, charcoal-grilled.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Beerlao (the national lager) or with lao-lao (sticky-rice spirit). For a proper meal: sticky rice in the centre, multiple small plates around it, raw vegetables and herbs in a basket, and the laap or tam mak hung dictating the flavour profile.
