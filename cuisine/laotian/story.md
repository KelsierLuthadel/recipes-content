# Laotian

*The kitchen of sticky rice. Eaten with the hands, dipped into a thousand dipping sauces, and served alongside grilled meats, raw-vegetable platters and the fiery papaya-and-fish-sauce salad that gave the world som tam.*

## A short history

Laotian cooking is the lesser-known sibling of Thai, sharing many dishes but with deeper roots in the indigenous Tai-Kadai tradition that predates the Thai kingdoms by centuries. The earliest farmers of the middle Mekong valley were domesticating glutinous rice as far back as 4,000 years ago; sticky rice (khao niao) is not just a Lao preference, it is the original southeast Asian rice that the rest of the region later abandoned for the easier non-glutinous varieties. Central Thailand eats sticky rice only in the north-east (which is culturally Lao), but in Laos it is the rice everyone eats, every meal, formed into balls in the hand and dipped into the side dishes.

The cuisine sits on the Mekong River: fish from the Mekong (especially the giant pa beuk catfish), grilled meats over open fires, fermented fish sauce (padaek, stronger than Thai nam pla), wild herbs and bitter greens foraged from the forest. The mortar and pestle is the central tool. The kingdom of Lan Xang (Land of a Million Elephants, 1353 to 1707), founded by Fa Ngum after his return from exile at the Khmer court, gave the cuisine its first courtly codification. Royal Lao cooking from the Luang Prabang palaces leaned on slow stews thickened with toasted-rice powder, banana-leaf parcels of seasoned fish, and the elaborate dipping-sauce (jeow) repertoire that still defines a proper Lao meal.

The 16th and 17th centuries brought the Columbian Exchange via Portuguese traders in Siam and Khmer territory. Chillies arrived from Mexico via Manila and the Spice Islands and quickly took over the kitchen: pre-chilli Lao food relied on pepper, ginger and galangal for heat, but within a few generations the bird's-eye chilli was central to laap, jeow and tam mak hung. Tomato, peanut, papaya and corn arrived on the same wave. Pounded laap (chopped meat salad with toasted rice powder and chilli) and tam mak hung (green papaya salad, Laos's original version of som tam) are the two signature plates, and neither would exist in its modern form without the Columbian Exchange.

French colonisation (1893 to 1953) brought the baguette, condensed milk in coffee, pâté and the café tradition that still runs through Vientiane and Luang Prabang. Khao jee pâté, the Lao baguette sandwich filled with pork pâté, pickled carrot, cucumber and chilli, is the local cousin of Vietnamese banh mi. The French also commercialised coffee growing on the Bolaven Plateau in the south, where Lao Arabica is still grown today. Independence in 1953 was followed by two decades of civil war, the secret American bombing campaign of 1964 to 1973 (Laos remains the most heavily bombed country per capita in history) and the 1975 communist takeover that drove an enormous Lao diaspora to France, Australia, the US (especially Minnesota and California) and Canada.

For decades the diaspora cooking was sold under Thai signage because Thai food had the brand recognition. That is finally changing. The 2010s and 2020s have seen a wave of openly Laotian restaurants in Minneapolis, Sydney, Paris and London, championed by chefs like Saeng Douangdara and the Hmong-Lao home-cook movement on social media. Back home, Vientiane and Luang Prabang carry a quiet restaurant renaissance built on Mekong fish, foraged forest ingredients and the deep dipping-sauce tradition. The kitchen reads as it has for centuries: sticky rice in the centre, small plates around it, herbs and raw vegetables in a basket alongside.

## Regional variations

- **Vientiane (capital)**: codified mainstream. Laap, tam mak hung, kao soi (the Lao version, different from the Thai-Burmese kao soi).
- **Luang Prabang (north)**: more elaborate. Or lam (jeow-thickened stew), sai oua (lemongrass sausage), the Royal Lao cuisine tradition.
- **Champasak (south)**: closer to Thai. The padaek tradition strongest here.
- **Isan (north-east Thailand, culturally Lao)**: same kitchen across the border. Som tam, laap, gai yang.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sticky rice (khao niao)**: glutinous rice, steamed in conical bamboo baskets. The starch of every meal.
- **Padaek**: fermented fish sauce, thicker and funkier than Thai nam pla.
- **Galangal, lemongrass, kaffir lime**: the aromatic trinity.
- **Bird's eye chillies**: very hot, used fresh or dried.
- **Lime**: the acid.
- **Toasted rice powder (khao kua)**: ground from dry-fried sticky rice; the textural anchor in laap.
- **Wild herbs**: pak phai (sawtooth coriander), holy basil, lemongrass leaves.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Laap](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/laap)**: chopped meat (beef, chicken, fish or duck) tossed with toasted rice powder, chilli, lime, fish sauce, mint, coriander and shallot. Eaten with sticky rice and a raw-vegetable platter. The national dish.
2. **[Tam Mak Hung](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/tam-mak-hung)**: green papaya pounded in a mortar with chilli, lime, fish sauce, tomato, long bean, garlic. The Lao papaya salad, funkier than Thai som tam (more padaek).
3. **[Khao Soi Lao](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/khao-soi-lao)**: Lao soup noodles with minced pork and fermented soybean. Different from the Burmese-Thai khao soi (no coconut curry here).
4. **[Or Lam](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/or-lam)**: Luang Prabang stew with buffalo or beef, wood-ear mushroom, sakhan (a peppery vine) and jeow bong chilli paste.
5. **[Sai Oua](/recipes/cuisine/laotian/sai-oua)**: pork sausage with lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf and chilli, charcoal-grilled.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Beerlao (the national lager) or with lao-lao (sticky-rice spirit). For a proper meal: sticky rice in the centre, multiple small plates around it, raw vegetables and herbs in a basket, and the laap or tam mak hung dictating the flavour profile.
