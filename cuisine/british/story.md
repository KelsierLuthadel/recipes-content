# British

*Island cooking built on the roast, the pie, the pub lunch and the chip shop. Often maligned, quietly excellent when done properly, and stitched together by hundreds of regional specialities most Britons will defend to the last.*

## A short history

British cooking's reputation took a beating after the Second World War, when rationing (which lasted until 1954, nine years after the war ended) hollowed out kitchen skill across a generation. The pre-war cuisine had been rich, regional, and built around farming the country still did: beef from the Midlands, lamb from Wales and the North, fish from every coast, dairy from the West Country, root vegetables from every garden. Rationing's fourteen years of careful spreading-thin and substitute-everything-with-Spam left a country that had forgotten what its own larder was supposed to taste like.

The recovery started in the 1980s with Marco Pierre White's London kitchens and accelerated through the 1990s with Rick Stein, Gary Rhodes, Delia Smith and Jamie Oliver bringing technique and produce back into focus. By the 2000s the gastropub had arrived; by the 2010s the country had Michelin stars in places no one would have predicted in 1985. The current British scene is among the most exciting in Europe — a working-out of what the cuisine actually is, with the regional larders back in active use.

Underneath the chef-led story is the home cooking that never went away. The Sunday roast (beef, lamb or chicken with Yorkshire pudding, potatoes, two veg and gravy), the cottage pie, the bangers and mash, the fish and chips, the bacon sandwich, the full English breakfast — all still anchored daily life through the lean decades and still do.

## Regional variations

- **England (North)**: rich, fatty, beef-and-pudding country. Yorkshire pudding, Lancashire hotpot, parkin, Eccles cakes, fish and chips at their birthplace.
- **England (South-West)**: dairy heaven. Clotted cream, cheddar, Cornish pasties, scrumpy cider, the cream tea tradition.
- **England (South-East)**: vegetable and fruit gardens. Kent's orchards and hops, Sussex pondings, the Surrey-Hampshire bacon-and-egg breakfast.
- **Wales**: lamb, leeks, laverbread (seaweed), Welsh rarebit, Glamorgan sausages, bara brith.
- **Scotland**: oats (porridge, oatcakes), haggis, salmon, whisky, the deep-fried Mars bar joke that everyone repeats and no one actually eats.
- **Northern Ireland**: the Ulster fry, soda bread, champ (mashed potato with spring onion), wheaten bread.

## Signature ingredients

- **Butter**: salted, on everything. Welsh and West Country dairy is the benchmark.
- **Cheddar**: West Country mature cheddar is the proper version — sharp, crumbly, the world's most copied cheese.
- **Beef and lamb**: grass-fed, rare-breed where possible. The Sunday roast spine.
- **Smoked fish**: kippers (Craster), smoked salmon (Scotland), smoked haddock (the base of cullen skink).
- **Mustard**: English mustard is much hotter than French. The proper accompaniment to roast beef.
- **Brown sauce, HP, Worcestershire**: the trinity of British table condiments, all built on tamarind, molasses and vinegar.
- **Potatoes**: roasted, mashed, boiled, chipped, baked — the spine starch.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the roast, the pie tradition, the breakfast plate and the chip shop. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Beef Wellington](/recipes/cuisine/british/beef-wellington)**: fillet of beef wrapped in mushroom duxelles and puff pastry, baked till the pastry crisps and the beef stays rare. The grandest of British roasts and the dish that announces Christmas in many houses.
2. **[Cottage Pie](/recipes/cuisine/british/cottage-pie)**: beef mince stewed with carrot, onion and stock under a mashed potato crust. Use lamb and it becomes shepherd's pie; either way it's the British weeknight one-pot.
3. **[Lancashire Hotpot](/recipes/cuisine/british/lancashire-hotpot)**: lamb chops with onion and potato slices, slow-baked till the potatoes brown on top. The North's signature stew and the most patient dish in the British repertoire.
4. **[Fish and Chips](/recipes/cuisine/british/fish-and-chips)**: battered haddock or cod with double-fried chips, mushy peas, malt vinegar, salt. The national dish, perfected by London immigrants in the 1860s.
5. **[Full English Breakfast](/recipes/cuisine/british/full-english-breakfast)**: eggs, bacon, sausage, mushrooms, tomato, baked beans, black pudding, fried bread, hash brown, toast. The hangover cure that became a national identity.
6. **[Bangers and Mash](/recipes/cuisine/british/bangers-and-mash)**: pork sausages, buttery mash, onion gravy. The cheapest, most satisfying pub plate in the country.
7. **[Cheese and Onion Pie](/recipes/cuisine/british/cheese-and-onion-pie)**: mature cheddar and softened onion under a shortcrust lid. The vegetarian classic that proves British pie can be more than meat.

Cook the Wellington and the cottage pie and you have the pie shelf. The hotpot and the bangers are the weeknight braise. The fish and chips, the breakfast and the cheese and onion pie are the British everyday at its best.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries Welsh classics (cawl, Welsh rarebit, leek cakes), Scottish staples (cullen skink, stovies, scotch broth), the regional pies (Cornish pasty, Forfar bridie, pork pie), and the breakfast and tea tradition (scones, crumpets, Yorkshire tea bread). British food is a regional cuisine pretending to be national; pick a corner and dig in.
