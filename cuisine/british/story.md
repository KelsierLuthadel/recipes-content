# British

*Island cooking built on the roast, the pie, the pub lunch and the chip shop. Often maligned, quietly excellent when done properly, and stitched together by hundreds of regional specialities most Britons will defend to the last.*

## A short history

The British kitchen starts in the Iron Age. The Celtic tribes who held the island before the Romans grazed cattle and sheep on the hills, kept pigs in the oak woods, hunted game in the forests, and baked unleavened bread on flat stones over open fires. The Romans arrived in 43 CE and stayed nearly four centuries, bringing wheat varieties that still grow here, the orchards (cherry, plum, apple), the herb garden (parsley, mint, fennel, mustard) and the first proper ovens. When they left, the Anglo-Saxons brought the open hearth and the iron cauldron, the brewing of ale from barley, and the broth-and-bread country table that ran the country for the next thousand years.

The medieval centuries layered spice on top of that. The Norman Conquest of 1066 dragged English cooking into the European mainstream, and the rich tables of the abbeys and castles ran on pepper, cinnamon, cloves, saffron and ginger imported through Venice and Bruges. The crusades brought sugar and almonds; the wool trade with Flanders paid for them. The earliest English cookbook, *The Forme of Cury* (1390, from the kitchens of Richard II), is a spice-heavy document closer to medieval Persian cooking than to anything modern. The pie tradition was already mature: a "coffin" of hot-water pastry baked around meat and gravy is a 14th-century invention and the direct ancestor of the pork pie and the Cornish pasty.

The Tudor and Stuart centuries codified the British roast. Henry VIII's kitchens roasted whole oxen on the spit; Sunday roast beef became the national dish by Elizabeth's reign, and the French nicknamed the English "les rosbifs" by the 17th century. The Columbian Exchange added the potato (slowly, treated with suspicion until the 18th century), the turkey (replacing the Christmas goose for most families by 1700), and tomatoes (even more slowly: well into the 19th century the English ate them in chutneys rather than salads). Tea arrived in the 1660s through the East India Company and within a century was the national drink. Sugar from the Caribbean plantations, refined in Bristol and Liverpool on the back of the slave trade, gave the country its sweet tooth.

The Industrial Revolution remade everything. By 1850 most Britons lived in cities, not on farms, and the cuisine pivoted to feed factory workers: fish and chips (perfected by Jewish-Portuguese immigrants in London's East End in the 1860s), pork pies sold from market stalls, condensed milk in the tea, jam on bread, the heavy puddings of the railway hotel. Mrs Beeton's *Book of Household Management* (1861) codified middle-class cooking for the next century. The Empire brought curry powder (an English invention, blending Indian spices for British storerooms), Worcestershire sauce (Lea & Perrins, 1837, an attempted copy of an Indian recipe), kedgeree, and the chutney shelf.

The two world wars and the rationing that followed broke the chain. Rationing lasted until 1954, nine years after the war ended, and it hollowed out kitchen skill across a generation. The pre-war cuisine had been rich, regional, and built around the farming the country still did: beef from the Midlands, lamb from Wales and the North, fish from every coast, dairy from the West Country, root vegetables from every garden. Fourteen years of careful spreading-thin and substituting-everything-with-Spam left a country that had forgotten what its own larder was supposed to taste like.

The recovery started in the 1980s when Marco Pierre White's London kitchens fired back up, and accelerated through the 1990s as Rick Stein, Gary Rhodes, Delia Smith and Jamie Oliver pushed technique and produce back into focus. By the 2000s the gastropub had arrived; by the 2010s the country had Michelin stars in places no one would have predicted in 1985. The current British scene is among the most exciting in Europe, a working-out of what the cuisine actually is, with the regional larders back in active use. Underneath the chef-led story is the home cooking that never went away. The Sunday roast (beef, lamb or chicken with Yorkshire pudding, potatoes, two veg and gravy), the cottage pie, bangers and mash, fish and chips, the bacon sandwich, the full English breakfast: they all anchored daily life through the lean decades and still do.

## Regional variations

- **England (North)**: rich, fatty, beef-and-pudding country. Yorkshire pudding, Lancashire hotpot, parkin, Eccles cakes, fish and chips at their birthplace.
- **England (South-West)**: dairy heaven. Clotted cream, cheddar, Cornish pasties, scrumpy cider, the cream tea tradition.
- **England (South-East)**: vegetable and fruit gardens. Kent's orchards and hops, Sussex pondings, the Surrey-Hampshire bacon-and-egg breakfast.
- **Wales**: lamb, leeks, laverbread (seaweed), Welsh rarebit, Glamorgan sausages, bara brith.
- **Scotland**: oats (porridge, oatcakes), haggis, salmon, whisky, the deep-fried Mars bar joke that everyone repeats and no one actually eats.
- **Northern Ireland**: the Ulster fry, soda bread, champ (mashed potato with spring onion), wheaten bread.

## Signature ingredients

- **Butter**: salted, on everything. Welsh and West Country dairy is the benchmark.
- **Cheddar**: West Country mature cheddar is the proper version, sharp, crumbly, the world's most copied cheese.
- **Beef and lamb**: grass-fed, rare-breed where possible. The Sunday roast spine.
- **Smoked fish**: kippers (Craster), smoked salmon (Scotland), smoked haddock (the base of cullen skink).
- **Mustard**: English mustard is much hotter than French. The proper accompaniment to roast beef.
- **Brown sauce, HP, Worcestershire**: the trinity of British table condiments, all built on tamarind, molasses and vinegar.
- **Potatoes**: roasted, mashed, boiled, chipped, baked. The spine starch.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the roast, the pie tradition, the breakfast plate and the chip shop. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Beef Wellington](/recipes/cuisine/british/beef-wellington)**: fillet of beef wrapped in mushroom duxelles and puff pastry, baked till the pastry crisps and the beef stays rare. The grandest of British roasts and the dish that announces Christmas in many houses.
2. **[Cottage Pie](/recipes/cuisine/british/cottage-pie)**: beef mince stewed with carrot, onion and stock under a mashed potato crust. Use lamb and it becomes shepherd's pie; either way it's the British weeknight one-pot.
3. **[Lancashire Hotpot](/recipes/cuisine/british/lancashire-hotpot)**: lamb chops with onion and potato slices, slow-baked till the potatoes brown on top. The North's signature stew and the most patient dish in the British repertoire.
4. **[Fish and Chips](/recipes/cuisine/british/fish-and-chips)**: battered haddock or cod with double-fried chips, mushy peas, malt vinegar, salt. The national dish, perfected by London immigrants in the 1860s.
5. **[Full English Breakfast](/recipes/cuisine/british/full-english-breakfast)**: eggs, bacon, sausage, mushrooms, tomato, baked beans, black pudding, fried bread, hash brown, toast. The hangover cure that became a national identity.
6. **[Bangers and Mash](/recipes/cuisine/british/bangers-and-mash)**: pork sausages, buttery mash, onion gravy. The most satisfying pub plate in the country.
7. **[Cheese and Onion Pie](/recipes/cuisine/british/cheese-and-onion-pie)**: mature cheddar and softened onion under a shortcrust lid. The vegetarian classic that proves British pie can be more than meat.

Cook the Wellington and the cottage pie and you have the pie shelf. The hotpot and the bangers are the weeknight braise. The fish and chips, the breakfast and the cheese and onion pie are the British everyday at its best.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries Welsh classics (cawl, Welsh rarebit, leek cakes), Scottish staples (cullen skink, stovies, scotch broth), the regional pies (Cornish pasty, Forfar bridie, pork pie), and the breakfast and tea tradition (scones, crumpets, Yorkshire tea bread). British food is a regional cuisine pretending to be national; pick a corner and dig in.
