# Mexican

*A cuisine of corn, chillies, and fire, built on the three-thousand-year-old Mesoamerican trinity of maize, beans and squash, then layered with Spanish, Lebanese and Asian inflections.*

## A short history

Mexican cooking starts with corn. Mesoamerican civilisations perfected nixtamalisation, soaking maize in alkaline water to free its niacin and produce *masa*, around 1500 BCE in what is now Guatemala and southern Mexico, and the technique still sits behind tortillas, tamales, sopes and pozole today. Without it, a corn-based diet causes pellagra. With it, you get the foundation of an entire culinary tradition. The Olmec, Maya and Zapotec civilisations that flourished between roughly 1500 BCE and 900 CE built their kitchens on the *milpa* (the three-sisters intercrop of corn, beans and squash), domesticated the turkey and the Muscovy duck, fermented cacao into a bitter ceremonial drink, and learned to dry and smoke chillies for year-round use.

The Aztec capital Tenochtitlan, at its height around 1500, was one of the largest cities in the world and ran an industrial-scale market in cacao, vanilla, chia, amaranth, tomato, tomatillo, avocado and the dozens of chilli varieties bred over thousands of years of selection. Aztec cooks prepared mole-like sauces (*molli*, the word literally means sauce) of toasted chillies, seeds and aromatics ground on the *metate*; tamales steamed in banana or corn leaves; pozole with hominy and meat; and the chocolate drink that emperors took foamy and unsweetened with chilli, vanilla and achiote.

The Spanish conquest of 1519 to 1521 brought pork, beef, lamb, dairy, wheat, rice, sugar cane, citrus, onions, garlic and the wine press, which folded into existing techniques rather than displacing them. The fundamental Mexican-Spanish trade ran both ways: Mexico sent the Old World tomatoes, chocolate, vanilla, corn, beans, squash, avocado, peanut, pineapple, papaya, turkey and every chilli that now defines half the cuisines of Europe and Asia (where would Italian, Indian, Thai, or Hungarian food be without that exchange?), and in return absorbed the techniques of frying in lard, baking enriched breads, stewing in cazuelas, and ageing cheese. The convent kitchens of Puebla, Oaxaca and Mexico City became the laboratories where Spanish nuns and indigenous cooks worked side by side to invent mole poblano, chiles en nogada, rompope and a hundred regional specialities still on the table today.

The colonial centuries from 1521 to 1821 entrenched the *casta* system and a layered food culture where high-status colonial kitchens kept Spanish-European technique while *mestizo* and indigenous cooks ran the everyday market. The Manila Galleon trade route, which ran from 1565 to 1815 between Acapulco and the Philippines, brought tamarind, mango, coconut and a deep Asian influence to the Pacific coast cuisine of Guerrero and Colima. Independence in 1821 began the slow process of building a national cuisine; the Porfirio Díaz era of the late 19th century saw French haute cuisine fashionable in the upper classes while a parallel rural tradition fed everyone else.

Late-19th-century immigration reshaped the cities. Lebanese arrivals to Puebla and the Yucatán in the 1880s and 1890s brought *shawarma*, the spit-roasted lamb tradition, and the kibbe; over the next half-century Mexican cooks adapted the spit to pork, marinated it with the dried-chilli paste already in the kitchen, and produced *tacos al pastor*, now the signature taco of Mexico City. The mid-20th-century arrival of Cantonese workers in Mexicali (drawn by the cotton boom and barred by the US Chinese Exclusion Act) shaped that border region's distinct Chinese-Mexican fusion: the city has more Chinese restaurants per capita than anywhere else in Mexico, and the Cantonese-style burrito is real.

The Mexican Revolution of 1910 to 1920 and its aftermath fed an explicit national pride in indigenous-mestizo food. The 1940s through 1960s saw cookbook authors like Josefina Velázquez de León codify regional recipes that had only existed in oral tradition. Industrial tortilla production, the spread of supermarkets, and the rise of fast food brought new pressures from the 1970s on, and a counter-movement of cocineras tradicionales (women carrying the regional recipes forward) has worked since the 1990s to keep the deep traditions alive. UNESCO inscribed traditional Mexican cuisine on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2010, the first cuisine ever to receive that distinction. The result is layered but rooted. A tortilla under almost everything; chillies of multiple varieties (ancho, guajillo, chipotle, pasilla) used for distinct flavour profiles rather than just heat; salsas built-on-the-table as seasoning; corn, beans, squash and tomato as the everyday vegetables.

## Regional variations

Mexico is fifty times the size of Belgium and the cuisine reflects that scale.

- **Oaxaca**: the mole capital. Seven moles, each a complex sauce of chillies, spices, seeds, nuts, sometimes chocolate, simmered for hours. Tlayudas, chapulines (grasshoppers), string cheese.
- **Yucatán**: Mayan plus Caribbean roots. Achiote (annatto) gives the signature orange to dishes like cochinita pibil. Habanero is the chilli of choice. Citrus marinades.
- **Puebla**: mole poblano (the most famous mole), chiles en nogada, cemita sandwiches. The state where Lebanese immigration shaped al pastor.
- **Northern (Sonora, Chihuahua)**: beef-heavy, wheat tortillas instead of corn, more grilled meats. Burritos, machaca, carne asada.
- **Mexico City and central**: tacos al pastor on tall trompo spits, street corn (elote), tortas, the world's most concentrated taquería culture.
- **Veracruz**: coastal, Caribbean and Spanish influences. Veracruz-style fish (huachinango a la veracruzana) is the signature.

A taco in Mexico City, a taco in Oaxaca and a taco in Tijuana are three different dishes united only by the wrap.

## Signature ingredients

- **Masa** (nixtamalised corn dough): the foundation of tortillas, tamales, sopes
- **Dried chillies**: ancho (sweet, raisin-like), guajillo (bright, tangy), pasilla (deep, plum-like), chipotle (smoky), morita, mulato. Each used for different flavour profiles.
- **Tomatillo**: the husk-wrapped green "tomato", base of salsa verde
- **Cumin, oregano (Mexican), coriander leaf, epazote**: the daily aromatic four
- **Lime**: squeezed over almost everything as a finisher
- **Avocado**: guacamole, in tacos, with eggs
- **[Fajita seasoning](../../base-ingredients/spices/fajita-seasoning-mix.md)** and **[Taco spice](../../base-ingredients/spices/taco-spice.md)**: house-blends for everyday cooking
- **Queso fresco, queso Oaxaca**: fresh plus string cheese; not the same as cheddar-style "Mexican cheese" sold abroad

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Salsa verde](salsa-verde.md). Start with salsa. Tomatillos and chillies blended raw or charred. Teaches the chilli-balance principle every Mexican sauce leans on.
2. [Salsa roja](snacks/salsa-roja.md). The red counterpart. Toasted dried chillies blended with tomato and aromatics. Teaches the toast-then-blend chilli-paste move that underpins moles too.
3. [Pork carnitas](pork-carnita.md). Pork slow-cooked in lard until tender then crisped. Teaches the long-cook-then-finish technique central to taquería cooking.
4. [Tacos al pastor](tacos-al-pastor.md). The Lebanese-influenced spit-roast pork, achiote-marinated, served with pineapple. Teaches al-pastor marination plus the late-pineapple sweet-and-sour balance.
5. [Mole poblano](mole-poblano.md). Puebla's chocolate-spiked sauce. Teaches the toasted-then-blended chilli paste process behind every mole.
6. [Chiles rellenos](chiles-rellenos.md). Poblanos stuffed with cheese, battered, fried. Teaches the egg-foam batter that makes chiles rellenos light not stodgy.
7. [Cochinita pibil](cochinita-pibil.md). Yucatecan pork in achiote and bitter orange. Teaches the banana-leaf-wrap braise that defines slow Yucatecan cookery.

After those seven you have the chilli vocabulary, the marinade technique, the mole framework, and the regional sense of how the cuisine layers.
