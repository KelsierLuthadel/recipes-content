# German

*The cuisine of pork, potato, bread and beer. A working kitchen that perfected the sausage, codified the pickle, and built the heaviest, most satisfying winter table in Europe.*

## A short history

German cooking grew up around the cold winter and the long growing seasons of the Baltic-Central European plain. The Germanic tribes that the Romans wrote about (the Suebi, the Saxons, the Franks) lived on rye and barley porridges, smoked pork, hunted game, wild honey and fermented mares' milk; the Romans were horrified by the butter on the table (Mediterranean kitchens used olive oil) and the heavy beer drinking. The forest-and-clearing pattern of settlement put pork at the centre, because pigs forage in oak woods on acorns and beech mast, and the smokehouse built into the chimney is still the right way to handle the autumn slaughter. Rye and wheat bread, cabbage (fresh and fermented), root vegetables, pork (the most efficient animal to overwinter), and beer (safer than water for centuries) make up the backbone.

The medieval centuries laid down the regional shelf. Charlemagne planted apple, pear and cherry orchards across the Rhineland in the 8th century, and the monastic breweries of Bavaria (Weihenstephan, founded 1040, still operating) and the Cistercian wine vineyards of the Rhine and Mosel turned the kitchen onto its drinking traditions. The Hanseatic League trade routes from the 1100s pulled in salt herring from the Baltic, dried cod from Norway, spices from the East and salt from Lüneburg, and the burgher kitchens of Hamburg, Lübeck, Bremen and Cologne wrote down the gingerbread and the sweet-and-sour braise (Sauerbraten started here). The Reinheitsgebot beer purity law of 1516 codified the country's beer culture five hundred years before any modern food regulation, and the regional Wurst (sausage) tradition spread into hundreds of named varieties; the Nuremberg, Thuringian, Frankfurt and Weisswurst styles all carry medieval guild lineages.

The Columbian Exchange of the 16th and 17th centuries dropped two ingredients into the German kitchen that changed everything. The potato arrived from Spanish ports in the late 1500s, Frederick the Great pushed it onto Prussian farmers from the 1740s ("the Potato Edicts"), and within a generation Germany was the most potato-dependent country in Europe. Boiled, mashed, fried, dumplinged (Klösse, Knödel), in salads, in pancakes: Kartoffel became the second staple after bread. Tomatoes, paprika and beans arrived too, but more slowly. The Thirty Years' War (1618-1648) ruined the country and starved a third of the population to death, and the recovery kitchen that emerged from the ashes leaned hard on cabbage, potato, pork and the long preservation traditions that kept families alive through famine.

Christmas baking is a story of its own. Stollen, Lebkuchen, Springerle, Pfeffernüsse and the Advent calendar all came out of German kitchens, and the Christmas market tradition runs back to the 14th century. The bread culture matches: Germany has the most diverse bread tradition in the world, with over 300 regional varieties under UNESCO intangible cultural heritage protection. The 19th-century industrial era packed workers into the Ruhr and Saxony, and the kitchen answered with the things you could eat with one hand at a factory bench (Currywurst's ancestors, Frikadellen, Brötchen with smoked sausage). German emigration to the Americas in the same decades exported the Frankfurter, the Hamburger, the pretzel and the lager beer to the United States, where they became "American" food without losing their German DNA.

The 20th century carried German food through the two world wars, the lean post-war years and the divided decades. The eastern (DDR) kitchen developed its own pantry under shortage conditions (Soljanka borrowed from the Soviet shelf, Letscho from Hungary, Würzfleisch as the everyday meat dish), and Reunification in 1990 brought those eastern dishes back into the broader repertoire. The post-war Gastarbeiter migrations brought Turkish, Italian and Greek cooking into German cities; the Berlin Döner kebab, invented by Turkish-German cooks in the 1970s, is now Germany's biggest fast-food category by sales. Today's German kitchen sits between the inherited everyday (schnitzel, sausage, sauerkraut, potatoes) and a quietly excellent fine dining scene led by Berlin, Hamburg and Munich.

## Regional variations

- **Bavaria (south)**: the most-known German shelf. Weisswurst (white sausage), pretzels, sauerkraut, Schweinshaxe (roast pork knuckle), the beer-garden tradition.
- **Swabia (south-west)**: spätzle country. Maultaschen (stuffed pasta-dumplings), käsespätzle, Schwarzwald (Black Forest) ham and gateau.
- **Rheinland (west)**: Sauerbraten (vinegar-marinated braised beef), Himmel und Erde (heaven and earth: black pudding, apple, potato).
- **North (Hamburg, Schleswig-Holstein)**: the maritime kitchen. Labskaus (corned beef hash), Matjes herring, Pannfisch.
- **Berlin and the East**: Currywurst, Königsberger Klopse (meatballs in caper sauce), Soljanka (sour stew).
- **Saxony**: Quark, Stollen (the Dresden Christmas cake), Wickelklösse (potato dumplings).

## Signature ingredients

- **Pork**: every part of it. The sausage tradition is the most diverse in the world.
- **Rye bread (Vollkornbrot, Pumpernickel)**: dense, sour, long-keeping. The everyday loaf.
- **Sauerkraut**: fermented cabbage. The vegetable of winter.
- **Potatoes**: boiled, mashed, fried, dumplinged (Klösse, Knödel).
- **Caraway and juniper**: the spice pair under most pork dishes.
- **Mustard**: medium-sharp German mustard sits on every Wurst plate.
- **Beer**: not just a drink. A cooking ingredient (Bierkuchen, Schweinshaxe in dark beer) and the universal pairing.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the schnitzel, the sausage, the sauerbraten and the spätzle. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Wiener Schnitzel](/recipes/cuisine/german/wiener-schnitzel)**: veal cutlet pounded thin, breaded, shallow-fried in clarified butter till golden. The Austrian-German classic; serve with parsley potatoes and a lemon wedge.
2. **[Jägerschnitzel](/recipes/cuisine/german/jagerschnitzel)**: pork schnitzel with mushroom cream sauce. The everyday German lunch and the easier door into the schnitzel shelf.
3. **[Sauerbraten](/recipes/cuisine/german/sauerbraten)**: beef marinated in vinegar, wine and spices for three to five days, slow-braised, served with a sweet-sour gingerbread-thickened gravy. The Rhineland's grand Sunday roast.
4. **[Schweinshaxe](/recipes/cuisine/german/schweinshaxe)**: pork knuckle slow-roasted till the skin crackles and the meat falls off the bone. Bavaria's beer-garden showpiece.
5. **[Käsespätzle](/recipes/cuisine/german/kasespatzle)**: hand-scraped egg noodles layered with grated Emmental and topped with caramelised onions. The Swabian equivalent of mac and cheese.
6. **[Königsberger Klopse](/recipes/cuisine/german/konigsberger-klopse)**: tender meatballs in a creamy caper-and-lemon sauce. The East Prussian classic, beloved on both sides of pre-reunification Germany.
7. **[Rouladen](/recipes/cuisine/german/rouladen)**: thin beef slices rolled around bacon, onion, pickle and mustard, braised slow in red wine. The Sunday family dish across northern and central Germany.

Cook the two schnitzels and you have the breaded-cutlet shelf. The sauerbraten and the rouladen are the long braise. The Schweinshaxe is the celebration roast. The Käsespätzle is the comfort food; the Klopse the everyday family meal.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the sausage shelf (Bratwurst, Weisswurst), the dumpling tradition (Maultaschen, Knödel), the bread family (Pretzel, Pumpernickel), and the Christmas baking canon (Stollen, Lebkuchen, Pfeffernüsse). Pair everything with a Pilsner, a Helles or a Weissbier and the cuisine sings.
