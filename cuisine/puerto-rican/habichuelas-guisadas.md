# Habichuelas Guisadas

![Habichuelas Guisadas](resources/habichuelas-guisadas.jpg)

*Puerto Rico's stewed pink beans: pink beans (or red kidney beans) slow-simmered in a sofrito base with cubed pumpkin (calabaza), tomato sauce, olives, capers and a small amount of sazón, into a thick savoury bean stew. The Boricua everyday vegetarian main, eaten over white rice as the canonical "arroz con habichuelas".*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus optional overnight bean soaking)

**Cook Time:** 1 hour 15 minutes

## Overview
Habichuelas guisadas ("stewed beans") is Puerto Rico's everyday bean stew and one of the most beloved staples of Boricua home cooking. Pink beans (red kidney, pinto or small red substitute) slow-simmer in a base of fragrant sofrito with cubed calabaza pumpkin (butternut squash works), sliced olives, capers, a touch of sazón and bay leaves, until the beans are soft, the pumpkin has half-dissolved into the broth and the pot has reduced into a thick savoury stew. Sofrito is the heart of the dish; without it, you have generic bean stew. The calabaza is the secret ingredient, half-dissolving during cooking to give the broth body and subtle sweetness that sets the Puerto Rican version apart. The olives and capers are essential too for the proper Boricua brininess. Vegetarian-friendly by default; some recipes add a smoked ham hock for extra depth. Eaten over plain white rice as arroz con habichuelas, the everyday meal across the island, or as a side alongside pernil or bistec encebollado.

## Ingredients

### Beans
- 500 g cooked pink beans (about 2 cans, drained and rinsed; or 250 g dried pink beans soaked overnight and pre-cooked till just tender)
- 600 ml hot water (or bean cooking liquid)
- 200 ml tomato sauce (or 3 tablespoons tomato paste + 200 ml water)

### Sofrito and aromatics
- 4 tablespoons olive oil
- 1 large onion (finely chopped)
- 1 medium green bell pepper (finely chopped)
- 6 garlic cloves (crushed)
- 1 medium tomato (chopped)
- 4 tablespoons sofrito (see existing puerto-rican sofrito recipe; or substitute: 2 tablespoons each of finely chopped fresh coriander, parsley, and culantro if available)
- 1 small bunch fresh coriander (chopped)

### Vegetables
- 300 g calabaza pumpkin (or butternut squash; peeled and cut into 2 cm cubes)
- 1 medium carrot (peeled and finely diced; optional but common)
- 1 small potato (peeled and cubed; optional, gives extra body)

### Seasoning
- 2 tablespoons tomato paste
- 1 tablespoon sazón (or substitute: 1 teaspoon achiote + 1 teaspoon garlic powder + ½ teaspoon ground coriander)
- 1 tablespoon adobo seasoning (or 1 teaspoon salt + 1 teaspoon garlic powder + 1 teaspoon oregano)
- 1 ½ teaspoons fine sea salt (taste before adding; sazón is salty)
- 1 teaspoon ground cumin
- 1 teaspoon dried oregano
- 1 teaspoon ground black pepper
- 2 bay leaves
- 80 g pitted green olives (sliced; manzanilla olives are canonical)
- 2 tablespoons capers (drained, optional)
- 1 small fresh chilli (deseeded, finely chopped; optional)

### To serve
- Cooked white rice (or arroz blanco / Puerto Rican rice)
- Fresh coriander leaves
- Avocado slices
- Pique (Puerto Rican vinegar hot sauce)
- Lime wedges

## Method

### Stage 1 - Sweat the aromatic base
1. Heat the olive oil in a large heavy saucepan over medium heat.
2. Add the chopped onion; cook 6-7 minutes till soft and starting to caramelise.
3. Add the chopped green pepper; cook 4 minutes more.
4. Add the crushed garlic; cook 30 seconds.
5. Add the chopped fresh tomato; cook 3-4 minutes till the tomato breaks down.

### Stage 2 - Add the sofrito and pastes
1. Add the sofrito and tomato paste; stir into the aromatic base.
2. Cook 2 minutes till the tomato paste deepens in colour.
3. Add the sazón, adobo, cumin, oregano and black pepper; cook 1 minute till fragrant.

### Stage 3 - Add liquid and beans
1. Pour in the tomato sauce, then the hot water (or bean cooking liquid).
2. Add the cooked beans, the cubed calabaza, the diced carrot (if using), and the cubed potato (if using).
3. Add the bay leaves.
4. Stir well; bring to a simmer.

### Stage 4 - Simmer
1. Reduce heat to low; partially cover with the lid.
2. Cook 50-60 minutes; stir occasionally to prevent sticking.
3. The pumpkin should begin to break down into the broth; the broth should thicken naturally as the starches release.
4. If the stew is too thick, add a splash of hot water; if too thin, simmer uncovered for 10 more minutes.

### Stage 5 - Finish
1. Add the sliced olives and capers; cook 5 more minutes.
2. Taste; adjust salt and pepper (carefully; olives and capers are salty).
3. Take off the heat; remove the bay leaves.
4. Stir in most of the chopped coriander; reserve some for garnish.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Spoon hot white rice into wide bowls.
2. Ladle generous portions of habichuelas guisadas over the rice.
3. Garnish with the reserved fresh coriander.
4. Top with sliced avocado.
5. Drizzle with pique for heat.
6. Squeeze lime over each bowl.

## Notes
- **Sofrito is essential:** the foundational Puerto Rican green sauce gives the dish its Boricua character. Use homemade or the substitute approximation (chopped fresh coriander, parsley, culantro). Without it, the dish lacks Puerto Rican identity.
- **Calabaza for body:** the cubed pumpkin half-dissolves during cooking and gives the broth body and sweetness. Butternut squash is the easy substitute outside Puerto Rico. Don't skip.
- **Olives and capers for brininess:** the Boricua flavour profile includes these as essential aromatic components. Skip only if you really don't have them; they make the dish.
- **Sazón gives the colour:** sazón (the orange-red commercial seasoning) gives the dish its characteristic deep colour. Achiote powder + garlic powder + spices is the workable substitute.
- **Pink beans are canonical:** small pink beans are the proper Puerto Rican choice. Red kidney beans are the closest substitute; pinto beans work too. Black beans give a different (Cuban-leaning) flavour profile.

## Variations
**With ham (habichuelas con jamón):** add 200 g of diced smoked ham or a smoked ham hock at the start of stage 3; cook with the beans. Common non-vegetarian variation.
**Black bean version (habichuelas negras):** swap pink beans for black beans; gives a deeper darker stew. Closer to Cuban moros y cristianos style.
**With chorizo:** add 150 g of sliced chorizo (Spanish-style) to the pan with the aromatics; renders the fat into the dish and adds spice.
**With ham hock and pig's tail:** the traditional "habichuelas con patitas" includes pig's tail or ham hock for extra depth; cook for 90 minutes with the beans for the meat to soften.

## Serving
Over plain white rice as arroz con habichuelas (the canonical Puerto Rican "rice and beans" combination) or as a side alongside pernil, bistec encebollado or pollo guisado. With tostones or maduros. With pique for heat and lime wedges. Drink: Medalla beer, agua de tamarindo, or a glass of cold mauby.

## Storage
- Keeps refrigerated 5 days; the flavour deepens noticeably overnight (many Puerto Rican cooks deliberately make it the day before).
- Reheat gently in a covered pan with a splash of water (or bean liquid) over low heat.
- Freezes 3 months in portioned containers; defrost in the fridge.
- Day-old leftover habichuelas guisadas is perfect for breakfast over hot toast or with a fried egg.
- Don't microwave aggressively; the beans go off-texture.
