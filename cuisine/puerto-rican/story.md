# Puerto Rican

*The kitchen of the Caribbean's most US-shaped island. Built on sofrito, adobo, plantain in every form, and the long Sunday lechón. Spanish, African and Taíno layers in every plate, with an American overlay since 1898.*

## A short history

Puerto Rican cooking rests on three roots: the indigenous Taíno (cassava, sweet potato, corn, the barbacoa cooking method); the Spanish colonisers from 1493 (rice, pork, citrus, beef, the sofrito tradition); and the enslaved West African population (plantain, okra, the slow-stewed bean dishes, the use of palm oil and bitter green vegetables). The cuisine is closely related to Cuban and Dominican but has its own distinct signatures — the heavier hand on adobo, the use of mofongo as a starch family rather than a single dish, and the obsession with plantain.

The 1898 US annexation brought English-language ingredient labels, McDonald's, and the diaspora migration north (boricuas to New York and Florida). The diaspora kitchens preserved the codified Puerto Rican shelf while the island absorbed American snacks and sandwich formats. The current scene swings between abuela-traditional and chef-driven nuevo criollo (José Enrique, 1919, the San Juan scene).

## Regional variations

- **San Juan and the coast**: seafood, lechón asado, the codified mainstream.
- **Mountains (Ponce, central interior)**: hearty cooking. Asopao (rice stews), guinea (pintade), more pork.
- **West (Mayagüez)**: brazo gitano (sweet jellyroll), heavier Spanish influence.
- **Diaspora (New York, Orlando)**: the Nuyorican kitchen with bodega sandwiches and lechoneras.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sofrito**: onion, garlic, bell pepper, ají dulce (sweet pepper), cilantro, recao (long-leaf coriander). The base of every stew.
- **Adobo**: dry seasoning of garlic powder, oregano, salt, pepper, turmeric. Rubs everything.
- **Sazón**: powdered spice blend with annatto and coriander. The orange-red colour you see on most Puerto Rican rice.
- **Plantain**: green for tostones (twice-fried) and mofongo; ripe for maduros (sweet, soft) and amarillos.
- **Ají dulce**: sweet small pepper, no heat. The flavour base of sofrito.
- **Bacalao (salt cod)**: Lenten staple, year-round favourite.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Mofongo](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/mofongo)**: green plantain mashed with garlic, salt and pork crackling, served with chicken or shrimp on top. The signature dish.
2. **[Pernil](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/pernil)**: pork shoulder slow-roasted overnight with garlic, adobo and oregano. The Christmas Eve centrepiece.
3. **[Arroz con Pollo](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/arroz-con-pollo)**: chicken-and-rice cooked together with sofrito, sazón, olives and capers.
4. **[Asopao de Pollo](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/asopao-de-pollo)**: chicken and rice in a soupy broth — the Puerto Rican answer to risotto.
5. **[Habichuelas Guisadas](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/habichuelas-guisadas)**: stewed pink beans with sofrito, sazón and pumpkin. The everyday side.
6. **[Pasteles](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/pasteles)**: grated root-vegetable masa filled with pork, wrapped in banana leaves, boiled. The Christmas tamale.
7. **[Carne Guisada](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/carne-guisada)**: beef slow-braised with sofrito, tomato sauce and potato. The Sunday dish.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven the catalogue carries the shellfish shelf (camarones al ajillo), the pollo guisado weeknight, the bistec encebollado (onion-smothered steak). Pair with Medalla beer or a piña colada (invented in San Juan, 1954).
