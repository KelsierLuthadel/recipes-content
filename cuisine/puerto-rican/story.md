# Puerto Rican

*The kitchen of the Caribbean's most US-shaped island. Built on sofrito, adobo, plantain in every form, and the long Sunday lechón. Spanish, African and Taíno layers in every plate, with an American overlay since 1898.*

## A short history

Puerto Rican cooking rests on three roots, and the first of them is older than the island's Spanish name. The Taíno people, an Arawak group who had settled the island they called Borikén by around 1000 CE, grew cassava (yuca), sweet potato (batata), corn, beans, squash and ají chillies on small forest clearings, fished the reefs and bays, and cooked meat over a wooden frame they called barbacoa (the word that became "barbecue"). Cassava bread (casabe), made from peeled, grated and pressed yuca toasted on a clay griddle, is still the most direct surviving Taíno food; Puerto Rican Spanish keeps dozens of Taíno words on the plate (yuca, batata, ají, maíz, jíbaro, guayaba, ñame), and the island's geographic place names are mostly Taíno too. The civilisation collapsed within fifty years of Columbus landing in 1493, but the kitchen kept its skeleton.

Spanish colonisation reshaped almost everything else. From the 16th century onwards the Spanish brought wheat (which struggled in the tropics), rice (which thrived and became the daily starch), pork, beef, citrus, garlic, onion, olive oil, vinegar and the slow stew. The sofrito tradition (a finely chopped, slow-cooked aromatic base of onion, garlic, peppers and herbs) crossed from the Spanish kitchen into the Puerto Rican one and stayed at the centre. The Spanish also imported enslaved West Africans from the early 16th century, eventually settling more than a hundred thousand people on the island, mostly from Yoruba, Igbo and Kongo cultures. That population brought plantain (which had reached West Africa from Southeast Asia through Arab trade), okra, the slow-stewed bean dishes, the use of palm oil and bitter green vegetables, and the deep frying tradition that produced the alcapurria and the bacalaíto.

The 19th century settled the kitchen into roughly its present shape. Spanish loyalists fleeing the independence wars in South America (after 1810) and free Black Caribbean families arriving from neighbouring islands all enriched the cooking. Mofongo, the dish that defines modern Puerto Rican food, emerged in the slave kitchens of this period as a Caribbean cousin to West African fufu: green plantain mashed with garlic, salt and pork crackling, served as a whole starch family rather than a single dish. The cuisine is close kin to Cuban and Dominican (the three islands share the sofrito, the rice-and-beans base and the long-roasted pork) but Puerto Rican cooking carries its own signatures: a heavier hand on adobo, mofongo as a whole genre, and an unshakeable obsession with plantain in every stage of ripeness.

The 1898 US annexation, after the Spanish-American War, turned Puerto Rico into an American territory and remade the food system within a generation. American agricultural companies took over the sugar industry, English-language ingredient labels appeared in the corner colmados, and US Department of Agriculture programmes in the early 20th century pushed wheat flour, canned milk and processed cheese onto the island. The Jones Act of 1917 made Puerto Ricans US citizens, opening the great diaspora migration to New York and later Chicago, Florida and beyond. Boricua kitchens in El Barrio (East Harlem) kept the Puerto Rican shelf intact and developed their own variants (the Nuyorican pernil sandwich, the bodega coffee) while the island absorbed American snacks and sandwich formats. The 1950s "Operation Bootstrap" industrialisation kept that exchange running both ways.

The post-Hurricane Maria recovery (after 2017) and the broader Caribbean food renaissance have brought Puerto Rican cooking into a sharper modern focus. The current scene swings between abuela-traditional cooking on home stoves and the chef-driven nuevo criollo movement (José Enrique on the San Juan waterfront, 1919, Vianda) that takes the island's larder (local pork, fresh-caught snapper, breadfruit, recao, coffee from the central highlands) and pushes it into a contemporary register. The lechoneras of Guavate, where whole pigs turn on spits over wood fires every weekend, sit at the other end of the same conversation: the cooking changes, but the long Sunday lechón doesn't move.

## Regional variations

- **San Juan and the coast**: seafood, lechón asado, the mainstream.
- **Mountains (Ponce, central interior)**: hearty cooking. Asopao (rice stews), guinea (pintade), more pork.
- **West (Mayagüez)**: brazo gitano (sweet jellyroll), heavier Spanish influence.
- **Diaspora (New York, Orlando)**: the Nuyorican kitchen with bodega sandwiches and lechoneras.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sofrito**: onion, garlic, bell pepper, ají dulce (sweet pepper), cilantro, recao (long-leaf coriander). The base of every stew.
- **Adobo**: dry seasoning of garlic powder, oregano, salt, pepper, turmeric. Rubs everything.
- **Sazón**: powdered spice blend with annatto and coriander. The orange-red colour you see on most Puerto Rican rice.
- **Plantain**: green for tostones (twice-fried) and mofongo; ripe for maduros (sweet, soft) and amarillos.
- **Ají dulce**: sweet small pepper, no heat. The flavour base of sofrito.
- **Bacalao (salt cod)**: Lenten staple, year-round favourite.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Mofongo](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/mofongo)**: green plantain mashed with garlic, salt and pork crackling, topped with chicken or shrimp. The signature dish.
2. **[Pernil](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/pernil)**: pork shoulder slow-roasted overnight with garlic, adobo and oregano. The Christmas Eve centrepiece.
3. **[Arroz con Pollo](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/arroz-con-pollo)**: chicken-and-rice cooked together with sofrito, sazón, olives and capers.
4. **[Asopao de Pollo](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/asopao-de-pollo)**: chicken and rice in a soupy broth, the Puerto Rican answer to risotto.
5. **[Habichuelas Guisadas](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/habichuelas-guisadas)**: stewed pink beans with sofrito, sazón and pumpkin. The everyday side.
6. **[Pasteles](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/pasteles)**: grated root-vegetable masa filled with pork, wrapped in banana leaves, boiled. The Christmas tamale.
7. **[Carne Guisada](/recipes/cuisine/puerto-rican/carne-guisada)**: beef slow-braised with sofrito, tomato sauce and potato. The Sunday dish.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven the catalogue carries the shellfish shelf (camarones al ajillo), the pollo guisado weeknight, the bistec encebollado (onion-smothered steak). Pair with Medalla beer or a piña colada (invented in San Juan, 1954).
