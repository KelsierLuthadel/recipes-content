# Crowdie

![Crowdie](resources/crowdie.jpg)

*Scotland's ancient fresh cheese: soured milk warmed till the curds separate from the whey, drained in muslin, mixed with salt and double cream, sometimes rolled in pinhead oatmeal.*

**Serves:** 6 (as a starter or cheese-board addition; makes about 300 g cheese)

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus 8-12 hours setting time)

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Crowdie is one of the oldest dairy products in the British Isles, with documented Scottish origins going back to at least the 8th century and possibly brought over by the Vikings. The technique is the same as the original "naturally soured" fresh cheeses found across northern Europe: whole milk sours naturally for a day or two, then warms gently till the curds separate from the whey, drains through muslin for several hours, then gets mixed with sea salt and a swirl of double cream. The finished texture sits somewhere between cottage cheese and ricotta but firmer than both. Sometimes flavoured with chopped chives or grated lemon zest, sometimes rolled in pinhead oatmeal or finely chopped toasted hazelnuts to form little round balls or a log. The traditional Highland breakfast cheese, spread on warm oatcakes with butter, alongside fresh radishes, or rolled into the famous "Caboc" with pinhead oatmeal.

## Ingredients

### For 300 g crowdie
- 2 litres whole milk (full-fat; not UHT)
- 2 tablespoons natural yogurt or buttermilk (the starter culture; or skip and let the milk naturally sour 24 hours)
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt (or to taste; do not over-salt - crowdie is subtle)
- 60 ml double cream
- Optional flavourings (pick one):
  - 2 tablespoons finely chopped fresh chives
  - 1 teaspoon finely grated lemon zest + 1 teaspoon chopped dill
  - 1 teaspoon coarsely cracked black pepper
- Optional coating (pick one):
  - 50 g pinhead oatmeal, lightly toasted (the traditional Caboc coating)
  - 50 g finely chopped toasted hazelnuts
  - 50 g cracked black peppercorns (the peppered version)

### Equipment
- A heavy-bottomed pot (don't use a thin pan - milk burns)
- A digital thermometer (essential)
- A square of muslin or cheesecloth (about 50 × 50 cm)
- A colander
- A bowl underneath to catch whey

### To serve
- Warm oatcakes (Nairn's or Walkers)
- Salted Scottish butter
- Sliced fresh radishes
- A small dish of heather honey (optional, for sweet contrast)
- A small dram of single-malt Scotch alongside

## Method

### Stage 1 - Sour the milk (24 hours ahead if doing naturally)
1. Pour the milk into a clean jar.
2. Stir in the yogurt or buttermilk (the starter culture).
3. Cover loosely; leave at room temperature (20-22°C) for 12-24 hours.
4. The milk should smell pleasantly sour and slightly tangy. If it's gone unpleasantly off (sharp, ammonia-like), discard.

### Stage 2 - Warm to separate the curds
1. Pour the soured milk into a heavy-bottomed pot.
2. Heat very gently over LOW heat, stirring occasionally.
3. Use the thermometer; bring the temperature slowly to 38-40°C (warm, not hot).
4. Continue holding at 38-40°C for 10-15 minutes.
5. The milk will gradually separate: cloudy white curds will float in clear yellowish whey.
6. Don't boil. Don't stir vigorously. Patience.

### Stage 3 - Drain
1. Line a colander with the muslin or cheesecloth (let it overhang the sides).
2. Place the colander over a deep bowl.
3. Gently pour the curds-and-whey through the muslin.
4. Let drain naturally for 30 minutes.
5. Then tie the muslin into a bundle (gather the corners and tie); hang from a hook or a tap over the bowl.
6. Drain a further 4-6 hours (or overnight) till the curds are firm but moist - somewhere between cottage cheese and ricotta.

### Stage 4 - Season and shape
1. Tip the drained curds into a clean bowl.
2. Add the salt; stir gently.
3. Stir in the double cream; the mixture should be soft and spreadable.
4. Stir in any optional flavourings (chives, lemon zest, dill, pepper).
5. Taste; adjust salt cautiously.

### Stage 5 - Shape (optional)
For Caboc-style:
1. Tip the crowdie onto a sheet of cling film.
2. Form into a log shape about 20 cm long and 4 cm diameter.
3. Spread the toasted pinhead oatmeal (or hazelnut or pepper) on a tray.
4. Roll the log in the coating to cover completely.
5. Wrap in cling film; chill 2 hours.

For balls:
1. Roll spoonfuls of crowdie into walnut-sized balls.
2. Roll each in the coating.
3. Place on a tray; chill 1 hour.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Slice the chilled log into 1 cm rounds.
2. Place on warm oatcakes with a curl of butter.
3. Add sliced radishes alongside.
4. Optional: a drop of heather honey for sweet contrast.
5. Serve with a glass of cold whisky-and-water.

## Notes
- **Gentle heat:** never boil. Boiling toughens the curds and the cheese gets rubbery.
- **Long drain:** 4-6 hours minimum; overnight is better. The texture is the point.
- **Don't over-salt:** crowdie is naturally tangy; over-salting masks it.
- **Use within a week:** fresh crowdie has no preservatives. Eat within 5-7 days.
- **The whey is useful:** save the whey for bread-making, scone-making, or smoothies.

## Variations
**Caboc (the traditional):** roll the log in toasted pinhead oatmeal. Highland Fine Cheeses of Tain made this commercial.
**Hazelnut crowdie:** roll in finely chopped toasted hazelnuts.
**Peppered crowdie:** roll in cracked black peppercorns (very Scottish; strong).
**Herb crowdie:** stir in chopped chives, dill, and parsley before serving.
**Whisky-soaked walnuts crowdie:** stir in 4 walnut halves that have been soaked in single malt overnight.
**With smoked salmon (canapé):** spread crowdie on oatcakes; top with a small piece of smoked salmon and a dill leaf.

## Serving
On warm oatcakes at a Scottish breakfast (the traditional setting) · on a Scottish cheese board alongside whisky and grapes · spread on oatcakes at a Highland picnic · rolled and sliced as a starter at a Burns Night supper · at a Scottish wedding canapé reception · at a tasting menu at a Highland restaurant alongside a small dram.

## Storage
- Fresh crowdie refrigerates 5-7 days, well-wrapped.
- Don't freeze (the texture suffers).
- The whey refrigerates 3 days; use for baking, soup, or smoothies.
- Caboc-style coated crowdie keeps better than plain (the oatmeal/nut coating creates a barrier).
- A small amount of crowdie blended with a little milk makes excellent salad dressing or sauce for fish.
