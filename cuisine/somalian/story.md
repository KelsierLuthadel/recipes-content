# Somali

*The Horn of Africa kitchen at the crossroads of East African, Arab and Indian Ocean trading traditions. Spiced rice (bariis), the camel-meat tradition, the famous Italian-Somali fusion (pasta as a colonial inheritance now fully naturalised), and a love of cardamom that runs through every meal.*

## A short history

Somali cooking goes back to one of the oldest maritime trade economies on the planet. The ancient land of Punt, which Egyptian pharaohs sent expeditions to from around 2500 BCE, sat somewhere along the modern Somali coast, and the frankincense, myrrh, gold and aromatic spices the Egyptians came for travelled the same Red Sea routes the Somali kitchen still draws on. The earliest cuisine of the Horn was built on what the pastoral economy could deliver: camel meat and milk from the dry interior, sheep and goats from the highlands, sorghum and pearl millet ground into flour for porridge and the canjeero (a sourdough flatbread similar to Ethiopian injera but lighter), wild honey for sweetening, and the long-stewed muqmad (dried-and-fried camel or goat preserved in ghee).

The medieval centuries pulled the cuisine into the Indian Ocean trading world. Arab merchants settled the coast from the 7th century on, founding the city-states of Mogadishu, Merca, Brava and Zeila that ran the gold-and-ivory trade between East Africa, the Persian Gulf and Gujarati India. Islam arrived early and quietly, banishing pork and embedding the lamb-and-rice celebration tradition. The Ajuran Sultanate (13th to 17th centuries) controlled the wells and the caravan routes; Mogadishu was a wealthy cosmopolitan port that Ibn Battuta visited in 1331 and described as a major centre of spice and cloth trade. Basmati rice came in from India by ship; cardamom, cinnamon, cloves and cumin from the Spice Islands and Yemen; the sambusa (the triangular fried pastry, a clear descendant of the Indian samosa) settled into the Ramadan iftar shelf.

The xawaash spice blend (cumin, coriander, cardamom, cinnamon, cloves and sometimes black pepper, ginger and turmeric) is the signature seasoning, and it tells the trade story in a single mortar: every spice in it came down a sea route or a caravan trail. The bariis iskukaris (the celebration rice with goat or lamb, raisins and the deeply caramelised onions) reads like the Somali cousin of biryani because, in a real sense, that's what it is, a dish built where Persian, Indian and Arab rice traditions met the East African meat shelf.

The Italian colonisation of southern Somalia (1889-1960, with British Somaliland in the north over the same span) left a deep mark, especially in Mogadishu. Pasta is now a Somali staple, with baasto iyo suugo (pasta with meat sauce) as everyday food and the local habit of eating pasta with a banana on the side (a Mogadishu speciality that mystifies outsiders, born from the colonial Italian habit of finishing a meal with fruit and naturalised into a single plate). The Italians also brought tomatoes, espresso (still drunk in Mogadishu cafés in the Italian style), the love of risotto-textured rice and the small-bite culinary social culture that Somali coffee houses inherited.

The post-independence decades, the brief unification of north and south, and then the collapse of central government in 1991 broke the country apart and built a robust Somali diaspora across Minneapolis, London, Stockholm, Toronto and Nairobi. The diaspora kitchens keep the tradition tight: Somali halal restaurants in Eastleigh, Cedar Riverside and east London now serve the canon to a new generation born outside Somalia. Inside the country, the home food has not changed much in centuries. The basic shelf, rice and spiced stews, lamb and goat, camel where you can get it, sambusas for Ramadan, anjero for breakfast, shaah (cardamom-and-clove sweet tea) all day, reads as it has for generations.

## Regional variations

- **Mogadishu and the south**: the mainstream. Bariis iskukaris, baasto iyo suugo, sambusa.
- **North (Somaliland, Hargeisa)**: more Arabian/Yemeni influence. More cardamom, more camel meat.
- **Pastoralist regions**: where the camel-meat tradition runs strongest. A milk-and-meat diet.

## Signature ingredients

- **Basmati rice**: in bariis, the everyday side.
- **Xawaash**: Somali spice blend (cumin, coriander, cardamom, cinnamon, cloves).
- **Camel meat**: in the north and pastoral regions.
- **Lamb and goat**: the everyday meat.
- **Bananas**: on the side of every plate (the Italian-Somali habit).
- **Cardamom**: in tea, in rice, in everything.
- **Lime**: the acid.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Bariis Iskukaris](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/bariis-iskukaris)**: basmati rice with goat or lamb and xawaash spices, topped with raisins. The Sunday-and-celebration plate.
2. **[Baasto iyo Suugo](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/baasto-iyo-suugo)**: pasta with spiced beef-and-tomato sauce. The Italian-Somali inheritance, eaten with a banana on the side.
3. **[Maraq Digaag](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/maraq-digaag)**: chicken soup with carrots, potato and xawaash. The everyday weeknight bowl.
4. **[Sambusa](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/sambusa)**: triangular pastry filled with spiced minced beef or lentils, deep-fried. The Ramadan iftar staple.
5. **[Suqaar](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/suqaar)**: small-cube beef stir-fried with onion, peppers and xawaash. The everyday quick meal.
6. **[Anjero](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/anjero)**: sourdough pancake (the Somali version of injera, lighter than Ethiopian). Breakfast staple.
7. **[Halwo](/recipes/cuisine/somalian/halwo)**: sweet, sticky, cardamom-and-ghee confection eaten at celebrations.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with shaah (cardamom-and-clove-spiced sweet tea with milk), or with camel's milk if you can find it. For a proper Somali table: bariis at the centre, a banana on the side (do not skip this; it's the universal Somali plate format), sambusa as starter, halwo to finish.
