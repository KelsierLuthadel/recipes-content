# Kuwaiti

*The kitchen of a Gulf trading port. Indian, Persian, Arabian and Bedouin layers, with the long-grain spiced rice (machboos) at the centre and date-stuffed sweets to close every meal.*

## A short history

Kuwait's kitchen begins with the desert and the date palm. The Bedouin tribes who roamed the northern Arabian peninsula long before any settled town existed lived on what their camels and goats could yield (milk, meat, hair-tents, leather) and on the date groves of the oases. Dates kept for a year without spoiling; mixed with samn (clarified butter) and ground grain they became the trail food that carried a caravan across the empty quarter. Harees, the slow-cooked wheat-and-meat porridge that still appears at every Ramadan iftar, is one of the oldest dishes in the Arab world, mentioned in 10th-century Baghdad cookbooks and almost certainly cooked over Bedouin fires long before that.

The founding of Kuwait Town in 1716 on a sheltered bay at the head of the Gulf turned a Bani Utub tribal settlement into a trading port. By the late 18th century, Kuwaiti dhows were sailing the Indian Ocean monsoon trade as far as Zanzibar, Mumbai and Calicut, and the kitchen filled up with the cargo: basmati rice from India, saffron and dried lime (loomi) from Persia, tamarind and turmeric and cardamom from the Indian Ocean spice trade, coffee from Yemen, sugar from East Africa. The baharat spice blend that defines Gulf cooking (allspice, black pepper, cumin, cardamom, cloves, cinnamon) is a direct fossil of that trade route. Pearl diving from May to September was the other economic engine: the divers ate dates, fish and harees on their dhows for four months at a stretch.

The early 20th century broke the old economy. Cultured Japanese pearls collapsed the Gulf pearl market in the late 1920s and Kuwait went briefly hungry. Oil discovery in 1938 and full commercial production from 1946 onwards transformed the country within a generation. The home table expanded: refrigeration meant fresh meat year-round, imported fruit became routine, and the country imported labour from the Indian subcontinent, Iran and the Philippines, layering curry, biryani, kebab and stir-fry onto the local shelf. Karak chai (the over-sweet, cardamom-spiced milk tea brought by Keralan workers) became the universal Kuwaiti street drink.

The 1990 Iraqi invasion and the seven-month occupation that followed scattered Kuwaitis across the region; the diaspora cooking that came back after liberation in 1991 carried Saudi, Bahraini and Egyptian touches. The everyday traditional table (machboos for lunch, harees for Ramadan, dates and gahwa for guests) has barely shifted across all of this. Modern Kuwait City has a restaurant scene that runs from machboos houses to high-end fusion, but the home kitchen still rests on rice, lamb, fish from the Gulf, dates and the Gulf spice cabinet.

Kuwaiti hospitality is intense. Guests get fed substantially even if they only meant to drop by. Refusing food twice is acceptable; refusing it three times is not.

## Regional variations

Kuwait is a small country and the cuisine is broadly homogenous, but there are nuances:

- **Coastal (the city)**: more seafood. Machboos samak (fish version), fried hammour.
- **Inland/Bedouin tradition**: more lamb, harees, the slow-cooked one-pot.
- **Sect-specific traditions**: Shia and Sunni households have slight variations, especially during Ramadan.

## Signature ingredients

- **Basmati rice**: long-grain, aged, the rice of choice.
- **Baharat**: warm Gulf spice blend (allspice, black pepper, cumin, cardamom, cloves, cinnamon).
- **Black lime (loomi)**: dried whole, dropped into rice and stews.
- **Saffron**: in machboos, in the celebration plates.
- **Tamarind**: in fish curries and chutneys.
- **Dates**: medjool and khalas; eaten daily, gifted constantly.
- **Rose water and cardamom**: in coffee and sweets.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Machboos](/recipes/cuisine/kuwaiti/machboos)**: spiced basmati rice with chicken, lamb or fish, topped with crispy fried onion, raisins and almonds. Served with daqoos (tomato-chilli sauce). The national dish.
2. **[Harees](/recipes/cuisine/kuwaiti/harees)**: cracked wheat and meat (lamb or chicken) slow-cooked for hours till they merge into a savoury porridge. The Ramadan staple.
3. **[Margoog](/recipes/cuisine/kuwaiti/margoog)**: lamb stew with hand-torn flatbread cooked in the broth till it absorbs everything.
4. **[Mathrooba](/recipes/cuisine/kuwaiti/mathrooba)**: chicken with rice and lentils mashed together into a savoury porridge.
5. **[Murabyan](/recipes/cuisine/kuwaiti/murabyan)**: prawn-and-rice dish with Kuwaiti spices and dried lime.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Arabic coffee (gahwa: bitter, cardamom-flavoured) served with dates, or with rose-cardamom-spiced karak chai (the milky tea brought by Indian expat workers). End with mahalabia (rose-water milk pudding) or gerss ogaily (the saffron-cardamom cake).
