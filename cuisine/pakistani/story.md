# Pakistani

*The cuisine of Punjabi-Persian crossroads. Karahi-fried meat, slow-simmered nihari, biryani at every wedding, and the heavy hand on ginger-garlic that distinguishes a Pakistani kitchen from its Indian neighbour.*

## A short history

Pakistani cooking shares its deep roots with North Indian (especially Punjabi) cuisine, and the deepest layer goes back to the Indus Valley Civilisation. Mohenjo-daro and Harappa, on what is now Pakistani soil, ran sophisticated cities from roughly 2500 BCE, growing wheat, barley, mustard, sesame and pulses, and herding the humped zebu cattle whose milk and ghee still anchor the kitchen. The Indus people were already grinding wheat into flatbread on stone querns four thousand years ago, and the chapati that comes off a Punjabi tava today is a direct descendant.

The Indo-Aryan migrations from around 1500 BCE brought the Vedic culture, and the medieval centuries layered Buddhist, Hindu and Jain traditions across the region. The big shift came with the Arab conquest of Sindh in 711 CE and the gradual arrival of Islam, which spread through Persian Sufi missionaries over the next five centuries. The Persian influence sits heavy in modern Pakistani cooking. Slow-cooked lamb, saffron, dried lime, pulao (the long-grain rice cooked with stock and whole spice, an idea that ran straight from Persia into the subcontinent), and the deep use of dried fruit and nuts in savoury dishes all trace to that long traffic. The Delhi Sultanate (1206 to 1526) brought Central Asian Turkic cooks who introduced the tandoor (a clay oven sunk in the ground, used for bread and kebab) and the seekh kebab. The Afghan kingdoms to the west fed in the chapli kebab, the kabuli pulao and the simple bread-and-meat tradition that still rules Peshawar.

The Mughal court (1526 to 1857), founded by Babur from Central Asia and based in Lahore, Delhi and Agra, codified what is now the high cuisine of the entire region. The Mughal kitchens fused Persian technique with Indian ingredient and produced biryani (layered rice and meat, sealed and slow-cooked over dough-lidded pots), nihari (overnight beef shank stew, originally a sunrise breakfast for soldiers), korma (yoghurt-based braised meat), shahi tukra (the bread-and-cream pudding), the slow-roasted whole lamb (raan), and dozens of kebab styles. Mughlai cooking is the backbone of the Pakistani restaurant menu wherever it travels. Chillies, tomatoes, potatoes, peanuts and coffee arrived through Portuguese trade ports from the 1500s on, and the kitchen absorbed every one. The chilli in particular became indispensable.

The British Raj (1858 to 1947) added the Anglo-Indian dishes that survive across the subcontinent (mulligatawny, the cutlet, the railway curry) but didn't fundamentally change the home kitchen. The partition of 1947 drew a line through what had been one continuous Mughal-influenced cooking, and the trauma of the displacement of fifteen million people reshaped both new countries' food maps. Refugee cooks from Delhi, Lucknow and Hyderabad brought their court traditions west into Karachi; refugee Punjabis carried their tandoor and karahi traditions both ways. Pakistan inherited Punjab, Sindh and the North-West Frontier (Pashtun) traditions; India kept the rest of North Indian cooking plus Hyderabad and the South.

The decades after partition saw distinct Pakistan-specific dishes codify. Karachi's Sindhi biryani became the spiciest biryani in the subcontinent. The karahi (wok-style cooking, where meat is fried hard with tomato, ginger and green chilli in a heavy iron pan) gave its name to a whole family of dishes and became the benchmark restaurant order. The chapli kebab from Peshawar, the Lahori chargha (whole spiced fried chicken), the Bohri thaal (the Karachi Dawoodi Bohra community's communal platter) all set themselves down in this period. The diaspora kitchen in the UK from the 1960s onward gave the world British curry house cooking; the BIR (British-Indian Restaurant) menu of chicken tikka masala, balti, lamb rogan josh and onion bhaji is more Pakistani in origin than Indian, mostly built by Mirpuri and Punjabi cooks from Azad Kashmir.

The modern Pakistani kitchen runs on the same Mughal-Punjabi-Pashtun-Sindhi foundation, with a heavier hand on ginger-garlic, chilli and yoghurt than its Indian neighbour, and the universal call to eat with proper hot tandoor naan and a glass of salted lassi.

## Regional variations

- **Punjab (Lahore, Faisalabad)**: the heartland. Karahi, nihari, paya, the tandoor tradition, the most-loved Pakistani regional cuisine.
- **Sindh (Karachi, Hyderabad)**: spice-heavy. Sindhi biryani (the most chilli-forward biryani), saag, the seafood from the Arabian Sea.
- **Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (Peshawar)**: Pashtun cooking. Chapli kebab, kabuli pulao, the simple lamb-and-bread tradition closer to Afghan food.
- **Balochistan**: sajji (whole roasted lamb seasoned only with salt), pit-cooked dishes, sparser ingredients.
- **Kashmir (Pakistan-administered)**: Kashmiri pulao, rogan josh, the saffron-and-dried-fruit tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Ginger-garlic paste**: heavy use, the base of nearly every meat dish.
- **Garam masala**: cardamom, cinnamon, clove, peppercorn, bay. The warming spice mix.
- **Dried red chilli**: more than fresh. Kashmiri red for colour, dehydrated red for heat.
- **Yoghurt**: marinade, sauce thickener, raita base.
- **Ghee (desi ghee)**: clarified butter. The luxury fat; the base of nihari and biryani.
- **Cumin and coriander seed**: the cumin-coriander pair under most curries.
- **Basmati rice**: long-grain, aged, the rice of choice for biryani and pulao.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the karahi, the nihari, the biryani and the haleem. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Chicken Karahi](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/chicken-karahi)**: chicken cooked in a wok-style karahi with tomato, ginger, green chilli and finishing coriander. The benchmark Pakistani dish and the most-ordered plate in every Pakistani restaurant.
2. **[Beef Nihari](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/beef-nihari)**: beef shank slow-cooked overnight with bone marrow, served with garnishes of ginger, chilli and fresh coriander. The Mughal slow-braise that became Karachi's signature breakfast.
3. **[Lamb Nihari](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/lamb-nihari)**: the lamb sibling of beef nihari. Lighter, sweeter, equally slow.
4. **[Sindhi Biryani](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/sindhi-biryani)**: layered rice and meat with potato, fried onion, mint and a heavy hand on the green chilli. The hottest biryani in the subcontinent.
5. **[Haleem](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/haleem)**: lentils, wheat berries and meat slow-cooked for hours till it becomes a thick savoury porridge. The Ramadan-month classic.
6. **[Paya](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/paya)**: lamb or beef trotters slow-cooked with spices till the connective tissue dissolves into the broth. The breakfast soup of Lahore and Karachi.
7. **[Murgh Cholay](/recipes/cuisine/pakistani/murgh-cholay)**: chicken cooked with chickpeas in a thick onion-tomato gravy. The everyday Pakistani lunch.

Cook the karahi and the murgh cholay and you have the everyday. The two nihari and the paya are the slow-braise breakfast tradition. The biryani is the wedding plate; the haleem is Ramadan.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the Lahori shelf (separate folder; try lahori-mutton-karahi, lahori-chargha), the bread tradition (naan, roti, aloo paratha), the kebab world (seekh, shami, chapli), the sauces and pickles (mint chutney, lime pickle), and the rice canon (chana pulao, keema rice). Pair everything with a salted lassi or a cup of strong chai.
