# Filipino

*The cuisine of the Pacific crossroads. Spanish, Chinese, Malay, Mexican and American kitchens all met in one archipelago and the result is the most distinctive Southeast Asian shelf — sour, garlicky, vinegary, fiercely individualistic.*

## A short history

Filipino cooking sits at the intersection of four colonial-era influences. The indigenous Malayo-Polynesian base provided rice, coconut, fish sauce (patis), the vinegar tradition, and the use of banana leaves. The Spanish colonisation (1565–1898) added pork, beef, tomato, olive oil, the adobo concept (vinegar-and-garlic preservation), and the sofrito. The Chinese trader influence over centuries brought noodles, soy sauce, dumplings (siopao, lumpia), and the stir-fry. The American occupation (1898–1946) brought corned beef, hot dogs, spaghetti with banana ketchup, and the Jollibee chain that codified Filipino-American street food.

The cuisine resists single-dish identification more than most. Adobo is the closest to a national dish but every family makes it differently — chicken, pork, both, soy sauce or not, vinegar style, sweetness level, with coconut milk or without. The Filipino approach is fiercely individualistic; "kanya-kanya" (each to their own) is a guiding cooking principle. Sour is the dominant flavour vector — sinigang's tamarind-sour broth is the most distinctive Filipino flavour signature.

The diaspora carried Filipino food to the US (where it's now mainstream), the UK, the Gulf states, Australia and Japan. The post-2010s "Filipino food moment" was overpredicted (it hasn't quite broken out the way Korean or Thai food did) but the cuisine is steadily entering the global conversation, with restaurants like Lasa (LA), Bonifacio Filipino Steakhouse (NYC) and Sarap (London) pushing it forward.

## Regional variations

- **Luzon (north, including Manila)**: the most codified shelf. Adobo, sinigang, lechón, the Manila and Pampanga food capitals.
- **Visayas (central islands)**: seafood-heavy. Inasal chicken (Bacolod), pancit Molo (Iloilo), the kinilaw (raw fish in vinegar) tradition.
- **Mindanao (south)**: more Malay and Muslim influence. Less pork, more goat, the rendang-adjacent dishes, the durian belt.
- **Pampanga**: the food capital. Sisig was born here; the kapampangan kitchen sets the standard.
- **Bicol**: chilli country (most Filipino food is mild; Bicol is the exception). Bicol express, laing — coconut-and-chilli everything.
- **Ilocos (north)**: dry, sparse. Bagnet (twice-fried pork), poqui-poqui (smoky eggplant), Ilocos empanada.

## Signature ingredients

- **Vinegar (suka)**: cane (suka iloko), coconut (sukang tuba), palm (sukang pinakurat). The defining acidity.
- **Soy sauce (toyo) and fish sauce (patis)**: the salt-umami pair.
- **Calamansi**: small green-skinned citrus, very sour, more limey than lemony. The Filipino lemon.
- **Garlic**: heavy, often crisp-fried as a garnish.
- **Bay leaf and peppercorns**: the adobo aromatic base.
- **Coconut milk and coconut vinegar**: especially in Bicol and the south.
- **Banana ketchup**: the American-era invention now ubiquitous; the burger and pasta condiment.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the adobo, the sour soup, the peanut stew and the celebration roast. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Chicken Adobo](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/chicken-adobo)**: chicken simmered in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay and peppercorns till tender, then reduced till glossy. The closest thing to a national dish.
2. **[Sinigang](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/sinigang)**: pork (or shrimp, or fish) in a sour tamarind broth with vegetables. The most distinctively Filipino flavour — sour-first, not chilli-first.
3. **[Kare-Kare](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/kare-kare)**: oxtail and tripe in a thick peanut sauce with bok choy and eggplant, served with bagoong (shrimp paste) on the side. The Filipino-Pakistani crossroads dish.
4. **[Chicken Inasal](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/chicken-inasal)**: chicken marinated in calamansi, lemongrass, ginger and annatto oil, charcoal-grilled. Bacolod's signature plate.
5. **[Pancit Canton](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/pancit-canton)**: stir-fried egg noodles with pork, prawn, vegetables, soy and calamansi. The birthday pancit — long noodles for long life.
6. **[Lumpia Shanghai](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/lumpia-shanghai)**: thin pork-filled spring rolls, deep-fried crisp, served with sweet chilli sauce. The party staple.
7. **[Ginataang Gulay](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/ginataang-gulay)**: vegetables (squash, long beans, eggplant) simmered in coconut milk. The everyday vegetarian Filipino plate.

Cook the adobo and the sinigang and you have the two pillars. The kare-kare is the celebration plate. The inasal and the pancit are the everyday. The lumpia is the party; the ginataang is the veg shelf.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries lechón (the whole roasted pig), sisig (the Pampanga pork-cheek dish), tortang talong (eggplant omelette), the breakfast canon (tapsilog, longsilog, tocilog), and the dessert tradition (halo-halo, leche flan, ube). Pair everything with a Tanduay rum or a San Miguel beer and the cuisine sings.
