# Filipino

*The cuisine of the Pacific crossroads. Spanish, Chinese, Malay, Mexican and American kitchens all met in one archipelago, and the result is the most distinctive shelf in Southeast Asia: sour, garlicky, vinegary, fiercely individualistic.*

## A short history

Filipino cooking sits at the intersection of four colonial-era influences, but the bedrock is older than any of them. Austronesian-speaking peoples settled the archipelago in waves from around 3000 BCE, arriving by outrigger canoe from Taiwan and the islands south of it, and they brought the foundational shelf: wet-rice cultivation, coconut, taro, banana, the pig and chicken, and the technique of preserving fish in salt and rice (the original burong isda and balao-balao still made in Pampanga). The indigenous Malayo-Polynesian base also gave the country fish sauce (patis), the vinegar tradition (palm vinegar, coconut vinegar, cane vinegar), and the habit of wrapping and steaming food in banana leaves.

Trade with China is ancient. Chinese merchants were sailing into Manila Bay by the 10th century and the Sung dynasty pottery in Filipino burial sites runs centuries before any European arrival. The Chinese brought noodles (pancit comes from the Hokkien pian e sit, "something quickly cooked"), soy sauce, dumplings (siopao is the Filipino rendering of the Cantonese siu pao, lumpia comes from the Hokkien lun pia), the stir-fry, and the tau-yu-bak (soy-braised pork) that became the soy-sauce-based adobo variations. Arab and Indian Ocean traders reached the southern islands earlier, bringing Islam to Mindanao and Sulu by the 14th century along with the spice-and-coconut cooking that still defines the Muslim south.

Magellan made landfall in 1521 and died in Cebu the same year. The Spanish came back to stay in 1565, and for 333 years they ran the Philippines from Mexico through the Manila galleon trade. This is the crucial point about Filipino cuisine: the Spanish colonial connection ran through New Spain (Mexico), and the Acapulco-Manila galleon (the longest sustained sea trade route in history, 1565 to 1815) carried Mexican ingredients across the Pacific. Tomato, corn, chilli, chocolate, peanut, avocado and the Mexican empanada arrived in Manila on the galleon, which is why kare-kare (peanut sauce) and dishes like menudo and afritada read as recognisably Hispanic. The Spanish themselves added pork in larger quantities, beef, olive oil, garlic, the adobo concept (the word is Spanish for "marinade" but the vinegar-and-garlic preservation technique long predated Spanish arrival), the sofrito (here called ginisa), and the morisqueta-style cooking of the friars' kitchens.

The 19th century was the codification century. Chinese mestizo families ran the trade and the kitchens (sisig, sinigang, kare-kare and most of the "national" dishes were polished into recognisable form in this era), Spanish friars ran the parishes and the panaderias, and the Philippine Revolution of 1896-1898 was led partly by men educated over polvoron and ensaymada in Madrid and Barcelona. Then the Americans arrived in 1898, took the country off Spain at the close of the Spanish-American War, and ran the Philippines as a colony until 1946. The American occupation brought corned beef, hot dogs, spaghetti with banana ketchup (an invention forced by wartime tomato shortages in the 1940s), evaporated and condensed milk in cans, and the school-canteen culture that gave the country its breakfast trinity of tapsilog, longsilog and tocilog.

The cuisine resists a single-dish identity more than most. Adobo is the closest thing to a national dish, but every family makes it differently: chicken, pork, both, with soy sauce or without, sour or sweet, coconut milk or no. "Kanya-kanya" (each to their own) runs as a guiding principle. Sour is the dominant flavour direction, and sinigang's tamarind-sour broth is the most distinctive Filipino flavour signature.

The post-independence decades brought Jollibee (founded in 1978, now bigger than McDonald's in the Philippines and one of the world's most-recognised Filipino brands), the Marcos era and the People Power Revolution of 1986, and the vast OFW (overseas Filipino worker) diaspora that carried Filipino food to the US (where it's now mainstream), the UK, the Gulf states, Australia and Japan. The "Filipino food moment" of the late 2010s was overpredicted (it hasn't broken out quite the way Korean or Thai food did) but the cuisine is steadily entering the global conversation, with restaurants like Lasa in LA, Bonifacio in NYC and Sarap in London pushing it forward.

## Regional variations

- **Luzon (north, including Manila)**: the most codified shelf. Adobo, sinigang, lechón. Manila and Pampanga are the food capitals.
- **Visayas (central islands)**: seafood-heavy. Inasal chicken (Bacolod), pancit Molo (Iloilo), the kinilaw (raw fish in vinegar) tradition.
- **Mindanao (south)**: more Malay and Muslim influence. Less pork, more goat, rendang-adjacent dishes, the durian belt.
- **Pampanga**: the food capital. Sisig was born here; the kapampangan kitchen sets the standard.
- **Bicol**: chilli country. Most Filipino food is mild; Bicol is the exception. Bicol express, laing, coconut-and-chilli everything.
- **Ilocos (north)**: dry, sparse. Bagnet (twice-fried pork), poqui-poqui (smoky eggplant), Ilocos empanada.

## Signature ingredients

- **Vinegar (suka)**: cane (suka iloko), coconut (sukang tuba), palm (sukang pinakurat). The defining acidity.
- **Soy sauce (toyo) and fish sauce (patis)**: the salt-umami pair.
- **Calamansi**: small green-skinned citrus, very sour, more limey than lemony. The Filipino lemon.
- **Garlic**: heavy, often crisp-fried as a garnish.
- **Bay leaf and peppercorns**: the adobo aromatic base.
- **Coconut milk and coconut vinegar**: especially in Bicol and the south.
- **Banana ketchup**: an American-era invention now everywhere. The burger and pasta condiment.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the adobo, the sour soup, the peanut stew and the celebration roast. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Chicken Adobo](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/chicken-adobo)**: chicken simmered in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay and peppercorns till tender, then reduced till glossy. The closest thing to a national dish.
2. **[Sinigang](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/sinigang)**: pork (or shrimp, or fish) in a sour tamarind broth with vegetables. The most distinctively Filipino flavour: sour-first, not chilli-first.
3. **[Kare-Kare](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/kare-kare)**: oxtail and tripe in a thick peanut sauce with bok choy and eggplant, served with bagoong (shrimp paste) on the side. The Filipino-Pakistani crossroads dish.
4. **[Chicken Inasal](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/chicken-inasal)**: chicken marinated in calamansi, lemongrass, ginger and annatto oil, charcoal-grilled. Bacolod's signature plate.
5. **[Pancit Canton](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/pancit-canton)**: stir-fried egg noodles with pork, prawn, vegetables, soy and calamansi. The birthday pancit; long noodles for long life.
6. **[Lumpia Shanghai](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/lumpia-shanghai)**: thin pork-filled spring rolls, deep-fried crisp, served with sweet chilli sauce. The party staple.
7. **[Ginataang Gulay](/recipes/cuisine/filipino/ginataang-gulay)**: vegetables (squash, long beans, eggplant) simmered in coconut milk. The everyday vegetarian Filipino plate.

Cook the adobo and the sinigang and you have the two pillars. The kare-kare is the celebration plate. The inasal and the pancit are the everyday. The lumpia is the party; the ginataang is the veg shelf.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries lechón (the whole roasted pig), sisig (the Pampanga pork-cheek dish), tortang talong (eggplant omelette), the breakfast canon (tapsilog, longsilog, tocilog), and the dessert tradition (halo-halo, leche flan, ube). Pair everything with a Tanduay rum or a San Miguel beer and the cuisine sings.
