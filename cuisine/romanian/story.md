# Romanian

*The cuisine of the Carpathian shepherd and the Danube farmer. Cornmeal mămăligă, slow-braised cabbage rolls, the mici (small grilled sausages), and a heavy hand of sour cream finishing everything.*

## A short history

Romanian cooking sits at the meeting of Eastern Europe, the Balkans and the former Ottoman territories, and the kitchen carries layers from every empire that ever rolled through. The Dacians, who held the Carpathian basin from the Iron Age into the early common era, were grain farmers and sheep herders; the Romans, who conquered Dacia in 106 CE and held it for less than two centuries, left a Latin-rooted language (Romania literally means "land of the Romans") and the wine-growing tradition that still defines Transylvania and Moldova. But the food carries more Slavic, Hungarian, Turkish and Greek influence than Italian. The shepherd's table (sheep's-milk cheeses, mămăligă, slow-cooked mutton and lamb) survived everything: the Slavic migrations of the 6th century, the Magyar invasions of the 9th, the Mongol incursions of the 13th. Mioritic sheep-herding culture is still the soul of the cuisine.

The medieval principalities of Wallachia and Moldavia, founded in the 14th century, became prosperous trading buffers between Hungary, Poland and the rising Ottoman Empire. Greek, Armenian and Jewish merchants moved through the markets of Iași and Bucharest, leaving an early layer of Balkan-Levantine cooking: the spiced meatball, the stuffed vegetable, the syrup-soaked pastry. Transylvania, meanwhile, sat under Hungarian and Saxon rule and developed a quite different regional kitchen built on paprika, sour cream, the Saxon layered casseroles and the German sausage-curing tradition. Romania has never been one cuisine, and the regional split goes back nearly seven centuries.

The Ottoman period (15th to 19th century, though Wallachia and Moldavia were never fully absorbed into the empire as Bulgaria and Serbia were) built in much of the modern shelf. The cabbage roll (sarmale, the national dish) descends from the Ottoman dolma family; the small grilled meatball (mici, the Romanian answer to köfte) came in through the same channel. So did the heavy use of dill and parsley, the Turkish coffee tradition, the syrup-soaked pastries (baklava as plăcintă in some regions), and the love of grilled meat and pickled vegetables. The Phanariot Greek princes who ruled the principalities for Istanbul through the 1700s brought further Greek and Levantine refinements to the boyar tables.

Corn arrived from the New World in the 17th century and pushed millet out as the staple grain within a few generations; mămăligă (polenta, but coarser and yellower than the Italian) turns up on most Romanian tables daily by 1800. Potatoes followed in the 18th century, the tomato in the 19th. Pork is the dominant meat, and the slaughter of the pig for Christmas (ignatul porcului, December 20) is a national tradition that still shapes rural life: the family pig is killed, the blood goes into sângerete (Romanian black pudding), the offal into drob, the fat is rendered into untură, every joint is cured or smoked or salted, and the work finishes with a communal meal around a steaming bowl of mămăligă.

Independence from the Ottomans (1877) and unification with Transylvania (1918) created modern Romania, and the inter-war period brought a brief French-influenced restaurant culture to Bucharest (the "Paris of the East" tag dates from this era). The Communist period (1947 to 1989) under Gheorghiu-Dej and especially Ceaușescu put the kitchen on rations through the 1980s, when state-export policies stripped the country's food supply to the bone. Romanians cooked through it with sarmale, mămăligă, garden vegetables and whatever pork they could put away from the family pig. Surviving the lean years became its own culinary tradition.

The post-Ceaușescu recovery has brought Romanian cuisine back into the international conversation slowly. The Saxon and Hungarian-Romanian regional traditions in Transylvania come through particularly well preserved (helped by tourism around the fortified Saxon villages and the slow-food movement), while Bucharest's young chefs are reaching back into the Phanariot, Jewish and boyar traditions that the Communist period flattened out. The home table, meanwhile, looks much as it did a century ago: sour cream on everything, mămăligă on the side, ciorbă (sour soup) to start, and the long Sunday around a wood fire with mici sizzling on the grill.

## Regional variations

- **Wallachia (south, Bucharest)**: the mainstream. Mici, sarmale, ciorbă.
- **Transylvania (centre, west)**: Hungarian-Romanian hybrid. Goulash variants, Saxon-German layered dishes.
- **Moldova (east)**: closer to Russian-Ukrainian cooking. More borscht, more dumplings.
- **Dobrogea (Black Sea coast)**: more Turkish and Greek. Fish-heavy, more cumin.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cornmeal (mămăligă)**: the everyday starch. Polenta-style but coarser.
- **Cabbage**: fresh in soups, fermented (sauerkraut) in sarmale and bigos-style stews.
- **Pork**: every part. Slănină (cured fat), cârnați (sausages), kaiser (cured ham).
- **Sour cream (smântână)**: finishing everything. Sometimes mixed with crumbled cheese on top of mămăligă.
- **Dill, parsley, lovage**: the herb trinity in soups.
- **Borscht (borș)**: fermented wheat-bran liquid, used to sour soups (different from Russian beetroot borscht).
- **Telemea**: white brine-cheese, the daily cheese.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Sarmale](/recipes/cuisine/romanian/sarmale)**: pickled cabbage leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-cooked in a tomato-bay-leaf broth. The national dish.
2. **[Mici](/recipes/cuisine/romanian/mici)**: small skinless minced-meat sausages (beef, lamb and pork, mixed with garlic and a pinch of baking soda for fluffiness), charcoal-grilled. The summer-barbecue standard.
3. **[Mămăligă cu Brânză](/recipes/cuisine/romanian/mamaliga-cu-branza)**: cornmeal porridge with crumbled telemea cheese and sour cream. The shepherd's lunch.
4. **[Ciorbă de Burtă](/recipes/cuisine/romanian/ciorba-de-burta)**: tripe soup with garlic, vinegar, sour cream and chilli. The Sunday morning hangover cure.
5. **[Ardei Umpluți](/recipes/cuisine/romanian/ardei-umpluti)**: bell peppers stuffed with rice and minced meat, simmered in tomato sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with țuică (plum brandy, often homemade, fierce) or a local red (Fetească Neagră). For a proper Romanian table: ciorbă as a starter, mici or sarmale as a main, mămăligă on the side, sour cream on everything.
