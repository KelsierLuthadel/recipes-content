# Barbadian (Bajan)

*The kitchen of the easternmost Caribbean. Flying fish and cou-cou as the national dish, sweet bread for tea, the strongest African retention in the British Caribbean, and the world's oldest continuously-running rum distillery.*

## A short history

Barbadian cooking is the food of a small island shaped by its colonial economy. Before any European set foot here, the island was inhabited by Arawak and later Kalinago (Carib) peoples, who fished the reefs, gathered conch, and cultivated cassava, sweet potato and corn. By the time the first English settlers arrived in 1627, the island was uninhabited, the indigenous population either killed by Spanish raids or fled to other islands.

The English colonised hard and fast. Within a generation Barbados had become the centre of the Caribbean sugar-and-slavery system, the model that the British later exported to Jamaica, the American South and the rest of the Caribbean. From the 1640s onwards, planters shipped enslaved West Africans (Igbo, Akan, Yoruba, Fon) to work the cane fields in numbers that quickly made the population majority African. They brought the cooking that survives today: okra and the cornmeal mush that became cou-cou (cousin of West African fufu and ugali), African leafy greens, pigeon peas, the slow-stewed one-pot, the deep-fried fritter, the use of every part of the pig. The Anglican-British planter class added sweet bread, tea, scones, the formal Sunday lunch, and a particular love for puddings and souse (pickled pork) that endures.

The 18th-century sugar trade made Barbados extravagantly rich, at unspeakable human cost. Mount Gay Rum, distilling since 1703, is the oldest continuously-producing rum distillery in the world; rum from these mills became the currency of the triangular trade, exchanged in West Africa for more enslaved people. Barbadians invented many of the rum-cocktail conventions still in use: the rum punch ratio ("one of sour, two of sweet, three of strong, four of weak") goes back to the planter mess of the 1700s. Falernum (the spiced sugar syrup) is a Bajan invention.

The 19th century brought emancipation in 1834, followed by an apprenticeship period that effectively ended slavery only in 1838. The freed population reshaped the island's food in their own image: the village rum shop, the chattel-house kitchen, the saltfish-and-bake breakfast, the Sunday-morning souse and pudding tradition (steamed sweet potato pudding with pickled pork, the most Bajan thing on a Sunday plate). Flying fish (which leap out of the water and were originally caught with handheld nets) became the everyday catch.

After independence in 1966, the country codified the cuisine: flying fish and cou-cou became the national dish, and the Bajan Christmas tradition of jug-jug (a one-pot of pigeon peas, salt meat and Guinea cornmeal, with techniques borrowed from Scottish settlers' haggis-style cooking) settled in as a holiday standard. Today the cuisine is bound up with rum, fish-fry Fridays at Oistins (the south-coast fishing village where the whole island turns up for grilled mahi-mahi, macaroni pie and Banks beer), and the most distinctive English-language Caribbean accent in the region. The republic moved on from the British monarchy in 2021 but the food still reads as a particular Anglo-Caribbean synthesis nowhere else has matched.

## Regional variations

Barbados is small (430 km²) and the cuisine is broadly homogenous, with sub-traditions by parish:

- **Bridgetown (capital, west)**: codified mainstream. Cou-cou, flying fish.
- **Oistins (south)**: the famous Friday-night fish fry. Whole-fried fish, macaroni pie, rice and peas.
- **Speightstown (north)**: more traditional, older recipes.
- **East coast (Bathsheba)**: surfer-and-tourist hangout. Beachside menus.

## Signature ingredients

- **Flying fish**: the national fish. Coats fly out of the water; Barbadians catch them with handheld nets.
- **Cornmeal**: cou-cou (the polenta-style mash with okra).
- **Salt meat (salt pork, salt beef)**: in jug-jug, in stews.
- **Bajan seasoning**: green herb-paste of parsley, marjoram, thyme, chive, garlic, hot pepper, lime. The marinade base.
- **Hot pepper sauce**: yellow Scotch bonnet, mustard, vinegar. On every table.
- **Rum**: Mount Gay, Foursquare, the oldest distillery tradition in the world.
- **Macaroni pie**: a Caribbean-British hybrid. Macaroni baked with cheese and Bajan seasoning.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cou-Cou and Flying Fish](/recipes/cuisine/barbadian/cou-cou-and-flying-fish)**: cornmeal-and-okra mash topped with steamed flying fish in tomato-onion sauce. The national dish.
2. **[Bajan Fried Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/barbadian/bajan-fried-chicken)**: chicken marinated in Bajan seasoning, dredged in seasoned flour, fried golden. The Sunday-lunch classic.
3. **[Bajan Stew Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/barbadian/bajan-stew-chicken)**: chicken browned with brown sugar then slow-braised in the Bajan-seasoning gravy. The everyday Bajan dinner.
4. **[Macaroni Pie](/recipes/cuisine/barbadian/macaroni-pie)**: baked macaroni with cheese, Bajan seasoning and mustard. The universal side at every Bajan party.
5. **[Jug-Jug](/recipes/cuisine/barbadian/jug-jug)**: pigeon peas, salt meat and Guinea corn meal slow-cooked together. The Bajan Christmas dish, a direct descendant of West African and Scottish (haggis-style) techniques.

## Where to go next

Mix a rum punch (Mount Gay, lime, sugar, water, bitters: "one of sour, two of sweet, three of strong, four of weak") or crack open a Banks beer. Go to Oistins on a Friday night for the fish fry: grilled marlin, dolphinfish, swordfish, rice-and-peas, macaroni pie, plenty of rum, dancing till the small hours.
