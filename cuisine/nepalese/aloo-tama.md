# Aloo Tama

![Aloo Tama](resources/aloo-tama.jpg)

*The Nepali sour-bamboo curry: potato, fermented bamboo shoot and black-eyed peas simmered in a turmeric-and-timur gravy. Tangy from the bamboo, earthy from the legumes, distinctively Nepali.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus overnight soak for the black-eyed peas)

**Cook Time:** 50 minutes

## Overview
Aloo tama (literally "potato bamboo") is one of Nepal's most distinctive dishes, the recipe that introduces visitors to the sour-funk side of the cuisine. The defining ingredient is tama, fermented bamboo shoot, which gives the curry its sharp, almost cheese-like sour note unlike anything else in the regional repertoire. Potato carries the body; black-eyed peas (bodi) provide the bite and the protein. The whole thing is spiced lightly with turmeric, cumin, coriander and a generous hand of timur (Sichuan-type pepper). Fermented bamboo is sold in jars or vacuum-packs at Nepali and Tibetan groceries. Tinned bamboo shoots are not a substitute; they're fresh-cooked, not fermented, and the dish loses its character. Fermented mustard greens (Sichuan suan cai) or sauerkraut substitute if you absolutely can't find tama. A staple of Newari and broader Nepali cooking, often served as part of a dal bhat plate.

## Ingredients
- 200 g dried black-eyed peas (soaked overnight in cold water): or 1 tin (400 g), drained
- 500 g waxy potatoes (peeled, cut into 2 cm chunks)
- 200 g fermented bamboo shoot (tama), drained, sliced thin
- 1 large onion (finely chopped)
- 4 garlic cloves (minced)
- 25 g fresh ginger (grated)
- 2 fresh tomatoes (chopped) or 200 g tinned tomatoes
- 2 dried red chillies (broken)
- 3 tbsp neutral oil or mustard oil
- 1 tsp cumin seeds
- ½ tsp fenugreek seeds (methi)
- 1 tsp ground turmeric
- 1 tsp ground cumin
- 1 tsp ground coriander
- ½ tsp ground timur (Sichuan pepper)
- 1 tsp salt (or to taste)
- 700 ml water
- 1 bay leaf
- 1 small green chilli (slit, optional)
- Small handful fresh coriander (chopped, to finish)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the black-eyed peas
1. If using dried: drain the soaked peas. Place in a pot with fresh water to cover by 5 cm. Bring to a boil, simmer 25-30 minutes until tender. Drain.
1. If using tinned: drain and rinse.

### Stage 2 - Bloom the spices
1. Heat the oil in a heavy pot over medium heat.
1. Add the cumin and fenugreek seeds. They will sizzle and pop in 10 seconds.
1. Add the dried chillies and bay leaf. Stir 10 seconds.
1. Add the chopped onion. Cook 6-7 minutes, until soft and just turning gold.
1. Add the garlic, ginger and slit green chilli. Cook 1 minute.
1. Stir in the turmeric, ground cumin, ground coriander and timur. Cook 30 seconds, until fragrant.

### Stage 3 - Build the base
1. Add the chopped tomatoes and salt. Cook 4-5 minutes, mashing with the back of a spoon, until the tomatoes break down into a thick base.

### Stage 4 - Add the bamboo and potato
1. Stir in the sliced fermented bamboo. Cook 2-3 minutes; the bamboo's sour aroma will perfume the whole pot. (Open a window if you are sensitive to fermented foods, the smell is strong but the cooked flavour is mellower.)
1. Add the potato chunks. Stir to coat in the spice base.
1. Pour in the water. Stir, bring to a gentle boil.

### Stage 5 - Simmer
1. Reduce heat to a low simmer. Cover and cook 20 minutes, until the potatoes are nearly tender (a knife slides in with slight resistance).
1. Add the cooked or tinned black-eyed peas. Stir.
1. Continue simmering 10-12 more minutes, uncovered, until the potatoes are completely tender, the peas have absorbed flavour, and the gravy has reduced to a thick saucy consistency (not soupy, not dry).
1. Taste. The dish should be tangy from the bamboo, earthy from the peas, lightly spiced. Adjust salt.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Off the heat. Stir in the fresh coriander.
1. Serve in shallow bowls over plain rice, or alongside [dal bhat](dal-bhat-tarkari.md) as part of the full Nepali plate.

## Notes
- **Fermented bamboo is the dish.** Without tama, this is just a potato-and-black-eyed-pea curry. With it, the sour funky note transforms the whole bowl. Look in Nepali, Tibetan or East Asian grocers for jarred or vacuum-packed tama.
- **Black-eyed peas, not chickpeas.** The smaller, more savoury black-eyed pea is correct. Chickpeas are too sweet and too firm; they push the dish toward an Indian register that it does not belong in.
- **Timur is the pepper.** Sichuan pepper is the closest substitute. Black pepper alone gives a duller result.
- **Mustard oil for the most authentic flavour.** Hot mustard oil has a pungency that complements the bamboo perfectly. Neutral oil is fine; the dish still works.
- **The smell when the bamboo hits the pan is fierce.** It mellows in 60 seconds. Don't panic.

## Variations
- **With kidney beans:** swap black-eyed peas for red kidney beans for a heartier version, especially common in winter.
- **With chicken:** add 400 g chopped chicken thigh in stage 3, browning briefly before the tomatoes go in. Cook through with the simmer.
- **Without legumes:** a simpler aloo tama with just potato and bamboo; faster, lighter, more of a side dish than a meal.
- **Spicier:** double the fresh and dried chillies; add a teaspoon of cayenne in stage 2.

## Serving
Eaten with plain rice or roti as a one-bowl meal, or as the tarkari component of a full [dal bhat tarkari](dal-bhat-tarkari.md) plate. A spoonful of plain yoghurt on the side cools the heat.

## Storage
- Improves overnight; the bamboo flavour deepens. Refrigerates 3 days.
- Reheats well on the hob with a splash of water.
- Freezes 2 months; the potato softens slightly on thaw but the dish remains pleasant.
