# Nepalese

*The kitchen of the Himalayas. Dal bhat (lentils and rice) eaten twice a day by most Nepalis. The momo (steamed dumplings), the gundruk (fermented leafy greens), and a quietly distinct cuisine standing between Indian, Tibetan and Chinese traditions.*

## A short history

Nepalese cooking sits at the meeting of South Asian and Central Asian-Tibetan traditions, and the geography wrote most of the menu. The Himalaya runs across the whole country and the elevation drops nearly nine kilometres from the high passes to the Terai plain, so what grows changes every few hundred metres. The basic shelf has been the working kitchen for centuries: rice in the lower elevations, millet (kodo) and buckwheat (phapar) in the mid-hills, barley and yak in the high Himalaya, dal (lentils) in dozens of forms, fermented leafy greens (gundruk and sinki, the Nepalese answer to sauerkraut), and a small set of spices used much more sparingly than in Indian cooking. The Khas, Magar, Gurung, Tamang, Rai, Limbu and Tharu peoples all brought their own kitchen to the layered cuisine.

The Kathmandu Valley grew rich on its position as a Himalayan trade crossroads. From at least the first millennium CE, traders carried salt, wool and turquoise down from Tibet and rice, sugar and spices up from the Indian plains. The Newar people of the valley built one of the most sophisticated culinary traditions in South Asia on the back of that trade. The Malla kings (12th to 18th centuries) ruled from Kathmandu, Patan and Bhaktapur, and the Newar feast tradition (the bhoj) codified dishes still eaten today: chatamari (rice-flour crepes), yomari (steamed sweet dumplings with molasses filling), bara (lentil patties), and the slow-cooked buffalo dishes that anchor a proper Newar wedding spread.

Momos (steamed dumplings) are the second pillar of the modern table. Newar traders who worked the Lhasa route carried the dumpling idea back from Tibet, and over generations it became its own Nepali thing, eaten with tomato-and-sesame chutney (achar) in every roadside teashop. The Tibetan influence shows up across the higher elevations: thukpa (noodle soup), tsampa (roasted barley flour), and the Sherpa kitchen of the Everest region all carry that lineage. The chillies, tomatoes and potatoes that landed in South Asia via Portuguese trade in the 16th century moved up into the hills slowly but completely; the potato now anchors aloo tama, aloo achar and the high-altitude diet of the Sherpa.

Prithvi Narayan Shah unified Nepal in 1768 and the Shah dynasty ruled (with the long Rana family interlude from 1846 to 1951) into the modern era. The country was closed to most outsiders for over a century, which preserved the regional cuisines almost intact. Indian influence flowed in slowly through the open southern border, particularly across the Terai plains where the cooking shades closer to Bihari food. Tibetan refugees arriving after 1959 added a strong Tibetan presence in Kathmandu and Pokhara, and the momo shops they opened are now everywhere.

Dal bhat (lentils-and-rice with a vegetable side and a small pickle) is the universal meal. "Dal bhat power, 24 hour" is a trekking-circuit slogan because the meal powers the porters who carry trekkers' loads up some of the steepest paths on the planet. Most Nepalis eat dal bhat twice a day, every day. The post-2000s trekking and restaurant economy has put Nepali food onto the international map, and a quiet new generation of Kathmandu chefs is starting to reframe Newar feast cooking for a wider audience. The everyday kitchen, though, still runs on the same dal, rice, vegetable, pickle and ghee that the Malla kings would recognise.

## Regional variations

- **Kathmandu Valley (Newar cuisine)**: the distinct, refined urban tradition. Yomari (steamed sweet dumplings), bara (lentil patties), chatamari (rice-flour crepes, the Newar "pizza").
- **Terai (southern plains)**: closer to Indian (Bihari) cooking. More rice, more curry-style dishes.
- **Hills (central, where most Nepalis live)**: dal bhat country.
- **Himalayan north (Sherpa, Thakali, Tamang)**: closer to Tibetan. Thukpa, sha-bha-lay, tsampa.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rice (bhat)**: the everyday staple.
- **Lentils (dal: red, yellow, black, green)**: in dal bhat.
- **Mustard oil**: the cooking fat in the hills.
- **Fenugreek seeds**: tempered into dal.
- **Cumin and coriander**: the spice pair.
- **Gundruk**: fermented dried leafy mustard greens. Tart and pungent.
- **Sesame seeds**: ground into achar (chutney).
- **Timur (Sichuan peppercorn)**: the local pepper.

## Learn it in three recipes

1. **[Dal Bhat Tarkari](/recipes/cuisine/nepalese/dal-bhat-tarkari)**: lentils, rice, vegetable curry, achar (pickle). The universal Nepali meal, eaten twice daily by most of the country. Refills are free; eat till you're full.
2. **[Chicken Momos](/recipes/cuisine/nepalese/chicken-momos)**: steamed dumplings filled with spiced minced chicken, served with achar (tomato-sesame chutney). The national snack.
3. **[Aloo Tama](/recipes/cuisine/nepalese/aloo-tama)**: potato-and-bamboo-shoot curry with black-eyed peas. The signature Newar dish.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with chiya (milky-sweet spiced black tea) or with raksi (the local rice or millet spirit). For a proper Nepali table: dal bhat at the centre, momos as a snack between meals, gundruk on the side, achar with everything. Eat with the right hand from the metal thali plate.
