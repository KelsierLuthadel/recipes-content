# Banana Hasá

![Banana Hasá](resources/curacao-plantain.jpg)

*Curaçao's fried sweet plantain: ripe plantains sliced thick on the diagonal, pan-fried in butter and a little brown sugar until the edges caramelise and the centres turn jammy, served beside almost everything.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 5 minutes

**Cook Time:** 12 minutes

## Overview
Banana hasá is the Curaçaoan way with sweet plantain, the side that sweetens a plate of stoba or yambo and turns up at every Sunday lunch. The plantains must be very ripe, the skins almost black, the flesh deep yellow and soft; this is when the natural sugars peak. They're sliced thick on the diagonal so each piece has a wide surface to brown, then pan-fried in butter with a small sprinkle of brown sugar that helps the edges glaze. The Curaçao move is to finish with a tiny pinch of cinnamon and salt: the cinnamon nods to the Dutch baking influence, the salt lifts the sweetness. The result is sticky, caramelised, slightly chewy at the edges, melting in the centre.

## Ingredients

- 3 very ripe plantains (skins heavily blackened, deep yellow inside)
- 30 g butter
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil
- 1 tbsp soft brown sugar (or palm sugar)
- ¼ tsp ground cinnamon (optional)
- A pinch of flaky sea salt

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the plantains
1. Trim both ends.
2. Score along the length of each skin with a sharp knife; peel away in strips.
3. Slice on a 45-degree diagonal into 1.5 cm pieces.

### Stage 2 - Fry
1. Heat the butter and oil together in a heavy wide frying pan over medium heat. The mix of butter (for flavour) and oil (for higher smoke point) stops the butter from burning.
2. Lay the plantain slices in a single layer; don't crowd. Work in two batches if needed.
3. Cook 3 minutes on the first side without moving; the surface should turn deep gold with a dark caramelised edge.
4. Sprinkle half the brown sugar over the top; flip the slices.
5. Cook 2-3 minutes more, lifting the heat for the last 30 seconds to glaze the bottoms.

### Stage 3 - Finish
1. Lift onto a warm plate.
2. Sprinkle with the cinnamon (if using) and a pinch of flaky salt while still hot.
3. Serve immediately, before the pieces lose their crisp glaze.

## Notes
- **Ripeness defines this dish:** Yellow plantains with no black are still too starchy and bland. The skins should be at least half black, ideally mostly black with deep yellow underneath. Soft to the touch.
- **Butter-oil mix:** Pure butter scorches at the temperature needed to caramelise the sugar. The oil raises the smoke point.
- **Sugar only after flipping:** Sprinkle on after the first flip; if you add it before, it burns to bitter carbon at the bottom of the pan.
- **Don't crowd the pan:** Plantains release a lot of moisture; pack the pan and they steam rather than caramelise. Two batches gives a better result than rushing.
- **The salt:** It pulls the dish from "dessert" into "side that goes with stew". A small pinch on each piece.

## Variations
- **Banana hasá ku keshi:** Top with a thin slice of melted young gouda, an island combination popular at breakfast.
- **Plantain with coconut:** Sprinkle desiccated coconut on the buttered pan with the sugar; it toasts as the plantain cooks.
- **Spiced:** Add a pinch of ground allspice with the cinnamon.
- **Banana hasá grandi:** Halve the plantains lengthways instead of slicing rounds; the slabs hold more caramelisation.
- **Brandy finish:** A teaspoon of dark rum tipped into the pan at the last minute, ignited if you're brave.

## Serving
Alongside karni stoba · with funchi and yambo · plate-edge garnish for fried fish · breakfast with eggs and bacon · a small bowl on its own with cream as a pudding.

## Storage
- Eat immediately; the caramelisation softens as they cool
- Leftovers refrigerate 2 days but lose their crisp edge
- Re-crisp in a hot dry pan for 1 minute per side; don't microwave (they turn to mush)
