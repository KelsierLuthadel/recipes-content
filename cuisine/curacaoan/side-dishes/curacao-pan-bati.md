# Pan Bati

![Pan Bati](resources/curacao-pan-bati.jpg)

*Curaçao's cornmeal-coconut pancake side, beaten thin in a hot oiled pan, served alongside stoba and fried fish to mop up the gravy.*

**Serves:** 4 (makes 4 pancakes)

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 20 minutes

## Overview
Pan bati ("beaten bread" in Papiamento) is the Curaçao cornmeal pancake that takes the place of bread on the daily plate. Cornmeal, plain flour, a little sugar and a splash of coconut milk are whisked into a loose batter, poured into a hot oiled pan, then beaten with the back of a spoon as it cooks (the move that gives the bread its name). The finished pancake is slightly thick, faintly sweet from the coconut, with a lacy edge and a soft middle. It sits alongside stoba (stewed meat) or fried fish, torn off with the fingers, used to scoop the gravy. The sweet edge balances strongly seasoned stews; the texture is somewhere between an English crumpet and a Caribbean roti.

## Ingredients

- 150 g fine yellow cornmeal
- 100 g plain flour
- 30 g caster sugar
- ½ tsp salt
- 1 tsp baking powder
- 250 ml coconut milk
- 150 ml water
- 1 egg
- 30 g butter, melted
- 4 tbsp vegetable oil, for the pan

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make the batter
1. Whisk the cornmeal, flour, sugar, salt and baking powder in a wide bowl.
2. In a jug, beat the coconut milk, water, egg and melted butter together.
3. Pour the wet into the dry, whisking until smooth. The batter should pour like double cream; loosen with a splash of water if too thick.
4. Rest 10 minutes so the cornmeal absorbs the liquid.

### Stage 2 - Cook
1. Heat 1 tbsp of the oil in a heavy 22 cm frying pan over medium heat.
2. Pour in a quarter of the batter (around a ladleful), tilting to spread to a 1 cm thickness.
3. Cook 3 minutes; small bubbles form across the surface.
4. With the back of a wooden spoon, beat the surface gently (this is the move that gives pan bati its name), pressing the centre to flatten and even out the cooking.
5. Flip; cook 2-3 minutes on the second side until pale gold with a lacy edge.
6. Lift to a warm plate, cover with a tea towel.
7. Repeat with the remaining batter, adding more oil to the pan each time.

## Notes
- **The coconut milk:** Use full-fat tinned coconut milk for the proper richness. Coconut cream is too thick, light coconut milk gives a thin pancake.
- **The "beating" step:** Press the surface gently with the back of a spoon while the underside cooks; this evens out the heat and shapes the pancake. Some old recipes call for actual beating, but light pressing is enough.
- **Medium heat, not high:** High heat burns the outside before the centre sets. Medium gives the lacy edge and soft middle that defines pan bati.
- **Sweet but not sweet:** Don't add more sugar; pan bati is meant to balance savoury stews, not be a dessert pancake.
- **Rest the batter:** Skip this and the cornmeal stays grainy; rested batter is silky and cooks smooth.

## Variations
- **Pan bati ku banana:** Mash half a ripe banana into the batter for a sweeter version.
- **Pan bati ku keshi:** Sprinkle grated young gouda on the surface as it cooks; it melts into the batter.
- **Pan bati ku kokada:** Add 30 g desiccated coconut to the batter for a more pronounced coconut flavour.
- **Plain pan bati:** Skip the coconut, replace with whole milk, for a more neutral pancake.
- **Spiced pan bati:** Add ¼ tsp grated nutmeg and a pinch of anise to the dry mix.

## Serving
With karni stoba · alongside fried fish · with a spoon of pika sauce · torn for scooping yambo · plain with butter and a sprinkle of brown sugar for breakfast.

## Storage
- Best eaten warm from the pan
- Refrigerates 2 days; reheat in a dry pan for 30 seconds per side
- Freezes 1 month; wrap individually in greaseproof paper
