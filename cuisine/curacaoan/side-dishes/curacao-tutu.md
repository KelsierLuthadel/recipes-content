# Tutu

![Tutu](resources/curacao-tutu.jpg)

*Curaçaoan tutu: mashed funchi stirred together with cooked black-eyed peas, browned onion and bacon, then pressed into a dome and turned out to slice.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus overnight bean soak)

**Cook Time:** 1 hour 15 minutes

## Overview
Tutu is the African-rooted side that lives in every Curaçao Sunday lunch alongside karni stoba. Cooked black-eyed peas are folded into a thick funchi (cornmeal mush) together with crisped bacon and slowly browned onion, then beaten hard until the mash takes a smooth glossy body. Pressed into a buttered bowl, inverted onto a platter, the tutu comes out as a smooth dome that gets sliced into thick wedges. The texture should be firm enough to hold its shape but soft enough that a fork slides through; the flavour is faintly smoky from the bacon, slightly nutty from the peas, with the corn carrying the body. It pairs with anything saucy: stoba, yambo, fried fish in tomato gravy.

## Ingredients

- 200 g dried black-eyed peas (soaked overnight; or 1 tin, drained, around 400 g cooked weight)
- 100 g smoked bacon or salted pork belly, finely diced
- 2 onions, finely chopped
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 250 g fine yellow cornmeal
- 1 litre water (or the bean cooking liquid plus water)
- 50 g butter
- 1 tbsp brown sugar
- 1 tsp salt
- ½ tsp ground allspice
- Black pepper

## Method

### Stage 1 - Cook the peas
1. If using dried: drain the soaked peas, cover with fresh water, simmer 50-60 minutes until tender. Reserve 800 ml of the cooking liquid; drain.
2. If using tinned: drain, rinse, set aside.

### Stage 2 - Brown the bacon and onion
1. In a wide pan over medium heat, cook the bacon for 6 minutes until the fat renders and the pieces crisp.
2. Add the onions; cook 12-15 minutes, stirring, until soft and deeply caramelised.
3. Add the garlic and allspice; 30 seconds. Reserve a few tablespoons for garnish if you like.

### Stage 3 - Make the funchi
1. Bring the bean liquid (topped up with water to 1 litre) and salt to a boil in a heavy pot.
2. Drop the heat to medium-low. Pour the cornmeal in a slow steady stream while whisking hard for 1 minute.
3. Switch to a wooden spoon. Stir constantly for 8 minutes until the funchi pulls away from the sides of the pot.

### Stage 4 - Combine
1. Tip the cooked peas, the bacon-onion mixture, butter, brown sugar and a heavy grind of pepper into the funchi.
2. Beat hard for 5 minutes with a wooden spoon, breaking down some of the peas but leaving plenty whole. The tutu should be smooth, glossy, holding its shape on the spoon.
3. Taste for salt and pepper.

### Stage 5 - Shape
1. Butter a 1.5 litre pudding bowl.
2. Pack the tutu in firmly, pressing into corners.
3. Rest 5 minutes; invert onto a warm platter; lift the bowl.
4. Garnish with the reserved crisp bacon and onion. Slice in thick wedges.

## Notes
- **Don't overcook the peas:** They should hold their shape, not turn to mush. Some break down in the beating, the rest stay whole.
- **Caramelise the onions properly:** A pale fry gives a flat tutu; deep brown onions are what give the dish its colour and depth.
- **The beating:** Five minutes of hard stirring is what turns a lumpy bean-corn mix into a glossy mash. Don't shortcut.
- **Salt the funchi base:** If you under-salt, the whole dish tastes bland. Taste before shaping.
- **Bean liquid is gold:** Cooking the funchi in the bean water adds a savoury depth you don't get from plain water.

## Variations
- **Vegetarian tutu:** Skip the bacon, use 2 tbsp coconut oil for richness, add a pinch of smoked paprika.
- **Tutu di kabaron:** Stir in 200 g cooked prawns at the end.
- **Tutu di piska:** Flake 150 g of cooked salt-cod into the mash.
- **Spiced tutu:** Add finely diced madame jeanette chilli to the onion fry.
- **Sweet tutu:** Increase brown sugar to 3 tbsp; serve as a dessert with cream.

## Serving
Slice into wedges and serve alongside karni stoba · with yambo ladled over the top · with fried fish and pika sauce · as a base for griddled prawns.

## Storage
- Refrigerates 3 days; reheat covered in a low oven with a splash of milk to loosen
- Slice cold leftover tutu and pan-fry in butter for breakfast (the best thing about leftovers)
- Doesn't freeze well; the texture goes grainy
