# Karni Stoba

![Karni Stoba](resources/karni-stoba.jpg)

*Curaçao's everyday beef stew: chuck or shin braised slow with onion, tomato, allspice and a little vinegar until the meat falls apart and the gravy turns deep mahogany, served with funchi or rice.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes

**Cook Time:** 2 hours 30 minutes

## Overview
Karni stoba is the meat stew Curaçaoan families eat once a week, the dish a grandmother makes when she wants to feed everyone. Chunks of beef chuck or shin are seared, then dropped into a pot with onion, garlic, green pepper, tomato and the island's defining spice trio of allspice, nutmeg and clove. A spoon of vinegar at the start brightens the gravy and helps the meat break down; the long slow cook (two and a half hours) renders every connective tissue down to silk. The finished stoba has a glossy reddish-brown gravy that coats the back of a spoon, with meat that pulls apart at the lightest pressure. Funchi (cornmeal mush) is the side that catches the sauce; white rice works just as well.

## Ingredients

- 1 kg beef chuck or shin, cut into 4 cm chunks
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil
- 2 large onions, sliced
- 1 green pepper, sliced
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tomato, diced
- 2 tbsp tomato puree
- 1 tbsp white vinegar
- 1 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
- 1 tsp ground allspice
- ½ tsp grated nutmeg
- ¼ tsp ground clove
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 tbsp fresh thyme leaves
- 1 madame jeanette chilli, whole (or a scotch bonnet; pierced once)
- 750 ml beef stock
- Salt and black pepper

## Method

### Stage 1 - Brown the meat
1. Pat the beef dry; season heavily with salt and pepper.
2. Heat the oil in a heavy lidded pot over high heat. Brown the beef in batches, 4-5 minutes per batch, taking a deep crust. Lift to a plate.

### Stage 2 - Build the base
1. Drop the heat to medium. Add the onion and green pepper; cook 10 minutes until soft and starting to caramelise at the edges.
2. Stir in garlic, allspice, nutmeg and clove; 30 seconds until fragrant.
3. Add tomato and tomato puree; cook 2 minutes until the puree darkens.

### Stage 3 - Braise
1. Return the beef and any juices to the pot.
2. Pour in the vinegar, Worcestershire and stock; tuck in bay, thyme and the whole chilli.
3. Bring to a simmer, lid on, drop heat to low.
4. Braise 2 hours, stirring every 30 minutes, until the meat is fork-tender and the gravy thick. If it dries out, top up with a splash of water.
5. Fish out the chilli and bay leaves; taste for salt.

## Notes
- **Whole chilli, no chop:** Madame jeanette and scotch bonnet give perfume without raw heat. Piercing once releases just enough flavour; chopping makes the stew burningly hot.
- **The vinegar:** A non-negotiable. It tenderises the meat and gives the gravy its faint sour edge that lifts the allspice.
- **Allspice is the signature:** This is what makes a stoba Caribbean rather than European. Don't substitute mixed spice.
- **Brown deeply:** A weak sear gives a pale stew. Pat the meat dry, work in batches, and don't move the pieces until they release on their own.
- **Reheats better:** Like most stews, karni stoba is better on day two. Make it the day before serving where possible.

## Variations
- **Karni stoba di kabritu:** Goat stew, same method, cooked 30 minutes longer.
- **Stoba di tapa:** Tongue stew, simmered first in salted water then finished in the stoba sauce.
- **Karni stoba ku banana:** A handful of ripe plantain chunks dropped in for the last 30 minutes.
- **Pumpkin stoba:** Add a few chunks of West Indian pumpkin (calabaza) for the last hour; they melt into the gravy.

## Serving
Funchi or white rice · fried plantain · a wedge of avocado · pickled cucumber on the side.

## Storage
- Refrigerates 4 days; flavour deepens overnight
- Freezes 3 months in a sealed tub
- Reheat over low heat with a splash of water or stock to loosen the gravy
