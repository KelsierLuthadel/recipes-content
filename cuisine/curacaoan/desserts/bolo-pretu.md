# Bolo Pretu

![Bolo Pretu](resources/bolo-pretu.jpg)

*Curaçao's wedding-and-Christmas black cake: dried fruit soaked for months in dark rum and port, beaten into a deep-brown spiced batter, baked low and slow, then fed with more rum as it matures.*

**Serves:** 16

**Prep Time:** 1 hour (plus 1 month minimum fruit soaking, ideally 3-6 months)

**Cook Time:** 2 hours

## Overview
Bolo pretu (literally "black cake") is the most ceremonious thing in the Curaçao kitchen, the cake that appears at every wedding, every christening, every Christmas table. It started life as the British plum pudding the colonial trade brought across the Caribbean, but Curaçaoan cooks turned it into something darker, denser, more spiced and far more alcoholic. Dried prunes, raisins, currants, cherries and orange peel are soaked for at least a month (ideally six months) in dark rum and ruby port until they swell and turn nearly black. The soaked fruit is then minced, beaten into a brown-butter, brown-sugar batter heavy with cinnamon, allspice, clove and nutmeg, plus a slug of burnt-sugar caramel that gives the cake its mahogany colour. Baked at low heat for two hours, brushed weekly with more rum, the cake keeps for years and is meant to be matured.

## Ingredients

### For the fruit (start at least 1 month, ideally 3-6 months ahead)
- 250 g pitted prunes
- 250 g raisins
- 200 g currants
- 150 g glacé cherries
- 100 g candied mixed peel (orange and lemon)
- 100 g chopped dried figs or dates
- 300 ml dark rum (good quality)
- 200 ml ruby port
- 100 ml cherry brandy

### For the burnt sugar
- 200 g caster sugar
- 100 ml hot water

### For the cake batter
- 300 g unsalted butter, softened
- 300 g soft dark brown sugar
- 6 large eggs
- 350 g plain flour
- 2 tsp baking powder
- 2 tsp ground cinnamon
- 1 tsp ground allspice
- 1 tsp ground clove
- 1 tsp grated nutmeg
- 1 tsp salt
- 100 g ground almonds
- Zest of 2 oranges and 1 lemon
- 2 tsp vanilla extract
- 100 ml extra dark rum (for feeding)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak the fruit (do this first, weeks ahead)
1. Mince or finely chop all the dried fruits together by hand or in two pulses of a food processor; you want a coarse dark mince, not a paste.
2. Pack into a sterilised jar.
3. Pour over the rum, port and cherry brandy; the fruit should be just submerged.
4. Seal, store somewhere cool and dark.
5. Shake the jar weekly. Soak for a minimum of 1 month; 3-6 months gives the proper depth.

### Stage 2 - Make the burnt sugar
1. Set the caster sugar in a heavy small saucepan over medium heat.
2. Cook without stirring until it melts and turns deep mahogany (not black; don't let it burn beyond that). About 5 minutes.
3. Stand back and pour in the hot water; the syrup will hiss and seize. Stir gently over low heat 2 minutes until smooth and pourable.
4. Cool fully.

### Stage 3 - Make the batter
1. Heat oven to 150°C. Line a 25 cm deep round tin with double greaseproof paper, sides taller than the tin.
2. Beat the butter and brown sugar with electric beaters for 8 minutes until pale and very fluffy.
3. Add the eggs one at a time, beating well between each.
4. Sift flour, baking powder, cinnamon, allspice, clove, nutmeg and salt over the batter.
5. Add the ground almonds, zest and vanilla.
6. Fold in with a large metal spoon until almost combined.
7. Tip in the soaked fruit (with all its liquid) and 200 ml of the burnt sugar. Fold until even; the batter will turn dark mahogany.

### Stage 4 - Bake
1. Spoon into the tin; level the top.
2. Bake 2 hours, until a skewer comes out clean. The top should be deeply dark; cover with foil after 90 minutes if it darkens too fast.
3. While still warm in the tin, brush 50 ml of the extra rum over the surface; let it soak in.
4. Cool fully in the tin.

### Stage 5 - Mature
1. Turn out; wrap tightly in greaseproof paper, then foil.
2. Once a week for 4-8 weeks, unwrap and brush 1 tbsp rum across the top. Re-wrap.
3. The cake is ready to eat after 4 weeks; better at 2 months; magnificent at 6.

## Notes
- **The soak is non-negotiable:** A 24-hour quick soak gives you a perfectly nice fruit cake, but it isn't bolo pretu. The depth comes from months in alcohol.
- **Burnt sugar, not just brown sugar:** The caramel is what gives bolo pretu its mahogany colour and a faint bitterness that cuts the sweetness.
- **Low oven, long bake:** 150°C for 2 hours. A hot oven dries the outside before the dense centre cooks; the long bake is what gives the cake its tight crumb.
- **Feed it weekly:** The rum brushing both preserves the cake and deepens the flavour; skip this and the cake dries.
- **Use the proper rum:** Dark Caribbean rum (the kind labelled añejo or oscuro) carries the right molasses notes; light rum is too clean and gives a thinner-flavoured cake.

## Variations
- **Wedding bolo pretu:** Iced with brilliant white royal icing and silver beads, the contrast with the black inside is the whole point.
- **Bolo pretu chiki:** Bake in 6 small loaf tins for 50 minutes instead.
- **Less alcohol:** Reduce the rum-port-brandy by half and top up the soak with strong cold black tea (not the same depth, but workable for non-drinkers).
- **Cherry-heavy version:** Double the glacé cherries, common in the Banda Bou region.
- **Spice-forward:** Add 1 tsp ground anise and ½ tsp cardamom for a more aromatic version.

## Serving
Slice thin (the cake is dense; a thick wedge is too much) · with a spoon of softly whipped cream · alongside strong black coffee · at Christmas, weddings, christenings · with a small glass of port.

## Storage
- Wrapped tightly, fed monthly, the cake keeps 12 months at room temperature
- The longer it sits, the better it gets; 6 months is widely considered the peak
- Don't refrigerate (dries the crumb); the alcohol is the preservative
