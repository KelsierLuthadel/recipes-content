# Souskaye

![Souskaye](resources/souskaye.jpg)

*Saint Lucian souskaye: soaked salt fish flaked cold and dressed in lime, raw onion, cucumber, scotch bonnet and oil. The eat-in-the-shade snack served with floats or crackers, sharp and refreshing.*

**Serves:** 4 as a snack

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus 12 hr salt fish soak)

**Cook Time:** 5 minutes

## Overview
Souskaye (sometimes spelled "sous-kaye" or "souse-kaye") is a Lucian cold-dressed salt fish salad, eaten as a midday snack with fried floats, crackers or bread. Salt cod or salt fish is soaked, briefly poached, flaked cold, then dressed in lime juice, finely sliced raw onion, cucumber, scotch bonnet pepper, garlic and a glug of oil. It eats sharp, bright and refreshing in the heat, sits in the fridge happily for a day, and turns up at lazy Sunday lunches and after-church gatherings across the island. The Lucian word "souskaye" carries the same root as the wider Caribbean "souse" (a pickled fish or pork preparation), and the technique is shared up and down the islands.

## Ingredients
- 300 g salt cod or salt fish (one piece)
- Water for soaking
- 1 small red onion, very finely sliced
- 1/2 cucumber, deseeded and finely diced
- 1 small green bell pepper, finely diced
- 1/2 scotch bonnet, very finely chopped (or to taste)
- 2 cloves garlic, finely grated
- 2 spring onions, finely sliced
- 2 tbsp fresh chive or parsley, chopped
- Juice of 3 limes (about 90 ml)
- 3 tbsp vegetable oil (or light olive oil)
- 1 tsp black pepper
- A pinch of salt (taste first)
- Optional: 1 ripe tomato, deseeded and diced

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak and cook the fish
1. Rinse the salt fish; place in a bowl with cold water; soak 12 hours (overnight), changing the water 2-3 times.
2. Drain; transfer to a small pan with fresh water; bring to a gentle simmer; cook 5 minutes.
3. Drain; cool completely.
4. Peel off any skin; pull out the bones; flake into rough pieces (not too fine - you want flakes you can pick up).

### Stage 2 - Sharpen the onion
1. Place the sliced red onion in a small bowl with the juice of 1 lime and a pinch of salt; let it sit 5 minutes. This softens the raw bite and turns the onion bright pink.

### Stage 3 - Dress
1. In a wide bowl, combine the flaked fish, sharpened onion (with its lime), cucumber, green pepper, scotch bonnet, garlic, spring onions and chive.
2. Add the remaining lime juice, oil, black pepper.
3. Toss gently so the dressing coats everything without breaking up the flakes.
4. Taste; salt only if needed (the fish carries salt).

### Stage 4 - Rest and serve
1. Let it sit 10-15 minutes for the flavours to mingle; eat at room temperature or lightly chilled.
2. Pile onto a plate; eat with floats, crackers or bread.

## Notes
- **Coarse flakes:** Don't shred the fish to threads; you want pieces big enough to spear with a fork or scoop into bread.
- **The lime is the engine:** Lime acid lifts the fish, sharpens the onion, brightens everything. Use real lime, not lemon, not bottled juice.
- **Heat to taste:** A whole scotch bonnet is the Lucian default. Halve it if you want manageable; deseed for the gentlest version.

## Variations
- **Shark souskaye:** Saint Lucian fishermen historically used small shark; substitute fresh white fish (cod, haddock, mahi) lightly poached and flaked in place of the salt fish - skip the long soak.
- **Smoked herring version:** Tinned smoked herring in oil, drained, flaked, dressed the same way. Ready in 15 minutes total.
- **Tinned mackerel souskaye:** The student-budget version, tinned mackerel, same dressing.
- **Avocado addition:** A diced ripe avocado folded in at the end turns it from snack to small lunch.
- **Tomato heavy:** Double the tomato for a souskaye-meets-ceviche character.

## Serving
Serve at room temperature or lightly chilled · alongside fried floats or crackers · a wedge of lime · cold mauby, sorrel or beer

## Storage
- Refrigerate 24 hours covered (the onion and lime keep it bright)
- After 24 hours the cucumber goes soft and the dressing loses its sharpness
- Do not freeze
