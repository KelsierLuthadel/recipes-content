# Saint Lucian

*The kitchen of the Caribbean island that ping-ponged between French and British colonisation 14 times. French Creole and British Caribbean traditions on one plate, with green fig (unripe banana) and saltfish as the national dish.*

## A short history

Before any European sail came over the horizon, Saint Lucia belonged to the Arawak and then the Kalinago (Island Carib) people, who paddled up the Lesser Antilles chain from the Orinoco basin from around 200 CE. They named the island Iouanalao ("the land of the iguana"), grew cassava on slash-and-burn forest plots, fished the reefs and roasted whatever the sea gave up over green-wood fires. The cassava bread, the pepperpot stew kept alive on the hearth for years at a time, and the open-fire cooking under banana leaves all trace to the Kalinago kitchen, and they sit under everything Saint Lucian cooks do today.

Saint Lucian cooking pulls two colonial inheritances onto a single plate. The French ran the island for most of the 17th and 18th centuries; the British took it definitively in 1814. The result is the only Caribbean nation where Creole French (Kwéyòl) is spoken alongside English, and the cuisine carries the same duality: French roux-based stews, court-bouillon fish and the Sunday family lunch sit beside British-Caribbean rice-and-peas and fried chicken.

The 17th and 18th centuries also rebuilt the population. Sugar planters cleared the rainforest, set up mills, and shipped enslaved West Africans (largely from the Bight of Benin and the Congo basin) to work the cane. The enslaved population brought green fig (unripe banana, the universal starch), saltfish (Atlantic salt cod traded into the Caribbean from Newfoundland and brought south as cheap protein for the plantations), callaloo (dasheen leaves), okra, pigeon peas and the slow-stewed one-pot. The Columbian Exchange ran through the kitchen in both directions: African yams, plantain and okra moved west; Caribbean cassava, cocoa, allspice and Scotch bonnet chillies moved east. The grand-mère cooking that anchors the Sunday table comes straight out of this period, with French technique laid over African ingredients.

Emancipation in 1834 freed the labour force without freeing the land. Most former slaves stayed near the estates as smallholders, growing the same provisions (yam, dasheen, green fig, breadfruit) on garden plots and trading at the Saturday markets that still run in Castries and Soufrière. Indian and Madeiran indentured labourers arrived from the 1850s, adding curry powder, roti and a touch of Portuguese sweet-bread tradition. Bananas replaced sugar as the cash crop by the 1920s, and the green-fig kitchen took on a deeper economic meaning: every Saint Lucian household ate the unripe ones that wouldn't ship to Britain.

Independence in 1979 made green fig and saltfish the national dish on paper; the bouyon (a thick stew with everything in it) settled in as the everyday Sunday plate. Today the cuisine travels in the same conversation as the country's two World Heritage-listed Pitons, the rum distilleries (Bounty Rum, Chairman's Reserve), and the lively local fish-fries at Anse La Raye every Friday night. A new generation of chefs has started cooking with what the rainforest actually grows (cocoa, breadfruit, soursop, wild thyme), but the home kitchen has barely shifted: the Saturday market basket and the Sunday pot read the same as they did a hundred years ago.

## Regional variations

Saint Lucia is small (617 km²) but the accents shift across it:

- **Castries (north, capital)**: the mainstream. Green fig and saltfish, bouyon.
- **Soufrière (south-west)**: French Creole strongest. Court-bouillon, the cocoa-tea tradition.
- **Anse La Raye (west coast)**: the Friday-night fish fry.
- **Vieux Fort (south)**: closer to St Vincent cooking, more conch dishes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Green fig**: unripe bananas, boiled, the universal starch.
- **Saltfish (salt cod)**: with green fig, in stew.
- **Callaloo (dasheen leaves)**: in soups and stews.
- **Coconut milk**: in pelau, in the bouillons.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.
- **Bay, thyme, scallion**: the herb trinity.
- **Cocoa**: Saint Lucia grows fine cacao; cocoa tea (a savoury-sweet hot drink with bay leaf and spices) is a regional speciality.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Green Fig and Saltfish](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/green-fig-and-saltfish)**: green bananas boiled then sautéed with salt cod, onion, tomato and herbs. The national dish.
2. **[Bouyon](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/bouyon)**: thick stew with provisions (green fig, plantain, yam), salt meat, dumplings and callaloo. The Sunday family lunch.
3. **[Pelau](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/pelau)**: rice cooked with brown-sugar-caramelised chicken, pigeon peas and coconut milk. The Caribbean rice classic, Saint Lucian style.
4. **[Lucian Curry Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/lucian-curry-chicken)**: chicken in Caribbean curry powder (turmeric, cumin, coriander) with potato, served over rice.
5. **[Callaloo Soup](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/callaloo-soup)**: pureed dasheen leaves with okra, crab or salt pork, coconut milk and Scotch bonnet.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Piton (the local lager) or a Bounty Rum punch. For Friday night, drive to Anse La Raye, order grilled fish, fried plantain, johnny cake and beer at one of the open-air shacks; eat with your fingers, watch the sun set over the Caribbean.
