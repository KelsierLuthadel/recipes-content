# Saint Lucian

*The kitchen of the Caribbean island that ping-ponged between French and British colonisation 14 times. French Creole and British Caribbean traditions on one plate, with green fig (unripe banana) and saltfish as the national dish.*

## A short history

Saint Lucian cooking is the synthesis of two colonial inheritances. The French controlled the island for most of the 17th and 18th centuries; the British took it definitively in 1814. The result is the only Caribbean nation where Creole French (Kwéyòl) is spoken alongside English, and the cuisine reflects the same duality — French roux-based stews, court-bouillon fish, and the Sunday family lunch alongside British-Caribbean rice-and-peas and fried chicken.

The West African enslaved population brought green fig (unripe banana, the universal starch), saltfish (Atlantic salt cod traded into the Caribbean), callaloo (dasheen leaves), and the slow-stewed one-pot. The 1979 independence codified green fig and saltfish as the national dish; the bouyon (a thick stew with everything in it) became the everyday Sunday plate.

Today the cuisine is associated with the country's two World Heritage-listed Pitons mountains, the rum distilleries (Bounty Rum, Chairman's Reserve), and the lively local-tradition fish-fries at Anse La Raye every Friday night.

## Regional variations

Saint Lucia is small (617 km²) but with distinct regional accents:

- **Castries (north, capital)**: codified mainstream. Green fig and saltfish, bouyon.
- **Soufrière (south-west)**: French Creole strongest. Court-bouillon, the cocoa-tea tradition.
- **Anse La Raye (west coast)**: the Friday-night fish fry.
- **Vieux Fort (south)**: closer to St Vincent cooking, more conch dishes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Green fig**: unripe bananas, boiled, the universal starch.
- **Saltfish (salt cod)**: with green fig, in stew.
- **Callaloo (dasheen leaves)**: in soups and stews.
- **Coconut milk**: in pelau, in the bouillons.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.
- **Bay, thyme, scallion**: the herb trinity.
- **Cocoa**: Saint Lucia produces fine cacao; cocoa tea (a savoury-sweet hot drink with bay leaf and spices) is a regional speciality.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Green Fig and Saltfish](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/green-fig-and-saltfish)**: green bananas boiled then sautéed with salt cod, onion, tomato and herbs. The national dish.
2. **[Bouyon](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/bouyon)**: thick stew with provisions (green fig, plantain, yam), salt meat, dumplings and callaloo. The Sunday family lunch.
3. **[Pelau](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/pelau)**: rice cooked with brown-sugar-caramelised chicken, pigeon peas and coconut milk. The Caribbean rice classic, Saint Lucian style.
4. **[Lucian Curry Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/lucian-curry-chicken)**: chicken in Caribbean curry powder (turmeric, cumin, coriander) with potato, served over rice.
5. **[Callaloo Soup](/recipes/cuisine/saint-lucian/callaloo-soup)**: pureed dasheen leaves with okra, crab or salt pork, coconut milk and Scotch bonnet.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Piton (the local lager) or with Bounty Rum punch. For Friday night, drive to Anse La Raye, order grilled fish, fried plantain, johnny cake and beer at one of the open-air shacks; eat with your fingers, watch the sun set over the Caribbean.
