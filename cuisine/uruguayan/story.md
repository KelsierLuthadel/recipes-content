# Uruguayan

*The South American kitchen that's essentially a beef-cult with an Italian-immigrant accent. Asado as the universal weekend, chivito (the loaded steak sandwich) as the everyday lunch, milanesas in every form, and the famous mate-drinking thermos that no Uruguayan goes anywhere without.*

## A short history

Uruguayan cooking starts with the Charrúa, Chaná and Minuán peoples who hunted rhea and small deer across the open grasslands and fished the Río de la Plata estuary before contact. Their everyday kitchen relied on toasted corn flour, smoked river fish, and the dried meat strips they cured in the wind, the technique that became *charqui* and eventually *carne seca*. Spanish ships first sighted the coast in 1516 (Solís was killed and reportedly eaten by Charrúa warriors near what is now Colonia), but the Spanish saw little of value in the region and barely settled it for over a century.

The transformation came in 1611, when Hernando Arias de Saavedra (Hernandarias) released a small herd of cattle and horses onto the Banda Oriental's empty grasslands. The land turned out to be ideal cattle country, and within a few decades the herds had multiplied into the hundreds of thousands of feral animals that roamed the pampas. By the early 1700s, the *gauchos* (the South American cowboy class, mixed Spanish, indigenous and African ancestry) were hunting these wild herds for hides and tallow, eating the meat fire-roasted on iron crosses driven into the ground beside the carcass. The asado tradition (long slow grilling over wood embers, the gaucho's everyday meal) dates from this period. Tallow exports to European candle and soap factories made the Banda Oriental commercially valuable; the meat itself was so abundant it was treated as nearly free.

Montevideo was founded in 1726 as a Spanish counter to Portuguese Brazil, and the 19th century brought independence in 1828, the long civil war between Blancos and Colorados, and the rise of the *saladero* (the salt-beef plant) that turned wild pampas cattle into jerked beef exported to Cuban and Brazilian slave plantations. The Liebig Extract of Meat Company at Fray Bentos, founded in 1865, industrialised the process further and pioneered the canned corned beef that fed both world wars. The same period saw the Columbian Exchange continue to land: tomatoes, maize, potatoes and chillies had already been part of indigenous cooking, but European wheat, citrus, wine grapes and onions arrived in commercial quantities through the saladero ports.

Mass immigration from the 1860s to the 1930s reshaped the country. Roughly half of all Uruguayans today have Italian ancestry, mostly from Liguria, Piedmont and Campania, and the Italian thread runs deep through the kitchen: pasta, milanesa (the breaded cutlet, named for Milan), pizza in a thinner Argentine-Uruguayan style, the gnocchi-on-the-29th-of-the-month tradition, focaccia, and the rich gelato culture of Montevideo. Spanish Basque and Galician immigrants in the same period brought octopus, paella variations, and the bacalao traditions. Smaller waves of Germans, Lebanese, Russian Jews and Armenians filled in around the edges, and each left dishes on the Montevideo table.

Independence-era Uruguay codified the meat-and-mate diet, but the chivito and the modern asado lineup are 20th-century inventions. The chivito was reportedly created in 1944 at a Punta del Este restaurant called El Mejillón, when an Argentine tourist asked for a baby goat (*chivito* in Spanish) and the cook, with no goat in the kitchen, improvised a piled-high steak sandwich and gave it the name anyway. It stuck. The asado lineup of asado de tira, vacío, morcilla, chorizo, chinchulines and provoleta solidified in the same mid-century period as the working-class Sunday institution that it remains.

Uruguay eats more beef per head than almost any nation (it trades the top spot with Argentina year to year). The asado is the universal weekend institution: long slow grilling of cuts over wood embers, eaten over hours with friends and family. The chivito is the everyday lunch institution. A thin pounded sirloin, mozzarella, ham, bacon, fried egg, lettuce, tomato, mayonnaise, on a soft bun. Pile-high and slightly chaotic. The "chivito al plato" version skips the bun and serves the lot on a plate with chips and salad, the format more common at restaurants. The Italian-immigrant influence runs deep, especially in Montevideo. Pasta, milanesa, and pizza are home-cook staples, and the country has the highest per-capita consumption of yerba mate in the world. The thermos and gourd go everywhere: to the beach, to the office, to a football match, into bed.

## Regional variations

The country is small (around 176,000 km²) and broadly homogeneous.

- **Montevideo and the coast**: the codified mainstream. Chivito, asado, the Mercado del Puerto grill stalls.
- **Interior (Salto, Tacuarembó)**: gaucho country. Asado, mate, ranch-style cooking.
- **Punta del Este (eastern coast)**: more seafood and cosmopolitan summer-tourist menus.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beef**: grass-fed, all cuts. Asado de tira, vacío, lomo, picaña.
- **Yerba mate**: the daily ritual drink. Carried in a thermos and gourd everywhere; Uruguayans drink more mate per head than anyone.
- **Dulce de leche**: caramelised milk. Filling alfajores, eaten by the spoon.
- **Provolone**: melted on the parrilla as provoleta.
- **Italian-Argentine pasta tradition**: weekly home-cooked pasta.
- **Bread (the Italian-Uruguayan kind)**: crusty, used for sandwiches.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Chivito](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/chivito)**: pile-high sandwich of thin sirloin steak, ham, bacon, mozzarella, fried egg, lettuce, tomato and mayonnaise on a soft bun (or "al plato", served on a plate with chips). The national dish.
2. **[Milanesa a la Napolitana](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/milanesa-a-la-napolitana)**: breaded beef cutlet topped with tomato sauce, ham, mozzarella and oregano. The Italian-Argentine-Uruguayan classic, shared with Argentina.
3. **[Pascualina](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/pascualina)**: spinach-and-egg pie. The Italian-immigrant inheritance. Eat cold for picnics or hot for Sunday lunch.
4. **[Puchero Uruguayo](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/puchero-uruguayo)**: boiled-beef-and-vegetable stew with chickpeas, potato, cabbage, sweet potato. The hearty winter Sunday plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Tannat (Uruguay's signature red wine, the most-cultivated grape; locals claim the world's best Tannat against French Madiran) or with a clericó (Uruguayan sangria). End with a chajá (a fluffy sponge-and-cream-and-peach-and-meringue dessert, a Paysandú invention) or with alfajores filled with dulce de leche.
