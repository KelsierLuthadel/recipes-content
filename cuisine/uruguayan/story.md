# Uruguayan

*The South American kitchen that's essentially a beef-cult with an Italian-immigrant accent. Asado as the universal weekend, chivito (the loaded steak sandwich) as the everyday lunch, milanesas in every form, and the famous mate-drinking-thermos that no Uruguayan goes anywhere without.*

## A short history

Uruguayan cooking shares its broad shape with Argentine — the same cattle-country pampas, the same Italian-Spanish immigration in the 19th-20th century, the same asado-and-empanada tradition. The two countries even share the dispute over which one invented the dulce de leche.

The country eats more beef per capita than nearly any nation (with Argentina, they fight for the top spot annually). The asado is the universal weekend institution — long slow grilling of cuts (asado de tira, vacío, morcilla, chorizo) over wood embers, eaten over hours with friends and family.

Chivito — the Uruguayan steak sandwich — is the everyday lunch institution. A thin pounded sirloin, mozzarella, ham, bacon, fried egg, lettuce, tomato, mayonnaise, on a soft bun. Pile-high and slightly chaotic. The "chivito al plato" version skips the bun and serves it on a plate with chips and salad — the format more common at restaurants.

The Italian-immigrant influence runs deep, especially in Montevideo. Pasta, milanesa, and pizza (a thinner Argentine-style version) are home-cook staples.

## Regional variations

The country is small (~176,000 km²) and broadly homogeneous:

- **Montevideo and the coast**: codified mainstream. Chivito, asado, the Mercado del Puerto grill stalls.
- **Interior (Salto, Tacuarembó)**: gaucho country. Asado, mate, ranch-style cooking.
- **Punta del Este (eastern coast)**: more seafood and cosmopolitan summer-tourist menus.

## Signature ingredients

- **Beef**: grass-fed, all cuts. Asado de tira, vacío, lomo, picaña.
- **Yerba mate**: the daily ritual drink. Carried in a thermos and gourd everywhere — Uruguayans use more mate per capita than anyone.
- **Dulce de leche**: caramelised milk. Filling alfajores, eaten by the spoon.
- **Provolone**: melted on the parrilla as provoleta.
- **Italian-Argentine pasta tradition**: weekly home-cooked pasta.
- **Bread (the Italian-Uruguayan kind)**: crusty, used for sandwiches.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Chivito](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/chivito)**: pile-high sandwich — thin sirloin steak, ham, bacon, mozzarella, fried egg, lettuce, tomato, mayonnaise, on a soft bun (or "al plato" — without the bun, served on a plate with chips). The national dish.
2. **[Milanesa a la Napolitana](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/milanesa-a-la-napolitana)**: breaded beef cutlet topped with tomato sauce, ham, mozzarella and oregano. The Italian-Argentine-Uruguayan classic, shared with Argentina.
3. **[Pascualina](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/pascualina)**: spinach-and-egg pie. The Italian-immigrant inheritance — eaten cold for picnics or hot for Sunday lunch.
4. **[Puchero Uruguayo](/recipes/cuisine/uruguayan/puchero-uruguayo)**: boiled-beef-and-vegetable stew with chickpeas, potato, cabbage, sweet potato. The hearty winter Sunday plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Tannat (Uruguay's signature red wine — the most-cultivated grape, with the country claiming the world's best Tannat against French Madiran) or with a clericó (Uruguayan sangria). End with a chajá (a fluffy sponge-and-cream-and-peach-and-meringue dessert, a Paysandú invention) or with alfajores filled with dulce de leche.
