# Salvadoran

*The kitchen of pupusas. Stuffed corn flatbreads cooked on a comal (beans, cheese, pork), the national dish, eaten everywhere, every day. Plus slow-cooked sopa de pata (cow's-foot soup) and the tamales pisques that reach back to the Pipil-Mayan tradition.*

## A short history

Salvadoran cooking sits on a deep Pipil-Mayan base. The Pipil migrated south from central Mexico in the 8th to 11th centuries, settling the western valleys around Cuscatlán (modern Santa Ana and Sonsonate) and bringing the Nahua agricultural triad of corn, beans and squash with them. The corn went through nixtamalisation: simmered with slaked lime, ground on the metate, slapped out on a comal as tortillas, pupusas and atoles. Loroco (the edible flower that turns up in fillings), tomatoes, cacao, chillies and the wild forest greens filled out the kitchen. Pupusas predate the Spanish; Pipil-Mayan archaeology at Joya de Cerén (the "Pompeii of the Americas", buried under volcanic ash around 600 CE) shows the comal, the corn-dough rounds and the storage jars of bean filling in working order.

The Spanish arrived in 1524 with Pedro de Alvarado. The colonial centuries layered rice, beef, dairy, pork, wheat bread and the Catholic feast calendar onto the corn kitchen, but the corn kitchen never left. Cattle ranching took over the central plateau; the Spanish introduced sugarcane on the coastal strip; cacao stayed an important Pipil crop until indigo replaced it in the 1700s as the colony's main export. The mestizo households that grew out of the colonial period kept tortillas as the daily bread, beans as the daily protein, and the comal as the centre of the kitchen.

Independence in 1821 and the brief Federal Republic of Central America (1823-1841) didn't change the food. The 19th-century coffee boom did: the volcanic highlands turned into one giant coffee plantation, and El Salvador became, for a moment, one of the world's largest coffee exporters. Coffee money built San Salvador's coffee oligarchy, but the everyday kitchen stayed on corn, beans and chicharrón, with the Sunday sopa de pata (cow's-foot soup) as the proper restorative.

Pupusas are unmistakably Salvadoran: thick corn flatbreads stuffed with beans, cheese, pork or loroco, cooked on a comal and served with curtido (lightly fermented cabbage slaw) and a thin tomato salsa. The 20th century turned them from a regional speciality into the universal everyday food. El Salvador now has a National Pupusa Day (second Sunday in November) and a serious pupusa export trade.

The civil war (1979-1992) pushed a lot of cooks abroad. The diaspora kitchens of Los Angeles, DC, Houston and Toronto have made pupusas international street food; Salvadoran takeout windows in MacArthur Park and Long Island City sell them by the thousand on a Sunday morning. The home kitchens in El Salvador have changed little: the tortilla press, the comal, the cabbage in brine on the counter and the pot of beans on the back of the stove read exactly as they did before the war. The cooking is what the diaspora carried with them and the country still cooks.

## Regional variations

The country is small (~21,000 km²) and the cuisine stays broadly consistent:

- **San Salvador and the central plateau**: the mainstream. Pupusas, sopa de pata.
- **East (San Miguel)**: distinct tamales, more fish from the Gulf of Fonseca.
- **West (Santa Ana)**: closer to Guatemalan cooking, with more Mayan influence.
- **Coast (La Libertad, Sonsonate)**: more seafood. The famous curiles (mangrove shellfish).

## Signature ingredients

- **Corn (maíz)**: nixtamalised for pupusas, tortillas and atole.
- **Beans (frijoles)**: refried in pupusas, in side dishes.
- **Loroco**: edible flower with a slightly funky-vegetal flavour, used in pupusas and atole.
- **Quesillo or queso fresco**: stretchy melted cheese in pupusas.
- **Chicharrón (pork crackling)**: ground into pupusa filling.
- **Cabbage (in curtido)**: lightly fermented with onion and oregano.
- **Tomato salsa**: thin, mild, served with everything.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Pupusas](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/pupusas)**: thick corn flatbreads stuffed with beans, cheese, pork (chicharrón) or loroco, cooked on a comal. Served with curtido and tomato salsa. The national dish.
2. **[Sopa de Pata](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/sopa-de-pata)**: cow's-foot soup with tripe, yuca, corn, plantain, cabbage and chili. The Sunday-morning hangover cure.
3. **[Tamales Pisques](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/tamales-pisques)**: corn-and-bean tamales without meat, wrapped in banana leaves. The Pipil-Mayan inheritance.
4. **[Gallo en Chicha](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/gallo-en-chicha)**: rooster (or chicken) slow-cooked in chicha (a corn-and-pineapple ferment), brown sugar and warm spices. The Christmas Eve plate.
5. **[Yuca con Chicharrón](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/yuca-con-chicharron)**: boiled or fried cassava with crispy pork and curtido. The street-food side.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with horchata (the Salvadoran version uses morro seeds, sesame, peanut and pumpkin seeds for a richer, creamier texture than the Mexican one) or with a Pilsener, the national lager. Finish with empanadas de plátano, fried sweet-plantain pies stuffed with custard or milk.
