# Salvadoran

*The kitchen of pupusas. Stuffed corn flatbreads cooked on a comal — beans, cheese, pork — the national dish, eaten everywhere, every day. Plus the slow-cooked sopa de pata (cow's-foot soup) and the tamales pisques that go back to the Pipil Mayan tradition.*

## A short history

Salvadoran cooking sits on a deep indigenous Pipil-Mayan base (corn — nixtamalised and made into pupusas, tortillas, atoles; beans; squash; chillies; loroco, the edible flower used in cooking and pupusa fillings; tomatoes; cacao) layered with a Spanish colonial overlay (rice, beef, dairy, pork, the Catholic feast calendar).

Pupusas — thick corn flatbreads stuffed with beans, cheese, pork or loroco flower, cooked on a comal, served with curtido (lightly fermented cabbage slaw) and tomato salsa — are unmistakably Salvadoran. The dish predates the Spanish and is documented in Pipil-Mayan archaeology. Today El Salvador has a National Pupusa Day (second Sunday of November) and a robust pupusa export trade. The Salvadoran diaspora kitchens (in Los Angeles, DC, Houston, Toronto) have made pupusas international street food.

The civil war (1979-1992) drove significant emigration. The diaspora kitchens preserve the codified tradition. Internally the cuisine remains traditional.

## Regional variations

The country is small (~21,000 km²) and the cuisine is broadly consistent:

- **San Salvador and the central plateau**: codified mainstream. Pupusas, sopa de pata.
- **East (San Miguel)**: distinct tamales, more fish from the Gulf of Fonseca.
- **West (Santa Ana)**: closer to Guatemalan cooking. More Mayan influence.
- **Coast (La Libertad, Sonsonate)**: more seafood. The famous curiles (mangrove shellfish).

## Signature ingredients

- **Corn (maíz)**: nixtamalised for pupusas, tortillas and atole.
- **Beans (frijoles)**: refried in pupusas, in side dishes.
- **Loroco**: edible flower with a slightly funky-vegetal flavour, used in pupusas and atole.
- **Quesillo or queso fresco**: stretchy melted cheese in pupusas.
- **Chicharrón (pork crackling)**: ground into pupusa filling.
- **Cabbage (in curtido)**: lightly fermented with onion and oregano.
- **Tomato salsa**: thin, mild — served with everything.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Pupusas](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/pupusas)**: thick corn flatbreads stuffed with beans, cheese, pork (chicharrón) or loroco, cooked on a comal. Served with curtido (cabbage slaw) and tomato salsa. The national dish.
2. **[Sopa de Pata](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/sopa-de-pata)**: cow's-foot soup with tripe, yuca, corn, plantain, cabbage and chili. The Sunday-morning hangover cure.
3. **[Tamales Pisques](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/tamales-pisques)**: corn-and-bean tamales without meat, wrapped in banana leaves. The Pipil-Mayan inheritance.
4. **[Gallo en Chicha](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/gallo-en-chicha)**: rooster (or chicken) slow-cooked in chicha (corn-and-pineapple fermented drink), brown sugar and warm spices. The Christmas Eve plate.
5. **[Yuca con Chicharrón](/recipes/cuisine/salvadoran/yuca-con-chicharron)**: boiled or fried cassava with crispy pork and curtido. The street-food side.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with horchata (rice-and-morro-seed drink — the Salvadoran horchata is different from the Mexican; it uses morro seeds, sesame, peanut and pumpkin seeds for a richer creamier texture) or with a Pilsener (the national lager). End with empanadas de plátano (fried sweet-plantain pies filled with custard or milk).
