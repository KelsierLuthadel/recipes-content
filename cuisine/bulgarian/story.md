# Bulgarian

*The kitchen of the southern Balkans. Cold yoghurt-and-cucumber soup in summer, slow-stewed pork in winter, banitsa pastry for breakfast, and the world's claim on yoghurt itself — Bulgaria's Lactobacillus bulgaricus is the bacterium that turns milk into yoghurt across half the planet.*

## A short history

Bulgarian cooking sits between Greek, Turkish, Serbian and Romanian traditions. The basic shelf — wheat and rye bread, dairy (especially sheep yoghurt), pork, beans, peppers (both fresh and pickled), tomato, and the slow-baked clay-pot cooking — has been the working kitchen for centuries. The Ottoman period (1396–1878) layered on the meze table, yufka pastry (the basis of banitsa), the use of mint, and the kebab tradition.

Yoghurt is the country's culinary identity. The Lactobacillus bulgaricus bacterium — named after Bulgaria when it was discovered by Stamen Grigorov in 1905 — is the strain responsible for Bulgarian-style yoghurt and used worldwide. The yoghurt-and-pickle table is the everyday opener; tarator (cold yoghurt-cucumber soup with dill and walnut) is the summer lunch.

The post-Communist period has been a slow recovery of regional dishes alongside the Sofia restaurant scene. The diaspora kitchens in Spain, Greece, Germany and the US carry the codified tradition.

## Regional variations

- **Sofia and the centre**: codified mainstream. Banitsa, shopska salata, kavarma.
- **Plovdiv and Thrace (south)**: closer to Greek cooking. More olive oil.
- **Black Sea coast (Varna, Burgas)**: more fish. The Black Sea mussel and mackerel tradition.
- **Rhodope mountains (south-west)**: smoked dairy, traditional Pomak (Bulgarian Muslim) dishes.
- **Northwest (Vidin)**: closer to Serbian and Romanian cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Yoghurt (kiselo mlyako)**: thick, tart sheep-or-cow's milk yoghurt.
- **Sirene**: brined white cheese (similar to feta).
- **Kashkaval**: hard yellow cheese.
- **Lyutenitsa**: roasted-pepper-and-tomato-and-eggplant relish.
- **Peppers (fresh, pickled, smoked)**: in nearly every dish.
- **Beans**: in bob chorba (white-bean soup).
- **Mint and dill**: the herb pair.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Banitsa](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/banitsa)**: layered phyllo pastry pie with sirene cheese, eggs and yoghurt. The everyday breakfast and snack.
2. **[Shopska Salata](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/shopska-salata)**: tomato, cucumber, onion, pepper and a grated sirene cheese mountain on top. The signature Bulgarian salad — colours mirror the flag.
3. **[Tarator](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/tarator)**: cold yoghurt-cucumber soup with dill, garlic and walnuts. The summer lunch.
4. **[Kavarma](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/kavarma)**: pork (or chicken) slow-stewed in a clay pot with onion, peppers, mushrooms and white wine.
5. **[Bulgarian Sarmi](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/bulgarian-sarmi)**: cabbage or vine leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-cooked in tomato broth.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with rakia (the universal Balkan grape or plum brandy — typically homemade, frighteningly strong) or with a Bulgarian red wine (Mavrud is the local indigenous grape). End with banitsa with halva, or with a glass of boza (the slightly fizzy fermented wheat drink).
