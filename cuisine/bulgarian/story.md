# Bulgarian

*The kitchen of the southern Balkans. Cold yoghurt-and-cucumber soup in summer, slow-stewed pork in winter, banitsa pastry for breakfast, and the world's claim on yoghurt itself: Bulgaria's Lactobacillus bulgaricus is the bacterium that turns milk into yoghurt across half the planet.*

## A short history

Bulgarian cooking starts on the Thracian plain. The Thracians who held this land from the Bronze Age through the Roman period were wine-makers (Homer praised Thracian wine), bread-bakers, sheep-herders and bee-keepers, and the everyday Bulgarian shelf still carries their legacy: sour milk fermented in clay pots, honey, walnuts, mountain herbs, lamb roasted on the spit for spring feast days. The Romans absorbed Thrace in the 1st century CE and added the olive, the vine on a wider scale, and the formal bread oven. When the South Slavs arrived in the 6th and 7th centuries and the First Bulgarian Empire formed around 681, they laid down the cabbage, the buckwheat, the rye bread and the sauerkraut barrel that still anchor the country kitchen.

The medieval Bulgarian empires (First and Second, running roughly 681 to 1396) sat astride the trade road from Constantinople to the Danube. The Orthodox Christianity adopted under Tsar Boris I in 864 brought the fast-and-feast calendar that shapes the cooking year (Lenten lentil and bean stews, Easter lamb, Christmas pork). Byzantine technique filtered in: stuffed vegetables (sarmi, dolma), the slow lemony soups, and the love of yoghurt as both ingredient and table dressing.

The Ottoman period from 1396 to 1878, nearly five centuries, transformed the kitchen most visibly. The meze table arrived with its small plates and rakija glasses. The yufka pastry, paper-thin sheets of dough, became the universal Bulgarian wrapping, layered into banitsa for breakfast and into countless sweet and savoury bakes. The kebab grill set up in every market town, mint and dill became the essential herbs, and Turkish coffee in tiny copper pots became the social ritual. Many Bulgarian dish names still carry their Ottoman origin (kavarma, kyufte, gyuvech, baklava), though the country's relationship with that period remains complicated. Christianity stayed strong through the centuries, so pork stayed central to the cooking in a way it never did in Bosnia or Albania.

Liberation in 1878 brought a return to country pride in indigenous ingredients. Stamen Grigorov, a young Bulgarian doctor studying in Geneva, isolated Lactobacillus bulgaricus in 1905 and named the strain after his country. It now does the work behind Bulgarian-style yoghurt and most yoghurts worldwide. The yoghurt-and-pickle table is the everyday opener, and tarator (cold yoghurt-cucumber soup with dill and walnut) is the summer lunch. The 20th-century cooperatives codified the breakfast banitsa, the shopska salata (named for the Shopi region around Sofia and given its current form in the 1950s as a tourism-branding exercise), and the export-grade sirene white cheese.

The Communist period (1944 to 1989) industrialised the food chain and pushed the country toward standardised national dishes. Since 1989, regional dishes have slowly come back alongside a livelier Sofia restaurant scene focused on indigenous grapes (Mavrud, Melnik, Rubin), heritage cheeses and old varieties of pepper and tomato. The diaspora kitchens in Spain, Greece, Germany and the US carry the codified tradition forward, and the yoghurt-and-banitsa breakfast still anchors most Bulgarian mornings exactly as it has for a hundred years.

## Regional variations

- **Sofia and the centre**: codified mainstream. Banitsa, shopska salata, kavarma.
- **Plovdiv and Thrace (south)**: closer to Greek cooking. More olive oil.
- **Black Sea coast (Varna, Burgas)**: more fish. The Black Sea mussel and mackerel tradition.
- **Rhodope mountains (south-west)**: smoked dairy, traditional Pomak (Bulgarian Muslim) dishes.
- **Northwest (Vidin)**: closer to Serbian and Romanian cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Yoghurt (kiselo mlyako)**: thick, tart sheep-or-cow's milk yoghurt.
- **Sirene**: brined white cheese (similar to feta).
- **Kashkaval**: hard yellow cheese.
- **Lyutenitsa**: roasted-pepper-and-tomato-and-eggplant relish.
- **Peppers (fresh, pickled, smoked)**: in nearly every dish.
- **Beans**: in bob chorba (white-bean soup).
- **Mint and dill**: the herb pair.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Banitsa](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/banitsa)**: layered phyllo pastry pie with sirene cheese, eggs and yoghurt. The everyday breakfast and snack.
2. **[Shopska Salata](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/shopska-salata)**: tomato, cucumber, onion, pepper and a grated sirene cheese mountain on top. The signature Bulgarian salad; the colours mirror the flag.
3. **[Tarator](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/tarator)**: cold yoghurt-cucumber soup with dill, garlic and walnuts. The summer lunch.
4. **[Kavarma](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/kavarma)**: pork (or chicken) slow-stewed in a clay pot with onion, peppers, mushrooms and white wine.
5. **[Bulgarian Sarmi](/recipes/cuisine/bulgarian/bulgarian-sarmi)**: cabbage or vine leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-cooked in tomato broth.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with rakia (the universal Balkan grape or plum brandy, typically homemade and frighteningly strong) or with a Bulgarian red wine (Mavrud is the local indigenous grape). Finish with banitsa and halva, or with a glass of boza, the slightly fizzy fermented wheat drink.
