# Bulgarian Cuisine

![Bulgarian Cuisine](resources/hero.jpg)

Bulgarian cooking sits where the Slav, Ottoman and Greek worlds press together, with the long-stew rhodope village tradition under it all. The shopska salata (tomato, cucumber, onion, roasted pepper, capped with a snow of grated sirene) is on every table from the seaside to the mountains, the country in a bowl. Yoghurt is the national obsession: thick, sour, set with the native Lactobacillus bulgaricus first identified in Sofia in 1905, drunk salted as ayran, stirred cold into tarator with cucumber and walnut, or eaten plain at breakfast with honey. The Ottoman layer brought banitsa (the coiled filo pastry stuffed with sirene that wakes every household), kebapcheta, sarmi and lokum; the Slav side brought sour cabbage, rye bread, root-vegetable stews and pickling. Paprika and savory (chubritsa, the Bulgarian herb of the house) season the long pork-and-vegetable kavarmas that simmer all afternoon in clay pots in the Rhodope villages. Lyutenitsa (the autumn red-pepper relish) is canned in every kitchen for the winter; rakia is poured before the meal; the salad arrives first; the conversation lasts until the small hours.
