# Zimbabwean

*The kitchen of southern Africa's plateau. Sadza maize porridge is the universal starch (eat it with the right hand, scoop up the stew), peanut butter goes into nearly every sauce, and slow-cooked oxtail or beef is the celebration plate.*

## A short history

Zimbabwean cooking grew out of the southern African farming-and-herding tradition that stretches back two thousand years. Bantu-speaking peoples moving south from central Africa reached the high plateau between the Zambezi and the Limpopo around the early centuries CE, bringing iron-smelting, cattle, sorghum and finger millet. The Shona built Great Zimbabwe between roughly 1100 and 1450 CE, a stone-walled city of fifteen thousand people that traded gold and ivory east to the Swahili coast and on to Arabia, India and China. The kitchen of that era turned on sorghum and millet pounded into porridge, beans simmered with wild greens, beef from the herds, and the wild bounty of the bush: pumpkin and pumpkin leaves, peanuts, sweet potato, mopane caterpillars dried for the lean months.

The Portuguese reached the Zambezi valley in the 16th century looking for the gold of Monomotapa (the Shona successor state), and they brought the Columbian Exchange with them. Maize arrived from the Americas, along with cassava, peanuts, chillies and tomato. The country absorbed maize so completely that within two centuries it had displaced sorghum and millet on the everyday plate; today sadza (stiff white-maize porridge) is the daily plate. Peanuts get ground into dovi, the universal peanut-butter sauce that flavours nearly everything, and chillies became the heat in every relish.

The 19th century reshaped the country. The Ndebele moved north from Zululand in the 1830s under Mzilikazi, fleeing Shaka's wars, and settled the south-western plateau around what became Bulawayo. They brought a more cattle-centred diet, fermented sorghum beer, and a strong tradition of communal feast-roasting that still defines the Matabele table. Cecil Rhodes' British South Africa Company arrived in 1890 and declared the territory Rhodesia, opening it to white settler farming and the gold-and-tobacco economy.

The British colonial period (Southern Rhodesia, 1923 to 1980) layered on tea, beef ranching, the Sunday roast, English-style baking and a commercial dairy industry. Mining compounds and farm labour lines fed workers on rations of maize meal, dried fish (kapenta from Lake Kariba, dammed in 1959) and beans, codifying sadza-and-relish as the urban working plate. The liberation war of the 1970s and independence in 1980 brought political change but kept the everyday food traditional: sadza-and-relish at most meals, eaten with the right hand.

Recent decades of political and economic turbulence (the land reform of the early 2000s, the hyperinflation crisis, the long emigration of Zimbabweans to South Africa, the UK and beyond) haven't shifted the home plate. Sadza, peanut-butter relishes, slow-cooked stews and pumpkin leaves cooked with peanut butter still carry the table. The Zimbabwean diaspora kitchens in Johannesburg, London and Birmingham keep the home cooking alive abroad, and a small wave of Harare chefs is now re-codifying the tradition for restaurant menus, with indigenous millets and the bush ingredients returning to the urban plate.

## Regional variations

- **Harare and Mashonaland**: the mainstream. Sadza with nyama (meat) and muriwo (greens).
- **Bulawayo and Matabeleland**: Ndebele country. More cattle dishes.
- **Manicaland (east)**: more cassava, leaning towards the Mozambican coast.

## Signature ingredients

- **Maize meal**: coarse-ground white maize, the spine of sadza.
- **Peanut butter (dovi)**: in nearly every relish.
- **Pumpkin leaves (muboora)**: cooked with peanut butter as a side.
- **Beef and goat**: the celebration meats.
- **Kapenta**: small dried fish from Lake Kariba. Salty, intense.
- **Mopane worms**: dried caterpillars, high in protein. The traditional bush food.
- **Tomato, onion, dried chillies**: the relish base.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Dovi](/recipes/cuisine/zimbabwe/dovi)**: chicken (or beef) slow-cooked in a peanut-butter-and-tomato sauce. The signature Zimbabwean dish.
2. **[Huku ne Dovi](/recipes/cuisine/zimbabwe/huku-ne-dovi)**: chicken in peanut sauce, the named version of the above, with pumpkin leaves stirred through at the end.
3. **[Nyama Stew](/recipes/cuisine/zimbabwe/nyama-stew)**: beef slow-cooked with tomato, onion and garlic. Serve it over sadza.
4. **[Oxtail Stew](/recipes/cuisine/zimbabwe/oxtail-stew)**: oxtail braised low and slow until the meat falls off the bone. The Sunday family plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Mazoe Orange Crush (the national cordial), or with a cold Zambezi or Bohlinger's lager. Finish with mukute fruit (chocolate berry) if it's in season, or with the simple rice-and-milk pudding (manhanga ne mukaka).
