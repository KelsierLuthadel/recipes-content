# Manhanga

![Manhanga](resources/manhanga.jpg)

*Sweet boiled pumpkin mashed with butter, sugar and a pinch of salt. A side, a snack, or a quick supper with leftover sadza. The pumpkin used in Zimbabwe is dense and floury (closer to kabocha than to the watery jack-o-lantern pumpkin); cook it until a fork goes through cleanly, then mash by hand.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Manhanga is the Zimbabwean sweet pumpkin mash, dense floury pumpkin boiled till tender and crushed with butter, brown sugar and a pinch of salt, eaten as a side alongside sadza and stew or scooped onto a plate as a quick supper with leftover sadza. The pumpkin matters: traditional Zimbabwean pumpkin is dense and floury (closer to kabocha than the watery jack-o-lantern type), so use kabocha, butternut or red kuri if you can't find the African variety. Carving pumpkins make watery mash and aren't worth the effort. Peel and cut a kilo of pumpkin into 4 cm chunks, drop into a wide pot with a teaspoon of salt and just enough water to cover. Bring to the boil and cook 12 to 15 minutes till a fork pierces a chunk easily. Drain in a colander and return to the dry pot, then place over low heat for a minute or two shaking occasionally to steam off the surface water (this is the step most home cooks skip; without it the mash stays watery). Off the heat, add 50 g of butter, two tablespoons of brown sugar (or honey), optional ground cinnamon and a splash of milk if you want a softer texture, then mash with a potato masher to a chunky-smooth consistency. Taste and adjust salt and sugar; the right balance is sweet enough to taste of pumpkin but not so sweet it turns into pudding. The deeply Zimbabwean variation swaps the butter for two tablespoons of smooth peanut butter, which gives a different and arguably better finish. Pile into a serving bowl and eat warm with sadza and stew.

## Ingredients

- 1 kg pumpkin (kabocha, butternut or red-kuri - dense, floury types), peeled and cut into 4 cm chunks
- 1 teaspoon salt
- 50 g unsalted butter
- 2 tablespoons brown sugar (or honey)
- ½ teaspoon ground cinnamon (optional)
- 50 ml whole milk (optional, for a softer mash)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Boil
1. Put the pumpkin chunks in a wide pot; add the salt and just enough water to cover.
1. Bring to a boil; cook 12-15 minutes until a fork pierces easily.

### Stage 2 - Dry
1. Drain in a colander; return to the dry pot.
1. Place over low heat 1-2 minutes, shaking occasionally, to steam off the surface water.

### Stage 3 - Mash
1. Add the butter, sugar, cinnamon and milk (if using).
1. Mash with a potato masher to a chunky-smooth texture (or smooth if preferred).
1. Taste; adjust salt and sugar.

### Stage 4 - Serve
1. Pile into a bowl. Eat warm alongside sadza, stew, or as a quick snack on its own.

## Notes
- **Pumpkin type:** Wet pumpkins (carving / jack-o-lantern) make watery mash. Use kabocha, butternut, red kuri or any dense winter squash.
- **Sweet not sugary:** A spoon of sugar lifts the pumpkin's natural sweetness; more turns it into pudding. Adjust to taste.
- **Peanut butter swap:** Replace butter with 2 tablespoons smooth peanut butter for a different (and deeply Zimbabwean) finish.

## Storage
- Refrigerate 3 days; reheat in a microwave or covered pan.
- Doesn't freeze well - turns watery.
