# Yemeni

*The kitchen of the southern Arabian Peninsula. Hotter, more chilli-forward and more distinct than the broader Gulf shelf. Saltah (the bubbling clay-pot stew with fenugreek foam), mandi (pit-cooked spiced lamb-and-rice), and the world's birthplace of coffee.*

## A short history

Yemeni cooking is among the oldest documented cuisines in Arabia. The Kingdom of Saba (Sheba) — biblical and Quranic — left a 2,500-year culinary tradition centred on Sana'a and the highlands. The cuisine draws on the country's geographic position between the African coast (Ethiopian and Eritrean influence on the spice profile), the Indian Ocean trade routes (turmeric, cardamom, the use of the spice cabinet), and the broader Arabian shelf (lamb, dates, the rice-on-platter tradition).

Coffee was discovered (or at least first cultivated commercially) in Yemen — the port of Mocha gave the world the name. The qishr (coffee husk drink with ginger) and the kahwa tradition are still everyday.

The civil war (since 2014) has limited the cuisine's international visibility. The Yemeni diaspora kitchens in the Gulf, the US (Dearborn, Michigan has a substantial Yemeni community), London and across East Africa preserve the codified tradition.

## Regional variations

- **Sana'a and the highlands**: codified mainstream. Saltah, mandi, helba.
- **Aden and the south coast**: more Indian Ocean trade influence. Fish curries, more chilli.
- **Hadramaut (east)**: closer to Omani cooking. Mandi (the famous spiced lamb-on-rice was perfected here).
- **Tihama (Red Sea coast)**: more Ethiopian/African. The fish-and-spice traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Fenugreek**: ground into helba (frothed fenugreek paste), the topping on saltah.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Slow-cooked, pit-cooked, the celebration centrepiece.
- **Basmati rice**: the rice of choice.
- **Hawayij**: Yemeni spice blend (black pepper, cumin, cloves, cardamom, turmeric).
- **Bisbas**: green or red chilli sauce; on every table.
- **Saltah pot**: traditional clay pot, brought to the table still bubbling.
- **Coffee (qahwa)**: bitter, golden, sometimes with ginger and cardamom.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Saltah](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/saltah)**: bubbling clay-pot stew (lamb, beef or chicken) topped with frothed fenugreek (helba) and served fiery hot with kubaneh (clarified-butter bread). The national dish.
2. **[Mandi](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/mandi)**: lamb cooked in a tannour (clay-oven pit) above a pot of basmati rice — the rendered fat drips into the rice. Served on a communal platter.
3. **[Hanith](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/hanith)**: slow-roasted lamb (similar to mandi but cooked low rather than over the rice). Served with spiced rice and bisbas sauce.
4. **[Fahsa](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/fahsa)**: slow-cooked lamb stew in a clay pot with fenugreek topping. Similar to saltah but meat-focused.
5. **[Zurbian](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/zurbian)**: spiced lamb-and-rice biryani-style dish with potato and tomato.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with qishr (coffee-husk drink with ginger and cardamom) or with strong Yemeni coffee. End with bint al-sahn (honey-flaked bread served with nigella seeds) or with sabaya (a layered bread eaten with honey).
