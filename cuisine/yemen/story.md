# Yemeni

*The kitchen of the southern Arabian Peninsula. Hotter, more chilli-forward and more distinct than the broader Gulf shelf. Saltah (the bubbling clay-pot stew with fenugreek foam), mandi (pit-cooked spiced lamb-and-rice), and the world's birthplace of coffee.*

## A short history

Yemeni cooking is one of the oldest documented cuisines in Arabia. The Sabaean kingdom (Sheba, biblical and Quranic) ran the southern Arabian highlands from roughly the 8th century BCE to the 3rd century CE, and grew wealthy on the overland incense trade in frankincense and myrrh. The terraced highland fields around Sana'a, some of them still farmed today, fed a settled urban civilisation when most of the peninsula was nomadic. The early Yemeni kitchen turned on what the terraces and the wadis offered: sorghum and millet flatbreads baked against the wall of a clay oven (the tannour), lamb and goat slow-cooked in clay pots, the bitter coffee-like drink made from leaves and bark long before the bean arrived.

Islam reached Yemen in the 7th century during the Prophet's lifetime, and the country became one of the earliest Muslim regions, with Sana'a and Zabid emerging as centres of religious scholarship. Medieval Yemeni cooks codified the lamb-and-rice platter, the slow clay-pot stew (precursor to today's saltah and fahsa), and the bread tradition: malooga, kubaneh and the rich clarified-butter doughs that anchor every Yemeni breakfast. The food draws on the country's geographic position between the African coast (Ethiopian and Eritrean traces in the spice profile), the Indian Ocean trade routes (turmeric, cardamom, the whole spice cabinet), and the broader Arabian shelf (lamb, dates, the rice-on-platter tradition).

Yemen gave the world coffee, or at least cultivated it commercially first. Sufi mystics in the highlands started brewing the bean in the 15th century to stay awake through night-long devotions, and by 1500 the port of Mocha on the Red Sea was shipping Yemeni coffee across the Muslim world. The drink reached Cairo, Istanbul, Venice and London through the 16th and 17th centuries, and Mocha's name became the word for coffee itself in dozens of languages. Yemen held a near-monopoly on the global coffee trade until the Dutch smuggled seedlings to Java in the late 1600s. Qishr (coffee-husk drink with ginger) and the kahwa tradition are still everyday.

The Ottoman occupations (16th to 17th centuries, then again 19th to early 20th) layered Turkish technique onto the Yemeni shelf: yoghurt sauces, stuffed vegetables, the heavy use of cumin and black pepper. The southern port of Aden became a British protectorate from 1839 to 1967 and absorbed Indian Ocean influence directly: Gujarati and Hadrami merchants moved between Aden, Bombay and Zanzibar, and the Yemeni curry-and-flatbread shelf of the south carries Indian DNA more openly than the northern highland cooking. The two Yemens (north and south) unified in 1990.

The civil war since 2014 has limited the cuisine's international visibility, but the kitchen survives in everyday households and abroad. The Yemeni diaspora kitchens in the Gulf, the US (Dearborn, Michigan has a substantial Yemeni community), London and across East Africa preserve the codified tradition. Yemeni coffee shops have opened across the West in the 2020s and are exporting the qishr and the kahwa back outward, the first global moment for Yemeni hospitality since the Mocha trade closed.

## Regional variations

- **Sana'a and the highlands**: the codified mainstream. Saltah, mandi, helba.
- **Aden and the south coast**: more Indian Ocean trade influence. Fish curries, more chilli.
- **Hadramaut (east)**: closer to Omani cooking. Cooks here perfected mandi.
- **Tihama (Red Sea coast)**: more Ethiopian and African. The fish-and-spice traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Fenugreek**: ground into helba (frothed fenugreek paste), the topping on saltah.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Slow-cooked, pit-cooked, the celebration centrepiece.
- **Basmati rice**: the rice of choice.
- **Hawayij**: Yemeni spice blend (black pepper, cumin, cloves, cardamom, turmeric).
- **Bisbas**: green or red chilli sauce; on every table.
- **Saltah pot**: traditional clay pot, brought to the table still bubbling.
- **Coffee (qahwa)**: bitter, golden, sometimes with ginger and cardamom.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Saltah](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/saltah)**: bubbling clay-pot stew (lamb, beef or chicken) topped with frothed fenugreek (helba) and served fiery hot with kubaneh (clarified-butter bread). The national dish.
2. **[Mandi](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/mandi)**: lamb cooked in a tannour (clay-oven pit) above a pot of basmati rice; the rendered fat drips into the rice. Served on a communal platter.
3. **[Hanith](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/hanith)**: slow-roasted lamb (similar to mandi but cooked low rather than over the rice). Served with spiced rice and bisbas sauce.
4. **[Fahsa](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/fahsa)**: slow-cooked lamb stew in a clay pot with fenugreek topping. Cousin to saltah, but meat-focused.
5. **[Zurbian](/recipes/cuisine/yemen/zurbian)**: spiced lamb-and-rice biryani-style dish with potato and tomato.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with qishr (coffee-husk drink with ginger and cardamom) or with strong Yemeni coffee. End with bint al-sahn (honey-flaked bread served with nigella seeds) or with sabaya (a layered bread eaten with honey).
