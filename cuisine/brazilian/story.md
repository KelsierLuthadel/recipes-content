# Brazilian

*The cuisine of the world's largest tropical country. Built on cassava, beans, rice, beef, dendê (red palm oil), and the African retention that made Bahia one of the best food regions on earth.*

## A short history

Brazilian cooking rests on three foundations, layered in over five centuries. The first foundation is indigenous. Before the Portuguese landed in 1500, the Tupi-Guarani peoples who lived along the Atlantic coast and the Amazonian groups inland had already domesticated cassava (mandioca), the bitter root they learned to grate, press the prussic-acid juice from, and toast into farinha. They grew corn in dozens of colours, cultivated peanuts, harvested açaí, cupuaçu and dozens of Amazonian fruits, and cooked in clay pots banked on coals. The base sauces of modern Brazilian food (the tucupi broth, the moqueca's banana-leaf wrapping, the farofa scattered over every plate) all come from this pre-contact kitchen.

The second foundation arrived with the Portuguese from 1500 onwards. They brought rice, wheat, beef cattle, pork, citrus, sugar cane (which would change everything), the Christian feast calendar, the salt-cod tradition from the North Atlantic, and the convent kitchen's love of egg yolks and sugar that became the Brazilian dessert shelf. The sugar plantations of the north-east, run on enslaved African labour from the 1530s, made Portugal rich and locked in the third and deepest foundation: the West African cooks who shaped what Brazilian food actually tastes like.

That African current ran deepest in Bahia. Salvador, on the north-east coast, was the slave-trade capital of the New World for two centuries, and the cooking there is essentially West African cuisine using Brazilian ingredients. Dendê (red palm oil from West African oil palms naturalised on Brazilian soil), okra, malagueta chillies, the slow-cooked one-pots, the deep-fried bean fritter (acarajé, identical in technique to West African akara), and the dual sacred-and-everyday status of food all came across with the enslaved peoples. Feijoada, the national black-bean-and-pork stew, grew up in the kitchens of the big houses and the slave quarters together, and the moqueca fish stew is essentially a Brazilianised version of a West African coastal one-pot.

The 19th century closed the slave trade (1850) and abolished slavery (1888), and the 19th and 20th centuries layered on a fourth wave of influence: Italian, German, Japanese, Lebanese, Polish and Ukrainian immigrants poured into São Paulo and the south, bringing pizza, schnitzel, sushi, kibbeh and pierogi to the Brazilian table. São Paulo now has the largest Japanese diaspora outside Japan and the largest Lebanese diaspora outside Lebanon, and both kitchens read as fully Brazilian now. The result is a cuisine that shifts sharply by region: Amazonian, Bahian, Mineiro (Minas Gerais), Paulista (São Paulo), Gaúcho (south).

The late 20th and 21st centuries brought international recognition. Alex Atala at D.O.M. in São Paulo pushed Amazonian ingredients onto the world stage in the 2000s; the gastronomic mapping of Brazil's regional cuisines is still being completed. But the everyday kitchen still runs on the three-layer foundation history laid down: cassava and corn from the indigenous shelf, rice and beans from the Portuguese, dendê and slow-cook from the African.

## Regional variations

- **Bahia (north-east)**: the most distinctive shelf. Moqueca, vatapá, acarajé (deep-fried bean fritters). Heavy dendê palm oil use.
- **Minas Gerais (south-east, inland)**: the most traditional. Pork, beans, cassava, cachaça. Tutu de feijão, feijão tropeiro.
- **Rio de Janeiro and the coast**: feijoada (the Saturday lunch), the beach-food shelf (pastéis, pão de queijo).
- **São Paulo**: the cosmopolitan kitchen. Italian-influenced pizzeria scene plus everything else.
- **South (Rio Grande do Sul)**: gaucho cooking. Churrasco (open-fire barbecue), chimarrão (yerba mate).
- **Amazon**: cassava and river fish. Tucupi, jambu, tacacá soup.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cassava (mandioca)**: as flour (farinha de mandioca, scattered on everything), as fritters, boiled.
- **Black beans**: the feijoada base.
- **Dendê (red palm oil)**: in Bahian cooking; gives moqueca its colour and richness.
- **Coconut milk**: in moqueca, vatapá and most northern coastal dishes.
- **Lime**: the universal acid.
- **Cachaça**: sugar-cane spirit. The base of caipirinha, also a cooking spirit.
- **Açaí**: Amazonian berry, served frozen as a bowl with granola and banana.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Feijoada](/recipes/cuisine/brazilian/feijoada)**: black beans slow-cooked with multiple cuts of pork (shoulder, ribs, ear, tail, smoked sausage). Served with rice, farofa (toasted cassava), kale and orange slices. The national dish.
2. **[Moqueca](/recipes/cuisine/brazilian/moqueca)**: fish stew with coconut milk, dendê oil, tomato, onion and coriander. The Bahian signature.
3. **[Bobó de Camarão](/recipes/cuisine/brazilian/bobo-de-camarao)**: prawns in cassava-and-coconut puree with dendê. The other Bahian classic.
4. **[Picanha](/recipes/cuisine/brazilian/picanha)**: top sirloin cap, grilled over open fire on a sword skewer. The gaucho churrasco signature.
5. **[Vatapá](/recipes/cuisine/brazilian/vatapa)**: bread-thickened seafood-and-coconut stew with peanuts and dendê. The Bahian wedding plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with caipirinha (cachaça, lime, sugar, ice; the national cocktail) or a cold Brahma beer. For a proper Saturday: feijoada at lunch, naps in the afternoon, and a long churrasco evening with cold beer and salt.
