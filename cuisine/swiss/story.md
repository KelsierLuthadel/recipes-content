# Swiss

*The kitchen of the alps. Cheese in everything (fondue, raclette, gruyère, emmental), cured meat from the high pastures, rösti as the universal potato, and the world's most-loved chocolate.*

## A short history

Swiss cooking is the synthesis of four linguistic-cultural regions — German-Swiss (Zürich, Bern), French-Swiss (Geneva, Lausanne), Italian-Swiss (Ticino), Romansh-Swiss (Graubünden) — each with its own borrowed culinary traditions but with shared alpine roots: cheese, dried meat, rye bread, butter, cream, the long winter cooking.

The cheese tradition is the deepest: Gruyère (PDO, made since the 12th century in the Fribourg region), Emmental (the holed cheese), Raclette (named after racler — to scrape), Appenzeller, Tête de Moine. Fondue (cheese melted with white wine, garlic, kirsch) and raclette (cheese melted on a hot stone or grill, scraped onto potatoes) are the two great cheese rituals.

The chocolate tradition began with Daniel Peter (Vevey, 1875) inventing milk chocolate by adding condensed milk to chocolate. Rodolphe Lindt invented the conching process (1879) that made chocolate smooth. Today Switzerland eats more chocolate per capita than any country (10kg+ per person per year).

## Regional variations

- **German-Swiss (Zürich, Bern, central)**: Zürcher Geschnetzeltes (veal in cream sauce), rösti as the universal side, the dairy tradition.
- **French-Swiss (Romandie, Geneva)**: fondue and raclette belt. Closer to French cooking.
- **Italian-Swiss (Ticino)**: polenta and risotto. Closer to Italian cooking with Swiss cheese.
- **Romansh-Swiss (Graubünden)**: Bündnerfleisch (air-dried beef), capuns (cabbage-wrapped pasta-and-meat).

## Signature ingredients

- **Cheese**: gruyère, emmental, raclette, appenzeller. The defining product.
- **Butter and cream**: heavy use. Swiss dairy is among the world's most-prized.
- **Potatoes**: in rösti, in raclette-side, in capuns.
- **Cured meat**: Bündnerfleisch, jambon de la Borne, the smoked-and-air-dried tradition.
- **Kirsch**: cherry brandy. In fondue, in desserts.
- **Chocolate**: in dessert, in baking, eaten plain.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Fondue](/recipes/cuisine/swiss/fondue)**: gruyère and vacherin (or emmental) melted with white wine, garlic and kirsch, served with bread cubes on long forks. The French-Swiss winter ritual.
2. **[Raclette](/recipes/cuisine/swiss/raclette)**: half-wheel of raclette cheese melted on a special grill, scraped onto boiled potatoes with pickles and dried meat.
3. **[Rösti](/recipes/cuisine/swiss/rosti)**: shredded potato pan-fried in butter into a golden cake. The universal Swiss side, eaten with anything.
4. **[Älplermagronen](/recipes/cuisine/swiss/aelplermagronen)**: alpine mac-and-cheese with potato, onion, gruyère and cream, topped with apple sauce. The most-loved everyday plate.
5. **[Zürcher Geschnetzeltes](/recipes/cuisine/swiss/zuericher-geschnetzeltes)**: veal strips in a white wine and cream sauce with mushrooms. The Zürich Sunday lunch, traditionally served with rösti.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with chasselas (Switzerland's signature dry white) or with a kirsch (cherry brandy) after dinner. End with a square of dark Swiss chocolate. For a proper alpine evening: fondue or raclette is the centre, the conversation should be slow, and the wine should be cold.
