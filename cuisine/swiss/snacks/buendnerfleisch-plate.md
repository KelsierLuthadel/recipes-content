# Bündnerfleisch Plate

![Bündnerfleisch Plate](resources/buendnerfleisch-plate.jpg)

*The Alpine aperitif plate: thinly sliced air-dried Bündnerfleisch (cured beef from Graubünden), Alpine cheese, pickles, dark bread. A 10-minute assembly that defines the Swiss apéro.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes

**Cook Time:** None

## Overview
Bündnerfleisch is the air-dried, salt-cured beef of Graubünden - lean topside or silverside is pressed into a brick shape, dry-cured with salt, pepper, juniper, garlic and bay, then aged in cold dry mountain air for months. Sliced wafer-thin against the grain, it has a deep red-brown colour and a savoury, faintly herbal flavour reminiscent of Italian bresaola but drier and more concentrated. In Switzerland it's never cooked - it's served raw and thinly sliced as the centrepiece of an apéro, the late-afternoon snack-plate ritual that bridges the gap between work and dinner. This is the assembly recipe: how to arrange the plate, what to put with it, how much per person.

## Ingredients

### The cured meats
- 120 g Bündnerfleisch (or substitute: bresaola, jambon cru, prosciutto)
- 100 g salami or saucisson sec, thinly sliced

### Cheese
- 150 g Gruyère, cut into thin shards or small wedges
- 100 g Sbrinz or aged Gruyère (or Parmesan), broken into chunks

### Pickles and condiments
- 80 g cornichons
- 60 g silverskin pickled onions
- 2 tbsp wholegrain mustard
- 50 g salted butter (in curls or a small pat)

### Breads
- Half a baguette or Swiss farmhouse loaf, sliced
- A small bowl of rye bread, sliced
- A few small breadsticks (grissini)

### Garnish
- A small handful of walnuts in shell (with a nutcracker)
- 6-8 dried apricots or figs
- A few sprigs of fresh thyme

## Method

### Stage 1 - Slice the meat
1. Bündnerfleisch should be sliced as thinly as you can manage with a sharp knife - the slices should be almost translucent.
2. Lay the slices flat on a board, slightly overlapping in a fan.
3. If using a commercial pre-sliced pack, separate the slices gently with a knife tip - they tend to stick.

### Stage 2 - Prep the cheese
1. Cut the Gruyère into long thin shards using a cheese plane (or shave with a vegetable peeler for the same effect).
2. Break the Sbrinz with a small knife - traditionally not cut clean, but broken into rough chunks along its natural fault lines.

### Stage 3 - Assemble the board
1. Use a wooden board, slate, or large platter.
2. Lay the meat slices in a loose fan on one half - Bündnerfleisch on the front, salami behind.
3. Pile the cheese in two small heaps on the other half.
4. Tuck the cornichons and onions into small bowls or directly on the board.
5. Place the mustard in a small bowl with a small spoon.
6. Add the butter (curled, scooped, or in a small ramekin).
7. Scatter the walnuts, dried fruit, and thyme sprigs around the board for colour and crunch.
8. Bread goes in a separate basket or on a board alongside.

### Stage 4 - Final touch
1. Cracks of black pepper on the cheese.
2. A tiny drizzle of good olive oil on the Bündnerfleisch if you like.

## Notes
- **Bündnerfleisch substitutes:** Bresaola is the closest - both are air-dried beef in the Alpine tradition. Prosciutto, coppa, and jambon cru work too but lose the dryness that distinguishes the Grisons style.
- **Cut against the grain:** Bündnerfleisch is sliced perpendicular to the muscle fibres so it's tender, not chewy. If you're slicing a whole block, look at the fibre direction and slice across it.
- **Wafer-thin only:** Thick slices of Bündnerfleisch are unpleasant and chewy. Use a sharp knife, light pressure, long strokes. If your knife isn't up to it, buy pre-sliced.

## Serving
- Serve as an apéro before dinner with a glass of dry Swiss white wine (Chasselas, Müller-Thurgau) or a Pinot Noir from Graubünden. The Swiss tradition is to assemble this around 6pm and let people pick at it for 30-40 minutes before sitting down to dinner.

## Storage
- Best assembled fresh; cured meats dry out on the board.
- Leftover meats and cheese refrigerate 3 days wrapped in baking paper (not cling film - they need to breathe).
- The pickles keep weeks in their brine.
