# Cajun

*The rural Acadian-French settler cuisine of south Louisiana bayou country, anchored on the holy trinity of onion, celery and bell pepper, a dark roux cooked patiently to the colour of pecan, and slow one-pots built over hours.*

## A short history

Cajun cooking is the food of the Acadians, and the story has to start in Nova Scotia. From 1604 onwards, French settlers from Brittany, Normandy and Poitou farmed the salt marshes of Acadia (modern Nova Scotia and New Brunswick) for a century and a half, developing a self-sufficient farming culture: dairy, root vegetables, wheat, salt cod, and a French country kitchen adapted to a cold North Atlantic climate. They built dykes to reclaim marshland, kept careful pigs and chickens, baked bread in clay ovens, and developed a habit of slow one-pot suppers that would later cross thousands of miles south with them.

The British expelled the Acadians from Nova Scotia in 1755 in *Le Grand Dérangement*, scattering roughly 11,000 people across the Atlantic seaboard, the Caribbean and back to France. Many drifted south through the colonies over the next two decades, and from the 1760s onward they began settling the bayou country of south Louisiana, then a remote French (and briefly Spanish) territory. They arrived poor, displaced, and far from the French market gardens of their grandparents, so they rebuilt a cuisine from whatever the swamp, the prairie and the Gulf gave them.

What they found shaped everything. Prairie rice from north of the basin replaced the wheat of Normandy. Crawfish from the bayous, alligator, catfish and shrimp from the Gulf, and pork raised on the high ground became the protein backbone. The Choctaw and other indigenous peoples already in the region taught them to thicken with filé, the ground young leaf of the sassafras tree, when okra was out of season, and showed them how to read the swamp's calendar of crawfish, frog and turtle. Black cooks, both free and enslaved, brought the West African spice instinct, the smoking traditions for sausage and ham, the okra and rice combinations, and the long-simmered one-pot logic that became gumbo. Spanish colonial rule from 1763 to 1800 added the bay leaf and the saffron-coloured rice traditions that influenced jambalaya.

By the early 19th century the cuisine had settled into a recognisable shape. Cajun families farmed rice, raised hogs, trapped fur in winter, and ate from a daily one-pot on the stove. The Louisiana Purchase of 1803 brought the territory into the United States, but the Acadiana region stayed French-speaking, Catholic and rural, largely separate from the city culture of New Orleans two hours east. Through the 19th and into the early 20th century the cooking changed slowly: roux got darker, the trinity (onion, celery, green bell pepper, the swamp answer to the French mirepoix with carrot swapped for pepper) became the universal base, the boudin sausage tradition flourished, and the Saturday-night fais do-do dance ran alongside long-cook gumbos and crawfish boils.

The 20th century brought oil-and-gas money, paved roads, and pressure on the French language. By the 1950s schoolchildren in Acadiana were punished for speaking French and the culture entered a long defensive period. The food never went away, but it became invisible to the rest of America until Paul Prudhomme opened K-Paul's Louisiana Kitchen in New Orleans in 1979 and put Cajun cooking on the national map. His blackened redfish technique (heavy spice crust, cast-iron pan smoking hot, fast sear) became the dish of the 1980s. The revival pulled in a younger generation of Cajun cooks who started recovering the old recipes and writing them down, and the French-language schools in Lafayette parish slowly came back.

The result is a cuisine of rural French technique married to swamp ingredients and West African heat. One pot on the stove for hours. A dark roux as the foundation flavour. The holy trinity instead of the French mirepoix. Time treated as an ingredient rather than a tax. People often confuse Cajun with Creole, the cuisine of the French Quarter in New Orleans, but the two are distinct. Creole is city food: more European refinement, tomato-rich, butter and cream, old-money kitchens with trained chefs. Cajun is country food: roux-rich, pork-fat-rich, built for big families and Sunday tables.

## Regional variations

Cajun is itself layered. The bayou country covers a wide stretch and the cooking changes parish to parish.

- **Acadiana and Lafayette parish**: the heartland of the cuisine. Crawfish étouffée in spring, boudin links sold from gas-station counters, the densest concentration of Cajun home cooks anywhere.
- **Bayou country (Atchafalaya basin)**: gumbo cooking at its peak, jambalaya in big black pots, fish-camp cooking built around what came up in the morning's traps and trotlines.
- **Cajun prairie (north of the basin, around Eunice and Ville Platte)**: rice-and-gravy plates, smoked pork shoulders, drier prairie-style cooking with less seafood, more sausage and cured meats.

The line between Cajun and Creole is the most important pairing to grasp. Creole cooks sit in the French Quarter and Garden District of New Orleans, lean on tomatoes, butter, cream and European technique, and turn out refined city cooking. Cajun cooks sit two hours west in the bayou and prairie country, lean on dark roux, pork fat and one-pot logic, and turn out rural French cooking gone Louisiana. Both are great. They are not the same.

## Signature ingredients

- **The holy trinity** (onion, celery, green bell pepper): the aromatic base under almost every Cajun dish, the swamp answer to the French mirepoix.
- **Dark roux**: flour and oil or lard cooked patiently to the colour of pecan or milk chocolate, then darker still for gumbo. The single most important Cajun technique.
- **Andouille sausage**: smoked pork sausage seasoned with garlic and pepper, sliced thick into gumbo and jambalaya.
- **Tasso ham**: heavily spiced, smoked pork shoulder used as a seasoning meat, almost like a Louisiana version of pancetta.
- **Filé powder**: ground sassafras leaf, added off the heat at the end of cooking, a Choctaw contribution. Used when okra is out of season to thicken gumbo.
- **Crawfish**: the heartland shellfish, in étouffée, boils, pies and bisques.
- **Long-grain rice**: the starch under everything; the prairie above the basin grows it.
- **[Cajun Spice Mix](../../base-ingredients/spices/cajun-spice-mix.md)**: the house blend of paprika, cayenne, black pepper, garlic, onion and oregano that seasons everything from blackened fish to dirty rice.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Gumbo](gumbo.md). The mother dish. A dark roux, the holy trinity, stock, and whatever protein the cook has on hand, simmered for hours and ladled over rice. Teaches the roux-and-trinity foundation that underpins almost every other Cajun dish.
2. [Chicken and sausage gumbo](chicken-and-sausage-gumbo.md). The country variant. Smoked andouille and dark-meat chicken in a deep roux, no seafood. Teaches the smoke-and-fat backbone that prairie Cajun cooking leans on.
3. [Jambalaya](jambalaya.md). Louisiana's rice-pot one-dish. Trinity, andouille, chicken or shrimp, rice cooked in the same pot to absorb the seasoning. Teaches the one-pot rice technique that distinguishes Cajun jambalaya (browner, no tomato) from its Creole cousin.
4. [Crawfish étouffée](crawfish-etouffee.md). A blond-to-medium roux, trinity, stock, crawfish tails, served over rice. Teaches the lighter-roux side of the cuisine and the trick of finishing shellfish gently so the tails stay tender.
5. [Blackened chicken](blackened-chicken.md). The Paul Prudhomme technique: heavy spice crust, cast-iron pan smoking hot, fast sear. Teaches the dry-rub-and-char move that brought Cajun cooking to a national audience in the 1980s.
6. [Dirty rice](dirty-rice.md). Long-grain rice cooked with chicken livers, ground pork, trinity and Cajun spice. The "dirty" is the colour from the livers and meat. Teaches the rice-as-vehicle principle and how organ meat is woven into everyday cooking.
7. [Red beans and rice](red-beans-and-rice.md). The traditional Monday dish, born of using up the Sunday-ham bone. Beans simmered with andouille, trinity and spice until creamy, served over rice. Teaches the slow-stew one-pot and the bone-stock economy that runs through the whole cuisine.

After those seven you have the dark roux, the holy trinity, the one-pot rice logic, the lighter étouffée style, the blackened-pan technique, and the bean-stew Monday dish. That is the working spine of the cuisine.
