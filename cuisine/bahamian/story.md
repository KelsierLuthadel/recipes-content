# Bahamian

*The kitchen of 700 Caribbean islands. Conch in every form (the national protein — chowder, fritters, ceviche, "cracked" pounded-and-fried), guava duff for dessert, stewed pigeon peas with rice, and the British colonial-Atlantic seafood synthesis.*

## A short history

Bahamian cooking is the cuisine of an archipelago of 700 small islands strung between Florida and Cuba. The Lucayan (indigenous Taíno) peoples were eradicated by Spanish slave-raiders within decades of Columbus's arrival; the islands were repopulated by British loyalists fleeing the American Revolution (and their slaves), and later by liberated Africans freed from slave ships by the British Navy.

The cuisine reflects this — British colonial cooking (Sunday roast, tea, sweet bread) layered on African slave-cuisine roots (the stewed-beans-and-rice tradition, the use of saltfish) on Atlantic seafood abundance (conch — the marine snail that's the national protein; grouper; spiny lobster; spiny crab). The Loyalist heritage left a quietly British formal-Sunday-lunch tradition that persists.

The defining dish is conch — eaten cracked (pounded thin and fried like schnitzel), in salad (ceviche-style with lime, onion, pepper), in chowder, in fritters. Sold from beachside stalls, eaten with hot sauce and a cold Kalik beer. The Bahamian conch fishery is the largest in the Caribbean.

## Regional variations

- **Nassau and New Providence**: codified mainstream. The cosmopolitan capital.
- **Out Islands (Eleuthera, Exuma, Andros)**: more traditional, less touristed.
- **Abaco and the northern islands**: stronger British-Loyalist heritage.
- **Long Island and Inagua (south)**: more seafood, distinct salt-pond traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Conch**: the national protein. Eaten in every form.
- **Pigeon peas**: in peas-and-rice (the universal side).
- **Coconut milk**: in some rice dishes.
- **Lime**: with conch ceviche, on everything.
- **Goat pepper**: small fierce variety of Scotch bonnet, endemic Bahamian.
- **Guava and coconut**: in desserts.
- **Salt cod**: in souse-and-bake, in fritters.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cracked Conch](/recipes/cuisine/bahamian/cracked-conch)**: conch pounded thin, dredged, deep-fried like schnitzel. Served with peas-and-rice and a wedge of lime.
2. **[Conch Fritters](/recipes/cuisine/bahamian/conch-fritters)**: minced conch in spiced batter, deep-fried into golden balls, served with hot sauce dip.
3. **[Stew Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/bahamian/stew-chicken)**: chicken slow-braised in brown sauce with onion, bell pepper, garlic and tomato. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Bahamian Souse](/recipes/cuisine/bahamian/bahamian-souse)**: pickled-pepper-and-lime broth with chicken, pig's feet or sheep's tongue. The Sunday-morning soup.
5. **[Fire-Roasted Jerk Shrimp](/recipes/cuisine/bahamian/fire-roasted-jerk-shrimp)**: shrimp in jerk seasoning (the Jamaican-inspired adaptation now Bahamian), grilled over coals.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Kalik (the national lager) or with Goombay punch (the local fruit drink) or with rum from Pirate's Republic distillery. End with guava duff (rolled steamed pudding with guava filling and butter-rum sauce) — the Bahamian dessert tradition's centrepiece.
