# Czech

*The kitchen of beer and dumplings. Pork roasted with caraway, knedlík (the bread-loaf dumpling sliced into rounds), thick sour-cream sauces, and the world's most-loved Pilsner.*

## A short history

Czech cooking sits at the heart of Central Europe, on the high Bohemian basin ringed by forested hills. The Slavic tribes who settled the land in the 6th and 7th centuries grew rye, oats, millet and barley on the cleared forest soils, kept pigs and dairy cattle in the woods, foraged mushrooms (a Czech national obsession to this day, with autumn weekends spent in the woods with a basket and a knife), gathered wild berries and honey, and brewed grain into the earliest local beer. The Great Moravian Empire of the 9th century already had a settled farming kitchen built on pork, grain porridge, cabbage, and the cured-and-smoked meat traditions that survive in every Czech butcher's shop today.

The medieval Kingdom of Bohemia, from the 10th century under the Přemyslid dynasty, codified the Christian feast calendar (Christmas carp, Easter mazanec, the Lenten fasting weeks). The 1212 Golden Bull of Sicily formalised Bohemia as a kingdom, and Prague under Charles IV (Holy Roman Emperor from 1355) became one of the great cities of Europe, with a sophisticated court cuisine drawing on Italian, French and Hungarian influences. The medieval centuries also gave Czech cooking its sweet-savoury instinct (fruit with meat, plums with pork, cranberry with venison) that still appears in svíčková with its lemon slice and cranberry sauce. The 14th and 15th centuries saw the rise of the great Bohemian brewing tradition (the Pilsen and Budweis breweries trace back to royal charters from this era), and dumplings (knedlíky) probably arrived from the Germanic neighbours in this period.

The Habsburg conquest in 1526 began nearly four centuries of Austrian rule. The Czech lands became the breadbasket and brewery of the Habsburg empire, and the long Austro-Hungarian period (1526 to 1918) codified the bourgeois middle-class dinner on top of the older peasant kitchen: schnitzel (smažený řízek, the breaded fried veal or pork cutlet that crossed every border in the empire), goulash (gulaš, borrowed from Hungarian neighbours), the Bohemian roast pork with caraway and sauerkraut, and the apple strudel that became a national obsession. The Columbian Exchange arrived in the kitchen through the 16th and 17th centuries: the potato finally caught on in the 18th century under Maria Theresa's encouragement, and bramborové knedlíky (potato dumplings) joined the bread-based knedlíky on the table. Tomatoes and peppers came in via Hungarian and Balkan trade; paprika seasons the Czech goulash to this day.

The 19th century was the century of beer and national revival. Josef Groll's pale-lager invention at the new burgher brewery in Plzeň in 1842 created Pilsner Urquell, the world's most-imitated beer style and the template for nearly every modern lager. Beer became central to Czech identity in the same century as the language and the literature got their revival under Mácha, Erben and the playwrights of the Provisional Theatre. The Czech Republic still drinks more beer per capita than any country on earth (159 litres per person per year, about three times the global average), and the beer-and-knedlík pairing is the everyday lunch across Bohemia. Industrialisation brought workers into Prague, Brno and the Ostrava coal basin, and the dumpling-and-pork lunch fed the factory shift.

The 20th century battered the cuisine. Two world wars, the Nazi occupation, the 1948 Communist takeover and forty years of state socialism flattened the restaurant scene and pushed cooking back into homes and the pub kitchen. The 1968 Prague Spring and its Soviet suppression closed the country tighter. The Velvet Revolution of 1989 reopened everything. The post-1989 recovery has put modern Czech food back in focus. Traditional pubs (hospodas) sit alongside contemporary Prague restaurants drawing on the South Bohemian larder: carp from the Třeboň fishponds (a tradition that goes back to medieval monastery aquaculture), Moravian wine country in the south-east, dumpling traditions across the regions, and the slow revival of the spa-town pastry tradition around Karlovy Vary.

## Regional variations

- **Bohemia (Prague, west)**: the codified mainstream. Knedlíky, vepřo-knedlo-zelo, the dumpling tradition.
- **Moravia (east)**: closer to Slovakian cooking. More slivovice (plum brandy), wine instead of beer, more potato dumplings.
- **South Bohemia**: carp country. The fish-pond aquaculture tradition; carp at Christmas.
- **Karlovy Vary (spa region)**: Karlovarské knedlíky (spa dumplings), more refined dining.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pork**: every part. The roast (vepřová pečeně), the schnitzel (smažený řízek), the smoked (uzené).
- **Caraway seeds**: with pork, with potatoes, in bread.
- **Bread dumplings (houskové knedlíky)**: white bread cubes folded into dough, boiled as a loaf, sliced.
- **Potato dumplings (bramborové knedlíky)**: the Moravian version.
- **Sauerkraut**: with pork roast.
- **Sour cream**: in svíčková sauce.
- **Beer**: cooking ingredient (in stews) and table drink.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Vepřo-Knedlo-Zelo](/recipes/cuisine/czech/vepro-knedlo-zelo)**: roast pork with knedlíky (bread dumplings) and sauerkraut. The national dish, the Czech equivalent of the British Sunday roast.
2. **[Svíčková](/recipes/cuisine/czech/svickova)**: beef sirloin slow-braised in a creamy root-vegetable sauce, served with knedlíky, cranberry sauce and a slice of lemon. The Sunday celebration plate.
3. **[Czech Goulash](/recipes/cuisine/czech/czech-goulash)**: thick beef-and-onion stew (more onion than the Hungarian version, less paprika) served over knedlíky.
4. **[Smažený Sýr](/recipes/cuisine/czech/smazeny-syr)**: breaded fried Edam cheese with tartar sauce and chips. The student-bar staple.
5. **[Párek v Rohlíku](/recipes/cuisine/czech/parek-v-rohliku)**: hot dog in a roll with mustard and ketchup; sometimes ham-and-cheese stuffed. The Prague street-food classic.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a Pilsner Urquell (the original Pilsner, brewed since 1842) or a Budvar (Budweiser, the original; the trademark dispute with the American beer of the same name is famously unresolved). End with palačinky (Czech crepes filled with quark or jam) or kolache (filled sweet pastry).
