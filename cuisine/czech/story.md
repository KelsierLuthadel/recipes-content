# Czech

*The kitchen of beer and dumplings. Pork roasted with caraway, knedlík (the bread-loaf dumpling sliced into rounds), thick sour-cream sauces, and the world's most-loved Pilsner.*

## A short history

Czech cooking sits at the heart of Central Europe, shaped by the Bohemian-Moravian agricultural traditions (pork, wheat and rye, cabbage, root vegetables, the dairy-and-mushroom forest tradition) and the long Austro-Hungarian period (1526–1918) that codified the bourgeois middle-class dinner: schnitzel, goulash, knedlíky, the Bohemian roast pork.

Beer is essentially a national identity. The Pilsner Urquell from Plzeň (1842) created the world's most-imitated beer style; the Czech Republic consumes more beer per capita than any country in the world (159 litres per person per year — about three times the global average). The beer-and-knedlík pairing is the everyday lunch across Bohemia.

The post-1989 recovery has brought modern Czech food into focus: traditional pubs (hospodas) sit alongside contemporary Prague restaurants drawing on the South Bohemian larder (carp from the Třeboň fishponds, Moravian wine country, dumpling traditions across the regions).

## Regional variations

- **Bohemia (Prague, west)**: the codified mainstream. Knedlíky, vepřo-knedlo-zelo, the dumpling tradition.
- **Moravia (east)**: closer to Slovakian cooking. More slivovice (plum brandy), wine instead of beer, more potato dumplings.
- **South Bohemia**: carp country. The fish-pond aquaculture tradition; carp at Christmas.
- **Karlovy Vary (spa region)**: Karlovarské knedlíky (spa dumplings), more refined dining.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pork**: every part. The roast (vepřová pečeně), the schnitzel (smažený řízek), the smoked (uzené).
- **Caraway seeds**: with pork, with potatoes, in bread.
- **Bread dumplings (houskové knedlíky)**: white bread cubes folded into dough, boiled as a loaf, sliced.
- **Potato dumplings (bramborové knedlíky)**: the Moravian version.
- **Sauerkraut**: with pork roast.
- **Sour cream**: in svíčková sauce.
- **Beer**: cooking ingredient (in stews) and table drink.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Vepřo-Knedlo-Zelo](/recipes/cuisine/czech/vepro-knedlo-zelo)**: roast pork with knedlíky (bread dumplings) and sauerkraut. The national dish — the Czech equivalent of the British Sunday roast.
2. **[Svíčková](/recipes/cuisine/czech/svickova)**: beef sirloin slow-braised in a creamy root-vegetable sauce, served with knedlíky, cranberry sauce and a slice of lemon. The Sunday celebration plate.
3. **[Czech Goulash](/recipes/cuisine/czech/czech-goulash)**: thick beef-and-onion stew (more onion than the Hungarian version, less paprika) served over knedlíky.
4. **[Smažený Sýr](/recipes/cuisine/czech/smazeny-syr)**: breaded fried Edam cheese with tartar sauce and chips. The student-bar staple.
5. **[Párek v Rohlíku](/recipes/cuisine/czech/parek-v-rohliku)**: hot dog in a roll with mustard and ketchup; sometimes ham-and-cheese stuffed. The Prague street-food classic.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a Pilsner Urquell (the original Pilsner, brewed since 1842) or a Budvar (Budweiser, the original — the trademark dispute with the American beer of the same name is famously unresolved). End with palačinky (Czech crepes filled with quark or jam) or kolache (filled sweet pastry).
