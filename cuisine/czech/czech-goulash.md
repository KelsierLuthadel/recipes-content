# Czech Goulash (Hovězí Guláš)

![Czech Goulash](resources/czech-goulash.jpg)

*Czech beef goulash, not Hungarian: tender beef chuck slow-braised with paprika, caraway and a lot of onions into a thick dark stew. Served with bread dumplings to soak up the sauce. The pub-and-Sunday-table classic.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes

**Cook Time:** 2 hours 15 minutes

## Overview
Czech goulash (hovězí guláš) is distinct from its Hungarian cousin: the Hungarian gulyás is a soupy paprika-rich stew; the Czech version is thicker, darker, and built on a slow caramelisation of a huge volume of onions until they melt into the sauce. Beef chuck slow-braised with sweet paprika, caraway and a touch of marjoram becomes meltingly tender; the sauce is rich and almost glossy from the onion-sugar Maillard reaction. The traditional companion is houskové knedlíky (bread dumplings) sliced into rounds and used to wipe up the sauce. The pub version is even richer, with a dark roux and a generous splash of dark beer. A bowl of goulash with a glass of Pilsner is the universal Czech tavern meal.

## Ingredients
- 1200 g beef chuck or shin, cut into 4 cm chunks
- 600 g onions (about 3 large), finely diced
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 4 tbsp lard, duck fat or vegetable oil
- 3 tbsp sweet Hungarian or Czech paprika
- 1 tsp smoked paprika (optional)
- 1 tsp caraway seeds, lightly crushed
- 1 tsp dried marjoram
- 1 bay leaf
- 2 tbsp tomato paste
- 800 ml beef stock
- 300 ml dark Czech lager (a Bohemian dark beer; substitute any dark lager)
- 2 tbsp plain flour (for thickening)
- 2 tsp salt
- 1 tsp freshly ground black pepper
- 1 tsp brown sugar (balances)
- 1 tbsp red wine vinegar

### To serve
- Czech bread dumplings (houskové knedlíky), sliced into rounds
- Sliced raw onion (a small mound on the plate; Czech tradition)
- 1 tbsp finely chopped flat-leaf parsley

## Method

### Stage 1 - Sweat the onions
1. Heat 2 tbsp of the lard in a large heavy pot over medium heat.
2. Add the diced onions; cook 15-20 minutes, stirring often.
3. The onions should go from raw to deeply golden-brown - taking their time matters. Don't rush this step; the colour and sweetness here is the foundation of the dish.

### Stage 2 - Sear the beef
1. While the onions cook, pat the beef chunks dry with kitchen paper.
2. In a separate large frying pan, heat the remaining 2 tbsp lard over high heat.
3. Sear the beef in 2 batches, 2-3 minutes per side, until browned all over.
4. Lift out; reserve.

### Stage 3 - Combine and add spices
1. When the onions are deeply golden, reduce heat to low.
2. Add the garlic; cook 1 minute.
3. Stir in the paprika, smoked paprika, caraway, marjoram, bay and tomato paste; cook 1 minute (paprika scorches if too hot - keep heat low).

### Stage 4 - Build the braise
1. Pour in the dark beer; let bubble vigorously 2 minutes to cook off the alcohol.
2. Add the beef stock.
3. Return the seared beef to the pot along with any resting juices.
4. Add the salt, pepper, sugar and vinegar.
5. Bring to a simmer.

### Stage 5 - Slow simmer
1. Cover with a tight lid.
2. Reduce heat to very low; simmer 1 hour 30 minutes - 2 hours, stirring every 30 minutes.
3. The beef should be fork-tender but holding shape.

### Stage 6 - Thicken
1. In a small bowl, whisk the flour with 100 ml cold water into a smooth slurry.
2. Pour into the stew; stir.
3. Cook another 10-15 minutes uncovered to thicken; the sauce should coat a spoon thickly.

### Stage 7 - Adjust
1. Taste; the goulash should be paprika-deep, slightly sweet, slightly sour, salty enough.
2. Adjust seasoning.

### Stage 8 - Serve
1. Ladle into deep warm bowls.
2. Place 3 rounds of sliced bread dumpling alongside (not under - they should stay slightly distinct so each spoonful can be the goulash, the dumpling, or both).
3. A small heap of finely sliced raw onion to one side (a Czech tradition; the sharp crunch contrasts the rich stew).
4. Scatter parsley.

## Notes
- **Onions to weight:** Czech goulash uses onions equal to about half the weight of the meat. This is non-negotiable - they melt into the sauce and provide the body and sweetness.
- **Don't burn the paprika:** Paprika is fragile; high heat turns it bitter. Reduce heat before adding it; cook just briefly before liquid hits.
- **Czech, not Hungarian:** This stew is thick and gravy-like, not soupy. The Hungarian version uses water and stays loose; the Czech version uses less liquid and thickens to coat.

## Serving
- The pub lunch of the Czech Republic. Served with bread dumplings, sliced raw onion, a glass of Pilsner Urquell, and a chunk of dark rye bread on the side.

## Storage
- Refrigerates 5 days; the flavour deepens overnight.
- Reheats beautifully in a covered pan over low heat.
- Freezes 3 months; thaw in the fridge before reheating.
