# Tennessee

*The state where Southern cooking, BBQ smoke, country-music kitchens and the world's most famous whiskey distillery all converge on one table. Memphis dry-rubbed ribs, Nashville hot chicken, and the catfish fry-out: Tennessee is barbecue country with a fried-everything afterthought.*

## A short history

Tennessee cooking begins with the Cherokee, Chickasaw and Shawnee peoples who lived on this land for thousands of years before European contact. They grew the three sisters (corn, beans, squash) in river-bottom fields along the Tennessee and Cumberland, hunted bear and deer in the hardwood forests, fished the rivers for catfish and bass, and ground hickory nuts into a milky paste that thickened stews. Sofkee (a fermented corn drink), bean bread, hominy from lye-soaked corn, and the slow-pit cooking of whole pig and deer all predate any European settlement. Corn, beans, pork, and the pit fire: those four foundations the colonial South would later inherit were already here.

Scots-Irish and English settlers crossed the Appalachians into Tennessee from the 1770s onward, bringing the upland-Southern cooking of corn pone, sorghum cane, salt-cured pork and the still. Tennessee became a state in 1796, and over the next half-century three distinct culinary regions hardened. East Tennessee (Knoxville, the Smoky Mountains) sits on Appalachian cooking: corn, beans, pork, the moonshine tradition that took shape because grain travelled the mountain roads more profitably as liquor than as flour. Middle Tennessee (Nashville, the Cumberland Plateau) developed plantation-influenced cooking: fried chicken, country ham, biscuits and gravy, the meat-and-three plate lunch. West Tennessee (Memphis, the Delta) sat at the head of the river cotton trade and absorbed the Mississippi Delta cooking, particularly the African-American barbecue tradition that came up from the lower South.

The Civil War devastated Tennessee (more battles fought here than any state except Virginia) and the post-war decades kept the home kitchen on whatever the land and the smokehouse could produce. African-American cooks across all three regions kept the techniques alive and added their own: the seasoning of greens with smoked pork, the perfection of fried chicken, the slow-pit barbecue method using a trench and hickory. Jack Daniel learned distilling from Nathan "Nearest" Green, a Black master distiller and freedman, and opened his Lynchburg distillery in 1866; the Lincoln County Process of charcoal-mellowing through sugar maple charcoal that defines Tennessee whiskey was Green's contribution as much as Daniel's. The whiskey industry locked into Tennessee's identity from then on.

Memphis BBQ developed its distinctive identity in the early 20th century at family-owned joints like Cozy Corner, Charles Vergos' Rendezvous (the dry-rubbed rib pioneers from 1948), and Payne's. The technique (long, slow smoke over hickory; a tomato-vinegar mop sauce; dry rub heavier than wet sauce on ribs) set the Memphis style apart from Texas (beef-led), Kansas City (sweeter sauce), and the Carolinas (mustard or vinegar sauces). Memphis also developed its own oddity, BBQ spaghetti, a regional crossover. Beale Street, the Memphis blues district that produced B.B. King and Howlin' Wolf, ran the same culinary lineage; the BBQ joints and the blues clubs grew up together on the same blocks.

Nashville hot chicken is the more recent regional invention. The story goes that Thornton Prince's girlfriend, suspecting him of running around, fried him chicken with so much cayenne it was meant as revenge; he loved it, and Prince's Hot Chicken Shack, founded in the 1930s, codified the now-famous extra-spicy fried chicken with cayenne-and-paprika-soaked oil brushed over the crust. The 2010s saw hot chicken explode nationally; Nashville now leans on it as a tourism magnet alongside the country-music industry. The contemporary Tennessee table sits on all of it at once: pulled pork and hot chicken and country ham and a glass of Jack, no single style winning out.

## Regional variations

- **West Tennessee (Memphis)**: BBQ capital. Dry-rubbed ribs, pulled pork sandwiches with slaw, smoked chicken, the BBQ-spaghetti hybrid.
- **Middle Tennessee (Nashville)**: hot chicken, country ham, meat-and-three plates, biscuits and milk gravy.
- **East Tennessee (Knoxville, Smokies)**: Appalachian. Country fried steak, soup beans, cornbread, moonshine.
- **Tennessee whiskey country (Lynchburg, Tullahoma)**: the Jack Daniel's and George Dickel distilleries. Charcoal-mellowed sour-mash bourbon style.

## Signature ingredients

- **Hickory wood**: the smoke of choice for Memphis BBQ.
- **Cayenne pepper**: the heat in Nashville hot chicken; lots of it.
- **Lard**: the frying fat of choice in country kitchens; pork-rendered, used for biscuits and fried chicken.
- **Buttermilk**: the soak for chicken before frying; the binder for biscuits.
- **Sorghum molasses**: the cane-syrup alternative grown across Tennessee. Sweetener and the glaze on certain hams.
- **Country ham**: dry-cured pork leg, salty and intense. Eaten in thin slices with biscuits and red-eye gravy.
- **Tennessee whiskey**: the cooking spirit and the table drink. Jack Daniel's is a marinade ingredient as much as a beverage.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover Memphis BBQ, Nashville hot chicken, country ham and the catfish fry. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Memphis Pulled Pork](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/memphis-pulled-pork)**: pork shoulder smoked over hickory for 10+ hours till it pulls apart, served on a brioche bun with slaw and a thin tomato-vinegar sauce. The Memphis standard.
2. **[Memphis Dry-Rub Ribs](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/memphis-dry-rub-ribs)**: spare ribs coated in a paprika-cayenne-brown-sugar rub, smoked over hickory, served dry (no sauce). The Rendezvous-codified rib style.
3. **[Memphis Brisket](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/memphis-brisket)**: beef brisket smoked low and slow with a Memphis spice rub. The Memphis answer to the Texas tradition; equally good, less famous.
4. **[Nashville Hot Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/nashville-hot-chicken)**: brined chicken pieces, double-coated and fried, finished with a spice paste of cayenne, paprika and the chicken's own frying oil. The Prince's-codified extra-hot fry.
5. **[Tennessee Catfish](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/tennessee-catfish)**: cornmeal-coated catfish fillets, pan-fried in lard, served with hush puppies and tartar sauce. The Friday-night family meal across the Tennessee River basin.
6. **[Tennessee Whiskey Pork Chops](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/tennessee-whiskey-pork-chops)**: pork chops marinated in Tennessee whiskey, brown sugar, soy and garlic, grilled. The most Tennessee thing you can cook.
7. **[Tennessee Meatloaf](/recipes/cuisine/tennessee/tennessee-meatloaf)**: ground beef with onion, breadcrumb and a brown-sugar-and-ketchup glaze. The meat-and-three plate's anchor.

Cook the pulled pork, the ribs and the brisket and you have Memphis. The hot chicken is Nashville. The catfish, the whiskey chops and the meatloaf are the everyday Tennessee table.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the country-fried steak shelf, the biscuits-and-gravy breakfast, more BBQ varieties, and the country-ham canon. Pair everything with a Tennessee whiskey or a cold Yuengling and the cuisine sings.
