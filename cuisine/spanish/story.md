# Spanish

*The cuisine of small plates and long lunches. Olive oil, garlic, ham, paprika and the late dinner hour; a country that eats five times a day and never seems to be in a hurry about any of them.*

## A short history

Spanish cooking begins with the Phoenician and Greek traders who set up colonies along the Mediterranean coast from the 8th century BCE, planting the olive trees and grapevines that still define the Andalusian landscape. The Romans arrived in 218 BCE and made Hispania the empire's olive-oil and garum (fermented fish sauce) heartland; the amphorae from Baetica supplied Rome itself for three centuries. The Roman shelf (olive oil, wheat, garlic, salt-cured pork, wine) sank into the bedrock of Iberian cooking and never left. The Visigoths who followed Rome added a heavier use of dairy and game and the slow-roasted lamb tradition that still runs through Castile.

The eight centuries of Moorish rule (711 to 1492) rewrote the kitchen entirely. The Umayyad and later Andalusi caliphates brought rice from China via Persia (the foundation of paella, eight hundred years later), saffron and almonds, citrus and pomegranate, sugar and aubergine, the irrigation systems that turned Valencia and Murcia into orchard country, and the entire Arab spice cabinet of cumin, coriander, cinnamon and clove. The mortar-and-pestle paste base (picada in Catalan, majado in Andalusian), the almond-thickened sauce, the love of saffron and the sweet-savoury crossover all date to Al-Andalus. Spain still eats more almonds per capita than almost anywhere in Europe.

The Reconquista finished in 1492, the same year Columbus sailed, and the Columbian Exchange flooded the country with tomatoes, peppers, potatoes, chocolate, vanilla, beans and the New World capsicum that the monks of Yuste would dry and grind into pimentón a century later. The synthesis is what makes Spanish food unmistakably Spanish: garlic and olive oil from Rome, saffron and almonds from Al-Andalus, tomato and paprika from the Americas. Pork eating became a public declaration of Christian identity after the expulsions of Jews and Muslims, which is why Spain became (and remains) the great pork country: jamón ibérico, chorizo, morcilla, lomo, the entire charcuterie shelf still carries that 15th-century politics.

The 18th and 19th centuries gave Spain the codified regional cooking it still has. Royal chefs at the Bourbon court in Madrid imported French technique, but the country house and the village fonda kept the older recipes alive. The tapas tradition (small free plates served with drinks in Andalusian bars) took shape in the 19th century, possibly from the custom of covering a sherry glass with a slice of ham or cheese to keep flies out. The phylloxera blight that destroyed French vineyards in the 1860s sent French wine merchants south into La Rioja, and the modern Rioja wine industry grew out of that overlap.

The 20th century was harder. The Civil War (1936 to 1939) and the long Franco dictatorship that followed left Spain hungry through the 1940s and 1950s; the rationing kitchen of garlic soup, lentils with chorizo, and tortilla española made from one egg stretched a long way kept families fed. The late-meal rhythm (lunch at 2, dinner at 10) that still organises daily life dates partly to that era, when Franco moved the clocks to align with Berlin. The post-Franco democratic period from 1975 brought the nueva cocina española revolution (Juan Mari Arzak in San Sebastián from the 1970s, then El Bulli under Ferran Adrià through the 1990s and 2000s, Mugaritz, the Basque chef collective), making Spain the most exciting fine-dining country in the world from roughly 1995 to 2010 and still one of the top three. The everyday kitchen sits underneath it all, unhurried, still on garlic and olive oil and a glass of cold fino at one in the afternoon.

## Regional variations

- **Andalusia (south)**: olive-oil country. Gazpacho, fried fish (pescaíto frito), Moorish-influenced sweets, jerez (sherry).
- **Catalonia (Barcelona, north-east)**: French-leaning, the most refined region. Crema catalana, mar i muntanya (surf and turf), allioli, pa amb tomàquet.
- **Basque Country (north)**: pintxos (bar snacks pinned with toothpicks), bacalao (salt cod), idiazabal (sheep cheese), txakoli (white wine), the densest Michelin star concentration in Europe.
- **Galicia (north-west)**: Atlantic seafood. Pulpo a la gallega, empanadas, padrón peppers, Albariño wine. The wettest, greenest, most fish-led region.
- **Valencia (east)**: rice country. Paella was born here. Citrus orchards, horchata, the rice-paddy tradition of the Albufera.
- **Asturias (north)**: cider country. Fabada (white-bean stew), cabrales (blue cheese), sidra poured from height. The most rustic shelf.
- **Castilla (centre)**: roast meats. Cochinillo (suckling pig), cordero asado (roast lamb), Manchego cheese, garlic soup. The high meseta's cold-weather cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Olive oil**: Andalusian extra virgin, the fat of Spanish cooking.
- **Pimentón (smoked paprika)**: sweet (dulce), bittersweet (agridulce) or hot (picante). The smoke under chorizo, paella and almost every Spanish stew.
- **Saffron**: La Mancha saffron is the world benchmark. Bloomed in warm oil for paella and rice dishes.
- **Jamón ibérico, jamón serrano**: cured ham from acorn-fed black pigs (ibérico) or white pigs (serrano). The defining pork product.
- **Chorizo and morcilla**: cured pork sausage spiced with pimentón; blood sausage with rice or onion.
- **Manchego, idiazabal, cabrales**: sheep's milk hard cheese (Manchego), Basque smoked sheep (idiazabal), Asturian blue (cabrales).
- **Sherry, cava, rioja**: dry fino for tapas, cava as the everyday fizz, rioja as the daily red.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the rice tradition, the cold soup, the tapas table and the slow-braise. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Paella Valenciana](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/paella-valenciana)**: rice with rabbit, chicken, snails (or green beans) and garrofó beans, cooked in a wide flat pan over fire till the bottom crisps into socarrat. The original paella; everything else is a variant.
2. **[Paella de Mariscos](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/paella-mariscos)**: the seafood paella. Same technique with prawns, mussels, clams, squid. The Mediterranean coast's signature plate.
3. **[Gazpacho](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/gazpacho)**: cold soup of ripe tomato, cucumber, pepper, garlic and olive oil. Andalusia's summer hydrator and the test of a kitchen's olive oil.
4. **[Tortilla Española](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/tortilla-espanola)**: thick potato-and-onion omelette, set softly so the centre stays just-cooked. The national tapa, eaten at any hour, hot or room-temp.
5. **[Fabada Asturiana](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/fabada-asturiana)**: white beans (faba) slow-cooked with chorizo, morcilla and pork belly. Asturias's grand winter stew, the Spanish answer to cassoulet.
6. **[Croquetas de Jamón](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/croquetas-de-jamon)**: béchamel-and-ham croquettes, panko-crusted and deep-fried. The most reliable tapa in any Spanish bar.
7. **[Pisto Manchego](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/pisto-manchego)**: tomato-pepper-courgette stew, topped with a fried egg. The Castilian vegetable workhorse; the Spanish ratatouille.

Cook the two paellas and you have the rice shelf. The gazpacho and the tortilla are the everyday table. The fabada is the long braise; the croquetas the tapas bar. The pisto is the home weeknight.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the Galician seafood (pulpo, empanadas), the Basque pintxos shelf (gilda, txangurro), the Castilian roast tradition (cochinillo, cordero asado), and the dessert canon (crema catalana, churros, polvorones). Spanish food rewards going regional; pick a corner and dig in.
