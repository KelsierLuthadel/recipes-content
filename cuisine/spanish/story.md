# Spanish

*The cuisine of small plates and long lunches. Olive oil, garlic, ham, paprika and the late dinner hour; a country that eats five times a day and never seems to be in a hurry about any of them.*

## A short history

Spanish cooking sits on three foundations: the Roman olive-oil-and-wheat shelf, the eight centuries of Moorish rule (711–1492) that brought rice, saffron, almonds, citrus, sugar and the spice cabinet, and the Columbian Exchange that flooded the country with tomatoes, peppers, potatoes, chocolate and the New World pepper that would become pimentón. The synthesis is what makes Spanish food unmistakably Spanish: garlic and olive oil from Rome, saffron and almonds from Al-Andalus, tomato and paprika from the Americas.

The Catholic Reconquista in 1492 set a culinary politics that lasts to this day. Pork eating became a public declaration of Christian identity, which is why Spain became (and remains) the great pork country: jamón ibérico, chorizo, morcilla, lomo, the entire charcuterie shelf. The expulsion of Jews and Muslims pushed Spain toward pork as a national signal as much as a food.

The 19th and 20th centuries gave Spain the tapas tradition (small free plates served with drinks in Andalusian bars) and the late-meal rhythm (lunch at 2, dinner at 10) that still organises daily life. The post-Franco democratic period brought the nueva cocina española revolution (El Bulli, Ferran Adrià, Mugaritz, the Basque chef collective), making Spain the most exciting fine-dining country in the world from roughly 1995–2010 and still one of the top three.

## Regional variations

- **Andalusia (south)**: olive-oil country. Gazpacho, fried fish (pescaíto frito), Moorish-influenced sweets, jerez (sherry).
- **Catalonia (Barcelona, north-east)**: French-leaning, the most refined region. Crema catalana, mar i muntanya (surf and turf), allioli, pa amb tomàquet.
- **Basque Country (north)**: pintxos (bar snacks pinned with toothpicks), bacalao (salt cod), idiazabal (sheep cheese), txakoli (white wine), the densest Michelin star concentration in Europe.
- **Galicia (north-west)**: Atlantic seafood. Pulpo a la gallega, empanadas, padrón peppers, Albariño wine. The wettest, greenest, most fish-led region.
- **Valencia (east)**: rice country. Paella was born here. Citrus orchards, horchata, the rice-paddy tradition of the Albufera.
- **Asturias (north)**: cider country. Fabada (white-bean stew), cabrales (blue cheese), sidra poured from height. The most rustic shelf.
- **Castilla (centre)**: roast meats. Cochinillo (suckling pig), cordero asado (roast lamb), Manchego cheese, garlic soup. The high meseta's cold-weather cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Olive oil**: Andalusian extra virgin, the fat of Spanish cooking.
- **Pimentón (smoked paprika)**: sweet (dulce), bittersweet (agridulce) or hot (picante). The smoke under chorizo, paella and almost every Spanish stew.
- **Saffron**: La Mancha saffron is the world benchmark. Bloomed in warm oil for paella and rice dishes.
- **Jamón ibérico, jamón serrano**: cured ham from acorn-fed black pigs (ibérico) or white pigs (serrano). The defining pork product.
- **Chorizo and morcilla**: cured pork sausage spiced with pimentón; blood sausage with rice or onion.
- **Manchego, idiazabal, cabrales**: sheep's milk hard cheese (Manchego), Basque smoked sheep (idiazabal), Asturian blue (cabrales).
- **Sherry, cava, rioja**: dry fino for tapas, cava as the cheap fizz, rioja as the everyday red.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the rice tradition, the cold soup, the tapas table and the slow-braise. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Paella Valenciana](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/paella-valenciana)**: rice with rabbit, chicken, snails (or green beans) and garrofó beans, cooked in a wide flat pan over fire till the bottom crisps into socarrat. The original paella; everything else is a variant.
2. **[Paella de Mariscos](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/paella-mariscos)**: the seafood paella. Same technique with prawns, mussels, clams, squid. The Mediterranean coast's signature plate.
3. **[Gazpacho](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/gazpacho)**: cold soup of ripe tomato, cucumber, pepper, garlic and olive oil. Andalusia's summer hydrator and the test of a kitchen's olive oil.
4. **[Tortilla Española](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/tortilla-espanola)**: thick potato-and-onion omelette, set softly so the centre stays just-cooked. The national tapa, eaten at any hour, hot or room-temp.
5. **[Fabada Asturiana](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/fabada-asturiana)**: white beans (faba) slow-cooked with chorizo, morcilla and pork belly. Asturias's grand winter stew, the Spanish answer to cassoulet.
6. **[Croquetas de Jamón](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/croquetas-de-jamon)**: béchamel-and-ham croquettes, panko-crusted and deep-fried. The most reliable tapa in any Spanish bar.
7. **[Pisto Manchego](/recipes/cuisine/spanish/pisto-manchego)**: tomato-pepper-courgette stew, topped with a fried egg. The Castilian vegetable workhorse; the Spanish ratatouille.

Cook the two paellas and you have the rice shelf. The gazpacho and the tortilla are the everyday table. The fabada is the long braise; the croquetas the tapas bar. The pisto is the home weeknight.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the Galician seafood (pulpo, empanadas), the Basque pintxos shelf (gilda, txangurro), the Castilian roast tradition (cochinillo, cordero asado), and the dessert canon (crema catalana, churros, polvorones). Spanish food rewards going regional; pick a corner and dig in.
