# Nigerian

*The cuisine of West African abundance. Built on yam, plantain, palm oil, the chilli-and-pepper soup that wakes the dead, and the rivalry over jollof rice that defines an entire region.*

## A short history

Nigerian cooking is shaped by the country's huge ethnic diversity — over 250 distinct groups, each with their own kitchen, broadly grouped into Yoruba (south-west), Igbo (south-east), Hausa-Fulani (north) and many minorities. The basic ingredient list — yam, plantain, cassava, palm oil, dried fish, peppers (especially the Scotch bonnet and the dried "ata rodo"), egusi (melon seed), beans (especially black-eyed) — has been in continuous use for centuries.

The trans-Atlantic slave trade carried Nigerian cooking to the Americas, where it became the foundational layer under Brazilian, Caribbean and US Southern cuisines (jambalaya, gumbo, hoppin' John, and most beans-and-rice traditions trace back to West African originals). The reverse traffic brought tomatoes, chillies, peanuts and corn from the Americas, all of which Nigerian cooking absorbed completely — modern Nigerian food without tomato or chilli is unimaginable.

The Jollof Wars are a real cultural phenomenon. Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, Liberia and Sierra Leone all claim the definitive version of jollof rice (a one-pot tomato-pepper-spiced rice); the debate is fierce, friendly and ongoing. Nigerian jollof tends to be smokier (cooked till the bottom catches), spicier, and made with long-grain parboiled rice. Ghanaian jollof tends to use jasmine rice and a lighter hand on the pepper. They are both correct and both will defend themselves.

## Regional variations

- **Yoruba (south-west, Lagos)**: amala-and-ewedu (yam-flour swallow with mucilaginous greens), efo riro (vegetable stew), the suya street-meat tradition.
- **Igbo (south-east, Enugu)**: ofe nsala (white soup), egusi soup with pounded yam, the palm-wine ritual.
- **Hausa-Fulani (north, Kano, Sokoto)**: suya was perfected here. Tuwo (mashed grain swallow), miyan (soup), masa (rice cakes).
- **Niger Delta (Calabar)**: the soup capital. Edikang Ikong (with pumpkin leaf and waterleaf), afang soup, the seafood-heavy shelf.
- **Middle Belt**: yam country. Pounded yam with everything.

## Signature ingredients

- **Palm oil (red, unrefined)**: the fat of southern Nigerian cooking. Distinct from the bland refined version sold in Western supermarkets.
- **Scotch bonnet pepper**: the heat. Fresh in stews, dried as ata rodo powder.
- **Egusi (melon seed)**: ground, used as a thickener in soups.
- **Crayfish (dried, ground)**: the umami in nearly every soup.
- **Dried fish and stockfish**: salt-cured, sun-dried fish. Adds funk and depth to soups.
- **Locust bean (iru, dawadawa)**: fermented, intensely pungent. The Nigerian miso.
- **Bitter leaf and ugu (pumpkin leaf)**: the greens. Used in soups, never raw.
- **Suya spice (yaji)**: ground peanut, ginger, paprika, chilli. The defining grill rub.

## Learn it in seven recipes

The catalogue's Nigerian shelf is concentrated; here are the standouts.

1. **[Jollof Rice with Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/jollof-rice-with-chicken)**: long-grain rice cooked in a pureed-tomato-and-pepper base till the bottom catches and the rice turns smoky-red. The single most-loved Nigerian dish and the centre of the West African food argument.
2. **[Egusi Soup](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/egusi-soup)**: ground melon seed cooked with palm oil, leafy greens, dried fish, crayfish and meat. The most universal Nigerian soup, eaten with pounded yam or fufu.
3. **[Efo Riro](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/efo-riro)**: spinach (or other leafy greens) cooked with palm oil, pepper, locust bean, smoked fish, and assorted meats. The Yoruba vegetable stew.
4. **[Suya](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/suya)**: thinly sliced beef coated in yaji (peanut-and-spice rub), grilled on skewers over open charcoal. The Hausa-perfected street meat now eaten across the country.
5. **[Moin Moin](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/moin-moin)**: steamed bean pudding made from peeled, blended black-eyed peas with pepper, onion and (often) boiled egg. The protein-rich vegetarian centrepiece.
6. **[Pepper Soup](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/pepper-soup)**: light broth with chilli, calabash nutmeg, uda and selim peppers — the "wake-the-dead" soup served when someone has a cold or is hungover.
7. **[Dodo](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/dodo)**: ripe plantain sliced and fried in palm or vegetable oil till the edges caramelise. The ubiquitous side dish.

Cook the jollof and the egusi and you have the two pillars. The efo riro and the moin moin are the everyday. The suya is the grill, the pepper soup the cure, the dodo the side that comes with everything.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries more soups (afang, edikang ikong, ogbono), the rice and yam shelf (white rice with stew, pounded yam, ofada rice with ayamase), and the bread-and-snack tradition (puff puff, akara, meat pie). Nigerian food rewards a heavy hand; don't be shy with the pepper or the palm oil and the cuisine sings.
