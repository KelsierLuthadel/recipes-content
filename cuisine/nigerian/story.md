# Nigerian

*The cuisine of West African abundance. Built on yam, plantain, palm oil, the chilli-and-pepper soup that wakes the dead, and the rivalry over jollof rice that defines an entire region.*

## A short history

Nigerian cooking is shaped by the country's huge ethnic diversity, over 250 distinct groups, each with their own kitchen, broadly grouped into Yoruba (south-west), Igbo (south-east), Hausa-Fulani (north) and many minorities including the Ibibio, Efik, Tiv and Edo. The deep roots go back thousands of years. The Nok culture of central Nigeria (roughly 1500 BCE to 500 CE) was already farming yams, oil palm and millet, smelting iron and firing remarkable terracotta sculptures. Yam in particular runs through every Nigerian kitchen with sacred weight; the yam festivals (the Iri ji of the Igbo, the Iwa ji of the Yoruba) still mark the August-September harvest with the year's first pounded yam at the family table.

The medieval centuries built several powerful kingdoms whose courts shaped the regional cooking. The Hausa city-states of Kano, Katsina and Zaria sat on the trans-Saharan caravan routes by the 11th century, and Islam arrived with the trade. The trade brought wheat, dates, mint, ginger and a tradition of grilling spice-rubbed meat over wood coals that became the foundation of suya. The Yoruba kingdom of Oyo and the Edo Kingdom of Benin (peaking 1400 to 1700) developed the elaborate palm-oil-based stews, the pounded yam tradition and the ritual cooking for royal courts. The Igbo communities of the southeast farmed yam, oil palm and kola, and traded north and east.

The trans-Atlantic slave trade from the 1500s to the 1860s carried Nigerian cooking to the Americas, where it became the foundational layer under Brazilian, Caribbean and US Southern cuisines. Jambalaya, gumbo, hoppin' John, akara fritters surviving as Brazilian acarajé, and most beans-and-rice traditions trace back to West African originals. The reverse traffic brought the Columbian-Exchange crops that now seem so Nigerian you can't imagine the cuisine without them: tomatoes, chillies (especially the Scotch bonnet), peanuts, cassava, maize and the new world sweet potato. Nigerian cooking absorbed every one of them completely. Modern Nigerian food without tomato or chilli is unimaginable, and cassava (boiled into fufu, fermented into garri) became a second staple alongside yam.

British colonisation from 1861 (when Lagos was annexed) to 1960 imposed an administrative shape on the diverse region and pulled the southern coast into the empire's trading economy. The colonial centuries added wheat bread, tea, palm-oil-and-coconut biscuits and a small set of Anglo-Nigerian habits, but the home kitchen stayed itself. Independence in 1960, the Biafran War (1967 to 1970) and the oil-boom decades that followed reshaped the country economically without breaking the food traditions. Lagos grew from a colonial port into one of the world's largest cities; the suya stand, the bukka (small eatery) and the bole-and-fish stall all moved into the streets and stayed there.

The Jollof Wars are a real cultural phenomenon. Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, Liberia and Sierra Leone all claim the definitive version of jollof rice (a one-pot tomato-pepper-spiced rice); the debate is fierce, friendly and permanently ongoing on social media. Nigerian jollof tends to be smokier (cooked till the bottom catches into a layer called "party jollof"), spicier, and made with long-grain parboiled rice. Ghanaian jollof tends to use jasmine rice and a lighter hand on the pepper. They are both correct and both will defend themselves vigorously.

The 21st-century Nigerian diaspora kitchen has gone global. London, Houston, Atlanta and Toronto all have serious Nigerian restaurant scenes, and chefs like Tunde Wey, Adejoké Bakare and Lopè Ariyo have pushed the cuisine onto international fine-dining lists. Afrobeats music and Nollywood film have carried Nigerian culture worldwide and the food has travelled with them. The kitchen at home, though, still runs on yam, palm oil, pepper and a generous hand on the pot.

## Regional variations

- **Yoruba (south-west, Lagos)**: amala-and-ewedu (yam-flour swallow with mucilaginous greens), efo riro (vegetable stew), the suya street-meat tradition.
- **Igbo (south-east, Enugu)**: ofe nsala (white soup), egusi soup with pounded yam, the palm-wine ritual.
- **Hausa-Fulani (north, Kano, Sokoto)**: suya was perfected here. Tuwo (mashed grain swallow), miyan (soup), masa (rice cakes).
- **Niger Delta (Calabar)**: the soup capital. Edikang Ikong (with pumpkin leaf and waterleaf), afang soup, the seafood-heavy shelf.
- **Middle Belt**: yam country. Pounded yam with everything.

## Signature ingredients

- **Palm oil (red, unrefined)**: the fat of southern Nigerian cooking. Distinct from the bland refined version sold in Western supermarkets.
- **Scotch bonnet pepper**: the heat. Fresh in stews, dried as ata rodo powder.
- **Egusi (melon seed)**: ground, used as a thickener in soups.
- **Crayfish (dried, ground)**: the umami in nearly every soup.
- **Dried fish and stockfish**: salt-cured, sun-dried fish. Adds funk and depth to soups.
- **Locust bean (iru, dawadawa)**: fermented, intensely pungent. The Nigerian miso.
- **Bitter leaf and ugu (pumpkin leaf)**: the greens. Used in soups, never raw.
- **Suya spice (yaji)**: ground peanut, ginger, paprika, chilli. The defining grill rub.

## Learn it in seven recipes

The catalogue's Nigerian shelf is concentrated; here are the standouts.

1. **[Jollof Rice with Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/jollof-rice-with-chicken)**: long-grain rice cooked in a pureed-tomato-and-pepper base till the bottom catches and the rice turns smoky-red. The single most-loved Nigerian dish and the centre of the West African food argument.
2. **[Egusi Soup](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/egusi-soup)**: ground melon seed cooked with palm oil, leafy greens, dried fish, crayfish and meat. The most universal Nigerian soup, eaten with pounded yam or fufu.
3. **[Efo Riro](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/efo-riro)**: spinach (or other leafy greens) cooked with palm oil, pepper, locust bean, smoked fish, and assorted meats. The Yoruba vegetable stew.
4. **[Suya](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/suya)**: thinly sliced beef coated in yaji (peanut-and-spice rub), grilled on skewers over open charcoal. The Hausa-perfected street meat now eaten across the country.
5. **[Moin Moin](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/moin-moin)**: steamed bean pudding made from peeled, blended black-eyed peas with pepper, onion and (often) boiled egg. The protein-rich vegetarian centrepiece.
6. **[Pepper Soup](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/pepper-soup)**: light broth with chilli, calabash nutmeg, uda and selim peppers. The "wake-the-dead" soup served when someone has a cold or is hungover.
7. **[Dodo](/recipes/cuisine/nigerian/dodo)**: ripe plantain sliced and fried in palm or vegetable oil till the edges caramelise. The ubiquitous side dish.

Cook the jollof and the egusi and you have the two pillars. The efo riro and the moin moin are the everyday. The suya is the grill, the pepper soup the cure, the dodo the side that comes with everything.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries more soups (afang, edikang ikong, ogbono), the rice and yam shelf (white rice with stew, pounded yam, ofada rice with ayamase), and the bread-and-snack tradition (puff puff, akara, meat pie). Nigerian food rewards a heavy hand; don't be shy with the pepper or the palm oil and the cuisine sings.
