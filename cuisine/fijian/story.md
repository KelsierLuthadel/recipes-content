# Fijian

*The kitchen of the Pacific. Indigenous iTaukei (Melanesian) root crops and underground pit cooking (lovo), layered with the deep Indian inheritance from the 19th-century indentured-labour migration. Coconut everywhere.*

## A short history

Fijian cooking is two distinct culinary traditions running on one island chain. The deepest layer is Lapita: around 3,500 years ago seafaring Austronesian-speaking people pushed east out of Melanesia in outrigger canoes, carrying with them the "transported landscape" of the Pacific (taro, yam, breadfruit, coconut, banana, sugarcane, pigs, chickens) and the distinctive dentate-stamped pottery that archaeologists use to track their movement. They settled Fiji around 1500 BCE, and from Fiji their descendants pushed further east to colonise Tonga, Samoa and eventually the whole Polynesian triangle. The iTaukei (native Fijian) kitchen still rests on that Lapita inheritance: taro (dalo), cassava (added later from the Americas via European traders), sweet potato (kumala), breadfruit, coconut, reef fish, pork, and the lovo (an underground stone pit) that turns a whole meal smoky and tender.

Pre-contact Fijian society had elaborate food protocols: kava (yaqona) ceremony for chiefs and elders, the lovo for celebration meals, the strict tabu around women preparing certain foods. Fish, pork and root crops cooked in earth ovens were the everyday; the great celebration was the lovo with whole pig and chicken wrapped in banana leaves. European contact began with Tasman in 1643 and Cook in 1774, but Fiji's reputation for cannibalism (which was real, though selective and ceremonial) kept most ships clear for another century. Sandalwood traders arrived in the early 1800s, beche-de-mer (sea cucumber) traders followed, and the first Christian missionaries (Methodist and Catholic) reached the islands in 1835 and converted most of the chiefly class by 1854.

Fiji became a British colony in 1874 when the paramount chief Seru Epenisa Cakobau ceded the islands to Queen Victoria, partly to settle his debts. The colonial sugar economy needed labour and the British found it in India. In 1879 the first shipload of Indian indentured labourers (girmityas, "agreement-people") arrived at Levuka, and over the next 37 years more than 60,000 Indians (from Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, Andhra) were brought to work the cane fields. Their descendants, Indo-Fijians, now make up around 37% of the population. They brought curry, roti, dhal, the full Indian spice cabinet, mustard seeds and curry leaves, and the South Indian tempering technique that still defines the Indo-Fijian Sunday curry. Two food traditions running in parallel with plenty of crossover: Indo-Fijian goat curry next to lovo-cooked pork at family events, kokoda (fish ceviche in coconut milk) alongside roti and dhal at the lunch table.

Independence came in 1970. The post-independence decades have been politically turbulent (coups in 1987, 2000 and 2006, often with ethnic tension between iTaukei and Indo-Fijians at the centre) and emigration has run heavy. Many Indo-Fijian families left after the coups, and Fijian emigration to Australia, New Zealand, Canada and the United States has built strong diaspora kitchens. The Fijian restaurants in Sydney, Auckland and Vancouver carry both halves of the cuisine. At home, the lovo still anchors the Sunday celebration, the curry still runs every Indo-Fijian weeknight, and the kava bowl still sits at the centre of every formal gathering.

## Regional variations

- **Coastal iTaukei (Viti Levu, Vanua Levu)**: seafood, coconut, the lovo tradition.
- **Highland iTaukei**: more taro, dalo, root crops.
- **Indo-Fijian (the sugar-belt towns of Lautoka and Ba)**: curry-and-roti tradition, Indian sweets at celebrations.
- **Urban (Suva, Nadi)**: cosmopolitan mix. Both traditions on the same menu.

## Signature ingredients

- **Coconut**: milk (lolo), cream, grated. In nearly every iTaukei dish.
- **Taro (dalo)**: root vegetable. Boiled, mashed, fried into fritters. The universal starch.
- **Cassava (tavioka)**: alongside taro.
- **Reef fish**: walu (Spanish mackerel), parrotfish, whatever came in that morning.
- **Curry powder**: in the Indo-Fijian kitchen.
- **Mustard seeds, cumin, fenugreek**: the South Indian temper for Indo-Fijian dishes.
- **Bele (Pacific spinach) and rourou (taro leaves)**: the leafy greens.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Kokoda](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/kokoda)**: white fish "cooked" in lime juice (think ceviche) with coconut milk, tomato, onion, chilli. The Fijian signature.
2. **[Lovo](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/lovo)**: pork, chicken, fish and root vegetables wrapped in banana leaves and cooked in an underground stone pit. The Sunday celebration meal.
3. **[Rourou](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/rourou)**: taro leaves cooked in coconut cream with onion. The universal side.
4. **[Palusami](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/palusami)**: corned beef (a colonial-era Pacific staple) wrapped in taro leaves with coconut cream and baked in the lovo.
5. **[Indo-Fijian Goat Curry](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/indo-fijian-goat-curry)**: goat curry with a South Indian spice profile (curry leaves, mustard seed, fenugreek), plus Fijian fresh chilli and coconut. The Indo-Fijian Sunday curry.

## Where to go next

Drink kava (yaqona, the ceremonial root drink: sedative, earthy, sipped from a coconut-shell bilo in formal kava circles) or a Fiji Gold beer. For a proper meal: lovo as the centrepiece, kokoda as a starter, rourou or palusami as sides, and fresh tropical fruit (papaya, mango, pineapple) to finish.
