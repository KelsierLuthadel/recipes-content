# Fijian

*The kitchen of the Pacific. Indigenous iTaukei (Melanesian) traditions of root crops and underground pit cooking (lovo), layered with the deep Indian inheritance from the 19th-century indentured-labour migration. Coconut everywhere.*

## A short history

Fijian cooking is the synthesis of two distinct culinary traditions on one island chain. The indigenous iTaukei (native Fijian) tradition is Pacific-Melanesian: taro, cassava, sweet potato (kumala), breadfruit, coconut, reef fish, pork, the lovo (underground stone pit) cooking method that turns a whole meal into smoky, tender perfection.

In 1879 the British colonial administration brought Indian indentured labourers (girmityas) to work the sugar-cane plantations. Their descendants (Indo-Fijians, now ~37% of the population) brought curry, roti, dhal and the complete Indian spice cabinet. The result: two food traditions running parallel, with crossover dishes (Indo-Fijian goat curry served alongside lovo-cooked pork at family events) and a national table that's unmistakably Pacific-Indian.

Recent emigration to Australia, New Zealand and Canada has created robust diaspora kitchens. The Fijian restaurant traditions in Sydney, Auckland and Vancouver carry both halves of the cuisine.

## Regional variations

- **Coastal iTaukei (Viti Levu, Vanua Levu)**: seafood, coconut, the lovo tradition.
- **Highland iTaukei**: more taro, dalo, root crops.
- **Indo-Fijian (sugar-belt towns — Lautoka, Ba)**: curry-and-roti tradition, Indian sweets at celebrations.
- **Urban (Suva, Nadi)**: cosmopolitan mix; both traditions on the same menu.

## Signature ingredients

- **Coconut**: milk (lolo), cream, grated, in nearly every iTaukei dish.
- **Taro (dalo)**: root vegetable, boiled, mashed, fritters; the universal starch.
- **Cassava (tavioka)**: alongside taro.
- **Reef fish**: walu (Spanish mackerel), parrotfish, the catch of the day.
- **Curry powder**: in Indo-Fijian cooking.
- **Mustard seeds, cumin, fenugreek**: the South Indian temper for Indo-Fijian dishes.
- **Bele (Pacific spinach) and rourou (taro leaves)**: the leafy greens.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Kokoda](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/kokoda)**: white fish "cooked" in lime juice (like ceviche) with coconut milk, tomato, onion, chilli. The Fijian signature.
2. **[Lovo](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/lovo)**: pork, chicken, fish and root vegetables wrapped in banana leaves and cooked in an underground stone pit. The Sunday celebration meal.
3. **[Rourou](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/rourou)**: taro leaves cooked in coconut cream with onion. The universal side.
4. **[Palusami](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/palusami)**: corned beef (a colonial-era Pacific staple) wrapped in taro leaves with coconut cream and baked in the lovo.
5. **[Indo-Fijian Goat Curry](/recipes/cuisine/fijian/indo-fijian-goat-curry)**: goat curry with the South Indian spice profile (curry leaves, mustard seed, fenugreek) plus Fijian fresh chilli and coconut. The Indo-Fijian Sunday curry.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with kava (yaqona — the ceremonial root drink, sedative, earthy, drunk from a coconut-shell bilo in formal kava circles) or with a Fiji Gold beer. For a proper meal: lovo as the centrepiece, kokoda as a starter, rourou or palusami as sides, fresh tropical fruit (papaya, mango, pineapple) to finish.
