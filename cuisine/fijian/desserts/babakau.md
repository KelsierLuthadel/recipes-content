# Babakau

![Babakau](resources/babakau.jpg)

*Fijian fried sweet bread: a yeasted dough fried into hot golden pillows, dusted with sugar and eaten with tea. The street-food and tea-time dessert across every Fijian town.*

**Serves:** Makes 12 pieces

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus 1 hour rise)

**Cook Time:** 20 minutes

## Overview
Babakau is the Fijian everyday sweet, sold from market stalls and tea shops across Suva, Nadi and every village in between. A simple enriched dough of flour, sugar, yeast, coconut milk and butter is left to rise once, shaped into small flat squares or rectangles, then deep-fried briefly until they puff into golden pillows. The outside is crisp, the inside is soft and slightly bready, and a final dusting of sugar makes them irresistible. Eaten warm with a cup of strong sweet tea (the British colonial heritage shows here) in the late afternoon, or as a breakfast with butter and jam.

## Ingredients
- 500 g plain flour
- 60 g caster sugar
- 1 tsp salt
- 7 g (1 sachet) fast-action dried yeast
- 300 ml coconut milk, warmed to lukewarm
- 50 g unsalted butter, melted
- 1 large egg, lightly beaten
- 1 tsp vanilla extract
- 1 litre vegetable oil for frying
- Caster sugar for dusting (about 4 tbsp)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Mix the dough
1. Combine flour, sugar, salt and yeast in a large bowl. Whisk to distribute the yeast evenly.
2. In a separate bowl, whisk together the warm coconut milk, melted butter, egg and vanilla.
3. Pour the wet ingredients into the dry; mix with a wooden spoon until a shaggy dough forms.
4. Turn out onto a lightly floured surface; knead 8-10 minutes until smooth and elastic. The dough should be soft but not sticky.

### Stage 2 - Rise
1. Place the dough in a lightly oiled bowl; cover with a damp tea towel.
2. Leave in a warm place 1 hour, until doubled in size.

### Stage 3 - Shape
1. Knock back the dough; turn onto a floured surface.
2. Roll out to a rectangle about 1.5 cm thick.
3. Cut into 12 squares or rectangles (roughly 8 x 5 cm each).
4. Cover with a tea towel; rest 10 minutes - the dough relaxes and rises slightly.

### Stage 4 - Fry
1. Heat the oil to 170 C in a wide deep pan or wok.
2. Fry 3-4 pieces at a time (do not crowd) for 1-2 minutes per side, until deep golden and puffed.
3. Lift onto kitchen paper to drain briefly.
4. While still warm, toss each piece in caster sugar to coat.

### Stage 5 - Serve
1. Pile onto a warm plate.
2. Serve immediately with hot tea.

## Notes
- **Coconut milk in the dough:** This is the Fijian touch. Substitute regular milk if needed, but the coconut gives babakau its signature richness.
- **Oil temperature:** 170 C is the sweet spot. Too hot and the outside burns before the inside cooks; too cool and the babakau absorbs oil and turns greasy.
- **Eat warm:** Babakau is at its best within an hour of frying. Cold babakau is still good but loses the contrast of crisp outside and soft inside.

## Serving
- Serve warm with strong sweet milky tea (chai-style or Fijian colonial-tea-style) as an afternoon snack. Butter and jam alongside if eating for breakfast.

## Storage
- Best the same day.
- The unfried shaped dough can be refrigerated overnight; bring to room temperature and fry the next day.
- Cooked babakau keeps 1 day in a tin; refresh briefly in a hot oven (160 C, 5 min) to restore the crisp shell.
