# Aruban Cuisine

![Aruban Cuisine](resources/hero.jpg)

Aruba sits in the warm southern Caribbean a short hop off the Venezuelan coast, the smallest and driest of the three Dutch ABC islands (Aruba, Bonaire and Curaçao). Its kitchen feels exactly like the island: sunny, generous, and a little bit Dutch, a little bit Caribbean and a little bit Latin all at once. Most homes still cook in Papiamento, the island's creole language, and recipes pass between aunties and grandmothers as freely as ever. The Sunday-lunch star is keshi yena, a hollowed-out Edam or Gouda shell stuffed with spiced chicken stew and baked till the cheese collapses around the filling. Stoba di cabritu, a slow-cooked goat stew bright with annatto, bay and tomato, is the family-table workhorse. The bakery-counter snack is pasthechi, a deep-fried turnover that comes with cheese, beef, or fish and is eaten on the way to work from breakfast onwards. Funchi (cornmeal mush) and pan bati (a thin coconut-cornmeal pancake) carry every plate; sopi di pisca, the aji-and-tomato fish soup, is the Friday tradition; and bolo di kashupete, the cashew layer cake, shows up at every wedding and birthday. To drink, the rum-spiked Aruba Ariba was invented at a Palm Beach bar in the 1960s, and the everyday morning cup is pega-pega coffee, sweetened heavy with condensed milk. Pull up a chair: there's always room.
