# Aruban

*The kitchen of the southern Dutch Caribbean. Papiamento-speaking, Spanish-Dutch-African-indigenous syntheses, with keshi yena (stuffed cheese) as the showpiece and stoba (the slow-stewed one-pot) as the everyday plate.*

## A short history

Aruban cooking is the cuisine of one of the ABC islands (Aruba, Bonaire, Curaçao), the southern Dutch Caribbean, just off the coast of Venezuela. The story starts with the indigenous Caquetio Arawak, who paddled north from the Venezuelan mainland around 1000 CE and settled the dry, cactus-strewn island. The Caquetio cooked maize, cassava and beans on flat clay griddles, fished the shallow reefs for parrotfish and grouper, and gathered the iguana, conch and sea-turtle that were the protein shelf of the pre-Columbian Caribbean. Pictographs in the caves at Fontein and Arikok still show their fishing and farming life. The Spanish under Alonso de Ojeda reached Aruba in 1499 and declared the islands islas inútiles (useless islands) because they couldn't grow sugar on the dry rocky terrain, then proceeded to enslave most of the Caquetio population and ship them to Hispaniola to mine.

The Dutch West India Company seized Aruba in 1636 and used it not for plantation sugar (the soil and rainfall wouldn't allow it) but as a livestock ranch and a strategic post in the salt and slave trades. Unlike Curaçao, which became the great Dutch slave-trading hub of the Caribbean, Aruba kept a small enslaved African population and a larger free-Black, mestizo and indigenous community. The mainland of Venezuela sits only 25 km away across a shallow strait, and Venezuelan influence is layered through the whole kitchen. Smuggling, ranching and fishing ran the economy for two and a half centuries, and the cooking that emerged was a true creole braid: Caquetio cornmeal and cassava traditions, Dutch dairy and bread, Spanish-Venezuelan sofrito and stews, West African one-pot technique, all working together.

The signature dish, keshi yena (literally "filled cheese"), is a remarkable colonial-era invention. Enslaved cooks in Dutch households scooped out a hollowed Edam or Gouda rind, stuffed it with spiced meat (often the leftover chicken or beef from the masters' table), and baked it. The dish became a national symbol of how the people made magic from castoffs, and a kind of culinary statement: take the cheese rind the masters threw away, fill it with raisins, olives, capers and seasoned meat, and produce something more interesting than what was on the master's plate. Variations of the technique exist on Curaçao and Bonaire too, but Aruba claims it most insistently.

The 19th and early 20th centuries were rough. Slavery in the Dutch colonies was abolished in 1863. Aruba scraped along on aloe-vera production, phosphate mining and salt until the Lago oil refinery opened at San Nicolas in 1924 and transformed everything. The refinery (a Standard Oil subsidiary) imported workers from across the British Caribbean, the US and Europe, and the southern town of San Nicolas became a melting-pot enclave with English, Spanish, Papiamento and a dozen other languages on the streets. The refinery years embedded curry-and-roti from the British West Indian workers, American hot dogs and hamburgers from the US managers, and Latin American salsa from the Venezuelan and Colombian arrivals. The post-1985 closure of the refinery hurt, but tourism (which had been growing since the 1960s) took over as the main economy.

Spanish, Dutch and Papiamento all sit on the island. Papiamento, the official creole language, braids Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch, English and African languages, and the cuisine carries the same hybridity. Today's Aruban table reads as a 400-year layered Caribbean creole: stoba di cabritu (goat stew) with funchi (cornmeal mash) at home, keshi yena for celebrations, pastechi (the fried pastry) for breakfast, fresh fish from the reef for dinner, and a Balashi beer or a glass of Venezuelan rum to finish.

## Regional variations

Aruba is small (180 km²) and the cuisine is broadly consistent. Sub-traditions are by community:

- **Oranjestad and the south**: codified mainstream. The cosmopolitan capital, tourist-influenced.
- **San Nicolas (south-east)**: more African-Caribbean, the more traditional shelf.
- **Noord (north)**: more rural, traditional family cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cheese (Edam, Gouda)**: in keshi yena.
- **Goat meat**: in stoba di cabritu.
- **Pumpkin**: in pumpkin pancakes (pan bati di pampuna), in stews.
- **Pisca (fish)**: in sopi di pisca.
- **Olives and capers**: in keshi yena filling.
- **Hot pepper**: Madame Janette is the local variety.
- **Coriander, basil, oregano**: the herb base.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Keshi Yena (Aruban)](/recipes/cuisine/aruban/keshi-yena-aruban)**: hollowed Edam cheese filled with spiced chicken or beef, raisins, olives and capers, baked till the cheese melts around the filling. The national dish.
2. **[Stoba di Cabritu](/recipes/cuisine/aruban/stoba-di-cabritu)**: goat slow-stewed with onion, tomato, garlic and hot pepper. Served with funchi (cornmeal mash).
3. **[Karni Stoba (Aruban)](/recipes/cuisine/aruban/karni-stoba-aruban)**: beef stew with raisins, olives and capers, the daily home cooking.
4. **[Sopi di Pisca](/recipes/cuisine/aruban/sopi-di-pisca)**: fish soup with potato, sweet potato, plantain and Madame Janette pepper.
5. **[Pasthechi (Aruba)](/recipes/cuisine/aruban/pasthechi-aruba)**: half-moon fried pastries filled with cheese, ground beef or chicken. The Aruban empanada.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Balashi beer (the national lager) or with ponche crema (the Venezuelan-influenced eggnog-style liqueur). End with pan bati (sweet cornmeal pancake) or with quesillo (the dense flan-style custard).
