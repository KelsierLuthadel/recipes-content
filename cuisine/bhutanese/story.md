# Bhutanese

*The Himalayan kingdom's kitchen. Built around chillies (fresh chillies treated as vegetables, not seasonings), the most-loved cheese-and-chilli stew (ema datshi) as the national dish, dried yak meat from the high pastures, and a fierce hand on the heat that catches outsiders by surprise.*

## A short history

Bhutanese cooking is the food of a high-altitude Buddhist kingdom that ranges from 100m subtropical south to 7,000m+ Himalayan north. The basic shelf has been the working kitchen for centuries: red rice (Bhutan's endemic variety, growing at high altitude), chillies (treated as a vegetable, eaten by the bowlful, never sparingly), datshi (sour homemade cow or yak cheese), dried meat, butter (in tea, in everything), and the deep yak-and-dairy tradition of the high pastures.

The earliest cooks in these valleys were yak herders and barley farmers who carried Tibetan techniques south across the high passes. From the first millennium onwards, the kitchen ran on what the altitude allowed: barley toasted and ground into tsampa, butter churned from yak milk and salted for tea, meat air-dried in the thin cold air to keep through winter, and buckwheat in the central valleys where rice would not grow. Salt arrived on yak-trains from the Tibetan plateau, traded for southern grain. The dairy bowl, the butter churn and the open hearth still anchor every traditional Bhutanese home.

The 8th century brought Vajrayana Buddhism with Padmasambhava, and the cuisine bent around it. Vegetarian leanings, the sacredness of animals limiting (though never abolishing) meat, and the cheese and dairy from the herding economy all trace back to the monastic kitchens. Monasteries became the keepers of fermentation knowledge, of butter tea ritual, and of the slow-cooked vegetable dishes that fed the lay community on holy days. The kitchen is closer to Tibetan than to Indian; the borders of taste run with the mountain passes, not the modern map.

The chilli came late, sometime after the 16th century, carried up from India where the Portuguese had landed it from the Americas. Bhutan adopted it more thoroughly than almost any country on earth. At high altitude, where vegetables struggle and winters are long, the chilli proved hardy, productive and storable (strung in bunches on every farmhouse roof to sun-dry). Within a few generations it stopped being a spice and became a vegetable, eaten by the handful rather than the pinch. Ema datshi, the green chilli and cheese stew that is now the national dish, almost certainly took its modern shape only in the last 200 years, but it feels older than the kingdom.

Bhutan unified as a state under Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal in the 17th century, and the monarchy that followed kept the country deliberately isolated. The 20th-century opening was cautious, and the world's strictest tourism policies (high-value, low-volume) still apply. The cuisine stays traditional at home, and the diaspora is small. It's the most distinctive Himalayan kitchen the world has, still cooked over the open hearth in many villages, still measured in chillies rather than grams.

## Regional variations

- **Western Bhutan (Thimphu, Paro)**: codified mainstream. Ema datshi, jasha maru.
- **Central (Bumthang)**: buckwheat country. Khule (buckwheat pancakes), distinctive cheese tradition.
- **East (Trashigang)**: more sweet potato, distinct chilli traditions.
- **Southern (Phuentsholing)**: closer to Indian-Nepali cooking. More vegetables, more rice.

## Signature ingredients

- **Chillies (fresh, dried, sun-dried)**: not a seasoning, a vegetable. The defining ingredient.
- **Datshi**: sour cow or yak cheese. The base of many stews.
- **Red rice**: Bhutan's endemic short-grain red rice.
- **Yak meat (sha)**: dried, fresh, in shapta.
- **Butter**: in suja (butter tea, salt-and-butter-mixed tea).
- **Buckwheat**: in central Bhutan.
- **Sichuan peppercorns**: in some highland dishes.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Ema Datshi](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/ema-datshi)**: green chillies (lots of them, treated as the main ingredient) stewed with datshi cheese and onion. The national dish. The chillies are the vegetable, not the spice.
2. **[Kewa Datshi](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/kewa-datshi)**: potatoes with datshi cheese and dried chilli. The everyday plate.
3. **[Shamu Datshi](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/shamu-datshi)**: mushrooms with datshi cheese.
4. **[Jasha Maru](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/jasha-maru)**: chicken stew with tomato, onion, ginger and dried chilli, served over red rice.
5. **[Phaksha Paa](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/phaksha-paa)**: pork (often dried) slow-stewed with daikon radish and dried red chillies.
6. **[Shakam Paa](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/shakam-paa)**: dried beef (yak in the highlands) with dried chillies and radish.
7. **[Jaju](/recipes/cuisine/bhutanese/jaju)**: simple chicken-and-spinach soup with milk. The mild option amid the chilli-rich plates.

## Where to go next

Drink suja (butter-and-salt tea, Tibetan-style) or ara (the local rice or wheat spirit). Eat with the right hand from a wooden bowl, sitting cross-legged on a cushion. That's the proper Bhutanese meal.
