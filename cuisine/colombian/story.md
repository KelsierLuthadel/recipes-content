# Colombian

*The cuisine of the Andes, the Caribbean coast, the Pacific and the Amazon all on one plate. Built on corn, beans, plantain and slow-cooked meats, with regional kitchens that read like different countries pretending to be one.*

## A short history

Colombian cooking begins on the high savannas around Bogotá and along the Caribbean and Pacific coasts, where indigenous people farmed maize, cassava, potato, beans, squash, peanuts and tropical fruit for thousands of years before Europeans arrived. The Muisca, who held the high cold Cundiboyacense plateau, cultivated more than a hundred kinds of potato, ate corn as a daily staple, drank chicha (fermented maize beer) and gave the world the modern tomato's wild relatives. The Tairona of the Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta terraced the mountainsides and farmed cocoa in the foothills, and the various Amazonian peoples of the southern lowlands brought cassava bread (casabe), the long-pepper shelf, and the leaf-wrapped cooking technique that became the tamale and the bocachico-leaf parcels still eaten on the Magdalena river.

The Spanish reached the Caribbean coast in 1499 and founded Cartagena in 1533. The conquest opened a single-direction Atlantic trade: gold and silver out, pork, beef, cattle, rice, wheat, sugar cane, citrus, olive oil and onion in. The Catholic feast calendar reshaped the eating year (lechona for celebrations, tamales for Christmas, ajiaco for Sundays). Cattle ranching took over the Llanos and the Caribbean savanna, and beef became the protein of the cowboy interior. Wheat never quite displaced corn, and the arepa survived as the daily bread.

Cartagena became one of the busiest slave ports in the Americas, and from the 1530s to the early 1800s hundreds of thousands of enslaved West and Central Africans arrived in Colombia. They were taken to the gold mines of the Pacific Chocó, the sugar plantations of the Cauca valley, and the cattle ranches of the Caribbean. They brought plantain (originally from Southeast Asia but routed through Africa), coconut, okra, the slow-cooked viscera tradition, the rice-and-bean shelf and the funked-up coconut rice that defines the coastal cuisine. The Pacific Chocó retained more African cooking traditions than anywhere else in the country, including the leaf-wrapped fish parcels, the coconut-base stew (encocado), and the use of the chontaduro peach palm fruit that is still sold from buckets on every Cali street corner.

The 19th and 20th centuries were shaped by coffee. Colombia planted its first commercial coffee in the 1830s, and by 1900 the country was one of the world's three biggest exporters. Coffee built the Antioquian middle class around Medellín, and the paisa region's famously gigantic lunch (the bandeja paisa with beans, rice, ground beef, fried egg, plantain, chicharrón, chorzo, avocado and arepa, all on one plate) emerged from the calorie needs of coffee-farm and muleteer work. Levantine immigration from Syria and Lebanon in the early 20th century planted kibbeh and stuffed grape leaves on the Caribbean coast (you still find them in Barranquilla and Maicao). Chinese contract labour built the Pacific railway and added the salteado stir-fry technique to the south-western kitchen.

The food stayed local for a long time. The internal armed conflict (roughly 1964 to 2016) kept Colombia off the tourist map and the kitchen out of the international story. The peace process opened the country, and Bogotá's restaurant scene (Leo, El Chato, Mini-Mal) now sits in the same conversation as Lima and Mexico City. Leo Espinosa's work cataloguing Colombia's biodiverse ingredient larder (more endemic fruits than almost any country on earth) has put a new spotlight on indigenous and Afro-Colombian cooking. Colombian cooking is one of South America's most exciting kitchens, and most of the catalogue is still being written outside the country.

## Regional variations

- **Cundiboyacense (Bogotá, Boyacá)**: the cold-weather Andean kitchen. Ajiaco (the chicken-potato-corn-guasca soup), changua (milk and egg breakfast), tamales, hearty stews.
- **Antioquia (Medellín, Paisa region)**: bandeja paisa country. Beans, rice, fried egg, plantain, chicharrón, chorizo, avocado, all on one plate. The most calorific lunch in South America.
- **Caribbean coast (Cartagena, Barranquilla)**: coconut rice, fried fish, arepas de huevo, the bollo-and-suero breakfast. African and Caribbean influences strongest here.
- **Pacific coast (Chocó, Buenaventura)**: the most distinctive regional kitchen. Coconut-base fish stews, the sudado tradition, more African retention than anywhere else in the country.
- **Valle del Cauca (Cali)**: Pacific-Andean fusion. Sancocho, lulada (lulo fruit drink), aborrajado (fried plantain with cheese).
- **Llanos (eastern plains)**: cowboy country. Mamona (whole calf roasted on a stake over open fire), beef, cassava, the carne llanera tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Arepa**: the corn cake. Every region has its own: arepa de queso (Antioquia), arepa de huevo (coast), arepa boyacense (sweet, anise).
- **Plantain**: green for patacones (twice-fried), ripe for tajadas or maduros (sweet, soft).
- **Guasca**: a herb in the daisy family, essential for ajiaco. Almost impossible to find outside Colombia.
- **Ají**: the spicy table salsa, ají de aguacate, ají picante, ají amarillo. Colombian cooking isn't spicy on the plate but the ají brings the heat.
- **Hogao**: tomato-onion-scallion-garlic sofrito. The mother sauce of Colombian cooking.
- **Panela**: unrefined cane sugar in solid blocks. The sweetener and the spine of Colombian drinks.
- **Achiote (annatto)**: red colourant from the Caribbean and Pacific kitchens.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the soup tradition, the bean-and-rice shelf, the corn cake and the slow roast. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Ajiaco](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/ajiaco)**: chicken and three kinds of potato simmered with guasca and corn, finished with cream, capers and avocado. Bogotá's national dish and the most distinct soup in South America.
2. **[Bandeja Paisa](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/bandeja-paisa)**: the platter. Beans, rice, ground beef, fried egg, plantain, chicharrón, chorizo, avocado, arepa. Antioquia's signature, and the most maximalist plate in the catalogue.
3. **[Sancocho de Gallina](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/sancocho-de-gallina)**: chicken-and-everything soup with cassava, plantain, corn, potato. The Sunday family dish in every region, with regional variations on the protein.
4. **[Arepas de Queso](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/arepas-de-queso)**: white-corn cakes stuffed with cheese, griddled till the cheese melts and the outside crisps. The most-eaten food in the country at any hour.
5. **[Lechona](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/lechona)**: whole roasted pig stuffed with rice, peas and herbs, slow-cooked overnight. The Tolima region's grand celebration plate.
6. **[Bistec a la Criolla](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/bistec-a-la-criolla)**: thin beef steak smothered in hogao (tomato-onion sauce). The everyday lunch plate in every Bogotá fonda.
7. **[Cazuela de Mariscos](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/cazuela-de-mariscos)**: coconut-base seafood stew with prawn, fish, mussel, calamari. The Caribbean coast's signature one-pot.

Cook the ajiaco and the sancocho and you have the soup shelf. The bandeja and the bistec are the Antioquia and Bogotá tables. The arepa and the lechona are the celebration plates. The cazuela is the coast.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the bofe (cow's lung) tradition, the chuzos (skewer) shelf, more arepa varieties (de huevo, boyacense), the tamale (regional, dozens of styles), and the breakfast canon (changua, calentado, huevos pericos). Colombian food is a regional cuisine pretending to be national. Pick a corner and dig in.
