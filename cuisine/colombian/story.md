# Colombian

*The cuisine of the Andes, the Caribbean coast, the Pacific and the Amazon all on one plate. Built on corn, beans, plantain and slow-cooked meats, with regional kitchens that read like different countries pretending to be one.*

## A short history

Colombian cooking sits on three roots: the indigenous Muisca, Tairona, and Amazonian peoples (corn, cassava, potato, beans, tropical fruits); the Spanish colonisers (pork, beef, dairy, wheat, rice, the Catholic feast calendar); and the African enslaved population brought through the Pacific and Caribbean coasts (coconut, plantain, the slow-fried bofe tradition, the funked-up rice). The country's vertical geography — coast at sea level, Bogotá at 2,640m, the Sierra at 5,000m — produced regional cuisines so distinct that a Caribbean Colombian and a Bogotá Colombian often eat as if from different countries.

The cuisine was poorly known internationally until the 21st century. The internal armed conflict (roughly 1964–2016) made Colombia a difficult place to visit, and the food story stayed local. The peace process opened the country to tourism, and Bogotá's restaurant scene (Leo, El Chato, Mini-Mal) became a regional reference point alongside Lima and Mexico City. Today Colombian cooking is one of South America's most exciting kitchens, with most of the catalogue still to be written outside.

## Regional variations

- **Cundiboyacense (Bogotá, Boyacá)**: the cold-weather Andean kitchen. Ajiaco (the chicken-potato-corn-guasca soup), changua (milk and egg breakfast), tamales, hearty stews.
- **Antioquia (Medellín, Paisa region)**: bandeja paisa country. Beans, rice, fried egg, plantain, chicharrón, chorizo, avocado, all on one plate. The most calorific lunch in South America.
- **Caribbean coast (Cartagena, Barranquilla)**: coconut rice, fried fish, arepas de huevo, the bollo-and-suero breakfast. African and Caribbean influences strongest here.
- **Pacific coast (Chocó, Buenaventura)**: the most distinctive regional kitchen. Coconut-base fish stews, the sudado tradition, more African retention than anywhere else in the country.
- **Valle del Cauca (Cali)**: Pacific-Andean fusion. Sancocho, lulada (lulo fruit drink), aborrajado (fried plantain with cheese).
- **Llanos (eastern plains)**: cowboy country. Mamona (whole calf roasted on a stake over open fire), beef, cassava, the carne llanera tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Arepa**: the corn cake. Every region has its own version — arepa de queso (Antioquia), arepa de huevo (coast), arepa boyacense (sweet, anise).
- **Plantain**: green for patacones (twice-fried), ripe for tajadas or maduros (sweet, soft).
- **Guasca**: a herb in the daisy family, essential for ajiaco. Almost impossible to find outside Colombia.
- **Ají**: the spicy table salsa — ají de aguacate, ají picante, ají amarillo. Colombian cuisine isn't spicy on the plate but the ají brings the heat.
- **Hogao**: tomato-onion-scallion-garlic sofrito. The mother sauce of Colombian cooking.
- **Panela**: unrefined cane sugar in solid blocks. The sweetener and the spine of Colombian drinks.
- **Achiote (annatto)**: red colourant from the Caribbean and Pacific kitchens.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the soup tradition, the bean-and-rice shelf, the corn cake and the slow roast. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Ajiaco](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/ajiaco)**: chicken and three kinds of potato simmered with guasca and corn, finished with cream, capers and avocado. Bogotá's national dish and the most distinct soup in South America.
2. **[Bandeja Paisa](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/bandeja-paisa)**: the platter — beans, rice, ground beef, fried egg, plantain, chicharrón, chorizo, avocado, arepa. Antioquia's signature, and the most maximalist plate in the catalogue.
3. **[Sancocho de Gallina](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/sancocho-de-gallina)**: chicken-and-everything soup with cassava, plantain, corn, potato. The Sunday family dish in every region, with regional variations on the protein.
4. **[Arepas de Queso](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/arepas-de-queso)**: white-corn cakes stuffed with cheese, griddled till the cheese melts and the outside crisps. The most-eaten food in the country at any hour.
5. **[Lechona](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/lechona)**: whole roasted pig stuffed with rice, peas and herbs, slow-cooked overnight. The Tolima region's grand celebration plate.
6. **[Bistec a la Criolla](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/bistec-a-la-criolla)**: thin beef steak smothered in hogao (tomato-onion sauce). The everyday lunch plate in every Bogotá fonda.
7. **[Cazuela de Mariscos](/recipes/cuisine/colombian/cazuela-de-mariscos)**: coconut-base seafood stew with prawn, fish, mussel, calamari. The Caribbean coast's signature one-pot.

Cook the ajiaco and the sancocho and you have the soup shelf. The bandeja and the bistec are the Antioquia and Bogotá tables. The arepa and the lechona are the celebration plates. The cazuela is the coast.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the bofe (cow's lung) tradition, the chuzos (skewer) shelf, more arepa varieties (de huevo, boyacense), the tamale (regional, dozens of styles), and the breakfast canon (changua, calentado, huevos pericos). Colombian food is a regional cuisine pretending to be national; pick a corner and dig in.
