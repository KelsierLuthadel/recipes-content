# Mozambican

*The kitchen of the East African Lusophone coast. Peri-piri prawns straight from the Indian Ocean, the Portuguese-African fusion that put coconut milk into chicken curry, and the matata seafood-and-peanut stew that defines the country.*

## A short history

Mozambican cooking starts with the Bantu migrations that reached the southern African coast over two thousand years ago. The Macua, Tsonga, Sena and Shona peoples who settled the long Indian Ocean shoreline farmed sorghum and millet, fished the reefs and estuaries, and built a slow-stewed one-pot kitchen around leafy greens, groundnuts and palm oil. Cassava and maize arrived later but folded in completely, and xima (a stiff maize porridge eaten with stew) is still the everyday plate inland.

The Swahili trading centuries (roughly 800 to 1500) layered the next history onto the coast. Arab and Persian dhows worked the monsoon winds between the Persian Gulf, India and East Africa, and ports like Sofala and Ilha de Moçambique became cosmopolitan trade towns long before the Europeans arrived. The trade brought coconut, rice, cloves, cardamom, ginger and the heavy use of lime that still distinguishes coastal Mozambican cooking from the interior. Matata (clams, peanuts and pumpkin leaves in coconut milk) carries the whole Swahili coast in one bowl.

Vasco da Gama landed at Mozambique Island in 1498 and Portugal claimed the territory in 1505. The colonial centuries (1505 to 1975) reshaped the kitchen permanently. Portuguese settlers brought wheat bread, olive oil, salt cod, wine, garlic, onions and a serious pastry tradition. They also carried the Columbian-Exchange crops they had picked up across the empire: tomatoes, the bird's-eye chilli that became peri-piri, the cashew tree (now Mozambique's signature nut), and maize from Brazil. The peri-piri marinade of crushed chilli, garlic, lemon and oil came together on this coast and travelled back through Portuguese kitchens worldwide.

Indian Ocean trade also brought a steady Goan and Indian presence. Coconut curries, the use of turmeric and cumin, samosas and the spiced fish dishes of the northern provinces all trace to that traffic. Peri-piri chicken (chicken butterflied flat, marinated in the chilli paste, grilled hard over open coals) became the national signature, exported worldwide through the Portuguese-Mozambican chain Nando's. At home you eat it on the beach with shrimp, cold beer and the slow Indian Ocean breeze.

The independence war (1964 to 1974) and the long civil war that followed (1977 to 1992) wrecked the economy and limited the cuisine internationally. Diaspora kitchens in Lisbon, Johannesburg and London kept the tradition alive through those decades. Since the 2000s the tourism economy along the Bazaruto and Quirimbas archipelagos has rebuilt the coastal restaurant scene, and a new generation of Maputo chefs has started codifying a confident modern Mozambican cooking that sits proudly between Africa, Portugal and the Indian Ocean.

## Regional variations

- **Maputo and the south**: the codified mainstream. Peri-piri prawns, matata, the urban restaurant tradition.
- **Beira and central**: more rural, more cassava-and-corn-based.
- **Quelimane and Zambezi**: galinha à zambeziana (the regional peri-piri chicken).
- **North (Nampula, Cabo Delgado)**: stronger Swahili-Indian influence. More coconut, more curry.

## Signature ingredients

- **Peri-piri**: African bird's-eye chilli, used in marinades and table sauces.
- **Coconut milk**: in matata, in fish curries.
- **Cashews**: ground into sauces, eaten as snacks.
- **Prawns and seafood**: Indian Ocean catch.
- **Cassava and maize**: the universal starches.
- **Palm oil and peanut**: in matata.
- **Lemon**: the acid in peri-piri marinades.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Frango Piri-Piri](/recipes/cuisine/mozambique/frango-piri-piri)**: chicken butterflied flat, marinated in peri-piri, lemon, garlic and oil, grilled over coals. The national dish.
2. **[Camarão Grelhado](/recipes/cuisine/mozambique/camarao-grelhado)**: tiger prawns marinated in peri-piri and grilled. The beach classic.
3. **[Matata](/recipes/cuisine/mozambique/matata)**: clams and prawns slow-cooked with peanuts and pumpkin leaves in coconut milk. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Galinha à Zambeziana](/recipes/cuisine/mozambique/galinha-zambeziana)**: Zambezi-style chicken with coconut milk, peri-piri and lime. The regional take.
5. **[Peixe Grelhado](/recipes/cuisine/mozambique/peixe-grelhado)**: grilled whole Indian Ocean fish (often kingfish or red snapper) with peri-piri and lime.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with 2M (Dois M, the national lager) or with vinho verde. End with bolo polana (cashew-and-potato cake), the Maputo signature dessert. For a proper coastal evening: peri-piri prawns, cold beer, the sun setting over the Indian Ocean.
