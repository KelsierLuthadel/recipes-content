# Syrian

*The kitchen of Damascus and Aleppo, two of the world's oldest continuously-inhabited cities and both legendary food capitals. Pomegranate, sumac, lamb, yoghurt, the deep mezze table, and a sweet-pastry tradition that produced baklava and kanafeh in their definitive forms.*

## A short history

Syrian cooking sits on the oldest agricultural ground on earth. The Fertile Crescent, running from the Levant up through Mesopotamia, is where humans first domesticated wheat, barley, lentils, chickpeas, sheep and goats around 10,000 BCE. The bulgur-and-lamb axis that still defines Syrian food (kibbeh, kebab, mansaf, the lamb-and-grain pilafs) is the direct descendant of that Neolithic pantry. Damascus and Aleppo were already old cities when the Babylonians and Assyrians fought over them; both have been continuously inhabited for over five thousand years, and the food culture has had every one of those millennia to refine itself.

The Phoenician and Greek traders who worked the Syrian coast from the 8th century BCE introduced olive oil, wine and the press technology behind both. Rome and Byzantium followed, leaving the bread oven, the cured-meat tradition (basterma, the air-dried spiced beef, has Byzantine roots), and the Levantine love of sour-sweet flavour combinations that the rest of the Mediterranean would later borrow. The Arab conquest of the 7th century made Damascus the capital of the Umayyad Caliphate from 661 to 750; the city's kitchens became the testing ground for the cooking that would spread across the Islamic world. The early Arabic cookbooks (Ibn Sayyar al-Warraq in 10th-century Baghdad, then Aleppo's own Ibn al-Adim in the 13th century with his *Kitab al-Wusla ila al-Habib*) recorded recipes that are still cookable today: stuffed vegetables, slow-cooked meat in fruit-and-nut sauces, sugar-syrup pastries, rose-water sweets.

The medieval centuries layered Persian, Byzantine, Turkic, Mongol and Crusader influences over the Arab base, and Syrian cooking absorbed them all. The Mongols (13th century) brought yoghurt-cooking techniques and the use of dried fruit in savoury dishes; Persian poets-and-cooks brought rose water, orange-blossom water and the saffron-rice pilaf; Aleppo's position on the Silk Road overland made it the wealthiest food city in the eastern Mediterranean by the 1400s. Aleppine kibbeh alone exists in dozens of regional variants codified in this period: kibbeh nayyeh (raw), kibbeh bil sanieh (baked), kibbeh bil laban (in yoghurt), kibbeh karaz (in sour-cherry sauce). The Ottoman conquest in 1516 unified the cooking of Syria with the wider empire, but the Aleppine and Damascene kitchens kept their distinct identity and were widely considered the most refined Arab traditions throughout the Ottoman period.

Aleppo in particular held the title of food capital of the Arab world right up to the 2011 war. The city's cooking (slow-cooked lamb, the kibbeh tradition in dozens of regional variants, sour-cherry kebab, muhammara the red pepper-walnut dip, and the Aleppine baklava) was the most refined Arabic cuisine going. The war and the resulting diaspora carried Syrian food worldwide; the diaspora kitchens in Berlin, Istanbul, Toronto, Detroit and across the Gulf are now the keepers of the codified Aleppine tradition. Damascus, less devastated than Aleppo, kept its restaurants and home cooking running, and shawarma was perfected here: the city's grills are the international standard, and the meze tradition (kibbeh, hummus, baba ghanoush, tabbouleh, fattoush, muhammara, labneh) is the most elaborate in the Levant.

The diaspora is now the global story. Syrian refugees and earlier emigrants built communities in Buenos Aires, São Paulo, Berlin, Stockholm, Detroit, Sydney and across the Gulf; everywhere they went, the kitchen came with them. Salma Hage, Anissa Helou and a generation of younger Syrian cookbook authors have done for the cuisine internationally what the war disrupted at home, and Syrian bakeries and restaurants are now among the fastest-growing food categories in European cities. The recipes that survived five thousand years on this ground will survive this century too.

## Regional variations

- **Damascus and south**: the codified mainstream. Shawarma, kibbeh, the deep meze table.
- **Aleppo (north)**: the most refined regional cuisine. Cherry kebab, muhammara, kebab karaz, the Aleppine baklava style.
- **Coast (Latakia, Tartus)**: more seafood, more Greek-Mediterranean influence.
- **Damascus countryside (Ghouta)**: orchard country: apricots, walnuts, the agricultural tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pomegranate molasses**: thick, sour-sweet. In muhammara, in stews, in dressings.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry. The signature seasoning.
- **Tahini**: ground sesame paste. Base of hummus and many sauces.
- **Sour cherries**: dried, in Aleppine kebab karaz; in jam.
- **Aleppo pepper**: medium-hot dried red flakes; the colour and warmth of Aleppine dishes.
- **Bulgur**: cracked wheat. In kibbeh, in tabbouleh.
- **Mint, parsley, coriander**: the herb trinity.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Shawarma Damascene](/recipes/cuisine/syria/shawarma-damascene)**: lamb (or chicken) marinated overnight in yoghurt, garlic, lemon and seven spices, stacked on a vertical spit, shaved into flatbread with tahini, pickle and tomato. The Damascus standard.
2. **[Kabsa Syrian](/recipes/cuisine/syria/kabsa-syrian)**: spiced rice with lamb or chicken; the Syrian take on the broader Arabian kabsa.
3. **[Kibbeh bil Laban](/recipes/cuisine/syria/kibbeh-bil-laban)**: kibbeh (lamb-and-bulgur croquettes) in warm yoghurt sauce.
4. **[Mahshi Warak](/recipes/cuisine/syria/mahshi-warak)**: vine leaves stuffed with rice and minced lamb, simmered with lemon. The Syrian dolma.
5. **[Shish Barak](/recipes/cuisine/syria/shish-barak)**: small meat-stuffed dumplings in yoghurt-and-mint sauce.
6. **[Yakhneh Bamia](/recipes/cuisine/syria/yakhneh-bamia)**: lamb-and-okra stew with tomato and pomegranate molasses.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with ayran (yoghurt drink) or with strong Turkish-style coffee. End with kanafeh (the cheese-stuffed semolina-and-syrup dessert; the original is Nabulsi or Aleppine, the city of Nablus across the border in Palestine claims it, both are right). Or with baklava: Aleppine pistachio is the world benchmark.
