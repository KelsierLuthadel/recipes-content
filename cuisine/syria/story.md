# Syrian

*The kitchen of Damascus and Aleppo — two of the world's oldest continuously-inhabited cities, both legendary food capitals. Pomegranate, sumac, lamb, yoghurt, the deep mezze table, and a sweet-pastry tradition that produced baklava and kanafeh in their definitive forms.*

## A short history

Syrian cooking is the most refined of the Levantine traditions. Damascus and Aleppo were among the great cities of the Islamic Golden Age; their kitchens drew on Persian, Byzantine, Arab, Mongol and Ottoman influences over a thousand years and refined them into a cuisine that's been the regional benchmark since.

Aleppo in particular was considered the food capital of the Arab world before the 2011 war. The city's cuisine — slow-cooked lamb, the kibbeh tradition in dozens of regional variants, sour-cherry kebab, the muhammara (red pepper-walnut dip), and the Aleppine baklava — was the most refined Arabic cooking. The war and the resulting diaspora carried Syrian food worldwide; the diaspora kitchens in Berlin, Istanbul, Toronto and across the Gulf are now the keepers of the codified Aleppine tradition.

Damascus, less affected by the war than Aleppo, maintained its restaurants and home cooking. Shawarma was perfected here (the city's grills are the international standard); the meze tradition (kibbeh, hummus, baba ghanoush, tabbouleh, fattoush, muhammara, labneh) is the most elaborate in the Levant.

## Regional variations

- **Damascus and south**: the codified mainstream. Shawarma, kibbeh, the deep meze table.
- **Aleppo (north)**: the most refined regional cuisine. Cherry kebab, muhammara, kebab karaz, the Aleppine baklava style.
- **Coast (Latakia, Tartus)**: more seafood, more Greek-Mediterranean influence.
- **Damascus countryside (Ghouta)**: agricultural — apricots, walnuts, the orchard tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pomegranate molasses**: thick, sour-sweet. In muhammara, in stews, in dressings.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry. The signature seasoning.
- **Tahini**: ground sesame paste. Base of hummus and many sauces.
- **Sour cherries**: dried, in Aleppine kebab karaz; in jam.
- **Aleppo pepper**: medium-hot dried red flakes; the colour and warmth of Aleppine dishes.
- **Bulgur**: cracked wheat. In kibbeh, in tabbouleh.
- **Mint, parsley, coriander**: the herb trinity.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Shawarma Damascene](/recipes/cuisine/syria/shawarma-damascene)**: lamb (or chicken) marinated overnight in yoghurt, garlic, lemon and seven spices, stacked on a vertical spit, shaved into flatbread with tahini, pickle and tomato. The Damascus standard.
2. **[Kabsa Syrian](/recipes/cuisine/syria/kabsa-syrian)**: spiced rice with lamb or chicken — the Syrian take on the broader Arabian kabsa.
3. **[Kibbeh bil Laban](/recipes/cuisine/syria/kibbeh-bil-laban)**: kibbeh (lamb-and-bulgur croquettes) in warm yoghurt sauce.
4. **[Mahshi Warak](/recipes/cuisine/syria/mahshi-warak)**: vine leaves stuffed with rice and minced lamb, simmered with lemon. The Syrian dolma.
5. **[Shish Barak](/recipes/cuisine/syria/shish-barak)**: small meat-stuffed dumplings in yoghurt-and-mint sauce.
6. **[Yakhneh Bamia](/recipes/cuisine/syria/yakhneh-bamia)**: lamb-and-okra stew with tomato and pomegranate molasses.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with ayran (yoghurt drink) or with strong Turkish-style coffee. End with kanafeh (the cheese-stuffed semolina-and-syrup dessert — the original is Nabulsi/Aleppine; the city of Nablus across the border in Palestine claims it; both are right). Or with baklava — Aleppine pistachio is the world benchmark.
