# New York

*The world's most concentrated immigration kitchen. Italian-American pizza, Jewish-American deli, the chopped salad lunch, the bodega bacon-egg-and-cheese, and the steakhouse: five boroughs that produced more cuisine identities than most countries.*

## A short history

New York's kitchen sits on Lenape ground. The Munsee-speaking Lenape peoples fished the Hudson and East River estuaries, dug oysters out of beds that lined the entire harbour (the original Pearl Street was paved with oyster shells), gathered the three sisters from the upland fields and ran controlled-burn deer drives across what is now Manhattan and Long Island. New York Harbor was the densest oyster ground on earth before the 20th-century pollution killed it; oysters were everyday food for everyone in the city right through the 1800s, and oyster cellars served them shucked, fried, stewed and roasted at every price point.

Dutch settlers founded New Amsterdam in 1624 and held it for forty years before the English took it and renamed it New York in 1664. The Dutch brought waffles, cookies (the word is Dutch), coleslaw, doughnuts and a tolerance for varied food traditions that became the city's defining habit. English colonists added the New England cooking shelf (clam chowder, pork-and-bean Saturdays). Enslaved and free Africans contributed deeply to the early city's food, from the oyster-shucking trade to the foundational cooking in colonial kitchens. The Columbian Exchange ran through New York harbour in both directions: tomatoes, peppers, potatoes and corn arrived; Old World grains, livestock and the kitchen of the new immigrants kept coming.

Then came the great immigration. Irish refugees from the 1840s famine landed in Lower Manhattan and brought corned beef (which the Jewish butchers down the block soon perfected). German immigrants from 1848 onward built the lager-and-sausage tradition that gave the city its bratwurst, sauerkraut and the original delicatessen format. Italians arrived from 1880 to 1920, mostly from Naples and Sicily; they built the pizza tradition (Lombardi's on Spring Street claims the first US pizzeria, 1905), the red sauce shelf, the espresso bar and the salumeria. Eastern European Jews from 1880 to 1924 built the deli tradition out of the Lower East Side. Katz's (1888), 2nd Avenue Deli, Carnegie, Russ & Daughters: the pastrami sandwich, the corned beef on rye, the matzo ball soup, the lox-and-bagel all came from those streets and shaped American sandwich culture forever.

The steakhouse is the third pillar. Peter Luger (Brooklyn, 1887), Keens (1885), Delmonico's (which opened in 1837 and gave the world the eponymous steak), Smith & Wollensky, Old Homestead: dry-aged porterhouse, German lager, hash browns. The format spread from New York to every American business district and remains the city's signature business-meal experience. Chinese immigrants opened the first chop suey houses on Mott Street in the 1880s, and Chinese-American food (chow mein, General Tso's, the fortune cookie) developed in New York and San Francisco in parallel through the 20th century.

The post-war waves rewrote the city again. Puerto Ricans arrived from the late 1940s and brought the rice-and-beans-and-pork tradition. The 1965 Immigration Act opened the doors more widely, and Dominicans, Mexicans, Bangladeshis, West Africans, Koreans, Sichuan Chinese and a hundred other communities each added their kitchen to the city. The bodega (the Spanish-language corner store with a flat-top grill) became the universal breakfast institution, where the bacon-egg-and-cheese on a kaiser roll feeds construction workers and finance bros for the same four dollars. The Halal Cart, run mostly by Egyptian and Bangladeshi vendors, gave the city the chicken-and-rice-with-white-sauce that is now its post-midnight food.

The 21st-century New York kitchen is the world's most concentrated immigration food scene, and the hyphenated cooking only deepens with each new wave. The pizza shops still open at 11 and close at 3am, the deli counters still slice pastrami to order, the steakhouses still age their porterhouse for 30 days, and somewhere in Queens right now a cook from a country most New Yorkers have never visited is opening a window onto the cuisine that will define the next decade.

## Regional variations

- **Manhattan**: the codified high cuisine. Steakhouse, Michelin-starred fine dining, the chopped salad lunch.
- **Brooklyn**: the bakery-and-pizzeria tradition. Coal-fired pizza (Grimaldi's, Patsy's, Roberta's), the bagel shops, the New American chef-driven scene of Williamsburg and Park Slope.
- **Bronx**: Arthur Avenue Italian-American kitchen, the original Little Italy.
- **Queens**: the world's most diverse food borough. Jackson Heights for Indian/Tibetan/Colombian; Flushing for Chinese; Astoria for Greek and Egyptian.
- **Staten Island**: Italian-American suburban shelf. Pork stores, family-style red sauce joints.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pastrami**: brined, dry-rubbed, smoked, steamed beef brisket. The deli backbone.
- **Bagels**: boiled then baked, with poppy seed, sesame, or "everything" topping. The breakfast staple.
- **Pizza dough**: high-hydration, hand-tossed, baked on a stone in 800°F+ ovens for about 2 minutes.
- **Italian-American "gravy"**: long-cooked tomato sauce with pork and sausage. The Sunday red sauce.
- **Lox and cream cheese**: cold-smoked salmon and schmear. The bagel pairing.
- **Halal Cart white sauce**: garlicky-mayo dressing on the universal chicken-and-rice plate.
- **NY tap water**: famously claimed to be the secret to the city's bagels and pizza dough; the chlorine-and-mineral balance is the urban legend.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the deli, the pizzeria, the steakhouse and the bodega. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[NY-Style Pizza](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/ny-style-pizza)**: thin, foldable hand-tossed crust with simple tomato sauce, fresh mozzarella and a drizzle of olive oil. The defining New York food.
2. **[Pastrami on Rye](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/pastrami-on-rye)**: hand-cut pastrami piled high on rye with yellow mustard, a half-sour pickle on the side. The Katz's standard.
3. **[NY Strip Steak](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/ny-strip-steak)**: dry-aged short loin, blackened in a cast iron pan or under a broiler, finished with butter. The steakhouse classic.
4. **[Manhattan Clam Chowder](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/manhattan-clam-chowder)**: tomato-based clam soup with vegetables, the contrarian alternative to New England's cream-based version. A defining East Coast culinary rivalry.
5. **[Eggs Benedict](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/eggs-benedict)**: poached eggs, Canadian bacon and hollandaise on a toasted English muffin. Invented in NYC (the Delmonico's or Waldorf-Astoria origin story is disputed).
6. **[Reuben Sandwich](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/reuben-sandwich)**: corned beef, Swiss cheese, sauerkraut and Russian dressing on toasted rye, griddled till the cheese melts. The deli sandwich's deli sandwich.
7. **[Buffalo Wings](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/buffalo-wings)**: chicken wings fried crisp, tossed in butter-Frank's-RedHot sauce, served with celery and blue cheese. Invented in Buffalo (upstate NY) at the Anchor Bar in 1964.

Cook the pizza and the pastrami sandwich and you have the two pillars. The Reuben and the eggs Benedict are the breakfast and lunch. The strip steak is dinner. The Manhattan chowder is contrarian; the Buffalo wings are upstate.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the bagel-and-lox shelf, the Halal Cart chicken-and-rice, the deli salads (egg salad, tuna salad), more pizza varieties (Sicilian, grandma), and the dessert tradition (cheesecake, cannoli, black-and-white cookie). Pair everything with a Brooklyn lager or a Manhattan cocktail and the cuisine sings.
