# New York

*The world's most concentrated immigration kitchen. Italian-American pizza, Jewish-American deli, the chopped salad lunch, the bodega bacon-egg-and-cheese, and the steakhouse — five boroughs that produced more cuisine identities than most countries.*

## A short history

New York food is the cuisine of immigration. Every wave of newcomers — Dutch and English first, then Irish (1840s famine), German (1840s-80s), Italian (1880-1920), Eastern European Jewish (1880-1924), Puerto Rican (post-WWII), Chinese, Dominican, Mexican, Bangladeshi, every other community since — landed in the city, opened a restaurant or a deli or a pushcart, and added their own kitchen to the New York mix. The food that emerged is hyphenated by default: Italian-American, Jewish-American, Chinese-American, Latino-American.

The deli tradition was built by Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe in the early 20th century. Katz's (1888), 2nd Avenue Deli, Carnegie, Russ & Daughters — the pastrami sandwich, the corned beef on rye, the matzo ball soup, the lox-and-bagel — all came out of the Lower East Side and shaped American sandwich culture forever. The pizza tradition was Italian-American — Lombardi's claims the first pizzeria in the US (1905); the New York-style thin crust, slightly chewy, foldable, hand-tossed pizza was perfected in the city's pizzerias through the 20th century.

The steakhouse is the third pillar. Peter Luger (Brooklyn, 1887), Keens (1885), Smith & Wollensky, Old Homestead — dry-aged porterhouse, German lager, hash browns. The steakhouse format spread from New York to every American business district and remains the city's signature business-meal experience.

The bodega is the fourth — the corner store with a flat-top grill, where the bacon-egg-and-cheese sandwich (BEC) was codified as the city's universal breakfast. Add Puerto Rican rice and beans, Dominican chimichurri (a different one from the Argentine), Mexican burritos, Halal Cart chicken and rice, and you have the everyday New York lunch.

## Regional variations

- **Manhattan**: the codified high cuisine — steakhouse, Michelin-starred fine dining, the chopped salad lunch.
- **Brooklyn**: the bakery-and-pizzeria tradition. Coal-fired pizza (Grimaldi's, Patsy's, Roberta's), the bagel shops, the New American chef-driven scene of Williamsburg and Park Slope.
- **Bronx**: Arthur Avenue Italian-American kitchen — the original Little Italy.
- **Queens**: the world's most diverse food borough. Jackson Heights for Indian/Tibetan/Colombian; Flushing for Chinese; Astoria for Greek and Egyptian.
- **Staten Island**: Italian-American suburban shelf — pork stores, family-style red sauce joints.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pastrami**: brined, dry-rubbed, smoked, steamed beef brisket. The deli backbone.
- **Bagels**: boiled then baked, with poppy seed, sesame, or "everything" topping. The breakfast staple.
- **Pizza dough**: high-hydration, hand-tossed, baked on a stone in 800°F+ ovens for ~2 minutes.
- **Italian-American "gravy"**: long-cooked tomato sauce with pork and sausage. The Sunday red sauce.
- **Lox and cream cheese**: cold-smoked salmon and schmear. The bagel pairing.
- **Halal Cart white sauce**: garlicky-mayo dressing on the universal chicken-and-rice plate.
- **NY tap water**: famously claimed to be the secret to the city's bagels and pizza dough; the chlorine-and-mineral balance is the urban legend.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the deli, the pizzeria, the steakhouse and the bodega. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[NY-Style Pizza](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/ny-style-pizza)**: thin, foldable hand-tossed crust with simple tomato sauce, fresh mozzarella and a drizzle of olive oil. The defining New York food.
2. **[Pastrami on Rye](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/pastrami-on-rye)**: hand-cut pastrami piled high on rye with yellow mustard, a half-sour pickle on the side. The Katz's standard.
3. **[NY Strip Steak](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/ny-strip-steak)**: dry-aged short loin, blackened in a cast iron pan or under a broiler, finished with butter. The steakhouse classic.
4. **[Manhattan Clam Chowder](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/manhattan-clam-chowder)**: tomato-based clam soup with vegetables — the contrarian alternative to New England's cream-based version. A defining East Coast culinary rivalry.
5. **[Eggs Benedict](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/eggs-benedict)**: poached eggs, Canadian bacon and hollandaise on a toasted English muffin. Invented in NYC (the Delmonico's or Waldorf-Astoria origin story is disputed).
6. **[Reuben Sandwich](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/reuben-sandwich)**: corned beef, Swiss cheese, sauerkraut and Russian dressing on toasted rye, griddled till the cheese melts. The deli sandwich's deli sandwich.
7. **[Buffalo Wings](/recipes/cuisine/new-york/buffalo-wings)**: chicken wings fried crisp, tossed in butter-Frank's-RedHot sauce, served with celery and blue cheese. Invented in Buffalo (upstate NY) at the Anchor Bar in 1964.

Cook the pizza and the pastrami sandwich and you have the two pillars. The Reuben and the eggs Benedict are the breakfast and lunch. The strip steak is dinner. The Manhattan chowder is contrarian; the Buffalo wings are upstate.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the bagel-and-lox shelf, the Halal Cart chicken-and-rice, the deli salads (egg salad, tuna salad), more pizza varieties (Sicilian, grandma), and the dessert tradition (cheesecake, cannoli, black-and-white cookie). Pair everything with a Brooklyn lager or a Manhattan cocktail and the cuisine sings.
