# Antiguan

*The kitchen of two small Caribbean islands. Fungie (cornmeal mash) as the universal starch, the slow-stewed pepperpot as the national dish, and the breadfruit-and-saltfish breakfast that's the same on Antigua as it is on Barbuda.*

## A short history

Antiguan and Barbudan cooking is the cuisine of a small island nation in the Eastern Caribbean, and the story begins long before any European ship arrived. The Arawak (Taino) people were settled on Antigua by around 1100 BCE, having canoed up the island chain from the Orinoco delta in northern South America. They grew cassava (yuca), maize, sweet potato, peppers and pineapple in conuco mound gardens, fished the reef with weirs and nets, gathered conch and lobster off the shoreline, and pressed bitter cassava into the flat round bread (cassava bread, the original Caribbean staple) that's still eaten across the region. The Kalinago (Caribs) arrived later from the south, and the two peoples held the islands when Columbus sighted Antigua in 1493 and named it after a Seville church.

Spanish, French and English ships circled the island for over a century, but European disease, raiding, and the harsh dry climate kept permanent settlement off until the British arrived in 1632 and set up the sugar plantations that defined the next two and a half centuries. Christopher Codrington built the first large estate in 1674, and by the early 18th century Antigua had over 150 sugar mills working African enslaved labour into one of the most profitable sugar islands in the Caribbean. The enslaved West African population (largely Akan and Igbo from the Bight of Benin) brought the cooking that survives today: okra, callaloo, the saltfish tradition (Atlantic salt cod traded north from the cod banks of New England and Newfoundland into the Caribbean as a fast, durable protein for plantation labour), the slow-cooked one-pot, the bush-tea herbal medicine shelf, and the pepperpot stew that's still the national dish.

The 18th and 19th centuries embedded two more crops that became Antiguan staples. Breadfruit was brought from Tahiti in 1793 to feed enslaved workers on Captain Bligh's second mission (the first attempt ended in the famous Mutiny on the Bounty in 1789, but Bligh successfully delivered the trees to St Vincent and Jamaica on the second try). Cornmeal arrived from the American mainland and got worked into fungie, the polenta-like cornmeal-and-okra mash that pairs with everything. Sugar, salt, molasses, rum and salt cod ran in and out of the harbour at St John's. Slavery ended in 1834. The plantation economy continued in shrunken form, and the kitchen kept evolving on its African-Caribbean spine with British colonial inputs (Christmas cake, tea, the curry-and-rice tradition learned from Indian indentured workers across the wider Caribbean).

The 20th century brought the long road to independence. Britain finally let Antigua and Barbuda go in 1981, and independence codified the national dishes. Fungie and pepperpot was declared the national plate, and the breadfruit settled in as the universal Antiguan starch alongside cornmeal. The 20th-century tourism economy (Antigua claims to have 365 beaches, "one for every day") brought hotel-restaurant fusion food and an annual Sailing Week that pulls visitors from across the world. Carnival, held in late July and early August to mark emancipation, anchors the cultural calendar; goat water is the universal late-night dish of the j'ouvert dawn parades.

The home kitchen carries on largely unchanged through all of this. Pepperpot at Sunday lunch, saltfish for breakfast, doucouna (sweet cornmeal dumplings) for tea, conch and dumplings on Friday, a piece of black pineapple (Antigua's exceptionally sweet pineapple variety) at the end of the meal.

## Regional variations

The two-island nation is small and the cuisine is broadly homogenous:

- **Antigua (the main island)**: codified mainstream. Pepperpot, fungie, conch.
- **Barbuda (the smaller island)**: more lobster and conch, less developed restaurant scene.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cornmeal**: in fungie, in doucouna.
- **Saltfish (salt cod)**: with breadfruit, in chop-up.
- **Breadfruit**: roasted whole or boiled, the universal starch.
- **Pumpkin (squash)**: in pepperpot, in soups.
- **Okra**: in pepperpot, in fungie.
- **Scotch bonnet pepper**: the heat.
- **Bay leaf, thyme, scallion**: the herb base.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Fungie and Pepperpot](/recipes/cuisine/antiguan/fungie-and-pepperpot)**: cornmeal-and-okra mash with slow-stewed beef, salt meat and pumpkin in a thick brown gravy. The national dish.
2. **[Saltfish and Chop-Up](/recipes/cuisine/antiguan/saltfish-and-chop-up)**: salt cod sautéed with tomato, onion and pepper, served with chop-up (mashed eggplant, okra and spinach). The Antiguan breakfast.
3. **[Goat Water](/recipes/cuisine/antiguan/goat-water)**: spicy goat stew with potato and dumpling, the carnival weekend dish.
4. **[Conch and Dumplings](/recipes/cuisine/antiguan/conch-and-dumplings)**: pressure-cooked conch in a brown gravy with flour dumplings.
5. **[Doucouna](/recipes/cuisine/antiguan/doucouna)**: sweet cornmeal dumplings with raisins, coconut and cinnamon, wrapped in banana leaves and boiled. The traditional Antiguan dessert.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Wadadli beer or with English Harbour rum (Antigua's premium rum brand). For Carnival (late July to early August), goat water is the universal late-night dish. Eat after the j'ouvert dawn parade with hot pepper sauce, a beer, and bare feet.
