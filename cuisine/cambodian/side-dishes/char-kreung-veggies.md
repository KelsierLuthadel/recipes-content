# Char Kreung Veggies

![Char Kreung Veggies](resources/char-kreung-veggies.jpg)

*Cambodia's lemongrass-paste stir-fry: mixed vegetables (long beans, eggplant, cabbage, mushrooms) stir-fried fast in a hot wok with Cambodian kreung paste, fish sauce, palm sugar and fresh chilli. The bright spice-forward Cambodian sauté that brings the country's signature lemongrass-galangal-turmeric paste to a quick weeknight vegetable side.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 25 minutes

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Char kreung is the basic Cambodian stir-fry technique built around kreung paste, the country's signature curry paste of lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime zest, turmeric, garlic and shallot: any combination of meat or vegetables flash-fried in a hot wok with the kreung blooming in oil first, then the harder vegetables, then the softer ones, finished with fish sauce, palm sugar and fresh chilli for the canonical warm-aromatic, lightly sweet, properly salty profile. The name "char kreung" translates as "stir-fry with kreung". This vegetable version (long beans, eggplant, cabbage, mushrooms) is one of the most common household applications. The two technique points: bloom the kreung in hot oil before the vegetables go in (releases the aromatic oils from the lemongrass and galangal), and stir-fry in a properly hot wok (a cool pan steams instead of searing). The finished dish should be deep orange-yellow from the turmeric, with the kreung clinging in a thin glossy coat and the vegetables tender-crisp rather than soft.

## Ingredients

### Kreung paste (simple homemade; or shop-bought 4-5 tablespoons)
- 2 stalks lemongrass (tough outer layers removed, bottom 8 cm only, finely chopped)
- 1 thumb (3 cm) fresh galangal (peeled and chopped; or substitute with extra ginger and a pinch of cardamom)
- 1 thumb (3 cm) fresh turmeric (peeled and chopped; or 1 teaspoon ground turmeric)
- 4 fresh kaffir lime leaves (central rib removed, finely chopped)
- 4 shallots (peeled and chopped)
- 6 garlic cloves (peeled)
- 3 dried red chillies (deseeded, soaked in hot water 10 minutes, drained and chopped)
- ½ teaspoon fine sea salt

### Vegetables (the mix; adjust by season and availability)
- 12 long beans (or 250 g green beans; cut into 4 cm lengths)
- 2 small Thai eggplants (or 1 medium globe eggplant; cut into 2 cm wedges)
- 200 g shiitake mushrooms (or oyster mushrooms; or button; halved or quartered if large)
- 200 g cabbage (cut into 3 cm pieces; use Chinese cabbage, Savoy or just regular green cabbage)
- 1 large red bell pepper (deseeded and cut into 2 cm pieces)

### Stir-fry seasonings
- 4 tablespoons vegetable oil (or coconut oil)
- 3 tablespoons fish sauce (or use 2 tablespoons soy sauce + 1 tablespoon fish sauce)
- 2 tablespoons palm sugar (or coconut sugar)
- 2 fresh red chillies (sliced)
- 100 ml water (or vegetable stock)

### To finish
- 4 fresh kaffir lime leaves (central rib removed, finely shredded)
- A small handful of fresh Thai basil leaves
- 2 spring onions (finely sliced)
- 1 tablespoon fresh lime juice (optional, to brighten)

### To serve
- Steamed jasmine rice

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make the kreung paste
1. Combine the chopped lemongrass, galangal, turmeric, kaffir lime leaves, shallots, garlic, soaked dried chillies and salt in a small food processor (or pound in a large mortar).
2. Process (or pound) to a coarse paste, adding 1-2 tablespoons of water to help it move if needed. Don't go to a smooth paste; keep some texture.
3. The kreung should be a rough orange-yellow paste with visible lemongrass fibres. Yields about 5 tablespoons.

### Stage 2 - Prepare the vegetables
1. Trim the long beans and cut into 4 cm pieces.
2. Cut the eggplants into 2 cm wedges; if using globe eggplant, salt the wedges with 1 teaspoon of salt and let sit 15 minutes to draw out moisture, then pat dry. Thai eggplants don't need this step.
3. Halve or quarter the mushrooms.
4. Cut the cabbage into 3 cm pieces.
5. Deseed and cut the bell pepper into 2 cm pieces.
6. Keep the vegetables separate by type; they go into the pan at different stages.

### Stage 3 - Bloom the kreung
1. Heat the oil in a wok (or wide heavy frying pan) over medium-high heat till just smoking.
2. Add the kreung paste (about 4-5 tablespoons).
3. Stir constantly for 2-3 minutes till the paste sizzles, the oil splits out (you'll see it pool), and the kitchen smells aromatic. The mixture turns a deeper orange-yellow as the turmeric blooms.

### Stage 4 - Add the harder vegetables
1. Add the long beans and the eggplant wedges to the pan; turn the heat up to high.
2. Stir-fry for 3-4 minutes, tossing frequently, till the beans turn bright green and the eggplant starts to soften.
3. The pan should be properly hot; you should hear the vegetables sizzle when they hit it.

### Stage 5 - Add the bell pepper
1. Add the bell pepper pieces; stir-fry 1 minute. Still want crisp.

### Stage 6 - Add liquid and softer vegetables
1. Pour in the 100 ml of water (or stock); this helps cook the eggplant through and creates a thin sauce.
2. Add the mushrooms and the cabbage.
3. Add the fish sauce, palm sugar and sliced fresh chillies.
4. Stir-fry for 2-3 minutes; the mushrooms should soften, the cabbage should wilt but still have a crisp bite, and the sauce should reduce to a glossy coating.

### Stage 7 - Finish
1. Take off the heat.
2. Stir in the shredded kaffir lime leaves.
3. Stir in the Thai basil leaves (they'll wilt from the residual heat).
4. Stir in the sliced spring onions.
5. Squeeze the lime juice over if using.
6. Taste; adjust with more fish sauce if needed or a little more palm sugar.

### Stage 8 - Serve
1. Transfer to a serving plate.
2. Garnish with extra Thai basil and a sprinkle of sliced fresh chilli.
3. Serve immediately alongside steamed jasmine rice.
4. The vegetables should be brightly coloured (green beans, orange-yellow from turmeric coating, deep purple eggplant, bright red pepper), tender-crisp in texture, with a glossy kreung-coated finish.

## Notes
- **Bloom the kreung properly:** 2-3 minutes of high-heat stirring with the kreung in oil till the oil splits out is the canonical Cambodian technique. Skipping this step gives raw-tasting kreung; the dish doesn't smell as aromatic.
- **Hot wok or hot heavy pan:** Cambodian stir-fries need high heat. If your stove can't get a wok screaming hot, use a heavy cast-iron pan; the residual heat will help.
- **Add vegetables in order of cooking time:** harder vegetables (beans, eggplant) need longer; softer (mushrooms, cabbage) need only 2 minutes. Throwing them all in at once gives uneven texture.
- **Don't overcook the cabbage:** the cabbage should wilt but keep a slight crunch. 2-3 minutes max once added; longer and it goes limp.
- **Thai eggplants are best:** small Thai eggplants (round or oval, white-and-green or all green) have a slightly firmer texture and bitterer flavour that work well in stir-fries. Globe eggplant works but needs salting first.

## Variations
**Char kreung with chicken:** add 300 g of diced chicken thigh to the pan after blooming the kreung; cook through (5-6 minutes) before adding the vegetables. Becomes a main dish.
**Char kreung with beef:** add 300 g of thinly sliced beef sirloin in the same way; flash-fry till browned before adding vegetables. Common Cambodian main.
**Char kreung pak (pork):** add 300 g of ground pork or sliced pork shoulder after blooming the kreung; common variation.
**Spicier version:** add 4-6 extra fresh chillies (Thai bird's eye) to the kreung; or stir 1 tablespoon of Thai chilli paste (nam prik pao) into the stir-fry. Properly fierce variation.

## Serving
On a serving plate alongside steamed jasmine rice; the rice catches the kreung sauce. Often part of a multi-dish Cambodian meal alongside [bai sach chrouk](../bai-sach-chrouk.md), [amok trey](../amok-trey.md), or other Khmer dishes. Drink: Angkor beer, jasmine tea, or Cambodian rice wine.

## Storage
- Keeps refrigerated 3 days; reheat in a hot wok or pan for 2 minutes with a splash of water (don't microwave; the vegetables go off-texture).
- The cabbage and Thai basil go drab after a day; the dish still tastes good but loses visual freshness.
- Freezes 2 months in portioned containers; defrost in the fridge and reheat in a hot pan. The vegetables will be softer after freezing.
- The prepared kreung paste keeps refrigerated 1 week and freezes 3 months in a sealed jar; make a double batch for future stir-fries and curries.
- Day-old char kreung makes a great filling for spring rolls or banh mi sandwiches.
