# Cambodian (Khmer)

*The kitchen of the ancient Khmer Empire. Subtler than Thai, less coconut-heavy than Lao, with the steamed fish curry amok as the national dish, the famous Khmer noodle soup, and a love of fresh herbs and prahok (fermented fish paste) that defines Cambodian flavour.*

## A short history

Cambodian (Khmer) cooking is the oldest documented cuisine in mainland Southeast Asia — recipes are recorded in the bas-reliefs of Angkor Wat (12th-century). The Khmer Empire (9th-15th centuries, the largest in pre-modern Southeast Asia) developed a refined court cuisine while the peasant kitchen ran on rice, fish (especially from Tonle Sap lake, one of the world's most productive freshwater fisheries), pork, leafy greens, and the omnipresent prahok (the fermented fish paste that's Cambodia's national umami).

The cuisine is closely related to Thai and Lao but distinctly milder. Cambodian dishes use less chilli (chillies arrived only with the Portuguese in the 16th century and never took the country's palate by force), more freshwater fish, and a particular love of subtle herb infusions (kaffir lime leaf, lemongrass, galangal, holy basil) rather than dominant single flavours.

The Khmer Rouge years (1975-1979) and the subsequent civil unrest devastated the country and the food culture; the recovery has been slow. The post-2000 tourism boom around Angkor Wat has helped revive interest in the cuisine, and the diaspora kitchens (in France, the US — especially the Long Beach Cambodia Town — and Australia) preserve the codified tradition.

## Regional variations

- **Phnom Penh and central**: codified mainstream. Amok, lok lak, the urban Chinese-Khmer crossovers.
- **Siem Reap and the Tonle Sap region**: freshwater fish heaven. Prahok-centred dishes, lake-fish amok.
- **Battambang (west)**: closer to Thai. Rice country.
- **Kampot (south coast)**: the famous Kampot pepper — the world's most-prized.

## Signature ingredients

- **Prahok**: fermented fish paste, the national umami source.
- **Kroeung**: spice paste (lemongrass, galangal, turmeric, kaffir lime, garlic) — the curry-base of nearly every dish.
- **Tuk trei**: fish sauce, but milder than Vietnamese or Thai versions.
- **Kampot pepper**: the world's best pepper, grown in Kampot province.
- **Palm sugar**: in nearly every sauce.
- **Lime**: the acid.
- **Coconut milk**: in amok, in some curries (but less than Thai cooking).

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Amok Trey](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/amok-trey)**: fish curry steamed in banana-leaf cups with kroeung paste, coconut milk and eggs till it sets like a savoury custard. The national dish.
2. **[Bai Sach Chrouk](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/bai-sach-chrouk)**: grilled marinated pork over broken rice with pickled vegetables and chilli-vinegar sauce. The everyday breakfast.
3. **[Lok Lak](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/lok-lak)**: cubed beef stir-fried with pepper and soy, served on lettuce with tomato-cucumber salad and a lime-and-Kampot-pepper dipping sauce.
4. **[Nhom Trav](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/nhom-trav)**: banana-flower salad with chicken, fresh herbs, peanuts and lime dressing.
5. **[Chruok](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/chruok)**: sour-leaved Cambodian salad with chicken or pork, fresh herbs and chilli.
6. **[Sangkhya Lapov](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/sangkhya-lapov)**: steamed pumpkin filled with coconut-egg custard. The Khmer dessert classic.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Angkor beer (the national lager) or with iced sugar-cane juice (popular street drink). End with cha houy teuk (jelly drink with palm seeds, coconut milk and beans) or with nom plae ai (sticky rice cakes with palm sugar and grated coconut).
