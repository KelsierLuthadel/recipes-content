# Cambodian (Khmer)

*The kitchen of the ancient Khmer Empire. Subtler than Thai, less coconut-heavy than Lao, with the steamed fish curry amok as the national dish, the famous Khmer noodle soup, and a love of fresh herbs and prahok (fermented fish paste) that defines Cambodian flavour.*

## A short history

Khmer cooking is the oldest documented cuisine in mainland Southeast Asia. Before the great empire, the lower Mekong basin already hosted sophisticated rice-and-fish cultures (the Funan kingdom from the 1st to 6th centuries, the Chenla kingdoms after) that built irrigation systems, traded with India and China, and developed the freshwater-fish-and-rice base that still defines the country's eating. The Tonle Sap, one of the world's most productive lakes, flooded the surrounding plains every monsoon and left behind such fish populations that the Khmer developed prahok, the fermented fish paste, as a way to preserve the annual glut. Three thousand years on, prahok is still the country's universal source of salt and umami, doing duty in nearly every savoury dish.

Indian merchants arrived from the early centuries CE and brought Hinduism, Buddhism, Sanskrit and a refined spice tradition. The kitchen absorbed turmeric, ginger, galangal, lemongrass and kaffir lime, and the practice of grinding them together into a fresh paste (kroeung) became the curry-base of Khmer cooking. Indian-influenced rice puddings and palm-sugar sweets joined the dessert shelf. Theravada Buddhism arrived later from Sri Lanka and gradually displaced Hinduism, but the Indianised technique stayed.

The Khmer Empire (9th to 15th centuries) was the largest in pre-modern Southeast Asia, ruling from Angkor over much of what is now Thailand, Laos and southern Vietnam. The bas-reliefs of 12th-century Angkor Wat record actual recipes and market scenes: cooks preparing fish, traders selling rice, women grinding spices. The court cuisine that grew up at Angkor was refined, herb-heavy and elaborately steamed (amok, the country's national dish, traces back to this court tradition), while the village kitchen ran on rice, prahok, leafy greens and freshwater fish. Trade with China through the same centuries layered on noodles, the wok and stir-fry technique, and the practice of pickling vegetables.

The empire's slow decline from the 13th century onward (overrun by Thai armies from the west in 1431) shrank Cambodia to a small kingdom between two stronger neighbours. The cuisine kept its identity but absorbed influence from both sides: more sour broths from the Thai shelf, more sausage and pickling from the Vietnamese east. Chillies arrived only with the Portuguese in the 16th century, brought through Goa and Malacca, and never took the country's palate by force. Cambodian cooks use less chilli than their neighbours, more freshwater fish, and a particular love of subtle herb infusions over single dominant flavours. The cuisine reads as a close cousin to Thai and Lao but consistently milder.

French colonial rule from 1863 to 1953 layered on a thin but enduring French shelf: baguettes (still the country's everyday bread, sliced and stuffed into the breakfast pâté sandwich), coffee, and a few cooking techniques that fed into the city kitchen. The Khmer Rouge years (1975 to 1979) and the civil unrest that followed devastated the country and the food culture; perhaps two million people died, and with them an unmeasurable share of the country's recipe knowledge. Cookbooks were burned, restaurants closed, market cooks killed or scattered. Survivors carried recipes in their heads to refugee camps and to the diaspora communities that grew up in Long Beach, Lowell, Paris and Sydney.

The recovery has been slow. The post-2000 tourism boom around Angkor Wat helped revive interest in classical Khmer cooking, and a generation of Cambodian-trained chefs (Luu Meng in Phnom Penh, Nite Yun in Oakland, Rotanak Ros writing the *Nhum* cookbook in 2020) have started codifying recipes and rebuilding the country's culinary memory. Diaspora kitchens (Long Beach's Cambodia Town has the largest population outside Cambodia) carry the tradition forward, and what comes back to the table feels older than any neighbouring cuisine: prahok in the bowl, kroeung in the pot, herbs heaped on the plate.

## Regional variations

- **Phnom Penh and central**: codified mainstream. Amok, lok lak, the urban Chinese-Khmer crossovers.
- **Siem Reap and the Tonle Sap region**: freshwater fish heaven. Prahok-centred dishes, lake-fish amok.
- **Battambang (west)**: closer to Thai. Rice country.
- **Kampot (south coast)**: home of Kampot pepper, the world's most-prized.

## Signature ingredients

- **Prahok**: fermented fish paste, the national umami source.
- **Kroeung**: spice paste (lemongrass, galangal, turmeric, kaffir lime, garlic); the curry-base of nearly every dish.
- **Tuk trei**: fish sauce, milder than Vietnamese or Thai versions.
- **Kampot pepper**: the world's best pepper, grown in Kampot province.
- **Palm sugar**: in nearly every sauce.
- **Lime**: the acid.
- **Coconut milk**: in amok, in some curries (but less than Thai cooking).

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Amok Trey](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/amok-trey)**: fish curry steamed in banana-leaf cups with kroeung paste, coconut milk and eggs till it sets like a savoury custard. The national dish.
2. **[Bai Sach Chrouk](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/bai-sach-chrouk)**: grilled marinated pork over broken rice with pickled vegetables and chilli-vinegar sauce. The everyday breakfast.
3. **[Lok Lak](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/lok-lak)**: cubed beef stir-fried with pepper and soy, served on lettuce with tomato-cucumber salad and a lime-and-Kampot-pepper dipping sauce.
4. **[Nhom Trav](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/nhom-trav)**: banana-flower salad with chicken, fresh herbs, peanuts and lime dressing.
5. **[Chruok](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/chruok)**: sour-leaved Cambodian salad with chicken or pork, fresh herbs and chilli.
6. **[Sangkhya Lapov](/recipes/cuisine/cambodian/sangkhya-lapov)**: steamed pumpkin filled with coconut-egg custard. The Khmer dessert classic.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Angkor beer (the national lager) or with iced sugar-cane juice (popular street drink). Finish with cha houy teuk (jelly drink with palm seeds, coconut milk and beans) or with nom plae ai (sticky rice cakes with palm sugar and grated coconut).
