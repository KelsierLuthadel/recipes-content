# New Mexico

*The American state that built its entire identity around two chilli colours and one tireless question: "red or green?" The only US cuisine with chillies as the constitutional centrepiece, and the only one to make posole, sopaipillas and stacked enchiladas central to a regional canon.*

## A short history

New Mexico cuisine sits on roughly 4,000 years of corn cultivation, longer than almost any kitchen in North America north of Mexico. The Ancestral Puebloan peoples who built the great houses at Chaco Canyon, Mesa Verde and Aztec Ruins were growing maize, beans and squash on the high desert by 1500 BCE, terracing the mesas, channelling snowmelt and storing dry corn in cliff granaries against the long winters. Blue corn is the oldest variety still in the kitchen, ground on metates, drunk as atole, baked into piki bread on hot stones. The 19 surviving Pueblo communities along the Rio Grande and out west to Acoma and Zuni still grow the heirloom corns their ancestors selected over forty centuries.

Athabaskan peoples arrived later. The Diné (Navajo) and the Apache moved south from the subarctic during the medieval centuries and were established across the wider region by the 1400s, hunting deer and rabbit, gathering pinon nuts and prickly pear, and trading with the settled Pueblo farmers for corn. The pinon-roasting fire and the slow lamb stew are still cornerstones of Diné cooking.

Spanish colonisation began in 1598, when Juan de Oñate led a settler expedition north from Mexico up the Rio Grande and founded the first European settlement at San Juan Pueblo, twenty-two years before the Pilgrims reached Plymouth. The Hispano settlers who followed (with deep roots in New Mexico long before the rest of the US Southwest) brought sheep, goats, cattle, wheat, garlic, onions, grapevines and orchard fruit. They also brought the Mexican chilli varieties that crossed with the local capsicum to produce the Hatch and Chimayó pods that anchor the modern kitchen. The Pueblo Revolt of 1680 briefly threw the Spanish out; when they returned twelve years later under terms that protected Pueblo land and religion, the two cultures sat down together at the same table for the first time. Stacked enchiladas, the Christmas posole, the carne adovada slow-braise and the sopaipilla all emerged from that long Hispano-Pueblo conversation.

The Hatch chilli (grown around Hatch, NM, in the Mesilla Valley) and the Chimayó chilli (grown in the high Sangre de Cristo foothills) are the two regional varieties that anchor everything. Both can be eaten green (fresh, roasted) or red (dried, rehydrated). The green-or-red question at every restaurant ("would you like red or green?") has been the state's friendly small-talk for generations. The "Christmas" answer (both) was elected the official state question in 1996 (genuinely; it is literally a state symbol). When the Hatch chilli harvest comes in late August, the smell of roasting chillies in propane drums sits over every parking lot from Las Cruces to Taos for six weeks.

US annexation in 1848 after the Mexican-American War brought the railroad, Anglo settlement and a slow erosion of the older Hispano land grants. The cuisine held its ground. The Atchison, Topeka and Santa Fe Railway pushed through in 1880 and the Fred Harvey Houses introduced "Mexican food" to American travellers in a sanitised form, but New Mexico's own kitchen stayed itself in the village homes and the Pueblo feast days. In the 20th century, restaurants like Rancho de Chimayó (1965) and the iconic Pueblo and Hispano family kitchens of Santa Fe and Taos codified the cuisine. New Mexico is the only US state where chilli (spelled "chile" here) has its own protected agricultural designation, an official state question, and a state-mandated harvest celebration; the kitchen reads as it has for centuries, with chile on everything that will hold it.

## Regional variations

- **Northern (Santa Fe, Taos, Chimayó)**: stronger Pueblo and Hispanic traditions. Chimayó chilli (medium heat, sweet, earthy), posole, sopaipillas, blue corn enchiladas.
- **Southern (Hatch, Las Cruces)**: Mexican-border influence. Hatch chilli (medium to very hot), more flour tortillas, more carne asada.
- **Eastern (Roswell, Clovis)**: Texas-leaning. More cattle, more BBQ, the "Llano Estacado" plains kitchen.
- **Pueblo communities (19 Pueblos across the state)**: distinct traditions. Blue corn meal, pinon (pine nut) breads, ceremonial feast foods.

## Signature ingredients

- **Hatch and Chimayó chillies**: green (fresh, roasted) or red (dried). The centrepiece of every dish.
- **Blue corn**: ground into tortillas, atole, posole. Sweet, slightly nutty, the Pueblo specialty.
- **Pinto beans**: cooked slowly with onion, garlic and (often) bacon; the universal side.
- **Cumin and Mexican oregano**: the spice pair under most stews.
- **Pinon (pine nuts)**: the local nut. In breads, in salads, as a garnish.
- **Sopaipillas**: deep-fried bread puffs, hollow inside; honey-filled for dessert, savoury with meals.
- **Cheese (Asadero, Queso Fresco)**: melting cheese in the centre of stacked enchiladas; fresh white cheese on top.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the stacked enchilada, the green and red chile stews, the chile relleno and the New Year's posole. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Stacked Red Enchiladas](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/stacked-red-enchiladas)**: layered (not rolled) corn tortillas with red chile sauce, cheese and onion, topped with a fried egg. The defining New Mexico format: flat-stacked, not rolled like elsewhere.
2. **[Green Chile Chicken Stew](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/green-chile-chicken-stew)**: chicken slow-cooked with Hatch green chillies, potato and onion. The everyday New Mexico bowl, with the answer "green" to the state question.
3. **[Chile Rellenos](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/chile-rellenos-nm)**: roasted whole green chillies stuffed with cheese, battered and fried. The classic of the Hatch chilli season (August to September).
4. **[Carne Adovada (NM-style)](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/carne-adovada)**: pork shoulder cubes slow-cooked in red chile paste. The northern New Mexico signature, distinct from the Mexican adobada.
5. **[NM Posole Rojo](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/nm-posole-rojo)**: hominy and pork stewed slow with red chile. The Christmas Eve and New Year's bowl across the state.
6. **[Calabacitas (New Mexican)](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/calabacitas-new-mexican)**: summer squash sautéed with corn, onion, green chillies and Asadero cheese. The signature vegetable side.
7. **[Sopaipillas Rellenos](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/sopaipilla-relleno)**: deep-fried bread puffs stuffed with seasoned beef or beans, smothered in red or green chile sauce. The diner classic.

Cook the stacked enchiladas and the carne adovada and you have the red-chile shelf. The green chile stew and the chile rellenos are the green side. The posole is the feast; the calabacitas the vegetable; the sopaipilla the bread that's also a meal.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the green chile cheeseburger (a state-icon dish, with an official "New Mexico Green Chile Cheeseburger Trail" mapping the best across the state), the NM frito pie (chilli con carne in a Fritos bag, eaten with a spoon), and the tamales rojos (red-chile pork tamales). Pair everything with a Tecate, a margarita, or a glass of New Mexico sangria.
