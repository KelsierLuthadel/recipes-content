# New Mexico

*The American state that built its entire identity around two chilli colours and one tireless question: "red or green?" The only US cuisine with chillies as the constitutional centrepiece, and the only one to make posole, sopaipillas and stacked enchiladas central to a regional canon.*

## A short history

New Mexico cuisine sits on a 4,000-year history of corn cultivation, beans and squash by the Ancestral Puebloans, and a 400-year Hispanic settlement that began with the Spanish colonisation in 1598 (predating the Pilgrims). The Hispanic settlers (Hispano, or *Spanish-American*, with deep roots in the state long before the rest of the US Southwest) built their kitchens around indigenous crops, brought sheep and goats with them, and codified the chilli-and-corn axis that still defines the state's food.

The Hatch chilli (grown around Hatch, NM, in the Mesilla Valley) and the Chimayó chilli (grown in the high Sangre de Cristo foothills) are the two regional varieties that anchor everything. Both can be eaten green (fresh, roasted) or red (dried, rehydrated). The green-or-red question at every restaurant — "would you like red or green?" — has been the state's friendly small-talk for generations. The "Christmas" answer (both) was elected the official state question in 1996 (genuinely — it's literally a state symbol).

The 20th century saw New Mexico cuisine codified through specific restaurants like Rancho de Chimayó (1965) and the iconic Pueblo and Hispano family kitchens of Santa Fe and Taos. The state is the only one in the US where chilli (spelled "chile" in New Mexico) has its own protected agricultural designation and a state-mandated harvest celebration.

## Regional variations

- **Northern (Santa Fe, Taos, Chimayó)**: stronger Pueblo and Hispanic traditions. Chimayó chilli (medium heat, sweet, earthy), posole, sopaipillas, blue corn enchiladas.
- **Southern (Hatch, Las Cruces)**: Mexican-border influence. Hatch chilli (medium to very hot), more flour tortillas, more carne asada.
- **Eastern (Roswell, Clovis)**: Texas-leaning. More cattle, more BBQ, the "Llano Estacado" plains kitchen.
- **Pueblo communities (19 Pueblos across the state)**: distinct traditions. Blue corn meal, pinon (pine nut) breads, ceremonial feast foods.

## Signature ingredients

- **Hatch and Chimayó chillies**: green (fresh, roasted) or red (dried). The centrepiece of every dish.
- **Blue corn**: ground into tortillas, atole, posole. Sweet, slightly nutty, the Pueblo specialty.
- **Pinto beans**: cooked slowly with onion, garlic and (often) bacon; the universal side.
- **Cumin and Mexican oregano**: the spice pair under most stews.
- **Pinon (pine nuts)**: the local nut. In breads, in salads, as a garnish.
- **Sopaipillas**: deep-fried bread puffs, hollow inside; honey-filled for dessert, savoury with meals.
- **Cheese (Asadero, Queso Fresco)**: melting cheese in the centre of stacked enchiladas; fresh white cheese on top.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the stacked enchilada, the green and red chile stews, the chile relleno and the New Year's posole. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Stacked Red Enchiladas](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/stacked-red-enchiladas)**: layered (not rolled) corn tortillas with red chile sauce, cheese and onion, topped with a fried egg. The defining New Mexico format — flat-stacked, not rolled like elsewhere.
2. **[Green Chile Chicken Stew](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/green-chile-chicken-stew)**: chicken slow-cooked with Hatch green chillies, potato and onion. The everyday New Mexico bowl, with the answer "green" to the state question.
3. **[Chile Rellenos](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/chile-rellenos-nm)**: roasted whole green chillies stuffed with cheese, battered and fried. The classic of the Hatch chilli season (August–September).
4. **[Carne Adovada (NM-style)](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/carne-adovada)**: pork shoulder cubes slow-cooked in red chile paste. The northern New Mexico signature, distinct from the Mexican adobada.
5. **[NM Posole Rojo](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/nm-posole-rojo)**: hominy and pork stewed slow with red chile. The Christmas Eve and New Year's bowl across the state.
6. **[Calabacitas (New Mexican)](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/calabacitas-new-mexican)**: summer squash sautéed with corn, onion, green chillies and Asadero cheese. The signature vegetable side.
7. **[Sopaipillas Rellenos](/recipes/cuisine/new-mexico/sopaipilla-relleno)**: deep-fried bread puffs stuffed with seasoned beef or beans, smothered in red or green chile sauce. The diner classic.

Cook the stacked enchiladas and the carne adovada and you have the red-chile shelf. The green chile stew and the chile rellenos are the green side. The posole is the feast; the calabacitas the vegetable; the sopaipilla the bread that's also a meal.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the green chile cheeseburger (a state-icon dish — the official "New Mexico Green Chile Cheeseburger Trail" maps the best across the state), the NM frito pie (chilli con carne in a Fritos bag, eaten with a spoon), and the tamales rojos (red-chile pork tamales). Pair everything with a Tecate, a margarita, or a glass of New Mexico sangria.
