# Cuban

*Caribbean cooking with a Spanish accent and an African undercurrent. Built on rice, beans, pork, plantain and the citrus-garlic marinade known as mojo; a kitchen forged on an island that has spent five centuries trading with the world.*

## A short history

Cuban cooking begins with the Taíno, the indigenous Arawak-speaking people who farmed and fished the island for centuries before Columbus made landfall at Bariay in October 1492. The Taíno grew cassava (their staple, processed into the flat hard casabe bread that still appears on rural Cuban tables), sweet potato, maize, beans, peanuts, peppers and tropical fruit like guava, pineapple and mamey. They cooked meat over a wooden grate set above embers, a technique they called *barabicu*, which entered Spanish as *barbacoa* and English as "barbecue". They also gave the wider world tobacco and the word for hammock. Spanish conquest, slavery and European disease wiped out almost the entire Taíno population within fifty years of contact, but their ingredients (cassava, sweet potato, ají chillies, malanga) and their pit-cooking method outlived them.

Spanish settlement from 1492 onwards built the colonial kitchen on Iberian foundations. The conquistadors brought pigs, cattle, chickens, rice, wheat, citrus, sugar cane, olive oil and the Andalusian sofrito tradition that became the base of nearly every Cuban dish. Havana, founded in 1519 and rebuilt at its current harbour location in 1519, became the rendezvous port for the Spanish treasure fleet returning from Mexico and Peru. Spanish ships stopped to provision before crossing the Atlantic, and the city grew rich on stevedoring, shipbuilding and provisioning. The lechón asado (whole roast pig in mojo) descends from Iberian Christmas tradition; the picadillo (ground beef with olives, raisins and capers) is a Moorish-Sephardic legacy that crossed with the Spanish.

Sugar transformed the island from the 1700s onwards. After the Haitian Revolution destroyed Saint-Domingue's sugar industry in the 1790s, Cuba became the world's largest sugar producer. Spain imported enslaved West and Central Africans on a vast scale through the 19th century (the country was the last in the Americas to abolish slavery, only doing so in 1886, three years before independence movements began in earnest). The African contribution to Cuban food is profound. Enslaved cooks brought plantain (originally from Southeast Asia but routed through Africa), okra, the slow-stewed congrí (rice and red beans cooked together, a Bantu-origin pairing), the funked-up moros y cristianos, and the spice and herb instinct that runs through Cuban cooking. The Yoruba religious tradition of Santería brought ritual food offerings (the corn-and-okra harina with quimbombó) that still appear at family celebrations.

The 19th century brought two more streams. Chinese contract labourers (around 125,000 of them between 1847 and 1874) arrived to work the sugar plantations after the abolition of the slave trade. Many stayed, settled Havana's Barrio Chino (once the largest Chinatown in Latin America), and added stir-fry technique, the rice-cooker discipline and the noodle shelf to the city's kitchens. The fritas cubanas (the Cuban hamburger with shoestring potatoes on top) emerged from Cuban-Chinese diners. The 1898 Spanish-American War ended Spanish rule, and US capital flooded in to rebuild the sugar industry.

Modern Cuban cooking took its codified shape during the early Republican period (1902 to 1959), when the island was rich, the United States was the dominant trading partner, and Havana was the playground of the Americas. The Bacardí rum empire (founded in Santiago in 1862), the cigar industry, and the daiquiri-and-mojito cocktail culture all date to this era. The 1959 revolution and the US embargo that followed froze the cuisine in time. Ration cards (the libreta) introduced in 1962 limited what was available. The Special Period after the Soviet collapse (1991 onwards) hollowed out the food supply for a generation. The diaspora kitchens of Miami, Tampa and Madrid kept the codified Cuban tradition alive while the island itself ate ration cards. The Cubano sandwich (roast pork, ham, Swiss, pickles, mustard, pressed flat) is a Tampa invention from the cigar-factory worker canteens of the 1900s, not actually a Havana dish.

Today Cuban cooking lives on two parallel tracks: the diaspora kitchen (richer, with the full ingredient list intact) and the island kitchen (improvisational, doing more with less, with a growing paladar private-restaurant scene since the 2011 reforms). The catalogue's Cuban shelf draws from both.

## Regional variations

- **Havana and the west**: the codified national cuisine. Ropa vieja, lechon asado, congrí, the picadillo-with-olives shelf.
- **Oriente (Santiago, Holguín, the east)**: more African influence. Stronger seasoning, more okra, the congrí oriental made with red beans rather than black.
- **Centro (Camagüey, Sancti Spíritus)**: the cattle country. Vaca frita, the beef-heavy shelf.
- **Diaspora (Miami, Tampa, Madrid)**: the codified Cuban-American kitchen. The Cubano sandwich was invented in Tampa; medianoche, croquetas and pastelitos at every ventanita.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sofrito**: onion, green pepper, garlic and tomato fried in olive oil. The base of almost every Cuban dish.
- **Mojo**: garlic, sour orange juice (or lime), olive oil, oregano, cumin. The marinade and finishing sauce for pork.
- **Sour orange (naranja agria)**: bitter Seville-type orange. The acid in mojo.
- **Cumin and oregano**: the spice pair under Cuban seasoning, both used heavily compared to other Caribbean kitchens.
- **Black beans**: paired with white rice as the everyday side; together they're moros y cristianos (Moors and Christians).
- **Plantain**: green for tostones (twice-fried), ripe for maduros (sweet, soft), unripe for chicharritas (chips).
- **Pork shoulder**: the meat. The lechón asado is the national feast.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the slow roast, the bean-and-rice shelf, the famous sandwich and the everyday stew. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Lechón Asado Cubano](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/lechon-asado-cubano)**: pork shoulder marinated overnight in mojo and slow-roasted till the skin crackles and the meat shreds. The Christmas Eve (Nochebuena) showpiece and the most-loved Cuban dish anywhere in the world.
2. **[Ropa Vieja](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/ropa-vieja)**: shredded flank steak slow-braised with sofrito and red wine, served over white rice. The national dish; "old clothes" because the shredded beef looks like rags.
3. **[Moros y Cristianos](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/moros-y-cristianos)**: black beans and white rice cooked together with sofrito and pork fat. The everyday Cuban side that turns into a meal with a fried egg.
4. **[Frijoles Negros](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/frijoles-negros)**: black beans slow-cooked with sofrito, bay leaf, cumin and a splash of vinegar at the end. Served over white rice; the everyday Cuban bowl.
5. **[Picadillo](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/picadillo)**: ground beef with sofrito, olives, raisins and capers, served over rice with sweet plantain on the side. The Cuban weeknight at its most reliable.
6. **[Cubano Sandwich](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/cubano-sandwich)**: roasted pork, ham, Swiss cheese, pickles, mustard on Cuban bread, pressed flat in a plancha. Tampa's contribution to the world.
7. **[Masitas de Puerco](/recipes/cuisine/cuban/masitas-de-puerco)**: pork shoulder cubes marinated in mojo, deep-fried till crisp on the outside. The lechón technique in small bites.

Cook the lechón and the ropa vieja and you have the celebration plates. The moros, frijoles and picadillo are the everyday table. The cubano and the masitas are the bar food.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the seafood shelf (camarones enchilados, arroz con pollo), the plantain canon (tostones, maduros, mariquitas), the breakfast plates (tortilla cubana, pan con bistec), and the dessert tradition (flan, arroz con leche, pastelitos de guayaba). Pair everything with a mojito or a Cuba libre and the cuisine sings.
