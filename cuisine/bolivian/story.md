# Bolivian

*The Andean kitchen at the highest altitudes in South America. The saltena (Bolivia's juicy answer to the empanada), the silpancho layered lunch, the famous anticuchos street-grilled beef heart, and an indigenous Aymara-Quechua tradition that's been refining itself for 4,000 years.*

## A short history

Bolivian cooking sits on a strong indigenous Aymara and Quechua base that goes back roughly four millennia. The Tiwanaku civilisation on the shores of Lake Titicaca (flourishing from about 500 to 1000 CE) had already perfected the techniques that still define the highland kitchen: terraced potato cultivation, freeze-drying tubers in the cold night air to make chuño, toasting and grinding quinoa for the porridge bowl, and grilling guinea pig and llama over coals. When the Inca pushed south into what is now Bolivia in the 15th century, they layered their own cooking onto the existing tradition: more corn, more state-run grain stores, the chicha morada purple-corn drink, and the practice of eating from a communal cloth (the aguayo) still set on the ground for festival meals.

The Spanish arrived in 1538 and founded Potosí four years later, on a mountain of silver that bankrolled the empire for two centuries. The colonial kitchen that grew up in Potosí and La Paz absorbed beef, dairy, wheat, onion, garlic and the European spice cabinet, and gave back potatoes, chillies and quinoa to the world. The salteña pastry, the empanada relatives, the slow-braised meats and the convent-kitchen sweets all trace to this period. The mines pulled in indigenous labour from across the highlands, and the working food of the silver mines (api, salteñas, the deep stews) became the working food of the cities.

The 19th and 20th centuries kept Bolivia rural, isolated and poor by South American standards, but the kitchen stayed inventive. The geographic vertical range, from Amazonian lowlands at 500m to La Paz at 3,600m, produced sharply different regional cuisines that mostly cooked for themselves. The salteña arrived in its modern form in the 1880s, brought across by an exiled Argentine family from Salta and naturalised within a generation. The Cochabamba valley, with its kinder climate and richer soil, emerged as the food capital, and silpancho and pique macho were born in its bar kitchens.

The cuisine is less internationally known than its neighbours Peru and Argentina, but the Andean larder is among the world's most distinctive. Bolivian chefs like Marsia Taha at Gustu in La Paz now lead a quietly excellent fine-dining scene focused on regional ingredients (Amazonian fish, Beni nuts, native potato varieties, fermented cacao from the Yungas), and the international quinoa boom of the 2010s reminded the world that the Andes were always a granary. The salteña remains Bolivia's most-loved everyday food, eaten mid-morning with strict etiquette: tilt forward, bite a small hole, suck out the juice first, then eat the rest.

## Regional variations

- **La Paz and the Altiplano (highlands)**: codified mainstream. Salteñas, chairo soup, freeze-dried potato dishes.
- **Cochabamba (the central valley)**: the food capital. Silpancho, pique macho, the best Bolivian dining.
- **Santa Cruz (eastern lowlands)**: more beef, more cassava. Closer to Brazilian and Argentine cooking.
- **Tarija (south)**: wine country. Sausage and the highest-altitude commercial vineyards in the world.
- **Sucre and Potosí (highlands)**: silver-mining-era traditions.

## Signature ingredients

- **Potato**: 4,000+ native varieties. Including freeze-dried chuño.
- **Quinoa**: the original Andean grain.
- **Aji amarillo and aji panca**: yellow and red Bolivian chillies, similar to Peruvian.
- **Llajwa**: the table salsa. Tomato, locoto chilli, quirquiña herb.
- **Beef**: especially in Cochabamba and Santa Cruz.
- **Cumin and oregano**: in marinades.
- **Locoto**: small fierce chilli.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Salteñas](/recipes/cuisine/bolivian/saltenas)**: oven-baked half-moon pastries filled with juicy chicken or beef stew with potato, peas, raisin, hard-boiled egg and olive. Eat them with strict etiquette: tilt, bite, suck, eat.
2. **[Silpancho](/recipes/cuisine/bolivian/silpancho)**: layered plate. Rice on the bottom, sliced boiled potato, breaded pounded-thin beef on top, then a fried egg, with a tomato-onion-locoto salsa. Cochabamba's signature.
3. **[Pique Macho](/recipes/cuisine/bolivian/pique-macho)**: chips topped with diced beef, sausage, onion, tomato, fried egg and locoto pepper. The Cochabamba bar plate.
4. **[Anticuchos Bolivianos](/recipes/cuisine/bolivian/anticuchos-bolivianos)**: beef heart marinated in aji panca and vinegar, charcoal-grilled on skewers. The street-food classic shared with Peru.
5. **[Bolivia Sopa de Maní](/recipes/cuisine/bolivian/bolivia-sopa-de-mani)**: peanut soup with beef, potato, noodles, herbs and crispy fried potato strings on top.

## Where to go next

Drink singani (Bolivian grape brandy, used in chuflay) or a cold Paceña (the national lager). Finish with api con pastel (a hot purple-corn drink with a sweet fried pastry) or with helado de canela (cinnamon ice cream).
