# San Marinese

*The kitchen of the world's oldest republic and Europe's third-smallest country. Italian by ingredient, San Marinese by recipe: pasta-and-pork dishes and stuffed pastas like the swallow's-nest baked pasta that you won't quite find anywhere else in Italy.*

## A short history

San Marino is a tiny enclave entirely surrounded by Italy, 61 km² with about 34,000 residents, and (by legend) has been an independent republic since 301 CE, when a Christian stonecutter from the Croatian island of Rab fled the Diocletian persecutions and climbed Mount Titano to live as a hermit. Marinus's small community of refugees became, over centuries, the world's oldest surviving sovereign state. The cuisine is essentially Romagnola Italian (the cooking of the surrounding Emilia-Romagna) with its own twist on a handful of signature dishes.

The earliest Sammarinese kitchen ran on what the hill could grow. Wheat for bread on the lower slopes, chestnuts and forage from the upper woods, sheep and goats grazed on the limestone, wild boar and game from the Apennine ridge running south. Roman influence laid down the olive oil and wine; the early medieval centuries added the heavy use of pork (cured and fresh) that still defines the cooking. Monastic communities at nearby Pennabilli and around the Marecchia valley spread the rabbit-and-herb dishes and the slow-stewed beef techniques that filtered onto the mountain.

The medieval centuries kept San Marino looking inward. The republic survived through clever neutrality (most famously sheltering 100,000 Italian refugees during the Second World War, a tenth of the country's modern population fitting in for safety on a single rocky outcrop) and through alliances with the surrounding Papal States and later the Kingdom of Italy. The kitchen reflected the inland-hill geography: pasta, pork (cured and fresh), wild herbs, mushrooms from the local forests, and the produce of nearby Adriatic markets reached by donkey-cart down to Rimini.

The Renaissance brought the fresh-pasta tradition that runs the Romagnola kitchen still. Sammarinese cooks rolled cappelletti (the "little hats") and tortellini by hand for the broth pot, especially for Christmas Eve, and built up the nidi di rondine (the "swallow's nests": pasta sheets rolled with ham, cheese and béchamel, sliced into rings, baked in cream sauce) as the country's signature dish. The Columbian Exchange brought tomatoes from the New World by the 17th century, and the slow-cooked ragù that simmers all day on a Sammarinese stove arrived with them. Mortadella di San Marino, the country's PDO cured pork, traces to this same period of refining the rural pig-killing tradition.

The Risorgimento (Italian unification, 1815-1871) reshaped the region around San Marino but left the republic intact. Garibaldi famously sheltered here in 1849 after the fall of the Roman Republic; the country thanked him by keeping its borders open and its kitchen untouched. The 20th century brought tourism (the three towers on Mount Titano draw two million visitors a year), and with it the restaurant scene that codified the dishes the home kitchen had cooked for centuries. Cappelletti in brodo, nidi di rondine, stracotto (slow-braised beef in wine): these are the recipes you can call distinctly San Marinese. The country exports more cured meats and pasta than most regions of comparable size.

## Regional variations

San Marino is too small to have regional sub-traditions, but you notice differences by elevation: the higher villages (Castello di Montegiardino, Borgo Maggiore) lean more rustic; the capital (San Marino City) more refined-restaurant.

## Signature ingredients

- **Pasta**: fresh and hand-rolled, especially cappelletti and tortellini.
- **Pork**: cured (mortadella di San Marino, salame) and fresh (slow-roasted).
- **Mushrooms**: porcini and other wild varieties from the local Apennine forests.
- **Olive oil and white wine**: Mediterranean staples.
- **Pecorino**: sheep's milk cheese.
- **Parsley and rosemary**: the herb pair.
- **Tomato**: in sauces, especially the slow-cooked ragù.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Nidi di Rondine](/recipes/cuisine/san-marinese/nidi-di-rondine)**: "swallow's nests", pasta sheets rolled with ham, cheese and béchamel, cut into rings, baked in cream sauce. The signature San Marinese dish.
2. **[Cappelletti in Brodo](/recipes/cuisine/san-marinese/cappelletti-in-brodo)**: small stuffed pasta "little hats" in clear chicken broth. The Christmas Eve plate.
3. **[Coniglio in Porchetta](/recipes/cuisine/san-marinese/coniglio-in-porchetta)**: rabbit stuffed with herbs, garlic and fennel, roasted in the porchetta (suckling-pig) style.
4. **[Stracotto San Marinese](/recipes/cuisine/san-marinese/stracotto-sanmarinese)**: beef slow-braised in red wine for hours, till the meat shreds and the gravy turns deep brown.
5. **[Pasta e Ceci San Marinese](/recipes/cuisine/san-marinese/pasta-e-ceci-sanmarinese)**: short pasta with chickpeas, rosemary and olive oil in a thick rustic soup-stew.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with San Marinese wine; the country produces small quantities of Sangiovese and Biancale (the local white). Finish with torta tre monti, a layered chocolate-and-wafer cake named for the country's three towers. The local Mistrà (an aniseed spirit) makes a proper digestif.
