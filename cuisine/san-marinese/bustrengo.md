# Bustrengo

![Bustrengo](resources/bustrengo.jpg)

*The savoury San Marinese polenta-and-bread cake: stale bread, polenta and milk baked into a dense, crusty wheel and served warm with mountain cheese.*

**Serves:** 6 to 8 as a course; 12 as a side

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus 30 minutes soaking)

**Cook Time:** 1 hour 10 minutes

## Overview
Bustrengo has a sweet incarnation and a savoury one. The savoury bustrengo is the older form: a frugal dish from the Republic's farmhouses to use up stale bread, the last of the polenta and a litre of milk, baked into a tight cake that can be sliced. It sits in a long lineage of central-Italian bread-puddings, but the San Marinese version adds polenta and bakes it slowly until the outside forms a deep brown crust and the inside stays moist. Eat it warm with a wedge of Pecorino di Fossa, a few walnuts and a glass of Sangiovese. It is the dish that survives every food fashion.

## Ingredients

- 250 g stale country bread, torn into pieces
- 750 ml whole milk
- 150 g coarse polenta (yellow cornmeal)
- 80 g 00 flour
- 3 eggs
- 60 ml olive oil, plus extra for the tin
- 1 small onion, very finely chopped
- 80 g pancetta in 5 mm dice
- 80 g aged pecorino, coarsely grated
- 60 g walnuts, roughly chopped
- 2 tbsp chopped rosemary
- Zest of 1 lemon
- 1 tsp fine salt
- Black pepper
- A handful of dried figs, chopped (optional, traditional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak the bread
1. Tear the bread into a large bowl. Pour the milk over and leave to soak for 30 minutes, breaking the bread up with a fork halfway through.
2. The mixture should be a soft loose porridge.

### Stage 2 - Cook the soffritto
1. Warm half the olive oil in a small pan. Add the chopped onion and the pancetta. Cook gently for 10 minutes until the onion is soft and the pancetta is rendered but not crisp.
2. Set aside to cool slightly.

### Stage 3 - Build the batter
1. Beat the eggs into the soaked bread and milk.
2. Whisk in the polenta and flour in two additions; the mixture should be the texture of a loose drop scone batter.
3. Stir in the cooled soffritto, the pecorino, walnuts, rosemary, lemon zest, salt, a good grind of black pepper, the remaining olive oil and the dried figs if using.
4. Let the batter stand 10 minutes so the polenta starts to hydrate.

### Stage 4 - Bake
1. Heat the oven to 180°C (160°C fan). Oil a 24 cm round cake tin or shallow ovenproof dish well, then dust with a thin layer of polenta to coat.
2. Pour the batter in; it should come most of the way up the sides.
3. Drizzle a little extra olive oil over the top.
4. Bake for 1 hour 5 to 1 hour 10 minutes, until the top is a deep mahogany and the sides have pulled away from the tin. A skewer in the centre should come out clean.

### Stage 5 - Rest and slice
1. Cool in the tin for 15 minutes. The crust will firm up.
2. Turn out onto a board and cut into wedges or thick slices.

## Notes
- **Polenta texture.** Coarse polenta gives the cake its proper chew; fine cornmeal turns it dense and slightly cakey.
- **Stale bread, not fresh.** Day-old or older bread soaks up the milk without going to mush; fresh bread will pulp.
- **Slow oven.** Do not rush it; the long bake is what builds the crust and dries the centre to a sliceable texture.

## Serving
Warm wedges with shavings of Pecorino di Fossa or aged pecorino, a small bowl of honey, a handful of walnuts, and a glass of young Sangiovese. Makes a one-dish lunch with a green salad.

## Storage
- Keeps 4 days refrigerated; reheats well in a low oven, slice-side down on a tray.
- Toasts excellently the next day on a dry pan.
- Freezes 2 months; thaw overnight before reheating.
