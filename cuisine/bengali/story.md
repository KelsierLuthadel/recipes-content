# Bengali

*East India's distinct kitchen: mustard oil, panch phoran, fish from the Padma and Ganges, and a particular sweet tooth that produced rasgulla, sandesh and the world's most refined milk-sweet tradition.*

## A short history

Bengali cooking is the cuisine of West Bengal (India) and Bangladesh (East Bengal — separate folder for the cross-border state). The shared culinary tradition pre-dates the 1947 partition by millennia. Rice and fish are the spine — Bengalis call themselves *mache-bhate Bangali* ("fish-and-rice Bengali"). The kitchen runs on mustard oil (sharp, pungent, completely unlike the bland oils of most South Asian cooking), panch phoran (a five-spice temper of cumin, fennel, fenugreek, mustard and nigella), and a fearless use of leafy greens, gourds and aubergines.

The colonial era brought the British Raj's tea tradition (Darjeeling grown here) and a fondness for puddings that fused with the Hindu Bengali sweet-shop tradition to produce the staggering range of mishti — rasgulla, sandesh, chamcham, rasmalai, the entire chenna (fresh cheese) family. The Brahmo Samaj 19th-century reform movement codified the everyday Hindu Bengali kitchen as vegetarian-leaning with fish allowed (an arrangement most other Indian Hindu traditions wouldn't recognise).

## Regional variations

- **Kolkata and West Bengal**: the codified mainstream. Mustard-oil-driven, fish-and-rice tradition.
- **Bangladesh (separate cuisine folder)**: heavier on fish, more rural, the bhuna (slow-fried) technique stronger.
- **Sylheti (north-east Bangladesh)**: the diaspora that built the British curry house tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Mustard oil**: sharp, pungent, traditionally heated to smoking and cooled before use.
- **Panch phoran**: cumin + fennel + fenugreek + mustard + nigella seeds, tempered whole.
- **Hilsa (ilish)**: the prized river fish; the national fish across the border.
- **Posto (poppy seeds)**: ground into paste, the base of aloo posto and other vegetable dishes.
- **Chenna (fresh cheese)**: the curd that becomes rasgulla and sandesh.
- **Jaggery**: nolen gur (date palm jaggery) is the winter speciality.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Machher Jhol](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/machher-jhol)**: light fish curry with potato and tomato. The everyday Bengali bowl.
2. **[Chingri Malai Curry](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/chingri-malai-curry)**: prawns in coconut milk. The wedding-feast classic.
3. **[Kosha Mangsho](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/kosha-mangsho)**: mutton slow-fried with onion, ginger, garlic and garam masala till the gravy is dark and thick.
4. **[Doi Maach](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/doi-maach)**: yoghurt-based fish curry with mustard.
5. **[Mangshor Jhol](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/mangshor-jhol)**: lighter goat curry with potato.
6. **[Murgir Jhol](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/murgir-jhol)**: light chicken curry — the home cook's weeknight.
7. **[Chanar Dalna](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/chanar-dalna)**: fresh cheese cubes in a spiced tomato gravy. The signature Bengali vegetarian protein dish.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with bhaat (plain rice — the bowl is the spine of every meal) and end with mishti doi (sweet thick yoghurt) or sandesh. Build a proper Bengali thali: rice, dal, two vegetables, fish curry, a chutney, and a sweet to finish.
