# Bengali

*East India's distinct kitchen: mustard oil, panch phoran, fish from the Padma and Ganges, and a particular sweet tooth that produced rasgulla, sandesh and the world's most refined milk-sweet tradition.*

## A short history

Bengali cooking is the food of West Bengal (India) and Bangladesh (East Bengal, separate folder for the cross-border state). The shared tradition pre-dates the 1947 partition by millennia. The Ganges-Brahmaputra delta is one of the most fertile river plains on earth, and rice has been growing here since the Neolithic; archaeologists have found cultivated rice in the Bengal lowlands dating to roughly 4000 BCE. Rice and fish are the spine; Bengalis call themselves *mache-bhate Bangali* ("fish-and-rice Bengali"). The kitchen runs on mustard oil (sharp, pungent, completely unlike the bland oils of most South Asian cooking), panch phoran (a five-spice temper of cumin, fennel, fenugreek, mustard and nigella), and a fearless hand with leafy greens, gourds and aubergines.

The early medieval Pala dynasty (8th to 12th centuries) ran the largest Buddhist civilisation east of the Himalayas from its capital at Pataliputra, with monasteries that ran a serious vegetarian kitchen. The dairy obsession that produced the Bengali sweet shop has roots in that monastic tradition, refined under the later Hindu Sena kings. From the 13th century the Delhi Sultanate took over, and from the 16th the Mughals made Bengal one of the richest provinces of their empire. Murshidabad's nawabi court kitchens layered Persian and Central Asian technique onto the rice-and-fish base: the slow biryanis, the saffron pulao, the kebab tradition, the rich kalia gravies finished with ghee.

The Portuguese arrived at the mouth of the Hooghly in the early 1500s and brought the most transformative gift in Bengali culinary history. They introduced the chilli (now central to Bengali cooking), the potato, the tomato, papaya, guava, cashew, and, crucially, the technique for splitting milk with acid to make fresh cheese. The chhana (fresh cottage cheese) was forbidden in older Hindu kitchens because deliberately spoiling milk was taboo, but the Portuguese-Bengali fusion broke that rule and unlocked the entire modern mishti tradition. Without chhana there is no rasgulla, no sandesh, no chamcham, no rasmalai. The 19th-century Calcutta sweetmaker Nobin Chandra Das invented the spongy syrup-soaked rasgulla in 1868, and the Bengali sweet shop took its final form.

The British East India Company took Bengal after the 1757 Battle of Plassey and ran it as the headquarters of their Indian empire for almost two centuries. Calcutta became the second city of the empire. The Raj brought the tea tradition (Darjeeling planted out from the 1840s), a fondness for puddings, the chop-and-cutlet tradition that still fills old Calcutta coffee houses (mutton chop, fish fry, mocha cutlet), and a Bengali-bourgeois reform culture (the Brahmo Samaj) that codified the everyday Hindu Bengali kitchen as vegetarian-leaning with fish allowed, an arrangement most other Indian Hindu traditions wouldn't recognise. The 19th-century Bengali Renaissance produced the country's first modern cookbooks, including Pragyasundari Devi's *Amish o Niramish Ahar* (1900), still in print.

Partition in 1947 cut Bengal in half along religious lines. The Hindu-majority west became West Bengal, the Indian state with Kolkata as its capital. The Muslim-majority east became East Pakistan and then, after the 1971 war, independent Bangladesh. Millions of refugees crossed in both directions, and the cuisines diverged on the small things while staying the same in the spine: West Bengal lost the heavy beef bhuna and the kacchi biryani that East Bengal kept, and Bangladesh lost some of the rich Hindu Brahmin vegetarian preparations that survived in Kolkata. The shared kitchen still reads as one cuisine to anyone who grew up in either half.

## Regional variations

- **Kolkata and West Bengal**: the codified mainstream. Mustard-oil-driven, fish-and-rice tradition.
- **Bangladesh (separate cuisine folder)**: heavier on fish, more rural, the bhuna (slow-fried) technique stronger.
- **Sylheti (north-east Bangladesh)**: the diaspora that built the British curry house tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Mustard oil**: sharp, pungent, traditionally heated to smoking and cooled before use.
- **Panch phoran**: cumin, fennel, fenugreek, mustard, nigella, tempered whole.
- **Hilsa (ilish)**: the prized river fish; the national fish across the border.
- **Posto (poppy seeds)**: ground into paste, the base of aloo posto and other vegetable dishes.
- **Chenna (fresh cheese)**: the curd that becomes rasgulla and sandesh.
- **Jaggery**: nolen gur (date palm jaggery) is the winter speciality.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Machher Jhol](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/machher-jhol)**: light fish curry with potato and tomato. The everyday Bengali bowl.
2. **[Chingri Malai Curry](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/chingri-malai-curry)**: prawns in coconut milk. The wedding-feast classic.
3. **[Kosha Mangsho](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/kosha-mangsho)**: mutton slow-fried with onion, ginger, garlic and garam masala until the gravy is dark and thick.
4. **[Doi Maach](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/doi-maach)**: yoghurt-based fish curry with mustard.
5. **[Mangshor Jhol](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/mangshor-jhol)**: lighter goat curry with potato.
6. **[Murgir Jhol](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/murgir-jhol)**: light chicken curry, the home cook's weeknight.
7. **[Chanar Dalna](/recipes/cuisine/bengali/chanar-dalna)**: fresh cheese cubes in a spiced tomato gravy. The signature Bengali vegetarian protein dish.

## Where to go next

Eat everything with bhaat (plain rice, the spine of every meal) and finish with mishti doi (sweet thick yoghurt) or sandesh. Build a proper Bengali thali: rice, dal, two vegetables, fish curry, a chutney, and a sweet to close.
