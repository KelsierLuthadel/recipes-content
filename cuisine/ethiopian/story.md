# Ethiopian

*The Horn of Africa kitchen that's like nothing else on the continent. Injera (the spongy fermented sourdough flatbread) as both plate and utensil, the berbere spice blend that defines the country, and a vegetarian tradition (because of the Ethiopian Orthodox Christian fasting calendar) that's among the world's best.*

## A short history

Ethiopian cooking is one of the world's most distinctive cuisines, shaped by the country's unique history. Ethiopia is one of the only African countries never to have been colonised (briefly Italian-occupied, 1936–1941, but the resistance held); the cuisine evolved without external food disruption. The Ethiopian Orthodox Christian church (since the 4th century CE — one of the oldest continuous Christian traditions) requires fasting (no animal products) on Wednesdays, Fridays and most of Lent — about 200 days a year. The result is a sophisticated vegetarian-vegan tradition that runs parallel to the meat-eating one.

The cuisine sits on three pillars: injera (the giant pancake-bread made from teff, the country's endemic grain — gluten-free, high-protein, fermented for two to three days); berbere (the red spice blend — chilli, fenugreek, ginger, allspice, cinnamon, cumin, coriander); and the wat (stew) family (doro wat, key wat, shiro wat, misir wat — each a different protein in spiced sauce). Meals are communal — everyone sits around a single giant injera, scoops the stews up with their right hand using torn pieces of bread.

The Ethiopian Diaspora (Washington DC has the largest Ethiopian population outside Ethiopia) has carried the cuisine worldwide. Ethiopian coffee — the country is the birthplace of coffee, originally cultivated in the Kaffa region — is among the world's most prized.

## Regional variations

- **Addis Ababa and central highlands**: codified mainstream. Doro wat, kitfo, the urban restaurant tradition.
- **Tigray (north)**: more sourness, the tihlo (barley dumplings) tradition.
- **Sidamo and the south**: the coffee heartland. Kocho (the false-banana root bread).
- **Harar (east)**: closer to Yemeni-Somali. More rice, distinct sauces.
- **Eritrea (separated 1993)**: shared cuisine, distinct national tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Teff**: tiny endemic grain. Fermented into injera.
- **Berbere**: red chilli spice blend. The defining flavour.
- **Mitmita**: hotter chilli powder, used at the table.
- **Niter kibbeh**: spiced clarified butter, the cooking fat.
- **Cardamom, cinnamon, allspice, ginger**: in berbere and elsewhere.
- **Lentils (red, brown, yellow)**: in misir wat, in shiro.
- **Chickpea flour (shiro powder)**: for the shiro stew.
- **Coffee**: the world's birthplace. Drunk three times in succession in the coffee ceremony.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Doro Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/doro-wat)**: chicken slow-cooked in a red onion-and-berbere sauce with niter kibbeh, served with hard-boiled eggs. The national dish.
2. **[Tibs](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/tibs)**: cubed lamb or beef sautéed with niter kibbeh, onion, rosemary and berbere. The everyday meat dish.
3. **[Kitfo](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/kitfo)**: raw or rare-cooked minced beef tartare with mitmita and niter kibbeh. The celebration dish — birthdays, weddings.
4. **[Misir Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/misir-wat)**: red-lentil stew with berbere and niter kibbeh. The most-loved fasting dish.
5. **[Shiro Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/shiro-wat)**: chickpea-flour stew with berbere, garlic and onion. The everyday fasting protein.
6. **[Key Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/key-wat)**: spicy red beef stew with berbere — the beef version of doro wat.
7. **[Atakilt Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/atakilt-wat)**: cabbage, potato and carrot stew with turmeric. The mild vegetarian side that balances the fiery wats.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with tej (honey wine — sweet, slightly fermented) or with Ethiopian coffee from the traditional three-cup ceremony. Eat with the right hand only, scoop stews with torn injera. No utensils. Share one platter. That's the proper Ethiopian meal.
