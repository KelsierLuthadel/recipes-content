# Ethiopian

*The Horn of Africa kitchen that looks like nothing else on the continent. Injera (the spongy fermented sourdough flatbread) doubles as plate and utensil, berbere spice defines the country, and the Ethiopian Orthodox fasting calendar built one of the world's great vegetarian traditions.*

## A short history

Ethiopian cooking is one of the world's most distinctive cuisines and the country's history made it that way. The Ethiopian highlands are one of the eight global centres of plant domestication (Vavilov's term), and the early farmers there domesticated teff (the tiny endemic grain that ferments into injera), niger seed, ensete (the false-banana that still feeds the southern highlands), the coffee plant, and a distinctive local barley. By 1500 BCE the Cushitic-speaking farmers of the highlands were grinding teff, fermenting it for two or three days into a sour batter, and baking it on hot clay pans into the spongy bread that has been the centre of every meal since.

The Aksumite kingdom (roughly 100 to 940 CE) made the highlands one of the great trading powers of the late ancient world, exchanging gold, ivory and frankincense with Rome, Byzantium and the Red Sea ports. King Ezana of Aksum converted to Christianity around 330 CE, making Ethiopia one of the earliest Christian nations on earth and embedding the Ethiopian Orthodox church at the heart of the food calendar. The church asks the faithful to skip animal products on Wednesdays, Fridays and most of Lent. That's roughly 200 days a year, and it built a sophisticated vegetarian-vegan shelf (misir wat, shiro wat, atakilt wat, kik alicha) that runs in parallel to the meat cooking.

The medieval centuries layered in Arab and Indian Ocean trade. Spices arrived through the Red Sea ports: black pepper from India, cardamom and ginger from the Malabar coast, cloves from the Spice Islands, all of them folded into the berbere blend that defines the modern kitchen. The chilli pepper came late, from Portuguese traders in the 16th century via the Indian Ocean route after Spanish ships had carried it east from the Americas, and Ethiopian cooks took to it with such enthusiasm that berbere is now unimaginable without it. Niter kibbeh (clarified butter steeped with cardamom, fenugreek, cinnamon, ginger, garlic) emerged in the same era as the cooking fat that carries the spices.

Ethiopia is one of the only African countries never colonised. The Italians invaded in 1895 and were defeated at Adwa in 1896 (the only decisive African defeat of a European colonial army in the 19th century), tried again in 1935 and occupied the country briefly from 1936 to 1941, but the resistance broke them. So the food evolved on its own terms. The Italian occupation did leave one durable mark, the espresso machine, which sits comfortably alongside the older coffee ceremony in modern Ethiopian cafes.

The kitchen rests on three pillars. Injera, the giant pancake-bread made from teff (gluten-free, high-protein, two-day-fermented). Berbere, the red spice blend of chilli, fenugreek, ginger, allspice, cinnamon, cumin and coriander. And the wat family of stews: doro wat, key wat, shiro wat, misir wat, each a different protein in spiced sauce. Meals are communal. Everyone sits around a single giant injera and scoops the stews up with the right hand, tearing pieces of bread to do it.

The Derg dictatorship (1974 to 1991) and its famines drove waves of emigration. The diaspora carried the food out: Washington DC has the largest Ethiopian population outside Ethiopia, and the restaurants there set the tone for the global spread. Ethiopian coffee, originally cultivated in the Kaffa region (the country is the birthplace of coffee, full stop, and the word "coffee" itself comes from Kaffa), is among the most prized in the world.

## Regional variations

- **Addis Ababa and the central highlands**: the codified mainstream. Doro wat, kitfo, the urban restaurant scene.
- **Tigray (north)**: more sourness, the tihlo (barley dumplings) tradition.
- **Sidamo and the south**: coffee heartland. Kocho, the false-banana root bread.
- **Harar (east)**: closer to Yemeni and Somali cooking. More rice, distinct sauces.
- **Eritrea (separated 1993)**: shared cuisine, distinct national tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Teff**: tiny endemic grain. Fermented into injera.
- **Berbere**: red chilli spice blend. The defining flavour.
- **Mitmita**: hotter chilli powder, used at the table.
- **Niter kibbeh**: spiced clarified butter, the cooking fat.
- **Cardamom, cinnamon, allspice, ginger**: in berbere and beyond.
- **Lentils (red, brown, yellow)**: in misir wat, in shiro.
- **Chickpea flour (shiro powder)**: for the shiro stew.
- **Coffee**: the world's birthplace. Drunk three times in succession in the coffee ceremony.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Doro Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/doro-wat)**: chicken slow-cooked in a red onion and berbere sauce with niter kibbeh, served with hard-boiled eggs. The national dish.
2. **[Tibs](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/tibs)**: cubed lamb or beef sautéed with niter kibbeh, onion, rosemary and berbere. The everyday meat dish.
3. **[Kitfo](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/kitfo)**: raw or rare-cooked minced beef tartare with mitmita and niter kibbeh. The celebration dish for birthdays and weddings.
4. **[Misir Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/misir-wat)**: red-lentil stew with berbere and niter kibbeh. The most-loved fasting dish.
5. **[Shiro Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/shiro-wat)**: chickpea-flour stew with berbere, garlic and onion. The everyday fasting protein.
6. **[Key Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/key-wat)**: spicy red beef stew with berbere. The beef version of doro wat.
7. **[Atakilt Wat](/recipes/cuisine/ethiopian/atakilt-wat)**: cabbage, potato and carrot stew with turmeric. The mild vegetarian side that balances the fiery wats.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with tej (honey wine, sweet, lightly fermented) or with Ethiopian coffee from the three-cup ceremony. Eat with the right hand only, scoop stews with torn injera. No utensils. Share one platter. That's the proper Ethiopian meal.
