# Aigir

![Aigir](resources/aigir.jpg)

*Papua New Guinean sago jelly served with smoked fish: sago flour whisked into a glossy bouncy jelly, eat warm with flakes of smoked river fish, the Highlands rivers-and-forest dish.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 20 minutes

## Overview
Aigir is the everyday sago dish of the Highlands and Sepik river country, where sago flour is whisked vigorously into boiling water until it sets into a translucent springy jelly that looks like a pale grey blancmange but eats like nothing else. The jelly is faintly nutty, neutral and bouncy, and it is always served with something smoky and salty alongside, traditionally smoked river fish (catfish, tilapia, perch) or smoked pork. The contrast is the point: clean starchy jelly, rich smoky protein, a squeeze of lime. A cook stirring a pot of aigir uses real strength to whisk the lumps out; the dish only works if the texture is smooth and glossy.

## Ingredients
- 250 g sago flour (or cassava starch as substitute)
- 1.2 litres water
- 1 tsp salt
- 1 tbsp coconut oil

### To serve
- 300 g smoked fish (smoked mackerel, smoked trout, or smoked tilapia), flaked
- 100 ml thick coconut cream
- 1 lime, cut into wedges
- 1 small red chilli, finely sliced
- 2 spring onions, finely sliced

## Method

### Stage 1 - Mix the slurry
1. In a small bowl, mix the sago flour with 200 ml of cold water from the measured amount.
2. Whisk to a smooth lump-free slurry.

### Stage 2 - Boil
1. Bring the remaining 1 litre of water to a rolling boil with the salt and coconut oil.
2. Lower the heat to medium.

### Stage 3 - Whisk in the slurry
1. Pour the slurry into the boiling water in a thin stream while whisking hard.
2. Keep whisking; the mixture goes cloudy, then begins to thicken in seconds.
3. Switch to a sturdy wooden spoon and stir vigorously, scraping the base of the pan so nothing catches.
4. Cook 4-5 minutes more. The mixture turns from white-cloudy to translucent and glossy, and pulls away from the sides of the pan in a single mass.

### Stage 4 - Set
1. Tip the aigir into a wet shallow bowl or a banana-leaf-lined tray.
2. Smooth the top with a wet spoon.
3. Rest 5 minutes to firm slightly; it should still be wobbly.

### Stage 5 - Serve
1. Scoop wedges of warm aigir into bowls.
2. Top each bowl with a generous pile of smoked fish.
3. Drizzle over the coconut cream.
4. Scatter the spring onion and chilli.
5. Squeeze a lime wedge over before eating.

## Notes
- **The whisking:** Aigir lives or dies on the whisk. Smooth glossy jelly is the goal; lumps mean you whisked too slowly or added the slurry too fast.
- **Sago flour:** True sago flour from the palm is the right thing; cassava starch (also called tapioca starch) is the realistic substitute. Cornflour will not set the same way.
- **The smoked fish:** Smoked mackerel from a supermarket is a fine stand-in for smoked river catfish; the role is salt and smoke against the clean jelly.

## Variations
- **Smoked pork aigir:** Replace the fish with shredded smoked pork or hot-smoked pork belly.
- **Plain aigir:** Some cooks eat aigir as a starch alongside any stew, like polenta.
- **Sweet aigir:** Stir 2 tbsp palm sugar into the water; serve cold with coconut cream and ripe banana as a dessert.
- **Coconut aigir:** Replace 400 ml of the water with coconut milk for a richer version.

## Serving
Eat warm in deep bowls · the smoked fish piled generously on top · lime wedges and chilli on the side · cold karpa or coconut water alongside · the texture is best in the first 30 minutes after cooking.

## Storage
- Eat aigir the day it is made; it firms and dries in the fridge.
- Refrigerate up to 2 days in a covered container; resoften with a splash of water in a covered pan over low heat.
- Do not freeze.
