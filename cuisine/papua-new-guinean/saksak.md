# Saksak

![Saksak](resources/saksak.jpg)

*Papua New Guinean sago pudding with banana and coconut: starch granules from the sago palm cooked in coconut cream with ripe banana until the whole thing sets to a pale, glossy, sweet-savoury jelly that is the islands' everyday starch.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus soak)

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Saksak is the lowland and coastal staple, the dish that turns the starch from the sago palm into food. Sago pearls (or fresh sago flour, if you can get it) are soaked, then cooked slowly in coconut cream with mashed ripe banana until the pearls turn translucent and the whole pot thickens into a glossy, faintly sweet pudding. In the villages it is poured into a banana-leaf-lined tray and left to set, then cut into slabs; at home a shallow bowl does the same job. The flavour is gentle, coconut-rich, lightly sweet from the banana, and the texture is the point: bouncy, slightly chewy, comforting. Eat warm with fish, or cold as a sweet snack.

## Ingredients
- 250 g sago pearls (small pearl tapioca is the closest substitute)
- 600 ml warm water for soaking
- 3 ripe bananas (the spotty-skinned soft ones)
- 400 ml thick coconut cream
- 200 ml coconut milk
- 60 g palm sugar (or soft brown sugar), grated
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1 banana leaf, softened over a flame (optional, for lining)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak the sago
1. Place the sago pearls in a bowl with the warm water; stir once.
2. Leave to soak 20 minutes. The pearls swell and soften but stay distinct.
3. Drain through a sieve and rinse briefly under cold water.

### Stage 2 - Mash the banana
1. Peel the bananas and mash with a fork to a coarse paste.

### Stage 3 - Cook the saksak
1. Pour the coconut milk and 100 ml of the coconut cream into a heavy pan over medium heat.
2. Add the palm sugar and salt; stir until the sugar dissolves.
3. Stir in the drained sago pearls and the mashed banana.
4. Cook gently, stirring constantly, for 15-18 minutes. The pearls turn from white to translucent; the pudding thickens.
5. When the spoon stands briefly in the centre and the mixture pulls away from the sides of the pan, it is ready.

### Stage 4 - Set
1. Line a shallow dish (about 20 cm square) with the softened banana leaf, or oil the dish.
2. Pour the saksak in; smooth the top.
3. Pour the remaining coconut cream over the surface in a thin glossy layer.
4. Rest 20 minutes to set firm enough to cut.

### Stage 5 - Serve
1. Cut into squares or wedges.
2. Eat warm or at room temperature.

## Notes
- **The sago:** Real PNG sago is the starch from the trunk of the sago palm, sold as flour or pearls. Pearl tapioca is the closest supermarket stand-in and behaves the same way.
- **The banana:** Must be very ripe; underripe banana stays starchy and doesn't sweeten the pudding.
- **The set:** Saksak should be firm enough to lift in a slab but still wobbly; not a stiff jelly.

## Variations
- **Plain saksak:** Skip the banana; serve warm alongside smoked fish or pork.
- **Coconut-heavy:** Replace the coconut milk with more coconut cream for a richer pudding.
- **Highlands version:** Some Highlands cooks add grated kaukau (sweet potato) for extra body.
- **Pandan-scented:** A tied knot of pandan leaf in the pan adds a green floral note.

## Serving
Eat warm in wedges as a light meal with smoked fish · cold and sweet as a snack with fruit · alongside the mumu as the starch component · with a spoonful of extra coconut cream on top.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 3 days, covered.
- Reheat gently in a covered dish with a splash of coconut milk.
- Freezing changes the texture; not recommended.
