# Mumu

![Mumu](resources/mumu.jpg)

*Papua New Guinean earth-oven feast: pork, kaukau, banana and aibika greens wrapped in banana leaves and slow-baked over hot stones buried in the ground, the centrepiece of every village ceremony in the Highlands and the islands.*

**Serves:** 8-10

**Prep Time:** 1 hour 30 minutes

**Cook Time:** 3 hours (plus pit-heating time)

## Overview
Mumu is the Papua New Guinean earth-oven, and the word covers the pit, the method and the feast all at once. A pit is dug; a fire of hardwood is laid in it; river stones are heated until they glow white; the food goes on top: a whole or jointed pig, kaukau (sweet potato), green bananas, taro, aibika and other leafy greens, all wrapped in layers of banana leaf. More hot stones go on top, then more leaves, then earth, sealing the steam in. Three hours later the mumu is opened: the pork falls apart, the kaukau is sweet and steam-soft, the greens have wilted into the meat juices. The kitchen-oven version below recreates as much of that as a domestic cook can manage without the pit.

## Ingredients

### The meat
- 2 kg pork shoulder, in 4 large pieces (bone-in if possible)
- 4 tbsp lime juice
- 2 tbsp sea salt
- 1 tbsp grated ginger
- 4 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 tsp ground black pepper

### The starches and greens
- 1.5 kg kaukau (sweet potato), peeled and cut into large chunks
- 4 green bananas (cooking bananas), peeled and halved
- 500 g taro, peeled and chunked (optional, use more kaukau if unavailable)
- 300 g aibika leaves (island spinach), or substitute spinach or kale
- 8 large banana leaves, softened over a flame
- 300 ml thick coconut cream
- 1 thumb of ginger, sliced
- 2 small red chillies, halved (optional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Season the pork
1. Rub the pork pieces all over with lime juice, salt, ginger, garlic and pepper.
2. Cover and refrigerate at least 4 hours, ideally overnight.

### Stage 2 - Soften the banana leaves
1. Pass each leaf briefly over a gas flame, 5 seconds per side, until pliable and darker green. This stops them tearing as you wrap.

### Stage 3 - Build the mumu pan
1. Heat the oven to 150 C.
2. Line a deep roasting tin with overlapping banana leaves, the leaves coming up the sides like a basket.
3. Scatter half the kaukau and half the green banana across the bottom.
4. Lay the pork pieces on top of the starches.
5. Drizzle over half the coconut cream and scatter the ginger slices and chillies.
6. Pile the aibika leaves over the pork.
7. Layer the remaining kaukau, banana and taro on top.
8. Pour the remaining coconut cream over everything.
9. Fold the banana leaves over to enclose the lot, and cover the tin tightly with foil.

### Stage 4 - Slow bake
1. Bake covered for 2 hours 30 minutes.
2. Remove the foil and top leaves for the last 30 minutes to let the surface colour slightly.
3. The pork should pull apart easily with a fork; the kaukau should be tender.

### Stage 5 - Open the mumu
1. Carry the tin to the table; fold the leaves open in front of everyone.
2. Lift the pork onto a wooden board and pull it apart with two forks.
3. Arrange the kaukau, banana, taro and wilted greens around the meat.
4. Spoon the pan juices over the lot.

## Notes
- **The real pit:** A true mumu is built outdoors with heated river stones and takes most of a day; this oven version captures the banana-leaf flavour and the slow steam, which is the best a home cook can do.
- **Banana leaves:** Sold frozen at Pacific, Asian and African shops. They contribute a gentle smoky-grassy aroma that foil cannot replicate.
- **Aibika substitute:** True aibika (island spinach) is hard to find off the islands. Spinach, kale or even silverbeet stand in well; the role is to wilt and catch the meat juices.

## Variations
- **Pig mumu:** A whole suckling pig in a real pit is the ceremonial standard; the joint version above is the home substitute.
- **Chicken mumu:** Substitute a whole chicken or chicken pieces for the pork. Reduce the bake to 1 hour 45 minutes.
- **Fish mumu:** Whole reef fish wrapped in leaves can be tucked in among the starches for the last 45 minutes.
- **Highlands version:** Heavier on kaukau and taro, lighter on banana, and more pig.

## Serving
Serve communally on a wooden board with the pork pulled apart. Eat with the fingers in the traditional way. Lime wedges, fresh chilli and a coconut chutney on the side · soft white rice if you want extra starch · cold water or fresh karpa to drink.

## Storage
- Refrigerate leftovers 3 days. Reheat in a covered dish at 150 C with a splash of water.
- Freezes 1 month; the kaukau texture suffers slightly on thaw.
- The picked pork is excellent the next day in a sandwich with leftover greens.
