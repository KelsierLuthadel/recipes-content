# Sweet Saksak Pudding

![Sweet Saksak Pudding](resources/saksak-pudding.jpg)

*The sweet version of the islands' sago staple: sago pearls cooked slowly in coconut cream with palm sugar until they set into a translucent dark-amber pudding served cold with extra coconut on top.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus soak)

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Sweet saksak is the dessert version of the everyday lowland staple, where the same sago pearls are cooked in coconut cream with grated palm sugar until the whole pot turns a dark glossy amber and the pearls go translucent. The pudding is poured into a banana-leaf-lined tray to set, then cut into slabs and served cold. The flavour is deep and gently smoky from the palm sugar, with the coconut cream rounding the edges, and the texture is the springy bouncy chew that sago always brings. A spoon of thick fresh coconut cream over the top before serving is the standard finish. A Sunday-and-feast pudding rather than an everyday one.

## Ingredients
- 250 g sago pearls (small pearl tapioca is the closest substitute)
- 600 ml warm water for soaking
- 500 ml thick coconut cream
- 300 ml coconut milk
- 150 g palm sugar, grated (or dark muscovado sugar)
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1 pandan leaf, knotted (optional)
- 1 banana leaf, softened (for lining)

### To finish
- 200 ml thick coconut cream
- A pinch of salt

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak the sago
1. Place the sago pearls in a bowl with the warm water; stir once.
2. Soak 20 minutes; the pearls swell and soften.
3. Drain through a sieve, rinse briefly, drain again.

### Stage 2 - Make the syrup
1. In a heavy pan, combine the coconut milk, 300 ml of the coconut cream, the palm sugar, salt and pandan leaf.
2. Warm over medium heat, stirring, until the palm sugar dissolves fully and the mixture is a deep amber.

### Stage 3 - Cook the sago
1. Stir the drained sago pearls into the warm syrup.
2. Cook over medium-low heat, stirring constantly, for 18-20 minutes.
3. The pearls turn from white to translucent dark amber; the pudding thickens until a spoon stands briefly upright.

### Stage 4 - Finish the body
1. Stir in the remaining 200 ml coconut cream.
2. Cook 2 minutes more, stirring; the pudding should be glossy and just thick enough to pour slowly.
3. Lift out and discard the pandan leaf.

### Stage 5 - Set
1. Line a shallow 20 cm square dish with the softened banana leaf, or oil the dish lightly.
2. Pour the saksak in; smooth the top.
3. Cool to room temperature, then refrigerate 2 hours to set firm enough to cut.

### Stage 6 - Serve
1. Cut into squares or wedges.
2. Stir a pinch of salt into the finishing coconut cream.
3. Spoon a generous puddle of coconut cream over each serving.

## Notes
- **Palm sugar:** Real palm sugar (gula melaka, gula jawa) gives the deep amber colour and smoky flavour. Dark muscovado is the closest supermarket stand-in. White sugar will not give the colour or the depth.
- **The set:** The pudding should be firm enough to lift in a slab but still soft and yielding; not a stiff jelly.
- **Banana-leaf lining:** Optional but contributes a subtle grassy aroma to the set pudding.

## Variations
- **Ripe banana saksak:** Mash 2 ripe bananas into the sago at the start of stage 3 for a fruitier version.
- **Coconut-on-coconut:** Serve with fresh young coconut flesh scooped over the top.
- **Mango saksak:** Top the chilled slabs with diced ripe mango.
- **Spiced:** Add 2 cardamom pods and a small piece of cinnamon to the syrup; lift out before pouring.

## Serving
Eat cold in wedges with a generous spoon of salted coconut cream on top · serve at the end of a mumu feast · alongside fresh tropical fruit · with a small cup of strong coffee for the adults.

## Storage
- Refrigerate up to 4 days in a covered container.
- The pudding firms further in the fridge; let it sit at room temperature 15 minutes before serving for the best texture.
- Does not freeze well; the sago turns grainy on thaw.
