# Greek

*The Mediterranean diet's original document. Olive oil, lemon, oregano, feta, lamb and the slow-roast oven; a cuisine that has been writing itself the same way for three thousand years.*

## A short history

Greek cooking is the longest continuously-documented cuisine in Europe. The kitchen begins in the Bronze Age palace economies of Minoan Crete and Mycenae, where Linear B tablets list olive oil by the amphora, sheep by the flock and wine by the vineyard. The Greek pantry of 1500 BCE is recognisably the Greek pantry of today: olives, wheat, barley, sheep, goat, honey, figs, wine, octopus and the long flat bread baked on a hot stone. Homer wrote about olives, wine and roasted lamb in the Iliad and Odyssey. The classical Greeks built their cities around the holy triad of bread, wine and oil; everything else was opson, the savoury "extra" that went with bread. Athenaeus of Naucratis compiled the earliest cookbook anthology (*Deipnosophistae*, c. 200 CE), and Apicius's Roman cookbook borrows heavily from Greek practice. The basic palette has barely shifted since classical times.

The Byzantine centuries (330-1453 CE) made Constantinople the food capital of Europe. The empire kept the classical kitchen alive while folding in Persian, Armenian and Levantine ideas: yoghurt-strained-into-cheese, the layered phyllo pastry, the spice trade that brought cinnamon, cumin and clove into Greek sweets and stews, the rosewater and orange-blossom water that still scent the dessert shelf. Lenten fasting (the Orthodox calendar bans meat and dairy for nearly half the year) drove the development of an enormous vegetarian and pulse-based repertoire that still defines Greek home cooking. Fasolada, gigantes plaki, lentil soup, octopus in wine, the dakos salad and a hundred dolmades variations all carry the Lenten lineage.

The Ottoman occupation (1453-1821) wove Greek cooking together with Turkish, Levantine and Balkan kitchens, and many "Greek" dishes (moussaka, dolmades, baklava, tzatziki) share a name and an idea with their Turkish counterparts. The argument about who invented what is mostly unresolvable, and most likely both kitchens evolved together over four centuries of shared cities. The Columbian Exchange dropped tomatoes, peppers and potatoes onto the Greek plate through the same centuries; the tomato in particular was slow to catch on but eventually rewrote the home kitchen. The pre-tomato moussaka was a different (and probably better balanced) dish, and the post-tomato moussaka is the one the world knows.

Independence in 1821 codified the shelf into a national identity, but the regional variations stayed sharp. The 1922 population exchange with Turkey was a culinary watershed: nearly 1.5 million Greeks expelled from Smyrna, Constantinople and the Pontic coast brought their Anatolian and Pontic cooking back into mainland Greece. Politiki cuisine (the Constantinopolitan kitchen) reshaped Athens and Thessaloniki; the bougatsa, the cumin-spiced meatballs, the imam bayildi and the soutzoukakia all arrived in the 1920s with the refugees. The same decades saw the great Greek diaspora to the United States, Australia, Germany and Britain, which built the taverna abroad and exported moussaka, gyros and feta to the rest of the world.

The 20th century brought the post-war chef-led revival and the global health export. From the 1970s the "Mediterranean diet" concept, championed by US researchers studying Crete after the war, turned Greek food into a global health success story. The current scene sits between the codified taverna tradition, the family table (mageiritsa, the Easter lamb, the village wedding feast) and a growing modern Greek fine dining scene led by Athens. The kitchen is still doing what it did in 500 BCE: olive oil, lemon, oregano, lamb, fish, bread, wine, honey. The continuity is the point.

## Regional variations

- **Athens and central Greece**: the codified national cuisine. Moussaka, pastitsio, the souvlaki shelf.
- **Crete**: olive-oil heavy, herb-forward, the source of the "Mediterranean diet" research. Dakos (rusk-topped salad), kaltsounia (cheese-and-herb pies), raki.
- **Peloponnese**: the lamb country. Spit-roasted lamb (kleftiko), Kalamata olives, the Mani peninsula's syglino (pork preserved in olive oil and orange).
- **Macedonia and the North**: Slavic and Turkish influences. Bougatsa (custard pie), boyo (tomato bread), more chillies and paprika.
- **Aegean Islands**: seafood country. Octopus, fresh sardines, taramasalata, the saganaki cheese tradition.
- **Cyprus** (recipes overlap heavily): halloumi, sheftalia (caul-fat sausage), keftedes. The Greek-Cypriot kitchen has its own shelf.

## Signature ingredients

- **Olive oil**: extra-virgin, used liberally. The fat of Greek cooking and the lubricant of the entire cuisine.
- **Feta**: brined sheep's milk cheese. Crumbled on salads, baked into dishes, served as a cheese course on its own.
- **Oregano (rigani)**: wild Greek oregano is the world benchmark. Dried, on everything.
- **Lemon**: the acid of choice. In avgolemono (egg-lemon sauce), on grilled fish, in marinades.
- **Lamb and goat**: the meat. Whole spit-roasted at Easter; in moussaka, in stews.
- **Yoghurt**: thick, strained, full-fat. Eaten with honey, in tzatziki, as a dessert.
- **Honey**: thyme honey (Crete), pine honey (north). Drizzled on yoghurt, soaked into baklava.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the casserole, the grill, the small plate and the spinach pie. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Moussaka](/recipes/cuisine/greek/moussaka)**: layered aubergine, potato and spiced lamb under a thick béchamel, baked till the top is golden. The Greek showpiece and the gateway to the casserole shelf.
2. **[Pastitsio](/recipes/cuisine/greek/pastitsio)**: layered pasta and spiced lamb under béchamel, baked. The pasta-bake sibling of moussaka.
3. **[Lamb Gyros](/recipes/cuisine/greek/lamb-gyros)**: marinated lamb roasted on a vertical spit, shaved into flatbread with tomato, onion, tzatziki and chips. The Greek street-food standard.
4. **[Pork Souvlaki](/recipes/cuisine/greek/pork-souvlaki)**: pork cubes marinated in lemon, olive oil and oregano, skewered and charcoal-grilled. The taverna classic.
5. **[Spanakopita](/recipes/cuisine/greek/spanakopita)**: spinach, feta and dill in a flaky filo crust, baked till the pastry shatters. The most-loved Greek vegetarian dish.
6. **[Gemista](/recipes/cuisine/greek/gemista)**: tomatoes and peppers stuffed with rice, herbs and minced meat (or just rice for the vegetarian version), slow-baked. The Sunday plate in every Greek family.
7. **[Fasolada](/recipes/cuisine/greek/fasolada)**: white bean soup with tomato, celery, carrot and olive oil. Considered Greece's unofficial national dish: the everyday Greek bowl.

Cook the moussaka and the pastitsio and you have the casserole shelf. The gyros and the souvlaki are the grill. The spanakopita and the gemista are the home table. The fasolada is the everyday soup.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the meze table (taramasalata, tzatziki, melitzanosalata, fava), the avgolemono soup, the kleftiko (slow-roasted lamb), the seafood plates (grilled octopus, calamari), and the dessert canon (baklava, galaktoboureko, loukoumades). Pair everything with an ouzo or a retsina and the cuisine sings.
