# Sopaipillas

![Sopaipillas](resources/sopaipillas.jpg)

*New Mexico's puffed pillow bread: small squares of yeasted flour dough deep-fried till they puff into hollow golden pillows, eaten warm with honey drizzled inside (the traditional sweet way) or alongside savoury New Mexico meals to mop up green chile stew. The Southwest's most iconic bread.*

**Serves:** 12 sopaipillas

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes (plus 1 hour dough resting)

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

## Overview
Sopaipillas (or "sopaipas") are New Mexico's iconic puffed fried bread, distinct from the related Argentine sopaipillas (which use pumpkin in the dough) and the Mexican sopaipas (smaller and often sweeter): a yeasted flour dough (sometimes with a touch of lard or oil) rolled to 5 mm thickness, cut into small squares or triangles, and deep-fried briefly till they puff dramatically into hollow golden pillows. Served warm - at New Mexican restaurants they arrive at the table fresh from the fryer with honey in a small pitcher (the traditional sweet way; tear a corner and pour honey inside). Or served alongside savoury Southwestern meals to mop up green chile stew, carne adovada or red chile sauce. The dish is what every New Mexican kid grows up eating; what every New Mexico restaurant table starts with; what defines Southwest bread.

## Ingredients

- 500 g plain flour
- 1 tablespoon caster sugar (just a touch)
- 7 g instant dried yeast
- 1 teaspoon baking powder
- 1 ½ teaspoons fine sea salt
- 3 tablespoons lard (or vegetable shortening; or olive oil)
- 300 ml warm water (or warm milk for richer)
- Vegetable oil for deep-frying (about 1 litre)

### To serve
- Honey (essential; New Mexico tradition)
- Powdered sugar (for the sweet version)
- Cinnamon (for sprinkling)
- Whipped cream (for fancier dessert version)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make the dough
1. In a wide bowl, whisk together flour, sugar, yeast, baking powder and salt.
2. Rub in the lard or oil.
3. Add warm water gradually; mix to a soft dough.
4. Knead 5-7 minutes till smooth.
5. Place in oiled bowl; cover; rest 1 hour.

### Stage 2 - Roll and cut
1. Knock back the dough.
2. Roll on a lightly floured surface to 5 mm thickness.
3. Cut into squares (6 cm × 6 cm) or triangles.

### Stage 3 - Heat oil
1. Heat the vegetable oil in a deep pan to 175°C (350°F).
2. Test with a small piece; should rise and puff immediately.

### Stage 4 - Fry
1. Lower sopaipillas into the hot oil in batches (don't overcrowd).
2. They should puff up almost immediately into hollow pillows.
3. Press down gently with a slotted spoon to encourage even puffing.
4. Fry 60-90 seconds till golden; turn; fry 60 seconds on the other side.
5. Lift out; drain.

### Stage 5 - Serve immediately
1. Serve warm with honey for drizzling inside (tear off a corner and pour honey through).
2. Or sprinkle with powdered sugar and cinnamon.
3. Or serve alongside savoury meals as bread.

## Notes
- **Puff dramatically:** if they don't puff, the oil is too cool or dough too thick.
- **Eat immediately:** lose their puff as they cool.
- **Honey is traditional:** the New Mexican sweet way.
- **Both savoury and sweet uses.**

## Variations
**Stuffed sopaipillas:** stuff with carne adovada or beans; sopaipilla relleno.
**Cinnamon-sugar:** dust generously with cinnamon sugar; dessert version.
**Smaller (mini):** 3 cm squares; party canapés.
**With chocolate:** drizzle with chocolate sauce; modern variation.

## Serving
Warm with honey at New Mexican restaurants. Or as bread alongside chile stew, carne adovada, enchiladas.

## Storage
- Best eaten immediately.
- Refrigerated 1 day; reheat briefly in oven.
- Don't freeze.
