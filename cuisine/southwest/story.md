# Southwest

*The cuisine of the desert, the Pueblo, the cowboy and the border. Built on roasted chillies, beans, corn, beef and the long open-fire tradition; the kitchen of Arizona, southern Colorado, southern Utah and the borderlands where the United States meets Mexico.*

## A short history

Southwest cooking begins with the Indigenous Puebloan, Hopi, Diné (Navajo), Apache and other peoples who farmed corn, beans and squash (the "three sisters") in the high desert for at least two thousand years. The Ancestral Puebloans built terraced fields and check-dam irrigation at Mesa Verde and Chaco Canyon from around 700 CE, ground corn on metates into the blue, white and red maize varieties that still anchor Pueblo cooking, and dried strips of game and wild greens for the winter pantry. The Diné and Apache, arriving later from the north, grazed sheep and hunted across the same desert; mutton and the slow Navajo broth come straight out of that older shelf.

Spanish colonisation from the 1540s reshaped the kitchen without erasing it. Coronado's expedition pushed up the Rio Grande in 1540, and within a generation Spanish missions had brought wheat, beef, sheep, garlic, onion, sugar, the Catholic feast calendar and the bread oven (horno) that the Pueblos folded into their own villages. The Spanish also brought the techniques of dry-roasting and rehydrating chillies that turned the small Indigenous peppers into the deep, smoky pastes the regional kitchen still leans on. Frijoles cooked slow in a clay olla, pan dulce from a horno, lamb stewed with chile: all of it dates to the mission centuries.

Mexican independence in 1821 reshaped the politics; the Mexican-American War (1846 to 1848) drew the current border but did nothing to separate the kitchens. Families on both sides of the new line carried the same recipes, the same chillies, the same horno bread. The railroad in the late 19th century brought Anglo settlers and the modern Texas-style cattle ranch, and chuck-wagon cooking (beans, biscuits, beef cooked over open fire by cooks travelling with the herds) added a third layer on top of the Indigenous-Spanish foundation. Dude ranches and the early 20th-century Fred Harvey hotel chain along the Santa Fe Railway started selling "Southwest" food to tourists as a marketable regional identity.

Southwest food gets confused with Tex-Mex, but the two are distinct. Tex-Mex is a specific Texas to border-Mexican hybrid (yellow cheese, ground beef, cumin-heavy chili con carne). Southwest extends further west into Arizona and the Pueblo cultures, with less Mexican-American restaurant cooking and more direct Indigenous-Spanish synthesis: Navajo tacos, posole, blue-corn dishes, chimichangas (an Arizona invention from Tucson's El Charro in 1922), Sonoran hot dogs. The mid-20th century saw the rise of fry bread on reservation kitchens, born from the surplus white flour, sugar and lard the government issued to Indigenous peoples on the Long Walk and after; the dish carries a complicated history but became the base of the Navajo taco and a staple of intertribal gatherings.

The chilli is central. Hatch and Chimayó (New Mexico, separate cuisine folder), Sonoran (southern Arizona, northern Sonora), and Pueblo (small, intensely flavoured Indigenous varieties) define the regional palate. Roast and rehydrate dried chillies; do not just chop fresh ones. That technique gives Southwest food its smoky depth. The modern revival from the 1980s onward (Mark Miller's Coyote Cafe in Santa Fe, the rise of the chile-pepper institute at New Mexico State, the Indigenous-led food sovereignty movement reclaiming heritage corn and tepary beans) put Southwest cooking on the national map and back in conversation with its older roots.

## Regional variations

- **Arizona (Tucson, Phoenix)**: Sonoran-influenced. Sonoran hot dogs (bacon-wrapped, in a soft roll, with beans, tomato, onion, mustard, mayo, jalapeño salsa), carne asada, chimichangas (deep-fried burritos, claimed to have been invented in Tucson).
- **Pueblo country (northern Arizona, New Mexico border, Hopi reservation)**: blue corn, posole, the Native American taco shelf.
- **Borderlands (Nogales, Yuma)**: Sonoran-influenced; tacos al carbón, the carne asada cookout, the Mexican-American cantina menu.
- **High desert (Flagstaff, Sedona)**: ranch food. Beef, beans, biscuits, the cowboy tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Dried chillies**: ancho, guajillo, chipotle, the Hatch and Pueblo varieties (when available). Reconstituted into pastes; the colour and the depth.
- **Pinto beans**: cooked slow with onion, garlic and (often) bacon; the everyday side.
- **Blue corn**: the Pueblo and Hopi specialty. In tortillas, in atole, in posole.
- **Beef**: ranch-raised, often grilled over mesquite or oak.
- **Cumin and oregano**: the spice pair under nearly everything.
- **Lime and cilantro**: the finishing pair.
- **Tortillas (corn and flour)**: corn for the Pueblo and Sonoran traditions; flour for the Arizona-Texas borderlands.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the chimichanga, the Navajo taco, the carne adovada and the green chile stew. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Chimichanga](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/chimichanga)**: a deep-fried burrito stuffed with shredded beef (or chicken), beans, rice and cheese, topped with sour cream and salsa. Claimed by Tucson's El Charro Café (1922) as their invention.
2. **[Navajo Tacos](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/navajo-tacos)**: fry bread topped with chilli, beans, lettuce, tomato, cheese and sour cream. The Indigenous reinvention of the taco using fry bread as the base, with a complicated cultural history attached.
3. **[Carne Adovada](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/carne-adovada)**: pork shoulder slow-braised in red chilli paste till the chilli is the sauce and the pork shreds apart. The grand dish of the New Mexico-Arizona borderlands.
4. **[Green Chile Stew](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/green-chile-stew)**: pork shoulder simmered with roasted green chiles, potato, onion and garlic. The everyday Pueblo-country bowl.
5. **[Pueblo Posole](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/pueblo-posole)**: hominy and pork stewed slow with red chile, served with shredded cabbage, radish, lime and oregano. The Pueblo feast soup, eaten at Christmas and harvest time.
6. **[Sonoran Hot Dog](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/sonoran-hot-dog)**: bacon-wrapped hot dog in a Mexican-style bolillo roll, topped with beans, chopped tomato, onion, mustard, mayo and jalapeño salsa. The Tucson street food signature.
7. **[Tortilla Soup](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/tortilla-soup)**: tomato-and-chilli broth with shredded chicken, crispy tortilla strips, avocado, queso fresco and lime. The Sunday-night Mexican-American comfort.

Cook the chimichanga and the Navajo taco and you have the regional headlines. The carne adovada and the green chile stew are the slow braise. The posole is the feast; the Sonoran hot dog the street; the tortilla soup the everyday.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the calabacitas (summer squash sauté), the southwest grilled trout, and the chiltepin-and-tepary-bean Sonoran traditions.
