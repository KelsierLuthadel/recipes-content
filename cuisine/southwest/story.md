# Southwest

*The cuisine of the desert, the Pueblo, the cowboy and the border. Built on roasted chillies, beans, corn, beef and the long open-fire tradition; the kitchen of Arizona, southern Colorado, southern Utah and the borderlands where the United States meets Mexico.*

## A short history

Southwest cooking begins with the Indigenous Puebloan, Hopi, Diné (Navajo), Apache and other peoples who farmed corn, beans and squash (the "three sisters") in the high desert for at least two thousand years. Spanish colonisation from the 1540s brought wheat, beef, sheep, garlic, onion, sugar and the Catholic feast calendar. Mexican independence in 1821 reshaped the politics; the Mexican-American War (1846–48) drew the current border but did nothing to separate the kitchens. The arrival of the railroad in the late 19th century brought Anglo settlers and the modern Texas-style cattle ranch.

Southwest food is sometimes confused with Tex-Mex, but the two are distinct. Tex-Mex is a specific Texas–border-Mexican hybrid (yellow cheese, ground beef, cumin-heavy chili con carne). Southwest extends further west into Arizona and the Pueblo cultures, with less Mexican-American restaurant cooking and more direct Indigenous-Spanish synthesis — Navajo tacos, posole, blue-corn dishes, chimichangas (an Arizona invention), Sonoran hot dogs.

The chilli is central. Hatch and Chimayó (New Mexico, separate cuisine folder), Sonoran (southern Arizona, northern Sonora), and Pueblo (small, intensely-flavoured Indigenous varieties) define the regional palate. Roasting and rehydrating dried chillies — never just chopping fresh ones — is the technique that gives Southwest food its smoky depth.

## Regional variations

- **Arizona (Tucson, Phoenix)**: Sonoran-influenced. Sonoran hot dogs (bacon-wrapped, in a soft roll, with beans, tomato, onion, mustard, mayo, jalapeño salsa), carne asada, chimichangas (deep-fried burritos, claimed to have been invented in Tucson).
- **Pueblo country (northern Arizona, New Mexico border, Hopi reservation)**: blue corn, posole, the Native American taco shelf.
- **Borderlands (Nogales, Yuma)**: Sonoran-influenced; tacos al carbón, the carne asada cookout, the Mexican-American cantina menu.
- **High desert (Flagstaff, Sedona)**: ranch food. Beef, beans, biscuits, the cowboy tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Dried chillies**: ancho, guajillo, chipotle, the Hatch and Pueblo varieties (when available). Reconstituted into pastes; the colour and the depth.
- **Pinto beans**: cooked slow with onion, garlic and (often) bacon; the everyday side.
- **Blue corn**: the Pueblo and Hopi specialty. In tortillas, in atole, in posole.
- **Beef**: ranch-raised, often grilled over mesquite or oak.
- **Cumin and oregano**: the spice pair under nearly everything.
- **Lime and cilantro**: the finishing pair.
- **Tortillas (corn and flour)**: corn for the Pueblo and Sonoran traditions; flour for the Arizona-Texas borderlands.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the chimichanga, the Navajo taco, the carne adovada and the green chile stew. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Chimichanga](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/chimichanga)**: a deep-fried burrito stuffed with shredded beef (or chicken), beans, rice and cheese, topped with sour cream and salsa. Claimed by Tucson's El Charro Café (1922) as their invention.
2. **[Navajo Tacos](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/navajo-tacos)**: fry bread topped with chilli, beans, lettuce, tomato, cheese and sour cream. The Indigenous reinvention of the taco using fry bread as the base — a complicated cultural history attached.
3. **[Carne Adovada](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/carne-adovada)**: pork shoulder slow-braised in red chilli paste till the chilli is the sauce and the pork shreds apart. The New Mexico–Arizona borderlands' grand dish.
4. **[Green Chile Stew](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/green-chile-stew)**: pork shoulder simmered with roasted green chiles, potato, onion and garlic. The everyday Pueblo-country bowl.
5. **[Pueblo Posole](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/pueblo-posole)**: hominy and pork stewed slow with red chile, served with shredded cabbage, radish, lime and oregano. The Pueblo feast soup, eaten at Christmas and harvest time.
6. **[Sonoran Hot Dog](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/sonoran-hot-dog)**: bacon-wrapped hot dog in a Mexican-style bolillo roll, topped with beans, chopped tomato, onion, mustard, mayo and jalapeño salsa. The Tucson street food signature.
7. **[Tortilla Soup](/recipes/cuisine/southwest/tortilla-soup)**: tomato-and-chilli broth with shredded chicken, crispy tortilla strips, avocado, queso fresco and lime. The Sunday-night Mexican-American comfort.

Cook the chimichanga and the Navajo taco and you have the regional headlines. The carne adovada and the green chile stew are the slow braise. The posole is the feast; the Sonoran hot dog the street; the tortilla soup the everyday.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the calabacitas (summer squash sauté), the southwest grilled trout, and the chiltepin-and-tepary-bean Sonoran traditions. The New Mexico cuisine sits in its own folder for the Hatch and Chimayó chilli world; cross-reference with Mexican (separate folder) for the southern half of the borderlands. Pair everything with a Mexican lager (Pacifico, Tecate) or a margarita.
