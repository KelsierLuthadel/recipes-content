# Macedonian (North Macedonia)

*The kitchen of the southern Balkans. Slow-roasted peppers (ajvar is the national condiment), sarma (cabbage rolls), the smoke-grilled lamb, and the bean tradition (tavče gravče) that's both peasant fundamental and national pride.*

## A short history

Macedonian cooking sits between Greek, Bulgarian, Serbian, Albanian and Ottoman traditions. The country (officially North Macedonia since 2019) has been ruled by Byzantines, Ottomans, Serbs and Yugoslavs over the centuries; each layer added something to a fundamentally Mediterranean-Slavic kitchen.

The Ottoman period (1430s–1912) was the longest single influence. The kebab tradition, ajvar (the slow-roasted-pepper-and-aubergine relish that's the national condiment), the burek family, the meze table, and the use of mint and parsley as everyday herbs all date from this era. The post-WWII Yugoslav period (1944–1991) layered on a broader Yugoslav-republican cuisine that mixed Macedonian with Serbian, Croatian and Bosnian traditions.

The independence period (since 1991) has codified a distinct Macedonian national cuisine. Tavče gravče (oven-baked beans with paprika) was named the national dish; ajvar production is a serious autumn ritual in every Macedonian household.

## Regional variations

- **Skopje and the centre**: codified mainstream. Tavče gravče, the city's çevap (skinless minced sausage) tradition.
- **Western (Tetovo, Gostivar)**: closer to Albanian cooking. More yoghurt sauces, more flia.
- **Southern (Bitola, Prilep)**: closer to Greek. Pastrmajlija (the famous flatbread with cured pork), more olive oil.
- **Eastern (Štip)**: more cured-meat tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Red peppers**: the slow-roasted basis of ajvar. The autumn harvest is the social ritual.
- **Aubergine**: roasted alongside peppers; second component of ajvar.
- **Beans (white)**: in tavče gravče.
- **Yoghurt**: dressing soups and stews; the side at every meal.
- **Sirenje (white brine cheese)**: the daily cheese.
- **Paprika**: hot or sweet, smoked.
- **Mint and parsley**: the herb pair.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Tavče Gravče](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/tavce-gravce)**: white beans baked in a clay pot (tava) with paprika, onion and dried red pepper. The national dish.
2. **[Sarma](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/sarma)**: pickled cabbage leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-cooked in tomato broth. The winter Sunday plate.
3. **[Ajvar-Stuffed Peppers](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/ajvar-stuffed-peppers)**: roasted peppers filled with the ajvar relish itself and rice.
4. **[Pastrmajlija](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/pastrmajlija)**: oval flatbread topped with cured pork and egg. The Bitola signature, the Macedonian "pizza".
5. **[Selsko Meso](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/selsko-meso)**: "village meat" — slow-baked pork with mushrooms and cheese in a clay pot.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with rakija (the universal Balkan grape or plum brandy) or with a Skopsko beer. End with tulumba (syrup-soaked fried dough) or with shutka (sweet wheat porridge with walnuts) for the more traditional sweet course.
