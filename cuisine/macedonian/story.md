# Macedonian (North Macedonia)

*The kitchen of the southern Balkans. Slow-roasted peppers (ajvar is the national condiment), sarma (cabbage rolls), smoke-grilled lamb, and the bean tradition (tavče gravče) that's both country fundamental and national pride.*

## A short history

Macedonian cooking sits between Greek, Bulgarian, Serbian, Albanian and Ottoman traditions. The country (officially North Macedonia since 2019) has passed through ancient Macedonian, Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman, Serbian and Yugoslav rule over the centuries; each layer added something to a fundamentally Mediterranean-Slavic kitchen rooted in the Vardar valley and the highland pastures around Lake Ohrid.

The deepest layer is ancient. The land that fed Philip II and Alexander the Great grew the same wheat, barley and grapes it grows today, and the highland shepherds carried the same kashkaval (a hard yellow sheep's-milk cheese) the modern delicatessen counter still slices. Slavic tribes arrived in the 6th and 7th centuries and folded their dairying instincts into the existing Mediterranean shelf, giving the kitchen its love of cabbage, sour cream, and slow-fermented vegetables. The medieval Byzantine and First Bulgarian Empire centuries put olive oil, honey and wheat at the centre of an everyday table that still reads as essentially Byzantine: bread, cheese, olives, oil, a herb-flecked stew.

The Ottoman period (1430s to 1912) was the longest single influence and the most determinative on the modern plate. Five hundred years of Constantinople rule brought the kebab tradition (ćevapi, šiš, the skewer culture of the meyhane), ajvar (the slow-roasted-pepper-and-aubergine relish that became the national condiment once peppers arrived from the New World and met the local roasting tradition), the burek family of filo pies, the meze table with rakija between every plate, baklava and tulumba in the sweet shop, and the use of mint and parsley as everyday herbs. Coffee culture, the kafana, and the syrup-soaked pastry all trace back to that era. The chillies and peppers that define ajvar themselves only arrived after Spanish colonisation of the Americas, working their way through Ottoman trade in the 16th and 17th centuries and finding perfect soil in the Strumica and Resen valleys.

The 19th-century national awakening, when Macedonia chafed under Ottoman rule alongside its Balkan neighbours, codified the country's food identity even before its political one. Cooks in Skopje, Bitola and Ohrid wrote down what their grandmothers cooked, and the rural pepper-roasting ritual became a marker of national identity. The Balkan Wars of 1912 to 1913 carved up Macedonian-speaking territory between Greece, Serbia and Bulgaria; the post-WWII Yugoslav period (1944 to 1991) layered on a broader Yugoslav-republican cuisine that mixed Macedonian with Serbian, Croatian and Bosnian traditions, industrialised the food supply, and built the urban restaurant culture of Skopje on imported European technique sitting over a Balkan base.

Independence since 1991 has codified a distinct Macedonian national cuisine. Tavče gravče (oven-baked beans with paprika) was named the national dish in the 2000s; ajvar-making is a serious autumn ritual in every Macedonian household, with neighbours sharing peppers, roasting drums and the long evenings of jarring. The wine industry around Tikveš has revived an ancient tradition that goes back to Philip II's vineyards, and a quietly excellent farm-to-table movement around Lake Ohrid is putting the country's lake trout, mountain lamb and wild herbs on European plates.

## Regional variations

- **Skopje and the centre**: codified mainstream. Tavče gravče, the city's çevap (skinless minced sausage) tradition.
- **Western (Tetovo, Gostivar)**: closer to Albanian cooking. More yoghurt sauces, more flia.
- **Southern (Bitola, Prilep)**: closer to Greek. Pastrmajlija (the famous flatbread with cured pork), more olive oil.
- **Eastern (Štip)**: more cured-meat tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Red peppers**: the slow-roasted basis of ajvar. The autumn harvest is the social ritual.
- **Aubergine**: roasted alongside peppers; second component of ajvar.
- **Beans (white)**: in tavče gravče.
- **Yoghurt**: dressing soups and stews; the side at every meal.
- **Sirenje (white brine cheese)**: the daily cheese.
- **Paprika**: hot or sweet, smoked.
- **Mint and parsley**: the herb pair.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Tavče Gravče](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/tavce-gravce)**: white beans baked in a clay pot (tava) with paprika, onion and dried red pepper. The national dish.
2. **[Sarma](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/sarma)**: pickled cabbage leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork, slow-cooked in tomato broth. The winter Sunday plate.
3. **[Ajvar-Stuffed Peppers](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/ajvar-stuffed-peppers)**: roasted peppers filled with the ajvar relish itself and rice.
4. **[Pastrmajlija](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/pastrmajlija)**: oval flatbread topped with cured pork and egg. The Bitola signature, the Macedonian "pizza".
5. **[Selsko Meso](/recipes/cuisine/macedonian/selsko-meso)**: "village meat", slow-baked pork with mushrooms and cheese in a clay pot.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with rakija (the universal Balkan grape or plum brandy) or with a Skopsko beer. End with tulumba (syrup-soaked fried dough) or with shutka (sweet wheat porridge with walnuts) for the more traditional sweet course.
