# Iraqi

*The kitchen of Mesopotamia, where farming began. Tigris-and-Euphrates river fish, slow-cooked lamb, the date palm shelf, and a Persian-Ottoman-Arabic synthesis you can taste in every plate.*

## A short history

Iraqi cooking is among the oldest documented cuisines on earth — Sumerian clay tablets from 4,000 years ago record stew recipes that read like modern Iraqi food. The country sits on the original wheat and barley fields; bread baking, the slow lamb stew, the date palm and the pomegranate all trace their cultivated origins to Mesopotamia.

The cuisine absorbed Persian (the rice tradition, baghali polo, the use of dried lime), Ottoman (the meze table, the spice rack, the kebab), and Arabian (the use of cumin and coriander as everyday spices) influences. The Iraqi Jewish kitchen (one of the oldest Jewish communities in the world before the 1950s exodus) contributed kubbeh, sambusak and the sabbath stew known as t'beet.

Decades of war and sanctions limited Iraq's culinary outreach in the late 20th century. The diaspora kitchens in Detroit, London and Stockholm preserve the codified tradition. Internally, the everyday food remains close to its Mesopotamian roots: rice, bread, fish, lamb and dates.

## Regional variations

- **Baghdad and central**: the codified mainstream. Masgouf grilled river fish, quzi (lamb-and-rice celebration plate), the Tigris dolma tradition.
- **Basra and the south**: closer to Kuwait and the Gulf. More seafood, harees, machboos.
- **Mosul and the north**: closer to Syrian-Turkish cooking. Kibbeh-heavy.
- **Kurdistan (north-east)**: hill cooking. Lamb, yoghurt, the dolma traditions overlap with Persian-Azeri.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rice (timman)**: long-grain, often turned red with tomato or yellow with turmeric.
- **Dates**: in cooking (date syrup as a sweetener), as a snack, in the morning coffee tradition.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Slow-cooked, grilled, in stews and dolmas.
- **Cardamom and rose water**: the perfume of Iraqi sweets and coffee.
- **Dried lime (loomi)**: the souring agent, dropped whole into stews.
- **Tamarind**: in fish dishes and meat marinades.
- **Pomegranate molasses**: in stews and as a finishing flourish.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Masgouf](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/masgouf)**: Tigris carp split butterfly-style, marinated with tamarind, turmeric and salt, then grilled vertically by an open wood fire. The national dish.
2. **[Quzi](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/quzi)**: whole roasted lamb on a bed of spiced rice with raisins, almonds and pine nuts. The wedding-feast plate.
3. **[Dolma Iraqi](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/dolma-iraqi)**: mixed dolma — vine leaves, peppers, courgettes, aubergines, all stuffed with spiced rice and minced lamb, simmered in tomato-tamarind sauce.
4. **[Bamia Iraqi](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/bamia-iraqi)**: lamb-and-okra stew with tomato and dried lime.
5. **[Timman Ahmar](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/timman-ahmar)**: red rice with tomato paste and warm spices. The everyday side.
6. **[Klaicha](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/klaicha)**: date-stuffed pastry, the Eid sweet that every Iraqi grandmother makes.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with chai (sweet, dark, in tiny glasses) or with arak (the aniseed spirit, watered down till milky). Build an Iraqi mezze with kubba, baba ghanoush, hummus, tabbouleh and finish with klaicha and dates.
