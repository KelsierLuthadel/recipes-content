# Iraqi

*The kitchen of Mesopotamia, where farming began. Tigris-and-Euphrates river fish, slow-cooked lamb, the date palm shelf, and a Persian-Ottoman-Arabic synthesis you can taste in every plate.*

## A short history

Iraqi cooking is among the oldest documented cuisines on earth. Sumerian clay tablets from 4,000 years ago, the Yale Babylonian Collection's three tablets from around 1750 BCE, record stew recipes that read like modern Iraqi food: lamb simmered with garlic, leek, coriander, cumin and sour milk, finished with a flatbread crumb. The country sits on the original wheat and barley fields where agriculture itself was invented; bread baking, the slow lamb stew, the date palm and the pomegranate all trace their cultivated origins to Mesopotamia. The Sumerians, Akkadians, Babylonians and Assyrians who ran the river civilisations between the Tigris and Euphrates ate broadly the same way for three thousand years.

The Abbasid Caliphate (750-1258 CE) made Baghdad the kitchen of the medieval world. The 10th-century cookbook *Kitab al-Tabikh* by Ibn Sayyar al-Warraq records hundreds of court recipes, and the Abbasid kitchen pulled together Persian rice technique, Indian spices, Byzantine pastry and the Arab roasted-meat tradition into a single sophisticated cuisine. Sugar, citrus, aubergine, spinach and rice all moved westward from this court to Europe, often through Iraqi cooks. The Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 destroyed the libraries but the recipes survived in family kitchens.

The Ottoman centuries (1534-1918) layered the meze table, the spice rack, the kebab and the coffee tradition onto the older Persianate base. Ottoman administrators in Baghdad and Basra brought baklava, the dolma family, kibbeh, and the Turkish-style mixed grill. Persian influence kept running too, especially in the use of dried lime, pomegranate molasses, rice cooked with herbs and the sour-sweet stew balance. Arab Bedouin contributions added cumin and coriander as everyday spices, the bread-baking traditions of the desert, and the date-palm-and-coffee hospitality ritual.

The Iraqi Jewish kitchen, one of the oldest Jewish communities in the world (continuous since the Babylonian Exile of 586 BCE) before the 1950s exodus, contributed kubbeh, sambusak and the sabbath stew t'beet. At its 1948 peak the community numbered 150,000 in Baghdad alone; after the mass migration to Israel only a handful remained. Iraqi-Jewish cooking now lives in Tel Aviv and London more than in Baghdad, but the dishes (kubbeh hamusta, sabich, amba pickle) shape modern Israeli food.

Decades of war and sanctions limited Iraq's culinary outreach through the late 20th century. The Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988), the Gulf War, the 2003 invasion and the sectarian violence that followed scattered cooks across the world. The diaspora kitchens in Detroit, London, Stockholm and Sydney keep the tradition codified, and the slow post-2010 stabilisation has begun to support a new Baghdad restaurant scene. Inside the country, the everyday food stays close to its Mesopotamian roots: rice, bread, fish, lamb and dates.

## Regional variations

- **Baghdad and central**: the mainstream. Masgouf grilled river fish, quzi (lamb-and-rice celebration plate), the Tigris dolma tradition.
- **Basra and the south**: closer to Kuwait and the Gulf. More seafood, harees, machboos.
- **Mosul and the north**: closer to Syrian-Turkish cooking. Kibbeh-heavy.
- **Kurdistan (north-east)**: hill cooking. Lamb, yoghurt, dolma traditions that overlap with Persian-Azeri.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rice (timman)**: long-grain, often turned red with tomato or yellow with turmeric.
- **Dates**: in cooking (date syrup as a sweetener), as a snack, in the morning coffee tradition.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Slow-cooked, grilled, in stews and dolmas.
- **Cardamom and rose water**: the perfume of Iraqi sweets and coffee.
- **Dried lime (loomi)**: the souring agent, dropped whole into stews.
- **Tamarind**: in fish dishes and meat marinades.
- **Pomegranate molasses**: in stews and as a finishing flourish.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Masgouf](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/masgouf)**: Tigris carp split butterfly-style, marinated with tamarind, turmeric and salt, then grilled vertically by an open wood fire. The national dish.
2. **[Quzi](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/quzi)**: whole roasted lamb on a bed of spiced rice with raisins, almonds and pine nuts. The wedding-feast plate.
3. **[Dolma Iraqi](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/dolma-iraqi)**: mixed dolma. Vine leaves, peppers, courgettes, aubergines, all stuffed with spiced rice and minced lamb, simmered in tomato-tamarind sauce.
4. **[Bamia Iraqi](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/bamia-iraqi)**: lamb-and-okra stew with tomato and dried lime.
5. **[Timman Ahmar](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/timman-ahmar)**: red rice with tomato paste and warm spices. The everyday side.
6. **[Klaicha](/recipes/cuisine/iraq/klaicha)**: date-stuffed pastry, the Eid sweet every Iraqi grandmother makes.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with chai (sweet, dark, in tiny glasses) or arak (the aniseed spirit, watered down until milky). Build an Iraqi mezze with kubba, baba ghanoush, hummus and tabbouleh, and finish with klaicha and dates.
