# Trinidadian

*The most genuinely multi-ethnic cuisine in the Caribbean: Indo-Trinidadian curry tradition, Afro-Caribbean stews, Chinese-Trini chow mein, Levantine kibbeh and shawarma, Spanish-Latin pastel, all working off one small twin-island kitchen.*

## A short history

Trinidad's food story starts with the Amerindians. The Arawak and Carib peoples who farmed and fished the island for some seven thousand years before contact had already worked out the indigenous shelf: cassava grated, pressed and baked into thin flatbreads on a clay griddle; smoked fish on a green-wood barbacoa frame (the word the Spanish carried home as *barbecue*); corn ground for pepperpot stews; and the chili-and-annatto seasoning paste that still tints Trini stews orange. Cassareep, the bitter cassava reduction that anchors pepperpot to this day, is pure Amerindian technique surviving in the modern kitchen.

Columbus arrived in 1498 and Spain claimed the island, but Madrid mostly ignored it. For nearly three centuries the place stayed thinly settled, with a handful of Spanish encomenderos and a depleted indigenous population planting cocoa under shade trees. The Columbian Exchange ran through Trinidad in both directions: cocoa and pineapple left for Europe; rice, sugar cane, citrus, mango, breadfruit and the plantain arrived. The 1783 Cedula of Population invited French Catholic planters and their enslaved workers from the wider Caribbean, who arrived in numbers and brought sugar, cocoa, and creole cooking. Britain took the island in 1797 and ran it as a sugar colony built on enslaved African labour, which laid down the West African cooking instincts that still show up everywhere: callaloo greens slow-stewed in coconut, one-pot rice dishes, scotch bonnet heat, the use of green herbs as a base seasoning rather than a garnish, and the burnt-sugar method that defines Trinidadian stewed chicken.

Emancipation came in 1838, and the planters replaced enslaved labour with East Indian indentured workers under contract. Between 1845 and 1917, roughly 145,000 Indians arrived, mostly from Bihar and the eastern Gangetic plain. They brought curry, dhal, roti, ghee, and the tandoor logic of cooking bread on a flat iron tawa. Nowhere else in the western hemisphere did Indian cooking land this deeply, and Trinidadian curry, doubles, dhal puri, and buss-up-shut are the result. The mid-1800s also brought Chinese migrants (mostly from Guangdong, contracted as labour and shopkeepers), who folded in chow mein and char siu, and from the 1890s a steady Syrian and Lebanese arrival, fleeing the late Ottoman collapse, who quietly added kibbeh, hummus, and shawarma to the street-food rotation. Portuguese Madeirans, Venezuelans crossing the narrow Gulf of Paria, and a small Jewish community each left their own dishes on the table.

The 20th century took these threads and made them national. The 1910s oil boom shifted the economy off sugar and put money into urban Port of Spain, where a new working class wanted fast, filling, walkable food. Doubles was invented in the 1930s by Indian street vendors in Princes Town who folded two bara around channa to keep customers' hands clean; it became the universal breakfast. The 1940s American military presence at Chaguaramas brought hot dogs and ketchup into the Trini condiment cabinet, which is why ketchup turns up in places no Indian or West African cook would think to put it. Independence in 1962 and the codification of Carnival as the national cultural event built a calendar around food: pelau on the savannah at fete time, bake-and-shark at Maracas on a Sunday, curry duck cooked outdoors in big pots for cricket matches and limes.

The result is the deepest curry tradition outside India in the western hemisphere, layered onto African stew technique, scotch bonnet heat, and a street-food culture built around doubles, bake-and-shark, and pholourie. Three anchors run through everything: green seasoning (the herb-and-pepper paste blended fresh and rubbed into meat the night before), Trini curry powder (mild, turmeric-forward, yellow rather than red), and the one-pot stewed-and-burnt-sugar method that browns chicken or beef in caramelised sugar before the liquid goes in. The modern diaspora (Brooklyn, Toronto, London) has carried Trini food worldwide, and every major Caribbean carnival now has a doubles cart somewhere on the parade route.

## Regional variations

Trinidad is small, so the splits run along community lines more than geography:

- **Indo-Trinidadian (the Caroni belt, Chaguanas, central plain)**: curry duck cooked outdoors over wood, doubles for breakfast, dhal puri roti stuffed with split-pea flour, sada roti for everyday, and paratha rolled and beaten on the tawa until it shreds, which is why locals call it buss-up-shut (busted-up shirt).
- **Afro-Trinidadian (Belmont, Laventille, Port of Spain east)**: pelau with pigeon peas and burnt-sugar chicken, callaloo cooked down with okra and coconut milk, stew chicken, oildown.
- **Tobago island cooking**: less curry, more salt-fish and breadfruit, conch dishes (curried, or in a chowder), crab and dumpling as the signature plate.
- **Chinese-Trini**: chow mein, fried rice, char siu pork that often turns up tucked inside a doubles wrap or alongside pelau at a Sunday lunch.
- **Levantine-Trini**: the corner-shop kibbeh, shawarma wrapped in roti instead of pita, hummus on the side.
- **Maracas Bay and the north coast**: bake-and-shark, the fried-bread sandwich loaded at a self-serve condiment bar with tamarind, chadon beni, pineapple, garlic sauce.

Drive thirty minutes in any direction and the dominant cooking shifts; eat across a single Saturday and you have crossed three continents.

## Signature ingredients

- **Green seasoning**: the everyday wet rub. Chadon beni, chive, thyme, garlic, onion, scotch bonnet, sometimes celery and parsley, blended to a coarse paste. Most cooks make a jar weekly and rub it into meat the night before.
- **Trini curry powder**: mild, turmeric-forward, fenugreek-leaning. Yellow, not red. Bloomed in oil with garlic and chadon beni before the meat goes in.
- **Chadon beni** (culantro, recao, ngo gai): the herb. Looks like a serrated dandelion leaf, tastes like cilantro turned up to eleven, holds its flavour through long cooking where cilantro fades.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the pepper. Fruity, floral, hot. Often left whole in a pot for perfume, pulled out before serving.
- **Geera (roasted cumin) and amchar masala**: geera for [pelau](pelau.md) and chutneys, amchar (a green mango masala with mustard seed, fenugreek, geera) for pickles and curry duck.
- **Salt cod**: legacy of the colonial salt-fish trade, still the filling for buljol and accras.
- **Pigeon peas (gungo peas)**: the rice-and-peas legume of choice on the island. Fresher and greener than red kidney beans.
- **Coconut**: milk for callaloo and oildown, grated for sweetbread, the oil for [doubles](snacks/doubles.md) bara dough.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Doubles](snacks/doubles.md)**. The national breakfast: two soft turmeric-yellow flatbreads (bara) folded around curried chickpeas (channa), with tamarind, cucumber, chadon beni, and a chosen heat level from slight to slight-slight to plenty. Start here. Every Trini you ever meet has an opinion on whose stall does it best.
2. **[Bunjay curry chicken](bunjay-curry-chicken.md)**. The home-kitchen Sunday curry. Green-seasoned chicken bunjayed (cooked dry with the lid on until the spices grip the meat) before any liquid joins. Mild, yellow, deeply savoury, the curry that proves Trini curry is its own thing rather than a copy of India.
3. **[Buss-up-shut roti curry](buss-up-shut-roti-curry.md)**. Paratha roti beaten on the tawa until it shreds into ragged flaky strips that look like a torn shirt, served with curry channa and aloo or curry goat. The bread is the cutlery: tear, scoop, eat.
4. **[Pelau](pelau.md)**. The single-pot rice dish that defines weeknight Trini cooking. Brown sugar caramelised black in hot oil, chicken seared in the bitter caramel, then rice, pigeon peas, and coconut milk all in. The burnt-sugar method is the trick; without it, it is just rice and peas.
5. **[Callaloo](side-dishes/callaloo.md)**. Dasheen (taro) leaves stewed down with okra, coconut milk, onion, garlic, thyme, and a whole scotch bonnet floating on top. The Sunday-lunch side that goes with anything: stew chicken, pelau, crab.
6. **[Bake-and-shark](bake-and-shark.md)**. Maracas Beach in a sandwich: fried bake (puffed bread), a piece of seasoned shark or kingfish, then you walk the condiment bar and load tamarind chutney, chadon beni sauce, garlic sauce, pineapple, slaw, hot pepper. Beach food at its most assured.
7. **[Pholourie](snacks/pholourie.md)**. Split-pea fritters with cumin and turmeric, fried in golf-ball rounds, served with tamarind or mango chutney. The roadside snack you eat from a paper bag while you wait for the doubles man.

Cook your way through those seven and you have the architecture: the bara dough, the green seasoning, the bunjay step, the burnt-sugar method, the coconut-and-okra stew, the fried-bread sandwich, the split-pea fritter. The rest of the cuisine, from [curry goat](trinidadian-curry-goat.md) to [aloo pie](snacks/aloo-pie.md) to [curry smelts](curry-smelts.md), uses the same moves in different combinations.
