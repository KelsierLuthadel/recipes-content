# Serbian

*The kitchen of the central Balkans. Ćevapi (small grilled minced-meat sausages, the universal Balkan street food), the pljeskavica burger, the slow-roasted lamb, stuffed cabbage, and the kafana (tavern) culture that revolves around plum brandy, grilled meat and song.*

## A short history

Serbian cooking sits at the heart of the Balkans. The basic shelf — bread (the pogača flatbread, the burek pastry), grilled meats (the Balkan kebab tradition perfected here as ćevapi), kajmak (the rich clotted cream), sour cabbage (in sarma and as kiselo zelje), peppers (paprika, fresh and dried), and the slow-stewed gulaš (goulash) tradition — reflects the country's Ottoman, Habsburg and Slavic crossroads position.

The Ottoman period (1389–1804) embedded the grilled-meat-and-flatbread tradition. The Habsburg-Austrian influence (especially in Vojvodina, the northern province) brought the schnitzel-and-strudel tradition. The Yugoslav era (1918–1992) created a shared South Slavic cuisine with Croatian, Bosnian, Macedonian and Slovenian overlaps.

Ćevapi (or ćevapčići) — small skinless minced-meat sausages charcoal-grilled — are the most-loved everyday food in Serbia (and across the broader Balkans). Eaten in a folded somun flatbread with chopped raw onion and kajmak; the proper plate is 5-10 little sausages, not one big serving.

## Regional variations

- **Belgrade and central Serbia**: codified mainstream. Ćevapi, pljeskavica, sarma.
- **Vojvodina (north)**: Habsburg-Hungarian influence. Goulash, strudels, fish from the Danube.
- **Southern Serbia (Niš, Leskovac)**: the famous Leskovac grill scene — ćevapi capital, more peppered, more chilli.
- **Šumadija (central south)**: rural cooking. Sour cabbage, slow-roasted lamb, the rakija distilleries.

## Signature ingredients

- **Minced meat (beef-and-lamb mix)**: for ćevapi and pljeskavica.
- **Kajmak**: rich clotted cream-cheese. On ćevapi, on bread.
- **Ajvar**: roasted-pepper-and-aubergine relish.
- **Paprika (sweet and hot)**: in everything.
- **Sour cabbage**: in sarma, with pork.
- **Plum brandy (šljivovica)**: the universal spirit.
- **Pljevaljski sir (Pljevlja cheese)**: brined white cheese.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Serbian Ćevapi](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/serbian-cevapi)**: small skinless minced-meat sausages (beef-and-lamb), charcoal-grilled, served on somun flatbread with chopped raw onion, kajmak and ajvar.
2. **[Pljeskavica](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/pljeskavica)**: large flat minced-meat patty (the Serbian "burger") — beef-and-lamb-and-pork, grilled. Served in lepinje (a soft flatbread) with onion, kajmak and ajvar.
3. **[Karađorđeva Šnicla](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/karadjordjeva-snicla)**: pork (or veal) cutlet rolled around kajmak and breaded, deep-fried. The "Karageorge schnitzel" named after the 19th-century Serbian leader.
4. **[Muckalica](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/muckalica)**: pork (and sometimes mixed meats) slow-stewed with peppers, tomato, onion and chillies.
5. **[Serbian Sarma](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/serbian-sarma)**: sour cabbage leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork-and-beef, slow-baked in tomato broth. The Christmas and Slava (family saint's day) plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with šljivovica (plum brandy, often homemade) or with Jelen pivo (the national lager). End with palačinke (Balkan crepes filled with eurokrem, jam or walnuts) or with vasina torta (the dark walnut cake).
