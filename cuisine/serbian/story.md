# Serbian

*The kitchen of the central Balkans. Ćevapi (small grilled minced-meat sausages, the universal Balkan street food), the pljeskavica burger, slow-roasted lamb, stuffed cabbage, and the kafana (tavern) culture that revolves around plum brandy, grilled meat and song.*

## A short history

Serbian cooking sits at the heart of the Balkans. The deep base goes back to the Slavic tribes who moved into the Balkans in the 6th and 7th centuries, settling the valleys of the Danube, Sava and Morava and finding a country that grew wheat, kept pigs in the oak forests and pastured sheep on the limestone hills. The early Slavic kitchen ran on porridge (kaša), sour cabbage put down in barrels for winter, slow-roasted lamb (jagnjetina) turned on a spit for celebrations, smoked pork from the autumn pig-killing, and the rakija (fruit brandy, especially from plums) that's been distilled in Serbian villages for at least a thousand years. The hearth-bread pogača and the sour-milk-and-cream traditions (kajmak, kiselo mleko) both trace back to this period.

The medieval Serbian kingdom (especially under the Nemanjić dynasty from 1166 to 1371) reached its peak under Tsar Dušan and built a refined court cuisine influenced by Byzantium: more spices, more honey, more roast game, more elaborate stuffed pastries. The Orthodox monastic tradition (Studenica, Sopoćani, Mileševa, Dečani) codified the fasting calendar, which still shapes Serbian eating: roughly 180 days of the year are technically meatless in the traditional calendar, which means the bean stews (prebranac), the spinach pies, the fish dishes and the pickled-vegetable shelf are as central as the grills. The fasting kitchen is still the most underrated part of Serbian food.

The Ottoman period (1389 to roughly 1804, after the Battle of Kosovo) embedded the grilled-meat-and-flatbread tradition that still defines the everyday kitchen. The Turks brought ćevapi, sarma (stuffed sour cabbage), börek-style burek, baklava, Turkish coffee, ajvar (the roasted-pepper relish that's now a national emblem), the love of paprika and aubergine, and the kafana (tavern) culture itself. The peppers and tomatoes that anchor the modern shelf only arrived during this same Ottoman span, carried east from the Columbian Exchange via Habsburg and Mediterranean trade.

Habsburg-Austrian influence (Vojvodina passed to the Austrians by treaty in 1718) brought the schnitzel-and-strudel shelf, the goulash tradition, the heavy use of sour cream and the dark rye bread of the northern province. The 19th-century Serbian uprisings against the Ottomans (1804, 1815) restored Serbian statehood gradually and codified a national cuisine, with the Karađorđeva šnicla (a kajmak-stuffed pork or veal cutlet, named after Karađorđe, the leader of the First Serbian Uprising) cooked up at the Hotel Moskva in Belgrade in the 1950s as a deliberate national showpiece.

The Yugoslav era (1918-1992) created a shared South Slavic cuisine with Croatian, Bosnian, Macedonian and Slovenian overlaps. The post-war socialist period spread restaurant ćevapi from the Bosnian and Macedonian centres (Sarajevo, Banja Luka, Tetovo) across the federation, and Leskovac in southern Serbia turned into the country's grilling capital with a roštilj (grill) festival that still runs every August. The wars of the 1990s scattered Yugoslav cooks across Europe and North America, where the kafana scene rebuilt itself in Chicago, Vienna and Sydney.

Ćevapi (or ćevapčići), small skinless minced-meat sausages charcoal-grilled, are the most-loved everyday food in Serbia and across the broader Balkans. Eat them in a folded somun flatbread with chopped raw onion and kajmak; the proper plate is 5-10 little sausages, not one big serving. The home kitchen today reads the same as it has for centuries: the rakija on the table, the kajmak in a clay pot, the grill out the back, the slow-cooked sarma waiting for Sunday.

## Regional variations

- **Belgrade and central Serbia**: the mainstream. Ćevapi, pljeskavica, sarma.
- **Vojvodina (north)**: Habsburg-Hungarian influence. Goulash, strudels, fish from the Danube.
- **Southern Serbia (Niš, Leskovac)**: the famous Leskovac grill scene; ćevapi capital, more peppered, more chilli.
- **Šumadija (central south)**: country cooking. Sour cabbage, slow-roasted lamb, the rakija distilleries.

## Signature ingredients

- **Minced meat (beef-and-lamb mix)**: for ćevapi and pljeskavica.
- **Kajmak**: rich clotted cream-cheese. On ćevapi, on bread.
- **Ajvar**: roasted-pepper-and-aubergine relish.
- **Paprika (sweet and hot)**: in everything.
- **Sour cabbage**: in sarma, with pork.
- **Plum brandy (šljivovica)**: the universal spirit.
- **Pljevaljski sir (Pljevlja cheese)**: brined white cheese.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Serbian Ćevapi](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/serbian-cevapi)**: small skinless minced-meat sausages (beef-and-lamb), charcoal-grilled, served on somun flatbread with chopped raw onion, kajmak and ajvar.
2. **[Pljeskavica](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/pljeskavica)**: a large flat minced-meat patty (the Serbian "burger"), beef-and-lamb-and-pork, grilled. Served in lepinje (a soft flatbread) with onion, kajmak and ajvar.
3. **[Karađorđeva Šnicla](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/karadjordjeva-snicla)**: pork (or veal) cutlet rolled around kajmak, breaded and deep-fried. The "Karageorge schnitzel", named after the 19th-century Serbian leader.
4. **[Muckalica](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/muckalica)**: pork (and sometimes mixed meats) slow-stewed with peppers, tomato, onion and chillies.
5. **[Serbian Sarma](/recipes/cuisine/serbian/serbian-sarma)**: sour cabbage leaves stuffed with rice and minced pork-and-beef, slow-baked in tomato broth. The Christmas and Slava (family saint's day) plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with šljivovica (plum brandy, often homemade) or with Jelen pivo (the national lager). Finish with palačinke (Balkan crepes filled with eurokrem, jam or walnuts) or with vasina torta (the dark walnut cake).
