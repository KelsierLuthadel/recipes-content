# Slatko

![Slatko](resources/slatko.jpg)

*Whole small fruits cooked slowly in a thick syrup until they go translucent, served by the spoonful with a glass of cold water. The Serbian welcome offered to every guest who crosses the threshold.*

**Serves:** about 3 small jars

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes (plus overnight soaking)

**Cook Time:** 1 hour

## Overview
Slatko (literally, the sweet thing) is the spoon-preserve at the heart of Serbian hospitality. When a guest arrives at a traditional household, they're not asked what they want; a small saucer arrives with a spoonful of slatko on it and a glass of cold water alongside. The guest eats the spoon of slatko slowly, drinks the water, and only then does the conversation begin. The fruit is whole or in pieces (slivers of green walnut, baby strawberries, sour cherries, wild rose hips, quince, blueberry, white cherry) cooked very slowly in a syrup made one-to-one with sugar until the fruit goes translucent and the syrup turns to a thick clear glaze. The result is intensely sweet, properly fruity, and meant to be eaten one careful spoon at a time. This recipe uses sour cherries (višnje), the classic; rotate fruit through the year.

## Ingredients

### Slatko
- 1 kg sour cherries, stems and pits removed (or wild blueberries, small strawberries, quince cubes)
- 1 kg granulated sugar (one-to-one ratio with the fruit weight)
- 1 lemon, juiced
- 1 small piece of vanilla pod, split (or a thin curl of lemon peel)

### Equipment
- 3 small clean glass jars with new lids (around 300 g each)
- A wide heavy preserving pan
- A skimmer

## Method

### Stage 1 - Macerate
1. Layer the pitted cherries and the sugar in a wide bowl or directly in the preserving pan.
1. Cover loosely; leave overnight or at least 8 hours at room temperature. The sugar draws out the juice and dissolves into a syrup.

### Stage 2 - Bring to a slow boil
1. Set the pan on low heat. Stir gently with a wooden spoon until the remaining sugar dissolves; don't break the fruit.
1. Raise the heat to medium; bring to a slow rolling boil.
1. Skim the foam off the top as it rises, three or four times in the first 15 minutes.

### Stage 3 - Cook slow
1. Add the lemon juice and vanilla.
1. Cook on a low rolling bubble for 45 to 60 minutes. The fruit will start translucent and bright, then turn glossy and slightly darker. The syrup will thicken to a heavy pour.
1. Test: drop a spoonful onto a chilled saucer; it should set to a soft, jam-but-looser consistency. Pull off the heat the moment it does.

### Stage 4 - Jar
1. Spoon the hot slatko into clean dry jars, making sure every jar gets fruit and syrup.
1. Seal while hot; invert for 5 minutes, then turn upright. The lids should pop down as they cool.

## Notes
- **Whole fruit.** Slatko is judged on the fruit staying whole and intact; cherries should look like cherries, not jam. Gentle stirring, slow boil, no breaking.
- **The maceration step.** Don't skip the overnight soak; it draws the juice out without crushing the fruit and means a shorter cook.
- **The skim.** Foam holds impurities and dulls the colour; lift it off carefully with a skimmer or slotted spoon.
- **One-to-one sugar.** Less sugar gives a runny jam, not slatko. The high sugar is what makes the fruit translucent and gives the preserve its long shelf life.

## Variations
- **Green walnut slatko (orahovo slatko).** The grandest version: thinly sliced young green walnuts (picked before the shell hardens, late May), soaked through several changes of water to draw out the bitterness, then cooked as above. Black-brown, intense.
- **Quince slatko (dunjevo slatko).** Peeled cubed quince cooked the same way; turns rose-red as it cooks.
- **Wild rose petal (od ruže).** Petals from the wild dog rose, cooked very gently in syrup; the prettiest pink colour, eaten with Turkish coffee.

## Serving
A single spoonful on a small saucer with a glass of cold water on the side as the welcome to every guest · alongside Turkish coffee at the end of a meal · spooned over thick Greek yogurt for breakfast · on warm fresh bread with kajmak

## Storage
- Sealed jars keep 12 months in a cool dark cupboard
- Once opened, refrigerate and use within 6 weeks
- Don't freeze
