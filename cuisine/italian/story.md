# Italian

*Italian cooking is regional, not national: twenty distinct traditions held together by good ingredients, restrained seasoning, and the conviction that technique matters more than invention.*

## A short history

Italian cooking sits on Roman foundations, and those foundations sit on Etruscan and Greek ones. The Greeks colonised southern Italy and Sicily from the 8th century BCE and called the region Magna Graecia; they planted the vines, the olives and the wheat that would later anchor the Roman pantry, and the Greek-style fish stews of the Sicilian coast are still cooked broadly the same way today. The Etruscans of central Italy contributed the pig-rearing tradition and the *pulmentum* (a thick grain porridge) that the Roman legions ate as standard rations and which evolved into modern polenta. Apicius, the cookbook compiled in the first century CE, already records the building blocks: olive oil, fish sauce (garum), herbs, slow-cooked pork, sweet-savoury pairings, the use of pasta-shaped dough fried in oil. The Romans planted vines and olives across the peninsula, exported the dry-cured pork of the Apennines, and gave the language its kitchen vocabulary.

Medieval monastery kitchens carried the techniques of fermentation, dry-curing and grain-milling through the centuries when the empire fragmented. The Benedictine and Cistercian houses kept parmigiano-style cheese-making, wine-making and dry-pork-curing alive across the Italian peninsula; the parmigiano-reggiano made today in Emilia-Romagna traces back to 12th-century Benedictine dairies. The Arab conquest of Sicily (827-1091) was the single biggest transformative event in Italian food history. The Arabs brought rice, sugar cane, citrus (lemons, oranges, bitter orange), pistachios, almonds, aubergine, spinach, and the technique of dried pasta. Sicily became the dried-pasta capital of the Mediterranean, and the recipe travelled north to Genoa and east to Naples through the medieval trade routes. The cannoli, the granita, the agrodolce sweet-sour principle and the marzipan of Sicilian sweets all date from this period.

By the Renaissance, the Italian courts ran the most sophisticated kitchens in Europe. When Catherine de' Medici left Florence to marry the future Henri II in 1533, she travelled with her own cooks, ice-cream makers and pastry chefs, and the dishes they introduced shaped what would later become classical French cooking. Columbus's voyages brought the Americas to the Italian table: the tomato landed first in Naples (the first Italian recipe using it appears in a 1692 cookbook), the potato in the Veneto, corn in the Friuli, chillies in Calabria, peppers and beans almost everywhere. The pizza margherita, the Bolognese ragù, the Sicilian caponata, the southern Italian pasta-with-tomato canon: none of these exist without the Columbian Exchange. It took two centuries for the tomato to move from suspicious New World oddity to the heart of southern Italian cooking.

For most of its history, Italy was a patchwork of city-states, kingdoms and foreign occupations. Unification in 1861 created the country, but the cooking stayed local. The everyday tradition of *cucina povera*, country cooking built on whatever the land and the household kept on hand, remained the daily reality across the peninsula: beans, bread, greens, a little cured pork, pasta made by hand. In 1891 a retired silk merchant named Pellegrino Artusi published *La Scienza in Cucina e l'Arte di Mangiar Bene*, gathering 790 recipes from across the new country into one shared tradition. It was the first book to treat regional Italian dishes as a single national repertoire, and it sold steadily for the next half-century.

The 20th century globalised Italian food through migration. Twenty-five million Italians emigrated between 1880 and 1970, mostly from the impoverished south, and Italian-American cooking in New York, the Italian-Argentine table in Buenos Aires, the Italian-Australian kitchens of Melbourne and the Italian-British fish-and-chip shop tradition all date from that diaspora. Mussolini's autarky policies of the 1930s pushed for self-sufficiency and codified some "national" recipes for political reasons, and post-war reconstruction brought refrigeration, the supermarket and an explosion in pasta production. The Slow Food movement, founded in Bra (Piedmont) in 1986 as a response to a McDonald's opening near the Spanish Steps in Rome, pulled the country back toward the regional, the seasonal and the artisanal.

The anchor principle survives every shift: *cucina di mercato*. Cook what is good today.

## Regional variations

There is no single Italian cuisine. There are twenty regional ones, and within each, dozens of local variants that change from one valley to the next.

- **Piedmont** (north-west): butter rather than oil, white truffle from Alba, hand-pinched *agnolotti*, Barolo and Barbaresco from the Langhe hills. The cooking leans toward France.
- **Lombardy and Milan**: rice country. Risotto alla milanese with saffron and bone marrow, ossobuco, panettone at Christmas. Butter, not olive oil.
- **Emilia-Romagna**: the cathedral of pasta. Bologna for tortellini and *ragù alla bolognese*, Parma for prosciutto and parmigiano-reggiano, Modena for balsamic. Egg-rich fresh pasta is the regional signature.
- **Tuscany**: olive oil, beans, day-old bread reborn as *panzanella* and *ribollita*, the great Florentine T-bone *bistecca alla fiorentina*. Restrained, confident cooking.
- **Lazio and Rome**: four pasta dishes hold the city together. *Cacio e pepe*, *carbonara*, *amatriciana*, *gricia*. Pecorino romano (sheep's milk) and guanciale (cured pork jowl) sit behind all of them.
- **Campania and Naples**: pizza's birthplace. San Marzano tomato grown on volcanic soil, mozzarella di bufala from the Sele plain, basil, oil, salt. Few ingredients, decisive technique.
- **Sicily**: the Arab kitchens of the ninth century left citrus, rice, almonds, aubergine and sugar-work behind. Caponata, pasta alla norma, cannoli, granita, swordfish.
- **Sardinia**: pecorino sardo, *pane carasau* (the thin shepherd's bread), suckling pig roasted on myrtle branches. An island kitchen that looks inward rather than out to sea.

A risotto cook in Milan and a sauce cook in Naples are practising different crafts that share a flag.

## Signature ingredients

- **Extra-virgin olive oil**: the daily fat of the south and centre. Used raw to finish soups, beans and bread as much as for cooking.
- **Parmigiano-reggiano**: aged eighteen to thirty-six months. Grated over pasta, shaved into salads, the rind dropped into minestrone for depth.
- **Fresh basil**: the leaf of the Mediterranean garden. Pesto in Liguria, torn over a margherita in Naples, into tomato sauce at the end.
- **San Marzano tomato**: the plum tomato grown on the volcanic plain south of Vesuvius. Lower acidity, denser flesh, the reference tomato for sauce and pizza.
- **Garlic**: used with restraint. A clove crushed and removed, or sliced thin and softened in oil, almost never raw in quantity.
- **Dried pasta**: bronze-extruded, slow-dried, from durum wheat semolina. The everyday format outside the egg-pasta north.
- **Eggs**: the binding ingredient of fresh pasta in Emilia-Romagna and Piedmont. Pasta dough, carbonara, frittata, zabaglione.
- **Anchovies**: salt-cured, dissolved into oil with garlic at the start of a sauce. The quiet umami behind puttanesca, vitello tonnato, pasta with broccoli.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Spaghetti marinara](spaghetti-marinara.md). Start with sauce. Tomato, garlic, oil, basil, salt. Teaches the southern foundation: a handful of ingredients cooked briefly, with the tomato doing most of the work.
2. [Bucatini carbonara](bucatini-carbonara.md). Rome's defining pasta. Guanciale, egg, pecorino, black pepper, pasta water. No cream. Teaches the emulsion principle that runs through cacio e pepe, gricia and amatriciana as well.
3. [Risotto milanese](risotto-milanese.md). The northern counterpart to pasta. Carnaroli or arborio rice, hot stock added a ladle at a time, saffron, butter, parmigiano stirred in at the end. Teaches *mantecatura*, the final beating that gives risotto its gloss.
4. [Bolognese](bolognese.md). Emilia-Romagna's slow ragù. Beef and pork, soffritto, milk, wine, tomato, time. Three hours minimum. Teaches the patient-meat tradition that defines northern Sunday cooking.
5. [Margherita pizza](pizza/margherita.md). Naples on a plate. Dough left to rise long and slow, San Marzano tomato torn by hand, fior di latte or bufala, basil, oil. Teaches dough hydration and the value of leaving things alone.
6. [Spaghetti alle vongole](spaghetti-alle-vongole.md). The clam pasta of the coast. Clams opened in oil, garlic, chilli and white wine, pasta finished in the pan with the sauce. Teaches the *risottare* move (cooking pasta in its own sauce) used across coastal Italian cooking.
7. [Tiramisu](desserts/tiramisu.md). The country's most-travelled dessert. Espresso-soaked savoiardi, mascarpone whipped with egg and sugar, cocoa dusted on top. Teaches the Italian dessert principle: no oven, no fuss, lean on a few good ingredients.

Seven dishes, seven techniques, four regions. Cook them in order and the rest of the repertoire opens up.
