# Indonesian

*The spice trade's home kitchen. Seventeen thousand islands, three hundred ethnic groups, and a single ferocious commitment to chilli, shrimp paste, coconut and the long slow simmer that produced the world's most aromatic curry tradition.*

## A short history

Indonesian cooking is the original spice cuisine. Nutmeg, cloves, mace, pepper, ginger, galangal, lemongrass, candlenut, turmeric: almost every aromatic the world uses passed through these islands first. The earliest layer is Austronesian. The seafaring peoples who reached the archipelago around 2000 BCE brought rice cultivation, coconut, sago palm and the outrigger canoe, and the wet-rice terrace systems they built on Java and Bali still feed the country today. They also brought the bumbu (spice paste) tradition: aromatics ground on a stone slab, fried in coconut oil to bloom, used as the structural base of every dish.

Indian traders arriving from roughly the 1st century CE brought Hindu-Buddhist culture, Sanskrit kitchen vocabulary, the curry concept and the use of turmeric and coriander as everyday spices. The Hindu-Buddhist period (roughly 200-1500 CE) left central and east Java with strong vegetarian traditions and the use of tempeh, the fermented soybean cake that is a uniquely Indonesian invention, first recorded in 16th-century Javanese manuscripts but probably much older. Chinese traders settled the coastal ports from the Tang dynasty onwards, bringing stir-fry technique, soy sauce, noodles, the wok and the bakso meatball tradition. The cross-pollination ran for over a thousand years before any European ship arrived.

Islam reached the archipelago through Gujarati and Arab merchants from the 13th century onward and reshaped the daily kitchen. Pork disappeared from most of Indonesia (Bali kept its Hindu kitchen and remains the pork exception), beef and lamb rose, and the slow-cooked rendang of West Sumatra grew out of the Minangkabau Muslim wedding tradition. The Banda Islands were the world's only source of nutmeg until the British smuggled trees to Grenada in the 18th century, which made them some of the most valuable real estate on the planet for two hundred years. Portuguese sailors arrived in Maluku in 1512 and brought the chillies, peanuts, tomatoes, corn and cassava of the Columbian Exchange. Within a century chilli had taken over the sambal shelf entirely.

Dutch colonisation (1602-1949) ran for over three centuries and shaped the kitchen heavily. The VOC (Dutch East India Company) ran the spice trade from Batavia (now Jakarta), worked enslaved labour on the clove and nutmeg plantations, and pulled the country into a single colonial economy that moved ingredients between islands for the first time. Rijsttafel ("rice table") was the Dutch codification of Indonesian cooking into a multi-dish banquet for colonial officers, and the Indo (Dutch-Indonesian) cuisine that emigrants carried back to the Netherlands is its own enduring shelf. The 19th-century arrival of indentured labourers from southern India and Hadhrami Arabs from Yemen added curry powders, the martabak stuffed pancake and the kambing (goat) soup tradition.

After independence in 1949 the country codified a national cuisine drawn from across the islands: rendang (West Sumatra), nasi goreng (the national fried rice), satay (universal but Java-perfected), bakso meatballs, soto chicken soup, gado-gado peanut salad. President Sukarno's nation-building project deliberately pulled regional dishes onto a shared table. The 2011 CNN poll that named rendang the world's most delicious food gave Indonesian cuisine an international moment, and it's been on a slow rise globally ever since.

## Regional variations

- **West Sumatra (Padang)**: the most famous regional cuisine. Rendang, gulai (curry), nasi padang (the rice-with-all-curries-at-once format). Heavy on coconut and dry-fried spice paste.
- **Java**: the most populous shelf. Sweet soy sauce (kecap manis), nasi goreng, soto ayam, tempeh, the gudeg (jackfruit curry) of Yogyakarta.
- **Bali**: Hindu, so pork is allowed. Babi guling (suckling pig), bebek betutu (slow-cooked duck), lawar (raw blood salad, restricted).
- **Sulawesi (Manado)**: chilli capital. Rica-rica (chilli-fried), the seafood and meat dishes dressed in fire.
- **Maluku (the Spice Islands)**: the original. Sago, fish, the spice trade's home plates.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sambal**: chilli paste in dozens of regional varieties. Sambal terasi (shrimp-paste sambal), sambal matah (Bali raw sambal), sambal oelek.
- **Kecap manis**: thick sweet soy sauce with palm sugar. The Indonesian condiment of choice.
- **Coconut milk**: the base of rendang, gulai, soto and dozens of curries.
- **Lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf**: the aromatic trinity.
- **Turmeric and candlenut**: in spice pastes (bumbu) ground in a stone mortar.
- **Shrimp paste (terasi)**: fermented, pungent, the umami in sambal.
- **Palm sugar (gula jawa)**: caramelly, used in both sweet and savoury dishes.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the rendang, the nasi goreng, the satay and the gado-gado. Cook them and the cuisine opens up.

1. **[Beef Rendang](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/beef-rendang)**: beef slow-cooked in coconut milk and spice paste until the liquid evaporates and the meat caramelises in its own oils. CNN crowned it the world's most delicious food in 2011, and it's probably the slowest-cooked beef on earth.
2. **[Beef Nasi Goreng](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/beef-nasi-goreng)**: fried rice with beef, kecap manis, sambal, fried egg and prawn crackers on top. The national fried rice, codified across the archipelago.
3. **[Gado-Gado](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/gado-gado)**: cooked vegetables, tofu, tempeh, hard-boiled egg, all dressed in a peanut sauce. The Indonesian salad and the most reliable vegetarian plate.
4. **[Sayur Lodeh](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/sayur-lodeh)**: vegetable curry in coconut milk with lemongrass and galangal. The Java home-table everyday plate.
5. **[Indonesian Chicken Soup](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/indonesian-chicken-soup)**: soto ayam, the yellow turmeric chicken soup with rice noodles, hard-boiled egg, and a lime-and-sambal finish. The breakfast-and-lunch staple.
6. **[Indonesian Meatballs](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/indonesian-meatballs)**: bakso, beef meatballs in a clear broth with noodles, sweet soy and chilli sauce on the side. The street-food legend, said to be Barack Obama's favourite childhood food.
7. **[Mee Goreng](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/mee-goreng)**: stir-fried noodles with kecap manis, garlic, chilli, prawn and vegetables. The night-stall classic.

Cook the rendang and the nasi goreng and you have the showpieces. Gado-gado and sayur lodeh are the vegetarian shelf. Soto and bakso are the soup; mee goreng is the noodle.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the satay tradition (chicken satay, lamb satay, beef satay), the sambal world (sambal terasi, sambal oelek), the rice canon (nasi kuning yellow rice), and the tempeh shelf (tempeh goreng, tempeh manis). Build a small Indonesian table: rice in the centre, two curries, a sambal, a vegetable. That's the proper home meal.
