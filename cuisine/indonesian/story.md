# Indonesian

*The spice trade's home kitchen. Seventeen thousand islands, three hundred ethnic groups, and a single ferocious commitment to chilli, shrimp paste, coconut and the long slow simmer that produced the world's most aromatic curry tradition.*

## A short history

Indonesian cooking is the original spice cuisine. Nutmeg, cloves, mace, pepper, ginger, galangal, lemongrass, candlenut, turmeric — almost every aromatic the world uses passed through these islands first. The Spice Trade made the Banda Islands (the world's only source of nutmeg before the British smuggled trees to Grenada in the 18th century) one of the most valuable bits of real estate on the planet for two hundred years. The Dutch colonisation (1602–1949) shaped the kitchen heavily — rijsttafel was the Dutch codification of Indonesian cooking, and the Indo (Dutch-Indonesian) cuisine is its own shelf.

The Hindu-Buddhist period (roughly 200–1500 CE) left central and east Java with strong vegetarian traditions and the use of tempeh (fermented soybean cake — a uniquely Indonesian invention). The arrival of Islam from the 13th century onward made pork rare across most of the country (Bali kept its Hindu kitchen and remains the pork exception). Chinese traders brought stir-fry technique and the noodle shelf; Indian traders contributed curry powders and the dosa-like serabi pancake.

The post-independence period saw Indonesia codify a national cuisine drawn from across the islands — rendang (West Sumatra), nasi goreng (the national fried rice), satay (universal but Java-perfected), bakso meatballs, soto chicken soup, gado-gado peanut salad. The 2011 CNN poll that named rendang the world's most delicious food gave Indonesian cuisine an international moment; the cuisine has been on a slow rise globally ever since.

## Regional variations

- **West Sumatra (Padang)**: the most-famous regional cuisine. Rendang, gulai (curry), nasi padang (the rice-with-all-curries-at-once format). Heavy on coconut and dry-fried spice paste.
- **Java**: the most populous shelf. Sweet soy sauce (kecap manis), nasi goreng, soto ayam, tempeh, the gudeg (jackfruit curry) of Yogyakarta.
- **Bali**: Hindu, so pork is allowed. Babi guling (suckling pig), bebek betutu (slow-cooked duck), lawar (raw blood salad — restricted).
- **Sulawesi (Manado)**: chilli capital. Rica-rica (chilli-fried), the seafood and meat dishes dressed in fire.
- **Maluku (the Spice Islands)**: the original. Sago, fish, the spice trade's home plates.

## Signature ingredients

- **Sambal**: chilli paste in dozens of regional varieties. Sambal terasi (shrimp-paste sambal), sambal matah (Bali raw sambal), sambal oelek.
- **Kecap manis**: thick sweet soy sauce with palm sugar. The Indonesian condiment of choice.
- **Coconut milk**: the base of rendang, gulai, soto and dozens of curries.
- **Lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime leaf**: the aromatic trinity.
- **Turmeric and candlenut**: in spice pastes (bumbu) ground in a stone mortar.
- **Shrimp paste (terasi)**: fermented, pungent, the umami in sambal.
- **Palm sugar (gula jawa)**: caramelly, used in both sweet and savoury dishes.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the rendang, the nasi goreng, the satay and the gado-gado. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Beef Rendang](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/beef-rendang)**: beef slow-cooked in coconut milk and spice paste till the liquid evaporates and the meat caramelises in its own oils. Crowned the world's most delicious food by CNN in 2011, and probably the slowest-cooked beef on earth.
2. **[Beef Nasi Goreng](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/beef-nasi-goreng)**: fried rice with beef, kecap manis, sambal, fried egg and prawn crackers on top. The national fried rice, codified across the archipelago.
3. **[Gado-Gado](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/gado-gado)**: cooked vegetables, tofu, tempeh, hard-boiled egg, all dressed in a peanut sauce. The Indonesian salad and the most reliable vegetarian plate.
4. **[Sayur Lodeh](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/sayur-lodeh)**: vegetable curry in coconut milk with lemongrass and galangal. The Java home table everyday plate.
5. **[Indonesian Chicken Soup](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/indonesian-chicken-soup)**: soto ayam — yellow turmeric chicken soup with rice noodles, hard-boiled egg, and a lime-and-sambal finish. The breakfast-and-lunch staple.
6. **[Indonesian Meatballs](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/indonesian-meatballs)**: bakso — beef meatballs in a clear broth with noodles, sweet soy and chilli sauce on the side. The street-food legend, said to be Barack Obama's favourite childhood food.
7. **[Mee Goreng](/recipes/cuisine/indonesian/mee-goreng)**: stir-fried noodles with kecap manis, garlic, chilli, prawn and vegetables. The night-stall classic.

Cook the rendang and the nasi goreng and you have the showpieces. The gado-gado and sayur lodeh are the vegetarian shelf. The soto and bakso are the soup; the mee goreng the noodle.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the satay tradition (chicken satay, lamb satay, beef satay), the sambal world (sambal terasi, sambal oelek), the rice canon (nasi kuning yellow rice), and the tempeh shelf (tempeh goreng, tempeh manis). Build a small Indonesian table — rice in the centre, two curries, a sambal, a vegetable. That's the proper home meal.
