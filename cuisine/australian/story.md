# Australian

*The kitchen of the BBQ, the Sunday lamb roast, the meat pie, and the cosmopolitan multicultural food capital of the Asia-Pacific. British colonial roots crossed with Asian and Mediterranean immigration, plus a growing Indigenous (bush food) revival.*

## A short history

Australian cooking sits on three roots. The Indigenous (Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander) tradition is the oldest, more than 60,000 years of bush foods: kangaroo, wallaby, emu, goanna, fish from rivers and coasts, wattleseed ground into damper-style cakes, lemon myrtle, finger lime, quandong, native yams. Cooking ran on ground ovens, hot ashes, and the long-burning hardwood fire. Different clans worked different country, and that produced one of the most regionally varied food traditions on earth, every wattle and saltbush and shellfish bed mapped into a seasonal calendar that ran for millennia before any European saw the coast.

The First Fleet landed at Sydney Cove in January 1788 and tried to grow British food in soil and weather that wouldn't cooperate. The early colony nearly starved. What survived was a stripped-back English country kitchen built on what could be raised: mutton and beef on the open runs, wheat in the southern downs, the Sunday roast adapted to the heat. The damper (flour, water, salt, baked in the ashes of a campfire) became the bushman's bread, and the billy can of tea became the national drink. By the late 19th century the lamb and beef industries were exporting to Britain at industrial scale, and the meat pie, the lamington (sponge dipped in chocolate icing and coconut), and the pavlova had codified the everyday plate.

The Gold Rush of the 1850s brought the first big wave of non-British immigration, including Cantonese miners who set up the cookhouses that later became the country-town Chinese restaurant. White Australia immigration policy then closed the door for decades, but a small Italian, Greek and German presence in places like Griffith, the Barossa and the inner cities held on.

Post-WWII immigration waves transformed Australian food. Italians (Sydney's Leichhardt, Melbourne's Carlton), Greeks (Melbourne holds the largest Greek population outside Greece, the second-largest Greek-speaking city in the world), Lebanese, Vietnamese after the fall of Saigon in 1975, Chinese, Thai, Japanese, Korean, Indian, Sri Lankan, Sudanese, Ethiopian. Every wave added its kitchen. The 1980s Sydney-Melbourne fine-dining scene under chefs like Stephanie Alexander, Cheong Liew and later Tetsuya Wakuda built a "Modern Australian" identity around the produce: Tasmanian salmon, Pacific oysters, Coorong mullet, South Australian tuna, Queensland prawns, the lamb and beef shelf, plus Asian-Mediterranean technique.

The Bushfood and Native Australian movement (rising since the 1980s, accelerating through the 2010s) has put Indigenous ingredients onto the mainstream menu. Wattleseed, lemon myrtle, pepperberry, finger lime, saltbush, Davidson plum, riberry. Restaurants like Attica (Melbourne) and Saint Peter (Sydney) lead the charge, and a growing Indigenous-owned producer network is finally turning a long-suppressed food culture into recognised national patrimony.

The Australian BBQ (the "barbie") is the social institution. Sausages, lamb chops, prawns, marinated chicken over a portable gas or charcoal grill, cold beer in hand, the everyday weekend ritual. The cuisine reads cosmopolitan in the cities and stripped-back-pastoral on the road, and both halves are honestly the country.

## Regional variations

- **Sydney (NSW)**: the cosmopolitan kitchen. Asian-fusion strongest, the seafood tradition, the harbour-side dining.
- **Melbourne (Victoria)**: Italian-Greek-Lebanese-Vietnamese. Coffee capital (the world's best espresso, by many measures). The brunch institution.
- **Tasmania**: cool-climate produce. Salmon, oysters, apples, the world's best dairy.
- **South Australia (Adelaide, Barossa)**: wine country. The German-Lutheran heritage (Adelaide Hills), Mediterranean influences.
- **Queensland**: tropical fruit, prawns, the Pacific seafood, the "Sunshine State" outdoor lifestyle.
- **Western Australia (Perth)**: Indian Ocean seafood, the most isolated big city, distinctive Asian fusion.
- **Northern Territory**: Indigenous bush food strongest, crocodile, barramundi, the outback cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb**: Australian lamb is the export benchmark.
- **Beef**: from the Northern cattle stations and the South.
- **Barramundi**: the iconic native fish.
- **Vegemite**: yeast-extract spread on buttered toast. The cultural icon (love it or hate it).
- **Pavlova**: meringue with cream and fresh fruit (claimed by Australia and New Zealand; the rivalry is permanent).
- **Macadamia nuts**: native to Australia.
- **Native ingredients**: wattleseed, lemon myrtle, finger lime, bush tomato, mountain pepper, kakadu plum.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Aussie Burger](/recipes/cuisine/australian/aussie-burger)**: beef patty with the works: bacon, fried egg, beetroot (the Australian touch), pineapple, lettuce, tomato, cheese.
2. **[Beef Meat Pie](/recipes/cuisine/australian/beef-meat-pie)**: a beef-and-gravy pie in flaky pastry. The everyday Australian lunch, sold at the bakery on every corner, eaten with tomato sauce (ketchup).
3. **[Roast Lamb with Rosemary](/recipes/cuisine/australian/roast-lamb-rosemary)**: Sunday lamb roast with rosemary and garlic, served with mint sauce and roast vegetables.
4. **[Pan-Fried Barramundi](/recipes/cuisine/australian/pan-fried-barramundi)**: Australia's native fish pan-fried with lemon, butter and herbs.
5. **[Charcoal Chicken Skewers](/recipes/cuisine/australian/charcoal-chicken-skewers)**: marinated chicken on skewers, the BBQ staple, with Lebanese-Australian seasonings.
6. **[Lamb Mint Chilli Burger](/recipes/cuisine/australian/lamb-mint-chilli-burger)**: lamb burger with mint, chilli and yoghurt, the Aussie-Mediterranean fusion.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Australian wine (Penfolds Shiraz, Margaret River Cabernet, Yarra Valley Pinot Noir) or with VB, XXXX or Coopers beer. End with a pavlova or with lamingtons (sponge cubes dipped in chocolate icing and rolled in desiccated coconut).
