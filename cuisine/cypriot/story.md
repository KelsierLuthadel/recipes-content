# Cypriot

*The kitchen of the Mediterranean's third-largest island. Halloumi (the country's most famous export — the squeaky grilling cheese), sheftalia (the lamb sausage wrapped in caul fat), souvla (lamb on big skewers over coals), and a Greek-Turkish-Lebanese-British colonial synthesis.*

## A short history

Cypriot cooking sits at the crossroads of three culinary spheres: Greek (the majority Greek-Cypriot tradition), Turkish (the Turkish-Cypriot tradition of the north), and broader Levantine (Lebanese, Syrian, Egyptian influence). The British colonial period (1878–1960) added tea, fish-and-chips and a few other dishes. The result is a fundamentally Mediterranean kitchen with strong Levantine and Anatolian layers.

Halloumi — the salty, squeaky semi-hard cheese made from sheep and goat milk — is the country's culinary icon. It's the most-exported Cypriot product and one of the few cheeses that doesn't melt when heated (so it can be grilled or fried whole, a Cypriot specialty). The cheese was PDO-certified in 2021 after a long Greek-Turkish-Cypriot dispute.

The other defining Cypriot tradition is the meze — like Greek but with more bread, more village cheeses (halloumi, anari), and the long lazy Sunday lunch tradition that brings family together for hours.

## Regional variations

- **Greek-Cypriot south**: codified mainstream. Souvla, sheftalia, kleftiko.
- **Turkish-Cypriot north**: more lamb, more bulgur. Distinct dishes (köfte, börek variations).
- **Paphos region**: more wild herbs (the famous wild thyme honey).
- **Larnaca and Limassol**: meze culture strongest.

## Signature ingredients

- **Halloumi**: sheep-and-goat milk cheese. Grilled or fried.
- **Anari**: ricotta-like fresh cheese (the byproduct of halloumi-making).
- **Olive oil**: heavy use.
- **Lamb and goat (yiouvarlakia, kleftiko)**: the celebration meats.
- **Bulgur (pourgouri)**: in pilafs.
- **Coriander seed**: used heavily.
- **Wild herbs**: oregano, thyme, mint.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cypriot Souvlakia](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/cypriot-souvlakia)**: marinated lamb (or pork — the Greek-Cypriot version) on small skewers, charcoal-grilled. Different from Greek mainland — smaller pieces, more coriander, served in pita with halloumi.
2. **[Kleftiko](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/kleftiko)**: lamb slow-cooked for hours in a sealed clay oven (or a heavy pot) with garlic, oregano, lemon and bay. The "stolen lamb" — historically cooked in pit ovens to hide from the ruling Ottomans.
3. **[Sheftalia](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/sheftalia)**: minced pork (or lamb in the Muslim north) with onion, parsley, cinnamon, wrapped in caul fat, grilled over coals.
4. **[Cypriot Stifado](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/cypriot-stifado)**: rabbit (or beef) slow-braised with pearl onions, red wine, cinnamon, cloves and bay.
5. **[Afelia](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/afelia)**: pork marinated in red wine and crushed coriander seeds, slow-braised. Served with bulgur pilaf and yoghurt.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Commandaria (the world's oldest named wine — Cypriot sweet dessert wine, in production since 1192) or with a Keo or Carlsberg lager (both brewed locally). End with loukoumades (honey-syrup-soaked doughnuts) or with shiamishi (semolina pastry with rose-water syrup).
