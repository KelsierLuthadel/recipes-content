# Cypriot

*The kitchen of the Mediterranean's third-largest island. Halloumi (the country's most famous export, the squeaky grilling cheese), sheftalia (the lamb sausage wrapped in caul fat), souvla (lamb on big skewers over coals), and a Greek-Turkish-Lebanese-British colonial synthesis.*

## A short history

Cyprus is one of the oldest continuously inhabited islands in the Mediterranean, and its food shows it. Neolithic settlers arrived around 10,000 years ago, bringing wheat, barley and the first domesticated sheep and goats; archaeologists at Choirokoitia have found cooking pots and grain stores dating to roughly 7000 BCE. The Bronze Age Cypriots traded copper (the island's name gave the metal its English word) across the eastern Mediterranean and brought back wine grapes from the Levant, olive cuttings from Crete and spice habits from Egypt. The Mycenaean Greeks settled in the 13th century BCE and brought olive oil, wine, the herb shelf (oregano, thyme, fennel) and the lamb-and-grain traditions that still anchor the cuisine. Phoenicians arrived in the 9th century BCE with the salt-cured fish trade.

Cyprus then passed through almost every empire that mattered. The Assyrians, Egyptians, Persians, Ptolemies and Romans each held the island. The Romans codified the wine trade. Commandaria, the sweet wine made from sun-dried Mavro and Xynisteri grapes in the Troodos foothills, is the world's oldest named wine in continuous production; Richard the Lionheart drank it at his 1191 wedding on the island. The Byzantine centuries (4th to 12th) made Cyprus a Greek Orthodox stronghold and gave the cuisine its lenten shelf (the bean stews, the wild herbs, the pulses for the long fasting weeks). Crusader knights ruled the island in the 12th and 13th centuries, planting sugar cane around Kolossi and exporting it across Europe (sugar from Cyprus and Madeira was the European sweet supply before the New World plantations took over).

Venice took the island in 1489 and held it for nearly a century. The Venetians fortified Famagusta and Nicosia, planted more vineyards, and introduced the pasta habits that survive in trahanas (the soured-grain pasta cooked into winter soup) and in some of the baked dough specialties. The Ottoman conquest of 1571 brought three hundred years of Turkish rule, and with it the bulgur pilafs, the lokma sweets, the lamb-skewer kebab tradition, the coffee culture, the leaf-wrapped dolma, and the population that became the Turkish-Cypriot community of the north. Greek-Cypriot and Turkish-Cypriot kitchens diverged over those three centuries on details but kept the same Mediterranean spine.

The British took the island in 1878 and held it until independence in 1960. The colonial period (1878 to 1960) added tea, fish-and-chips, the cucumber-sandwich English cake tradition, the use of brown sauce on grilled meat, and the British-style breakfast that still appears in Cyprus tourist hotels. It also industrialised halloumi production and exported the cheese to the British market, planting the seeds of today's global trade. The 1974 division of the island has shaped the modern food story: the north (Turkish-Cypriot) leans further into bulgur, more lamb, the kebab-and-mezze tradition; the south (Greek-Cypriot) keeps the codified mezze culture and the Orthodox feast calendar. Levantine immigration over the same period (Lebanese, Syrian, Armenian) added the broader spice shelf, the kibbeh and the za'atar that increasingly appear on Cypriot menus.

Halloumi, the salty, squeaky semi-hard cheese made from sheep and goat milk, is the country's culinary icon. It's the most-exported Cypriot product and one of the few cheeses that doesn't melt under heat, so it grills or fries whole (a Cypriot specialty). It got PDO status in 2021 after a long Greek-Turkish-Cypriot dispute. The other defining Cypriot tradition is the meze. Greek in shape but with more bread, more village cheeses (halloumi, anari), and the long lazy Sunday lunch that brings family together for hours. What you get is a fundamentally Mediterranean kitchen with strong Levantine and Anatolian layers.

## Regional variations

- **Greek-Cypriot south**: codified mainstream. Souvla, sheftalia, kleftiko.
- **Turkish-Cypriot north**: more lamb, more bulgur. Distinct dishes (köfte, börek variations).
- **Paphos region**: more wild herbs (the famous wild thyme honey).
- **Larnaca and Limassol**: meze culture strongest.

## Signature ingredients

- **Halloumi**: sheep-and-goat milk cheese. Grilled or fried.
- **Anari**: ricotta-like fresh cheese (the byproduct of halloumi-making).
- **Olive oil**: heavy use.
- **Lamb and goat (yiouvarlakia, kleftiko)**: the celebration meats.
- **Bulgur (pourgouri)**: in pilafs.
- **Coriander seed**: used heavily.
- **Wild herbs**: oregano, thyme, mint.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cypriot Souvlakia](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/cypriot-souvlakia)**: marinated lamb (or pork, the Greek-Cypriot version) on small skewers, charcoal-grilled. Different from the Greek mainland: smaller pieces, more coriander, served in pita with halloumi.
2. **[Kleftiko](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/kleftiko)**: lamb slow-cooked for hours in a sealed clay oven (or a heavy pot) with garlic, oregano, lemon and bay. The "stolen lamb", historically cooked in pit ovens to hide from the ruling Ottomans.
3. **[Sheftalia](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/sheftalia)**: minced pork (or lamb in the Muslim north) with onion, parsley, cinnamon, wrapped in caul fat, grilled over coals.
4. **[Cypriot Stifado](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/cypriot-stifado)**: rabbit (or beef) slow-braised with pearl onions, red wine, cinnamon, cloves and bay.
5. **[Afelia](/recipes/cuisine/cypriot/afelia)**: pork marinated in red wine and crushed coriander seeds, slow-braised. Served with bulgur pilaf and yoghurt.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Commandaria (the world's oldest named wine, a Cypriot sweet dessert wine in production since 1192) or with a Keo or Carlsberg lager (both brewed locally). End with loukoumades (honey-syrup-soaked doughnuts) or with shiamishi (semolina pastry with rose-water syrup).
