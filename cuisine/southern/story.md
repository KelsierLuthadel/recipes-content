# Southern

*The American South's home cooking. Fried chicken, slow-braised greens, cornbread, beans, biscuits and the meat-and-three plate; a cuisine built on African, European and Indigenous traditions and held together by lard, time and the front porch.*

## A short history

Southern cooking is the most important regional American cuisine, and its roots run deeper than the country itself. The deepest layer is Indigenous: the Cherokee, Creek, Choctaw, Chickasaw and Seminole nations farmed the Three Sisters (corn, beans and squash) across what's now the South for at least a thousand years before any European set foot on the coast. Corn became the great Southern staple in every form (grits, cornbread, hominy, pone, mush), beans the universal protein, squash the everyday vegetable. The Indigenous techniques (nixtamalising corn with wood ash, slow-pit-cooking meat under banked coals, smoke-curing fish over hickory) shaped what Southern cooks still do, and the language reflects it: pone from the Algonquian apan, hominy from rockahominie.

The 17th and 18th centuries layered European traditions on top. English, Scots-Irish, Welsh and German settlers brought wheat, pork, cabbage, the biscuit tradition, the dairy shelf and the long Sunday roast. Pork especially became the Southern meat, with hogs running half-wild in the oak forests of Virginia, the Carolinas and Tennessee, fattening on acorns and getting slaughtered every November for the year's ham, bacon, sausage, lard and chitterlings. The country ham tradition (salt-cured, smoke-aged for a year or more, sliced paper-thin) traces straight back to the English Yorkshire method, naturalised onto Smithfield, Surry and the Appalachian smokehouses.

The deepest layer, and the one that shaped the cuisine the most, is West African. Enslaved cooks brought to the South through the Atlantic slave trade carried okra (from the Twi nkruma), the black-eyed peas, the long-grain rice cookery of the Senegambian rice coast, the gumbo (from the Kikongo word for okra), the long-braised greens, the deep-fry technique that became Southern fried chicken, the watermelon, the sesame (benne in the Lowcountry), the yam, and the one-pot rice-and-beans tradition that became hoppin' John. The plantation economy and slavery meant the people doing the cooking, mostly enslaved African women in big-house kitchens, set the rules of Southern food, even as they were denied the credit and the freedom. After Emancipation the same cooks moved into commercial kitchens, boarding houses and family restaurants; the cuisine they built spread across the entire region.

The Lowcountry rice culture of Charleston and Savannah deserves its own paragraph. Carolina Gold rice, brought from West Africa with the enslaved labour that grew it, made Charleston the wealthiest city in colonial America by the 1770s. The Senegambian and Mande cooks who ran the rice fields also ran the kitchens, and dishes like hoppin' John, red rice, shrimp purloo and the Frogmore stew (the Lowcountry boil) come straight from the African rice tradition. The Gullah Geechee communities along the Sea Islands preserved more West African retention than any other African American population, and the food shows it. Sweet potato pie, benne seed wafers and the famous Gullah okra soup all trace through Charleston back to Senegal.

The Great Migration of African Americans north to Chicago, Detroit, Cleveland and New York in the early 20th century carried Southern cooking into urban America, where it picked up the name soul food. The civil rights era of the 1960s and 70s recodified soul food as a political identity; the term became a way of insisting that the cuisine Black Americans had built, often in segregated kitchens that couldn't seat the cooks themselves, was worthy of being called what it was. The 20th century also gave the South the meat-and-three plate (one meat, three sides, cornbread, a sweet tea) as the standard restaurant format, with Nashville and Memphis as the headquarters.

The 21st-century Southern food revival (Sean Brock at Husk, the recovery of Edna Lewis's quiet 1960s masterpieces, the Southern Foodways Alliance documenting cooks and farmers across the region, the new wave of African American chefs reclaiming the cuisine's authorship) pushed Southern cuisine into the international restaurant conversation. The home kitchen meanwhile reads the same as it has for a century. The cast iron skillet, the buttermilk on the counter, the pot of greens on a low simmer, the cornbread in the oven, the sweet tea pitcher in the fridge.

## Regional variations

- **Lowcountry (Charleston, Savannah)**: rice country (Carolina Gold). Shrimp and grits, hoppin' John, Frogmore stew. Strongest African retention.
- **Appalachia (West Virginia, Kentucky, East Tennessee)**: soup beans, cornbread, sorghum, country ham, the moonshine tradition.
- **Cajun and Creole** (separate folders): the French-influenced Louisiana shelf; gumbo, jambalaya, étouffée.
- **Texas BBQ** (separate folder): beef brisket and beef-led smoke.
- **Atlanta and the New South**: the contemporary fine-dining recodification. Chef-led, ingredient-forward, draws on the whole region.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lard**: pork-rendered fat. The frying medium and the biscuit shortening.
- **Cornmeal**: in cornbread, in hush puppies, as a coating for fried fish.
- **Buttermilk**: the marinade for fried chicken; the liquid in biscuits.
- **Pork**: every part. Bacon, ham, hocks (for greens), trotters, jowls (the bacon-er bacon).
- **Collards, mustard greens, turnip greens**: cooked long with pork.
- **Black-eyed peas**: the New Year's good-luck dish (hoppin' John).
- **Hot sauce**: vinegar-pepper based (Tabasco, Crystal, Texas Pete). On greens, on chicken, on everything.
- **Sweet tea**: the universal table beverage. Strong, sweet, iced.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the fried chicken, the slow greens, the stew tradition and the breakfast plate. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Southern Fried Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/southern/southern-fried-chicken)**: buttermilk-soaked chicken, double-dredged in seasoned flour, deep-fried in lard till the crust shatters. The defining Southern dish.
2. **[Brunswick Stew](/recipes/cuisine/southern/brunswick-stew)**: tomato-base stew with pulled pork (or chicken), corn, lima beans and potato. The Sunday-supper stew of Georgia and Virginia.
3. **[Shrimp and Grits](/recipes/cuisine/southern/shrimp-and-grits)**: prawns and bacon over creamy stone-ground grits with cheese. The Lowcountry standard, originally a breakfast.
4. **[Hoppin' John](/recipes/cuisine/southern/hoppin-john)**: black-eyed peas, rice, salt pork and onion. The New Year's good-luck dish; eat it with collards (folding money) and cornbread (gold).
5. **[Chicken and Dumplings](/recipes/cuisine/southern/chicken-and-dumplings)**: chicken simmered with rolled flour dumplings and broth. Sunday-night comfort.
6. **[Country Captain](/recipes/cuisine/southern/country-captain)**: curried chicken with raisins, almonds and tomato over rice. A Charleston dish with Anglo-Indian roots, a favourite of FDR.
7. **[Country Ham with Red-Eye Gravy](/recipes/cuisine/southern/country-ham-red-eye-gravy)**: salt-cured country ham pan-fried in its own fat, deglazed with strong black coffee. The Appalachian breakfast.

Cook the fried chicken and the shrimp and grits and you have the headline dishes. The hoppin' John and the chicken and dumplings are the everyday. The Brunswick stew and country captain are the regional gems; the country ham and red-eye gravy is breakfast.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the low-country boil, more BBQ (pulled pork), the breakfast canon (biscuits and gravy, grits, country sausage), the vegetable shelf (collards, fried green tomatoes, succotash) and the dessert tradition (banana pudding, pecan pie, sweet potato pie). Pair everything with sweet tea or a bourbon old fashioned and the cuisine sings.
