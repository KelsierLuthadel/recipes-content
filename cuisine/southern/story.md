# Southern

*The American South's home cooking. Fried chicken, slow-braised greens, cornbread, beans, biscuits and the meat-and-three plate; a cuisine built on African, European and Indigenous traditions and held together by lard, time and the front porch.*

## A short history

Southern cooking is the most important regional American cuisine. Its roots run deeper than the country itself — West African cooks brought from the slave trade carried the okra, the black-eyed peas, the rice cookery, the gumbo, the long-braised greens, and the deep-fry technique that became Southern fried chicken. European settlers brought wheat, pork, cabbage and the biscuit tradition. Indigenous peoples contributed corn (in every form — grits, cornbread, hominy), beans, squash and the smoke-cured fish.

The plantation economy and the slavery system meant that the people doing the cooking — mostly enslaved African women in big-house kitchens — set the rules of Southern food. After Emancipation, the same cooks moved into commercial kitchens, boarding houses and family restaurants; the cuisine they created spread across the entire region. The Great Migration of African Americans north to Chicago, Detroit and New York in the early 20th century carried Southern cooking into urban America, where soul food became its distinctive name.

The 20th century gave the South the meat-and-three plate (one meat, three sides, cornbread, a sweet tea) as the standard restaurant format. The 1960s-70s civil rights era recodified soul food as a political identity. The 21st-century Southern food revival — Sean Brock, Edna Lewis's recovered legacy, the Southern Foodways Alliance — pushed Southern cuisine into the international restaurant conversation.

## Regional variations

- **Lowcountry (Charleston, Savannah)**: rice country (Carolina Gold). Shrimp and grits, hoppin' John, Frogmore stew. Strongest African retention.
- **Appalachia (West Virginia, Kentucky, East Tennessee)**: soup beans, cornbread, sorghum, country ham, the moonshine tradition.
- **Cajun and Creole** (separate folders): the French-influenced Louisiana shelf — gumbo, jambalaya, étouffée.
- **Texas BBQ** (separate folder): beef brisket and beef-led smoke.
- **Atlanta and the New South**: the contemporary fine-dining recodification. Chef-led, ingredient-forward, draws on the whole region.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lard**: pork-rendered fat. The frying medium and the biscuit shortening.
- **Cornmeal**: in cornbread, in hush puppies, as a coating for fried fish.
- **Buttermilk**: the marinade for fried chicken; the liquid in biscuits.
- **Pork**: every part. Bacon, ham, hocks (for greens), trotters, jowls (the bacon-er bacon).
- **Collards, mustard greens, turnip greens**: cooked long with pork.
- **Black-eyed peas**: the New Year's good-luck dish (hoppin' John).
- **Hot sauce**: vinegar-pepper based (Tabasco, Crystal, Texas Pete). On greens, on chicken, on everything.
- **Sweet tea**: the universal table beverage. Strong, sweet, iced.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the fried chicken, the slow greens, the stew tradition and the breakfast plate. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Southern Fried Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/southern/southern-fried-chicken)**: buttermilk-soaked chicken, double-dredged in seasoned flour, deep-fried in lard till the crust shatters. The defining Southern dish.
2. **[Brunswick Stew](/recipes/cuisine/southern/brunswick-stew)**: tomato-base stew with pulled pork (or chicken), corn, lima beans and potato. The Sunday-supper stew of Georgia and Virginia.
3. **[Shrimp and Grits](/recipes/cuisine/southern/shrimp-and-grits)**: prawns and bacon over creamy stone-ground grits with cheese. The Lowcountry standard, originally a breakfast.
4. **[Hoppin' John](/recipes/cuisine/southern/hoppin-john)**: black-eyed peas, rice, salt pork and onion. The New Year's good-luck dish; eaten with collards (folding money) and cornbread (gold).
5. **[Chicken and Dumplings](/recipes/cuisine/southern/chicken-and-dumplings)**: chicken simmered with rolled flour dumplings and broth. Sunday-night comfort.
6. **[Country Captain](/recipes/cuisine/southern/country-captain)**: curried chicken with raisins, almonds and tomato over rice. A Charleston dish with Anglo-Indian roots, a favourite of FDR.
7. **[Country Ham with Red-Eye Gravy](/recipes/cuisine/southern/country-ham-red-eye-gravy)**: salt-cured country ham pan-fried in its own fat, deglazed with strong black coffee. The Appalachian breakfast.

Cook the fried chicken and the shrimp and grits and you have the headline dishes. The hoppin' John and the chicken and dumplings are the everyday. The Brunswick stew and country captain are the regional gems; the country ham and red-eye gravy is breakfast.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the low-country boil, more BBQ (pulled pork), the breakfast canon (biscuits and gravy, grits, country sausage), the vegetable shelf (collards, fried green tomatoes, succotash) and the dessert tradition (banana pudding, pecan pie, sweet potato pie). Pair everything with sweet tea or a bourbon old fashioned and the cuisine sings.
