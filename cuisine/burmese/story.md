# Burmese

*The kitchen of the crossroads: India to the west, Thailand to the east, China to the north. Built on tea leaves, fermented fish, turmeric, chickpea flour and the long slow simmer.*

## A short history

Burmese cooking grew up in the river valleys of the Irrawaddy and Sittaung, where wet-rice cultivation began at least 3,000 years ago and the Pyu city-states (running roughly 100 BCE to 900 CE) built the first organised kingdoms. The Pyu and the Mon peoples who came after them established the early kitchen: rice as the daily grain, freshwater fish from the river and from the great lake at Inle, leafy greens, the long slow simmer of meat and vegetables over a clay stove, and the use of fermented fish paste (ngapi) as the universal source of salty depth. Three thousand years on, ngapi is still in nearly every Burmese savoury dish, working much the way Parmesan works in Italy.

The Bagan period (9th to 13th centuries) brought Theravada Buddhism from Sri Lanka and the southern Mon kingdoms, and the kitchen bent around it. Pagodas, monasteries and the daily alms round shaped the cooking rhythm; the morning rice offering to monks made breakfast the most important Burmese meal. Vegetarianism never took complete hold, but the country built a deep vegetable kitchen alongside the meat one, and the practice of feeding monks at home on holy days kept dozens of refined slow-cooked dishes alive in household repertoires.

The geography did the rest. India to the west sent turmeric, garam masala, chickpea flour, the curry tradition and the bread shelf. Trade routes from the medieval centuries onward carried Indian merchants and Indian cooks into Lower Burma, and the result was a curry tradition that uses Indian spices but cooks them differently (longer in the oil, with less dairy, finished with fish sauce rather than yoghurt). The Tai-Shan peoples east of the Salween River sent lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime and the fermented-fish-sauce family. China sent noodles, the stir-fry, tofu and the practice of cooking over a high-heat wok. What emerged is a kitchen of its own: milder than Thai, less dairy-heavy than Indian, with a distinctive love of bitterness and astringency that no neighbour matches. Laphet, the fermented tea leaf eaten as a salad with peanuts and dried shrimp, is the clearest single example, and no other country uses tea this way.

Three Burmese empires (the Pagan, the Toungoo and the Konbaung) ran central Burma between the 11th and 19th centuries, and the court cuisines of Mandalay and Ava layered refinement on top of the village kitchen. The 16th-century arrival of chillies from the Americas, brought through Portuguese trade with Goa, added heat to a previously mild palate, though Burmese cooks never embraced chillies with the intensity their Thai neighbours did. Tomatoes, peanuts and corn arrived through the same Columbian Exchange channel and folded into the existing dishes.

British colonial rule from 1824 to 1948 added bread, tea (drunk sweet and milky in the Indian style), the Anglo-Burmese curry, and the particular hill-station hybrid cooking of Maymyo (now Pyin Oo Lwin). The colonial period also pulled large Indian populations into Rangoon, and the modern street-food shelf still carries the Indian-Burmese hybrids: samosas, biryanis and parathas eaten alongside mohinga and laphet thoke. Independence in 1948 was followed by 25 years of parliamentary government and then nearly five decades of military rule, and the resulting isolation kept Burmese food a relative unknown internationally.

Only since the country began opening in the 2010s has the cuisine entered the global conversation. Diaspora kitchens (Burmese-American restaurants in California, San Francisco's Burma Superstar, Burmese tea-leaf salad sellers in London markets) carry the tradition forward, and the political turbulence at home has scattered cooks across three continents. The kitchen still reads as it always did: a confluence of three culinary worlds, simmered slow over a single flame.

## Regional variations

- **Yangon and the south**: coastal, fish-heavy. Mohinga (the fish-noodle soup considered the national dish) was born here.
- **Mandalay and central**: drier, Indian-leaning. Curries and biryanis are stronger.
- **Shan State (east)**: closer to Thailand. Khao soi, sticky rice, more lemongrass.
- **Rakhine (west)**: chilli-forward, more seafood.

## Signature ingredients

- **Ngapi**: fermented shrimp or fish paste. The umami base.
- **Laphet**: fermented tea leaves. Eaten as a salad with peanuts, sesame, garlic, dried shrimp.
- **Turmeric**: in nearly every curry; the colour and bitterness.
- **Chickpea flour**: thickens curries instead of dairy.
- **Tamarind**: the souring agent.
- **Mohinga noodles**: thin rice noodles, the breakfast vehicle.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Mohinga](/recipes/cuisine/burmese/mohinga)**: catfish-noodle soup with lemongrass and chickpea flour. The national breakfast.
2. **[Ohn No Khao Swe](/recipes/cuisine/burmese/ohn-no-khao-swe)**: coconut-chicken noodle curry. The other breakfast classic.
3. **[Wet Tha Hin Lay](/recipes/cuisine/burmese/wet-tha-hin-lay)**: pork curry slow-cooked with garlic, ginger and turmeric.
4. **[Beef Sibyan](/recipes/cuisine/burmese/beef-sibyan)**: rich beef curry with fish sauce and turmeric.
5. **[Lahpet Thoke](/recipes/cuisine/burmese/lahpet-thoke)**: tea-leaf salad with tomato, cabbage, peanuts and fried garlic. The signature Burmese plate.
6. **[Sanwin Makin](/recipes/cuisine/burmese/sanwin-makin)**: semolina cake with coconut and poppy seeds. The everyday Burmese dessert.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Burmese tea (sweet, milky, served in tiny cups) or laphet ye (cold sour-plum drink). For a fuller table, build around mohinga at breakfast, a curry-and-rice lunch, lahpet thoke as the afternoon snack, and a coconut-rich dinner.
