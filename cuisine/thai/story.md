# Thai

*A cuisine of deliberate balance, where sweet, sour, salty, spicy and bitter are tuned against one another in every dish, anchored by fresh herbs, fish sauce, lime and the unmistakable lift of bird's eye chilli.*

## A short history

Thai cooking, called *Siamese* until the country's renaming in 1939, grew out of a fertile crossroads. The earliest layer belongs to the river-delta peoples who farmed sticky rice in the Chao Phraya basin, fished the canals for catfish and snakehead, fermented the smaller fish into the salty *pla ra* that still seasons the Isan pot, and shaded their courtyards with banana, mango, lime and tamarind trees. The wok and the stir-fry came later, in the slow migration south of the Tai-speaking peoples from what is now Yunnan, who brought rice cultivation, the bamboo steamer, and the habit of pounding aromatics in a clay mortar.

Indian traders working the coastal routes from the first millennium onwards layered in dried spices, curry leaves, the idea of the wet spice paste, and the slow-simmered coconut-milk sauce. Khmer cooks at Angkor refined the curry tradition further and passed it north as the kingdoms of Sukhothai and Ayutthaya rose between the 13th and 18th centuries. Persian and Arab merchants visiting Ayutthaya in the 15th and 16th centuries left their fingerprints on dishes like massaman, with its cinnamon, cardamom, star anise and the cardamom-pod aroma that still feels foreign even when the dish itself feels native.

The Portuguese arrived in the 1510s, and the bird's eye chilli came with them as part of the Columbian Exchange. Thai cooks adopted the fruit so quickly and so completely that within a century it had displaced the older black-pepper-and-galangal heat profile, and chilli became the spine of the cuisine. The same Portuguese traders introduced egg-rich custards and sweet-egg-yolk desserts (foi thong, thong yip, thong yot) through a Portuguese cook named Maria Guyomar de Pinha, who served the Ayutthayan court in the 1680s. Those gilded yolks are still on temple-fair sweet trays today.

The royal court of Bangkok refined the principle that anchors all of it (*rod chart*, the balance of tastes) into an ornate, plate-conscious cuisine in the 19th century, while market cooks and rural households kept the same balance running through plainer everyday food. A bowl of *tom yum* is not just sour and spicy: it is sour, salty, sweet, hot and slightly bitter from the kaffir lime leaf, with each note checked against the others. Adjusting the seasoning at the end, a squeeze of lime here, a splash more fish sauce there, is not an afterthought but the central skill.

The 20th century turned Thai cooking into a global cuisine. Pad thai itself was a 1930s nationalist invention, promoted by Prime Minister Phibunsongkhram to build a modern Siamese identity around rice noodles and reduce the country's wheat dependency. Post-war prosperity, the rise of Bangkok as a regional hub, and the Thai-restaurant boom that began in the United States and Australia in the 1970s carried the cuisine outward. The diaspora codified the green-curry-and-pad-thai shorthand the world now recognises, while inside the country market stalls, royal kitchens and home cooks keep refining the same five-taste principle that has run through Thai food for centuries.

## Regional variations

Thailand stretches over a thousand miles from the Lao border to the Malaysian peninsula, and the food shifts dramatically along the way.

- **Central (Bangkok and the river plains)**: the green, red and massaman curries most of the world knows, balanced with coconut milk, palm sugar and fish sauce. Refined, slightly sweeter, and the heart of the restaurant repertoire abroad.
- **Northern (Chiang Mai and Lanna)**: cooler climate cooking, less coconut, more pork and herbs. Sticky rice eaten by hand, *khao soi* curry noodles with their crisp topping, *sai oua* herb-stuffed sausage. Burmese and Yunnanese influences run through it.
- **Northeastern (Isan)**: the Lao-influenced food of the Khorat plateau. *Som tam*, *larb*, grilled meats and sticky rice. Fiercely hot, fermented fish sauce *pla ra* in place of clear fish sauce, and the country's most direct everyday cooking.
- **Southern**: the hottest region, with more turmeric, more dried spice and Muslim-influenced dishes from the Malay border. *Gaeng som* sour curry, *khua kling* dry curry, and seafood pulled straight off the Andaman and Gulf coasts.
- **Royal court cuisine**: an ornate palace tradition of carved fruit, miniature dumplings and exacting plating that survives in a handful of Bangkok dining rooms and shaped how the cuisine presents itself.

A bowl of noodles in Chiang Mai, a plate of som tam in Khon Kaen and a green curry in Bangkok are three different cuisines, held together by the same instinct for balance.

## Signature ingredients

- **Fish sauce (*nam pla*)**: salt, savouriness and umami in one bottle. The everyday seasoning that fills the role salt plays in Western cooking.
- **Palm sugar**: caramelly, less sharp than white sugar. Provides the sweet edge in dressings and curries.
- **Kaffir lime leaf**: glossy, double-lobed leaves with a clean citrus-pine aroma. Torn or finely shredded into curries, soups and salads.
- **Lemongrass**: woody stalks bruised and sliced for soups and pastes, or pounded into curry bases.
- **Galangal**: a hard, peppery rhizome related to ginger but sharper and more medicinal. Not interchangeable with ginger.
- **Thai basil**: anise-scented, purple-stemmed, holds up to brief heat. Different again from holy basil (*krapow*), which is hotter and more peppery.
- **Bird's eye chillies**: tiny, fierce, used fresh and dried. The heat backbone of the cuisine.
- **Shrimp paste (*kapi*)**: fermented, pungent, transformed by cooking. The funky depth that makes a curry paste taste built rather than mixed.
- **Coconut milk**: cracked first to release the oil, then thinned with the rest of the can. The carrier for most curries.
- **[Thai green curry paste](../../base-ingredients/curry-paste/thai-green-paste.md)** and **[Thai red curry paste](../../base-ingredients/curry-paste/thai-red-paste.md)**: the two house pastes worth keeping on hand once you are cooking Thai food often.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Thai green curry paste](pastes/thai-green-curry-paste.md). Green chillies, lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, coriander root, shrimp paste, pounded to a fragrant paste. Teaches the wet-paste foundation every coconut-milk curry leans on, and the cracking-the-coconut-cream step that turns oil-slicked paste into a proper sauce.
2. [Basil chicken stir-fry](basil-chicken-stir-fry.md). *Pad krapow gai*, the street-stall lunch eaten across the country. Teaches the high-heat wok-tossed protein-plus-aromatic-plus-leafy-herb formula that underpins most Thai stir-fries.
3. [Green papaya salad](salads/green-papaya-salad.md). *Som tam*, pounded in a clay mortar with chillies, garlic, palm sugar, fish sauce, lime, dried shrimp and shredded unripe papaya. Teaches the *rod chart* balance in its purest form: five tastes, no cooking, adjust until it sings.
4. [Traditional pad thai](traditional-pad-thai.md). Rice noodles tossed with tamarind, fish sauce and palm sugar, with prawns, egg, beansprouts and peanuts. Teaches the noodle-pan technique of softening, sauce-coating and finishing in one wok.
5. [Tom yum goong](soup/tom-yum-goong.md). Prawn soup with lemongrass, kaffir lime, galangal, lime juice, chilli and fish sauce. Teaches the hot-sour broth that defines clear Thai soups, where the aromatics infuse but are not eaten.
6. [Gai yang](gai-yang.md). Isan-style grilled chicken marinated in lemongrass, coriander root, white pepper, garlic and fish sauce. Teaches the herb-paste-and-grill technique behind northeastern Thai meat cookery, and gives you the dipping-sauce vocabulary that goes with sticky rice.
7. [Mango sticky rice](desserts/mango-sticky-rice.md). *Khao niao mamuang*. Glutinous rice steamed, dressed with sweetened salted coconut cream, served with ripe mango. Teaches the sticky-rice steaming method and the sweet-salty coconut sauce that closes most Thai meals.

After those seven you have the wet-paste foundation, the wok-stir-fry rhythm, the pounded salad balance, the noodle wok technique, the hot-sour broth, the grill-and-dip vocabulary and the sticky-rice dessert. The rest of the Thai catalogue is variation on these moves.
