# Swedish

*The kitchen of the long winter and the midsummer feast. Pickled herring, salt-cured salmon, meatballs in cream sauce, lingonberry on everything, and the world's most-loved coffee-break tradition (fika).*

## A short history

Swedish cooking begins on the Baltic shore in the Viking age and earlier. The 9th to 11th-century Norse who built Birka and Sigtuna fished the herring shoals, smoked salmon and eel in chimney smokehouses, ground rye and barley on rotary querns, and preserved everything they couldn't eat fresh in salt, smoke, vinegar or whey. The fermented fish tradition that eventually produced surströmming (sour herring, the world's most pungent food) comes straight out of that Viking pantry: a way of using small barrels of salt to last a herring catch the whole winter. The cold-climate shelf the kitchen still leans on (rye bread, dairy, salt-and-smoke fish, root vegetables, pork) was already locked in by the year 1000.

The medieval centuries tied Sweden to the Hanseatic League through Stockholm and Visby, and the German salt-and-spice trade flowed in along with the merchants. Lübeck salt cured the herring; cardamom, ginger, saffron and pepper became luxury ingredients in monastery and noble kitchens. The saffron pancake of Gotland and the gingerbread (pepparkakor) tradition both date to that German-Baltic overlap. The Catholic feast and fast calendar codified fish-eating: cod, herring and pike anchored Lent and Friday meals across the country until the Reformation cut Sweden loose from Rome in 1527.

The 16th and 17th centuries saw Sweden become a Baltic empire under Gustavus Adolphus and Queen Christina, and the royal court at Stockholm imported French chefs, German pastry techniques, and the formal long-table service that became the smörgåsbord. The word literally means "sandwich table"; the codified spread of cold herring, salmon, cured meats, cheese, bread and warm dishes took its shape in the 1600s royal court and still anchors Christmas and Midsummer feasts. The potato arrived in the 18th century (championed in Sweden by King Gustav III, who served it at state dinners to convince sceptical farmers it was worth growing) and within a generation became the second staple after bread; Janssons frestelse and the various potato gratins all date to the late 1700s.

The 19th and 20th centuries spread Swedish food internationally through emigration (1.3 million Swedes moved to America between 1850 and 1930, taking pickled herring, meatballs and lingonberry with them; the Swedish-American Christmas table in Minnesota looks exactly like the one in Stockholm) and through the cookbook tradition. Cajsa Warg's 1755 *Hjelpreda i Hushållningen* and Hagdahl's 1879 *Kokkonsten* codified the home cooking; later, Vår Kokbok (first published 1951) became the household standard, with millions of copies sold. The two world wars (Sweden stayed neutral but rationed) and the post-war social-democratic decades institutionalised husmanskost, the country home cooking refined into school-canteen and restaurant standards. IKEA picked up the meatball plate in the 1980s and turned it into one of the world's most-eaten Swedish dishes.

The 21st-century New Nordic moment (Magnus Nilsson at Faviken, the Swedish-Norwegian-Danish foraging-and-fermentation movement that grew alongside Copenhagen's Noma) gave Swedish food a global second wind. Wild garlic, sea buckthorn, lingonberry, juniper and the long history of preservation got rebranded as cutting-edge gastronomy. The everyday Swedish kitchen still rotates around fika (coffee plus a sweet bun) twice a day, and the structure of the meal, the rhythm of the year, the foods of Christmas and Midsummer and crayfish-party August, all read the same as they did a hundred years ago.

## Regional variations

- **Stockholm and Mälardalen**: the codified mainstream. Köttbullar, gravlax, Janssons frestelse.
- **Skåne (south)**: closer to Denmark. Goose at Mårten Gås (St Martin's), eel traditions, the sweet tooth.
- **Norrland (north)**: the Sami country. Reindeer, cloudberries, fermented herring (surströmming, the world's most-pungent food).
- **Gotland (Baltic island)**: lamb, fresh asparagus, the most-loved Swedish saffron-pancake (saffranspannkaka).

## Signature ingredients

- **Herring**: pickled in dozens of varieties (mustard, dill, curry, onion, sherry).
- **Salmon**: gravlax (cured), graved (rare-cooked), smoked.
- **Potatoes**: boiled, mashed, sliced into the famous Jansson's casserole.
- **Lingonberry**: as jam with everything; meatballs, pancakes, sausages.
- **Dill**: heavy use. In gravlax, in pickles, in sauces.
- **Cardamom**: in pastries (especially the cardamom bun, kardemummabulle).
- **Crispbread (knäckebröd)**: the rye-based snack and everyday bread.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Köttbullar](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/kottbullar)**: meatballs (pork-and-beef) in cream sauce, served with lingonberry jam, mashed potato and pickled cucumber. The national dish.
2. **[Gravlax](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/gravlax)**: salmon cured with salt, sugar, dill and aquavit; served thinly sliced with mustard-dill sauce.
3. **[Janssons Frestelse](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/janssons-frestelse)**: potato-and-anchovy gratin with onion and cream. The Christmas Eve staple.
4. **[Inlagd Sill](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/inlagd-sill)**: pickled herring in vinegar-sugar-onion brine. The smörgåsbord cornerstone.
5. **[Pyttipanna](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/pyttipanna)**: hash of diced potato, onion and leftover meat, topped with a fried egg and beetroot.
6. **[Tunnbrödsrulle](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/tunnbrodsrulle)**: thin flatbread wrapped around mashed potato, sausage, prawn salad and mustard. The Stockholm late-night snack.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with snaps (aquavit, served chilled in small glasses, with a sung skål between courses) or with Christmas glögg (mulled wine with raisins and almonds). For Midsummer (mid-June), gravlax plus new potatoes plus fresh dill plus a strawberry tart, all eaten outside till the sun finally sets at midnight.
