# Swedish

*The kitchen of the long winter and the midsummer feast. Pickled herring, salt-cured salmon, meatballs in cream sauce, lingonberry on everything, and the world's most-loved coffee-break tradition (fika).*

## A short history

Swedish cooking sits on the cold-climate Baltic-Nordic tradition: rye bread, salt-and-smoke preservation of fish (herring, salmon, eel), root vegetables, dairy and pork. The smörgåsbord — the long table of cold and warm small dishes — was codified in the 16th-century royal court and remains the structure of Christmas and Midsummer feasts.

The 19th-20th century saw Swedish food spread internationally through emigration (1.3 million Swedes moved to America between 1850–1930) and IKEA (the meatball plate sold by the kitchen-furniture company is now one of the world's most-eaten Swedish dishes). The post-war Swedish kitchen institutionalised "husmanskost" — peasant home cooking refined into restaurant standards.

The 21st century New Nordic moment (Faviken, the Swedish-Norwegian-Danish foraging-and-fermentation movement) gave Swedish food a global second wind. The everyday Swedish kitchen continues to anchor around fika (coffee + sweet bun) twice a day.

## Regional variations

- **Stockholm and Mälardalen**: the codified mainstream. Köttbullar, gravlax, Janssons frestelse.
- **Skåne (south)**: closer to Denmark. Goose at Mårten Gås (St Martin's), eel traditions, the sweet tooth.
- **Norrland (north)**: the Sami country. Reindeer, cloudberries, fermented herring (surströmming — the world's most-pungent food).
- **Gotland (Baltic island)**: lamb, fresh asparagus, the most-loved Swedish saffron-pancake (saffranspannkaka).

## Signature ingredients

- **Herring**: pickled in dozens of varieties (mustard, dill, curry, onion, sherry).
- **Salmon**: gravlax (cured), graved (rare-cooked), smoked.
- **Potatoes**: boiled, mashed, sliced into the famous Jansson's casserole.
- **Lingonberry**: as jam with everything — meatballs, pancakes, sausages.
- **Dill**: heavy use. In gravlax, in pickles, in sauces.
- **Cardamom**: in pastries (especially the cardamom bun, kardemummabulle).
- **Crispbread (knäckebröd)**: the rye-based snack/everyday bread.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Köttbullar](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/kottbullar)**: meatballs (pork-and-beef) in cream sauce, served with lingonberry jam, mashed potato and pickled cucumber. The national dish.
2. **[Gravlax](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/gravlax)**: salmon cured with salt, sugar, dill and aquavit; served thinly sliced with mustard-dill sauce.
3. **[Janssons Frestelse](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/janssons-frestelse)**: potato-and-anchovy gratin with onion and cream. The Christmas Eve staple.
4. **[Inlagd Sill](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/inlagd-sill)**: pickled herring in vinegar-sugar-onion brine. The smörgåsbord cornerstone.
5. **[Pyttipanna](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/pyttipanna)**: hash of diced potato, onion and leftover meat, topped with a fried egg and beetroot.
6. **[Tunnbrödsrulle](/recipes/cuisine/swedish/tunnbrodsrulle)**: thin flatbread wrapped around mashed potato, sausage, prawn salad and mustard. The Stockholm late-night snack.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with snaps (aquavit, served chilled in small glasses, with a sung skål between courses) or with Christmas glögg (mulled wine with raisins and almonds). For Midsummer (mid-June), gravlax + new potatoes + fresh dill + a strawberry tart, all eaten outside till the sun finally sets at midnight.
