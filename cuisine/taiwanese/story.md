# Taiwanese

*The Chinese-Japanese-aboriginal hybrid kitchen. Beef noodle soup as the national dish, gua bao buns as the world-famous export, oyster omelettes from the night markets, and the world's most-loved bubble tea.*

## A short history

Taiwanese cooking is the synthesis of three traditions. The indigenous Austronesian peoples (~2% of the population today) contributed millet, root vegetables and the use of wild herbs. The 17th-century Han Chinese migrations (mostly Hokkien from Fujian and Hakka from Guangdong) brought the dominant culinary tradition — Hokkien stir-fry, the noodle-soup tradition, the use of soy sauce and rice wine. The 1895–1945 Japanese colonisation layered on bento culture, sashimi, the use of dashi.

The 1949 mainland-Chinese migration (the Kuomintang retreat after the civil war) brought beef noodle soup (the dish was invented in Taiwan by Sichuanese KMT veterans missing home — there's no equivalent dish on the mainland). Today beef noodle soup is the national dish, with an annual Beef Noodle Soup Festival in Taipei.

The night market culture is the defining everyday food scene. Taipei's Shilin, Raohe and Ningxia night markets are world-famous; the food categories are huge — gua bao, oyster omelettes, stinky tofu, lu rou fan, popcorn chicken, bubble tea (invented in Taichung in the 1980s).

## Regional variations

- **Taipei and the north**: codified mainstream. Beef noodle soup, night-market food.
- **Tainan (south)**: the oldest Han settlement. Distinct dishes, sweeter palate.
- **Kaohsiung and southern**: more seafood, distinct Hakka traditions.
- **Hakka regions (Hsinchu, Miaoli)**: lei cha (ground tea-and-grain drink), preserved-mustard-green dishes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Soy sauce**: light, dark, sweet. The seasoning.
- **Rice wine**: cooking and drinking.
- **Sesame oil**: the finishing oil.
- **Five-spice powder**: in braised dishes (lu rou fan).
- **Shacha sauce**: BBQ-style condiment.
- **Stinky tofu**: fermented, pungent, deeply loved.
- **Tapioca pearls**: in bubble tea.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Beef Noodle Soup](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/beef-noodle-soup)**: slow-braised beef shank in soy-and-chilli broth with wheat noodles, pickled mustard greens and spring onion. The national dish.
2. **[Gua Bao](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/gua-bao)**: steamed white buns folded around slow-braised pork belly with crushed peanuts, pickled mustard greens and coriander. The night-market sandwich that went global.
3. **[Lu Rou Fan](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/lu-rou-fan)**: minced or diced pork belly slow-cooked in soy sauce, rice wine, sugar, five-spice and shallots, served over rice. The most-eaten Taiwanese dish.
4. **[Three Cup Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/three-cup-chicken)**: chicken braised in equal parts soy sauce, sesame oil and rice wine, with Thai basil at the end. The Hakka-influenced classic.
5. **[Oyster Omelette](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/oyster-omelette)**: small oysters folded into a sweet-potato-starch egg pancake, drizzled with sweet-and-savoury sauce. The Shilin night-market icon.
6. **[Bawan](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/bawan)**: large translucent dumplings with pork, bamboo shoot and shiitake, steamed then deep-fried, served with sweet chilli sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with bubble tea (the Taiwanese invention now ubiquitous globally) or with Taiwan Beer. End with pineapple cakes (the most-gifted Taiwanese souvenir food) or with the night-market shaved ice with mango and condensed milk.
