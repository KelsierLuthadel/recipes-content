# Taiwanese

*The Chinese-Japanese-aboriginal hybrid kitchen. Beef noodle soup as the national dish, gua bao buns as the world-famous export, oyster omelettes from the night markets, and the world's most-loved bubble tea.*

## A short history

Taiwanese cooking begins with the indigenous Austronesian peoples who have lived on the island for at least six thousand years. Sixteen recognised tribes (the Amis, Atayal, Bunun, Paiwan, Rukai and others, about 2% of the population today) farmed millet, taro and sweet potato on the mountain slopes, hunted wild boar and deer, fermented rice into millet wine, and used the indigenous herbs and wild greens that still appear in tribal cooking. The pre-Han kitchen ran on root vegetables, freshwater fish, and the open-fire stone-grilling techniques that some tribes still practise at harvest festivals. This older layer survives in the mountains and along the eastern coast, less visible than the Chinese-Japanese mainstream but very much alive.

The 17th century brought two waves of outside settlement that would dominate the next four hundred years. Dutch traders ran a colony at Fort Zeelandia near modern Tainan from 1624 to 1662, introducing sugar cultivation and Western trading networks. Then Han Chinese migrations from Fujian (Hokkien speakers) and Guangdong (Hakka) crossed the strait in increasing numbers, bringing the dominant culinary tradition: Hokkien stir-fry, soy sauce and rice wine seasoning, the noodle-soup format, oyster cultivation along the south coast. The Hokkien settlers brought oyster omelette, danzai noodles, and the savoury soy-braised pork that became lu rou fan. The Hakka brought salt-preserved pork, leibu cha (the ground tea-and-grain drink), and the use of dried and fermented vegetables.

The Qing dynasty annexed Taiwan in 1683 but kept it as a frontier territory; the island stayed Hokkien-Hakka-aboriginal in character until the 19th century, when the Qing finally invested in it. After defeat in the First Sino-Japanese War, China ceded Taiwan to Japan in 1895, and the Japanese ran the island as a colony until 1945. The Japanese decades layered on bento culture, sashimi, the use of dashi, soy sauce brewing on industrial scale, beer (Taiwan Beer descends from the Japanese Takasago Brewery), and tempura-style frying. The island's cooking absorbed all of it; even now Taiwanese soy sauce, miso and pickle traditions show their Japanese inheritance.

The 1949 mainland-Chinese migration changed the kitchen for good. Two million Kuomintang soldiers, officials and refugees fled to Taiwan after Chiang Kai-shek lost the civil war, bringing the cooking of every Chinese province with them. Beef noodle soup was invented in Taiwan by Sichuanese KMT veterans missing home, married to local Hokkien soy-braising; there's no equivalent dish on the mainland. The new arrivals brought xiao long bao (the Shanghai soup dumplings now perfected at Din Tai Fung, Taipei), Sichuan pickled mustard greens, Shandong wheat noodles, Hunan smoked meats. Taiwanese food became, almost overnight, a compressed survey of regional Chinese cooking, with Hokkien-Japanese roots underneath.

The night market culture is the defining everyday food scene. Taipei's Shilin, Raohe and Ningxia night markets are world-famous; the food categories run long: gua bao, oyster omelettes, stinky tofu, lu rou fan, popcorn chicken, bubble tea (invented in Taichung in the 1980s, either at Chun Shui Tang or Hanlin Tea Room, both still claim it). Today beef noodle soup is the national dish, with an annual Beef Noodle Soup Festival in Taipei; bubble tea is the global export; and the post-1990s democratic era has produced a confident food-tourism culture where the night markets are as much an attraction as the Palace Museum.

## Regional variations

- **Taipei and the north**: codified mainstream. Beef noodle soup, night-market food.
- **Tainan (south)**: the oldest Han settlement. Distinct dishes, sweeter palate.
- **Kaohsiung and southern**: more seafood, distinct Hakka traditions.
- **Hakka regions (Hsinchu, Miaoli)**: lei cha (ground tea-and-grain drink), preserved-mustard-green dishes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Soy sauce**: light, dark, sweet. The seasoning.
- **Rice wine**: cooking and drinking.
- **Sesame oil**: the finishing oil.
- **Five-spice powder**: in braised dishes (lu rou fan).
- **Shacha sauce**: BBQ-style condiment.
- **Stinky tofu**: fermented, pungent, deeply loved.
- **Tapioca pearls**: in bubble tea.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Beef Noodle Soup](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/beef-noodle-soup)**: slow-braised beef shank in soy-and-chilli broth with wheat noodles, pickled mustard greens and spring onion. The national dish.
2. **[Gua Bao](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/gua-bao)**: steamed white buns folded around slow-braised pork belly with crushed peanuts, pickled mustard greens and coriander. The night-market sandwich that went global.
3. **[Lu Rou Fan](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/lu-rou-fan)**: minced or diced pork belly slow-cooked in soy sauce, rice wine, sugar, five-spice and shallots, served over rice. The most-eaten Taiwanese dish.
4. **[Three Cup Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/three-cup-chicken)**: chicken braised in equal parts soy sauce, sesame oil and rice wine, with Thai basil at the end. The Hakka-influenced classic.
5. **[Oyster Omelette](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/oyster-omelette)**: small oysters folded into a sweet-potato-starch egg pancake, drizzled with sweet-and-savoury sauce. The Shilin night-market icon.
6. **[Bawan](/recipes/cuisine/taiwanese/bawan)**: large translucent dumplings with pork, bamboo shoot and shiitake, steamed then deep-fried, served with sweet chilli sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with bubble tea (the Taiwanese invention now ubiquitous globally) or with Taiwan Beer. End with pineapple cakes (the most-gifted Taiwanese souvenir food) or with the night-market shaved ice with mango and condensed milk.
