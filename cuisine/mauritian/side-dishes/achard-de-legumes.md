# Achard de Légumes

![Achard de Légumes](resources/achard-de-legumes.jpg)

*Mauritius's vegetable pickle: a turmeric-yellow mix of cabbage, carrot, French beans, cauliflower and chilli briefly cooked in vinegar with mustard seed, garlic, ginger and chilli, served warm or cold. The crunchy aromatic relish that turns up alongside biryani, rougaille and curries on every Mauritian table.*

**Serves:** 6-8 (as a side or relish)

**Prep Time:** 25 minutes

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes (plus 30 minutes resting)

## Overview
Achard de légumes is Mauritius's signature vegetable pickle, brought to the island by Indian indentured workers in the 19th century and now thoroughly Mauritian: cabbage, carrot, French beans, cauliflower, onion and green chilli briefly tossed in a turmeric-and-mustard-seed-spiked vinegar with garlic, ginger and oil till the vegetables soften just slightly but keep their crunch, then rested for the flavours to develop. The result is a bright golden-yellow relish, aromatic from toasted mustard seed and ginger, with a sharp vinegar profile and gentle chilli warmth. It sits between an Indian achar (more deeply fermented) and a French pickle (more briefly brined): lightly cooked rather than fully fermented, but with enough vinegar and spice to keep a week or two refrigerated. The vegetables go in raw and only swirl briefly in the hot vinegar; longer turns them soft and ruins the texture. The mustard seeds must toast properly in hot oil before the vinegar hits; the pop tells you they are ready.

## Ingredients

### Vegetables
- 200 g white cabbage (cored, thinly shredded)
- 2 medium carrots (peeled, julienned)
- 150 g French beans (or green beans; topped and tailed, cut into 4 cm lengths)
- 200 g cauliflower (cut into small florets, about 2 cm across)
- 1 medium white onion (very thinly sliced)
- 4 fresh green chillies (slit lengthwise; deseed for milder; or substitute with milder chillies)

### Spice base
- 4 tablespoons vegetable oil
- 1 tablespoon black mustard seeds
- 1 tablespoon yellow mustard seeds (or use all black if yellow aren't available)
- 6 garlic cloves (finely chopped)
- 1 thumb (3 cm) fresh ginger (finely grated)
- 1 tablespoon ground turmeric
- 1 teaspoon ground cumin
- 1 teaspoon ground coriander
- ½ teaspoon ground fenugreek (optional but very Mauritian)

### Pickling liquid
- 200 ml white vinegar (or apple cider vinegar)
- 3 tablespoons caster sugar
- 2 teaspoons fine sea salt
- 50 ml water

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the vegetables
1. Shred the cabbage thinly with a sharp knife (about 5 mm wide).
2. Julienne the carrots; cut the French beans into 4 cm lengths; cut the cauliflower into small florets; thinly slice the onion.
3. Slit the green chillies lengthwise (don't chop; you want them to be visible in the finished achard).
4. Combine all vegetables and chillies in a wide bowl.
5. Sprinkle with 1 teaspoon of the salt (from the pickling liquid quantity); toss together.
6. Let stand 15 minutes while you make the spice base; this draws out a small amount of moisture and lightly cures the cabbage so it stays crunchy after cooking.

### Stage 2 - Toast the mustard seeds
1. Heat the oil in a wide heavy saucepan (or large frying pan) over medium heat.
2. When the oil is hot but not smoking, add both mustard seeds.
3. Cook 30-60 seconds, stirring with a wooden spoon, till the seeds pop and crackle. The kitchen will smell properly mustardy.
4. Don't let them burn; if the popping starts to slow and any seeds go very dark, move on quickly.

### Stage 3 - Add the aromatics
1. Add the chopped garlic and grated ginger to the pan.
2. Cook 30 seconds till fragrant; the garlic should turn pale gold but not brown.

### Stage 4 - Add the ground spices
1. Add the turmeric, cumin, coriander and fenugreek (if using).
2. Stir for 20 seconds; the spices bloom in the hot oil. The pan should turn a deep golden-orange from the turmeric.

### Stage 5 - Add the pickling liquid
1. Pour in the vinegar, the remaining sugar and water, plus the salt (1 teaspoon remaining if you used 1 teaspoon to wilt the cabbage).
2. Stir to dissolve the sugar.
3. Bring to a low simmer.

### Stage 6 - Add the vegetables and cook briefly
1. Tip in the drained vegetable mix (press out any liquid from the salted cabbage if it's wet).
2. Stir everything together; the vegetables should be coated in the hot spiced vinegar.
3. Cook for just 2 minutes over medium heat, stirring frequently; the vegetables should soften slightly but keep most of their crunch.
4. Take off the heat.

### Stage 7 - Rest and serve
1. Transfer to a wide bowl or clean jar.
2. Let rest at room temperature 30 minutes; the vegetables continue to absorb the flavours.
3. Serve warm as a side; or refrigerate and serve cold the next day (the flavour deepens overnight).

## Notes
- **Toast the mustard seeds properly:** the toasting is what gives achard its distinctive flavour. Wait for the popping sound; that's when the seeds are ready. Don't let them burn; charred mustard seeds taste bitter.
- **Briefly cooked, not boiled:** the vegetables go into the hot vinegar for just 2 minutes. Longer cooking gives soft mushy vegetables; achard should be crunchy.
- **Salt the cabbage first:** salting the cabbage for 15 minutes before adding to the pan draws out moisture; the cabbage stays properly crunchy after cooking instead of going limp.
- **Vinegar choice:** white vinegar gives the brightest tang; apple cider vinegar gives a slightly sweeter mellower profile. Both work; choose to taste.
- **Let it rest:** achard tastes better after a 30-minute rest at room temperature, and even better the next day. Make ahead if possible.

## Variations
**Achard de carrots (carrot only):** make with 400 g of julienned carrot alone; gives a sweet bright orange pickle that's particularly good with biryani.
**Achard de palm heart (achard coeur de palmier):** swap half the cauliflower for sliced palm hearts (canned); gives a tropical Mauritian variation.
**Spicier achard:** double the green chillies and add 1 teaspoon of red chilli powder to the spice base; the proper Mauritian fierce version.
**With fresh turmeric:** swap the ground turmeric for 2 thumbs of fresh turmeric (peeled and finely grated); gives a brighter fresher flavour and the most vivid golden colour.

## Serving
Alongside biryani Mauritian-style, rougaille, any curry, or as a relish for grilled fish or chicken. A small spoonful next to the main dish; it cuts through richer flavours and adds a crunchy aromatic note. Often part of the Mauritian Sunday lunch table alongside dholl puri and curries.

## Storage
- Keeps refrigerated 2 weeks in a clean sealed jar; the flavour deepens noticeably over the first 3-4 days.
- Don't freeze; the texture suffers completely (the cabbage and cauliflower turn to mush).
- For longer storage, you can process the jar in a boiling water bath for 15 minutes to make a shelf-stable pickle; keeps 6 months sealed.
- Day-old achard is excellent in sandwiches, on cheese boards, or chopped through fried rice.
