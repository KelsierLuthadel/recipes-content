# Mauritian

*The kitchen of the Indian Ocean melting pot. Hindu Indian, Tamil South Indian, Chinese Hakka, French Creole and African slave traditions on one small island, all cooked together until you can't tell where one ends and another begins.*

## A short history

Mauritian cooking is the most synthetic cuisine in the Indian Ocean. The Dutch (1638), the French (1715), and the British (1810) successively colonised the uninhabited island; slaves from East Africa, then indentured labourers from India and contract workers from China built the population we see today. Each group brought its food and cooked it side by side; the resulting cuisine is genuinely creolised — a curry on the same plate as a Chinese stir-fry, served with French baguette and African chilli sauce.

The Indian inheritance is the deepest. Roughly 67% of Mauritians are of Indian descent (Hindu and Muslim from Bihar, Uttar Pradesh and Tamil Nadu); the dhal puri (the lentil-stuffed flatbread filled with curry — a Bihari import) is essentially a national dish. The Chinese (Hakka) community brought stir-fry technique and noodles; the African-Creole tradition contributed rougaille (the tomato-based stew), the use of small chillies, and the love of saltfish.

The French colonial period left a deep mark too — baguettes for breakfast, Bordeaux wines, the French language as a lingua franca.

## Regional variations

The island is small and the cuisine is broadly consistent. Sub-traditions are by community:

- **Indo-Mauritian**: dhal puri, biryani, curries with the South Indian temper.
- **Sino-Mauritian (mostly Hakka)**: bouchons (small dumplings), mine frite (stir-fried noodles).
- **Creole**: rougaille, vindaye (sour fish curry), the African-Malagasy flavour profile.
- **Franco-Mauritian**: the Cordon Bleu tradition, retained in the upscale restaurants.

## Signature ingredients

- **Small green chillies (piment cabri)**: very hot, in nearly every relish.
- **Coriander leaf**: heavy use.
- **Cumin and turmeric**: in the curry powders.
- **Coconut milk**: in some curries, in rice dishes.
- **Saltfish**: with rice, with vindaye.
- **Tamarind**: in vindaye, in chutneys.
- **Roti and farata (parathas)**: the Indian breads.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Dholl Puri](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/dholl-puri)**: yellow-split-pea-stuffed flatbread, often filled with bean curry, coriander chutney and chilli relish. The unofficial national dish.
2. **[Rougaille](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/rougaille)**: tomato-and-onion-based stew (with fish, sausage or eggs), the Creole signature.
3. **[Vindaye Poule](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/vindaye-poule)**: chicken cooked in mustard seeds and turmeric oil — the local descendant of Goan vindaloo. Often served cold the next day.
4. **[Cari Poulet Pomme de Terre](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/cari-poulet-pomme-de-terre)**: chicken curry with potato — the everyday Mauritian curry.
5. **[Cari Poulet Hakka](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/cari-poulet-hakka)**: Hakka-Mauritian chicken curry with soy sauce and ginger — the Chinese-creole hybrid.
6. **[Octopus Curry](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/octopus-curry)**: ourite curry — tender octopus in a turmeric-tomato sauce with coriander.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Phoenix beer (the national lager) or with Green Island Rum (the local sugar-cane rum). End with napolitaine (small jam-and-icing biscuits) or with sliced mango with chilli and salt.
