# Mauritian

*The kitchen of the Indian Ocean melting pot. Hindu Indian, Tamil South Indian, Chinese Hakka, French Creole and African traditions on one small island, all cooked together until you can't tell where one ends and another begins.*

## A short history

Mauritian cooking is the most synthetic cuisine in the Indian Ocean, and uniquely so because the island had no indigenous population. When the Portuguese first sighted it in 1507 and the Dutch landed in 1598, Mauritius was an empty volcanic outcrop in the middle of the ocean, home to the dodo and the ebony forest and nothing human. Every cooking tradition on the island arrived by ship, which means the kitchen is a record of who came, who stayed, and what they could grow in the volcanic soil.

The Dutch established the first colony in 1638, planting sugar cane (from Java), introducing the Java deer for hunting, and exterminating the dodo within a few decades. They abandoned the island in 1710, having barely cooked at all. The French took over in 1715, renamed it Île de France, and built the colonial economy that shaped everything since. French planters established sugar plantations across the lowlands and brought enslaved East and Southeast Africans (from Mozambique, Madagascar and the Swahili coast) to work them, along with smaller numbers from West Africa and India. The African-Malagasy kitchen of the slave quarters cooked rougaille (the tomato-and-onion-based stew, whose name comes from the French *rougeoyer*, to redden), used the small ferocious bird's-eye chillies that thrived in the tropical climate, and developed the saltfish tradition that turned poor-quality ration cod into something worth eating. The French colons themselves kept Bordeaux wines, baguette breakfasts and Cordon Bleu technique alive in the planter houses.

The British seized the island in 1810 during the Napoleonic Wars and renamed it Mauritius, but allowed the French planter class to keep its language, law and Catholicism. The decisive change came with abolition in 1835. Plantation owners needed labour to replace the freed slaves, and the British solution was the indentured labour system: between 1834 and 1910, around 450,000 indentured workers arrived from India, mostly Hindu Bhojpuri-speakers from Bihar and Uttar Pradesh, with smaller numbers of Tamils from the south and Muslims from across the subcontinent. They brought the entire Indian kitchen with them: roti and farata, dhal puri (the Bihari flatbread stuffed with yellow split peas that is now essentially the national dish), the masala dabba spice box, the temper of mustard seed and curry leaf, biryani, samosa. The Aapravasi Ghat, where indentured workers landed in Port Louis, is now a UNESCO World Heritage site. Roughly 67% of modern Mauritians are descended from those arrivals.

Hakka Chinese migrants from Guangdong arrived in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, mostly as small traders and shopkeepers, and built a parallel kitchen of mine frite (stir-fried noodles), bouchons (small steamed pork dumplings) and the Cantonese roast meats that fill the Chinese restaurants of Port Louis. They opened the corner shop (la boutique chinois) that anchored every village, and the cross-cultural exchange happened naturally: Indo-Mauritian housewives bought soy sauce off the same shelf as the lentils, Sino-Mauritians learned to like dhal puri for lunch. By the early 20th century the cuisine had genuinely creolised; a curry sits on the same plate as a Chinese stir-fry, served with a French baguette and a sharp Creole chilli sauce.

Independence came in 1968 and republic status in 1992. The post-independence decades have built a wealthy, multi-ethnic society where Hindi, French, Creole, English and Mandarin all share the school grounds and the family table. The cuisine continues to evolve: a younger generation of chefs is putting indigenous Mauritian ingredients (octopus from the lagoon, palm hearts, breadfruit) into restaurant cooking that draws on all four founding traditions at once.

## Regional variations

The island is small and the cuisine is broadly consistent. Sub-traditions are by community:

- **Indo-Mauritian**: dhal puri, biryani, curries with the South Indian temper.
- **Sino-Mauritian (mostly Hakka)**: bouchons (small dumplings), mine frite (stir-fried noodles).
- **Creole**: rougaille, vindaye (sour fish curry), the African-Malagasy flavour profile.
- **Franco-Mauritian**: the Cordon Bleu tradition, kept alive in the upscale restaurants.

## Signature ingredients

- **Small green chillies (piment cabri)**: very hot, in nearly every relish.
- **Coriander leaf**: heavy use.
- **Cumin and turmeric**: in the curry powders.
- **Coconut milk**: in some curries, in rice dishes.
- **Saltfish**: with rice, with vindaye.
- **Tamarind**: in vindaye, in chutneys.
- **Roti and farata (parathas)**: the Indian breads.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Dholl Puri](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/dholl-puri)**: yellow-split-pea-stuffed flatbread, often filled with bean curry, coriander chutney and chilli relish. The unofficial national dish.
2. **[Rougaille](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/rougaille)**: tomato-and-onion-based stew (with fish, sausage or eggs), the Creole signature.
3. **[Vindaye Poule](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/vindaye-poule)**: chicken cooked in mustard seeds and turmeric oil, the local descendant of Goan vindaloo. Often served cold the next day.
4. **[Cari Poulet Pomme de Terre](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/cari-poulet-pomme-de-terre)**: chicken curry with potato, the everyday Mauritian curry.
5. **[Cari Poulet Hakka](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/cari-poulet-hakka)**: Hakka-Mauritian chicken curry with soy sauce and ginger, the Chinese-creole hybrid.
6. **[Octopus Curry](/recipes/cuisine/mauritian/octopus-curry)**: ourite curry, tender octopus in a turmeric-tomato sauce with coriander.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Phoenix beer (the national lager) or with Green Island Rum (the local sugar-cane rum). End with napolitaine (small jam-and-icing biscuits) or with sliced mango with chilli and salt.
