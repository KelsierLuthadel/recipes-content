# Persian (Iranian)

*The cuisine of slow rice, sour fruits and the long stewed khoresh. Built on saffron, dried lime and dried sour cherry, with a kitchen tradition that's been refining itself for 2,500 years.*

## A short history

Persian cooking is the longest continuously codified cuisine in the Middle East. The Achaemenid court (550 to 330 BCE), ruling from Persepolis across an empire that ran from the Indus to the Aegean, was already eating recognisably Persian food: slow stews thickened with ground walnuts and almonds, fragrant rice with herbs, lamb spit-roasted with sumac, sour fruit cooked into meat. Greek sources describe the Persian royal table as the most elaborate in the ancient world, the king served from gold dishes, the meals running to dozens of courses. The technique the empire pioneered (cooking meat slowly with sour fruit and nuts) became the khoresh family that still anchors the Persian table today.

The Islamic conquest of the 7th century absorbed Persia into a wider Arab caliphate but Persian cooks effectively conquered Arab cuisine back. The Abbasid palace kitchens in Baghdad from the 8th century onwards ran on Persian recipes, Persian rice technique and Persian spice work. The world's earliest surviving cookbook in any language, Ibn Sayyar al-Warraq's *Kitab al-Tabikh* (10th century), is essentially a record of Persian palace cooking written in Arabic. The sweet-sour balance, the slow stew, the herb pilaf and the use of dried fruit in savoury dishes all crossed from Persia into Arab, Turkish, Mughal Indian and Andalusian cooking through this period.

The Safavid era (1501 to 1736) is when modern Persian cooking codifies itself. Court chronicles and cookbooks from Shah Abbas's Isfahan record recipes essentially unchanged today: fesenjan (the walnut and pomegranate chicken), gheymeh (the split-pea and dried-lime stew), khoresh-e bademjan (aubergine and lamb). Saffron from Khorasan, rose water from Kashan, pistachios from Rafsanjan, dried limes from the Gulf coast: the regional ingredient map settled in this period too. The signature is a balance of sweet and sour (dried fruit with dried lime, pomegranate molasses with walnut) and the long slow stew, the khoresh family that anchors most meals.

The rice tradition has no equal in the region, and it crystallised under the Safavids and the Qajars. Persian polo is steam-cooked twice: the rice is parboiled, drained, then layered back into a buttered pot with saffron and gently steamed under a cloth-wrapped lid. The bottom layer (tahdig) is coaxed into a crispy golden crust of rice, sometimes with a layer of potato or thin flatbread, that's the most-prized part of the dish. A cook's reputation rises or falls on the tahdig. The Qajar court (1789 to 1925) added French and Russian influences to the table without disturbing the rice tradition: a few French sauces, the samovar, certain ice-creams and biscuits, but polo and khoresh stayed central.

The Pahlavi modernisation (1925 to 1979) and the oil boom of the 1960s put Western kitchens in Tehran homes and Western menus in Tehran restaurants, but the home cooking held firm. The 1979 Revolution and the isolation that followed kept Persian food largely in diaspora kitchens for a generation. Los Angeles (home to the largest Iranian community outside Iran), London and Toronto preserved the recipes; cooks and writers in those cities (Najmieh Batmanglij's *Food of Life*, Yotam Ottolenghi's New York Times pieces with Sami Tamimi, Sabrina Ghayour's *Persiana*) brought Persian food back into the global conversation. The scene inside Iran is reopening slowly, and Tehran's young chefs are starting to reach back into the regional traditions (Gilani fish dishes, Shirazi salads, Tabrizi dolmas) that the diaspora restaurants never had room for.

## Regional variations

- **Tehran and the centre**: the mainstream. Khoresh-led, saffron-rice tradition.
- **North (Caspian Coast)**: fish-heavy, more sour (sumac, vinegar); the Gilan and Mazandaran kitchens.
- **South (Bushehr, Hormozgan)**: Gulf-influenced, more chilli, more seafood, Indian curry notes.
- **Tabriz and Azerbaijan**: a distinct dolma tradition, sour-leaning khoreshes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Saffron**: the most-loved spice. Bloomed in hot water, poured over rice.
- **Dried lime (limu omani)**: in stews. Dropped whole into the khoresh and pierced.
- **Barberries (zereshk)**: small dried red berries, sour. The garnish on royal rice dishes.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry. The table salt.
- **Rose water and orange-blossom water**: in sweets, in drinks, in some savoury dishes.
- **Advieh**: warm spice blend (cinnamon, cardamom, cumin, rose petals).
- **Pomegranate molasses**: in fesenjan (walnut-pomegranate chicken) and as a finishing flourish.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Chelo Kabab Koobideh](/recipes/cuisine/persian/chelo-kabab-koobideh)**: minced lamb hand-mixed with onion, pressed onto flat skewers, charcoal-grilled. Served with saffron rice and a grilled tomato. The national dish.
2. **[Zereshk Polo ba Morgh](/recipes/cuisine/persian/zereshk-polo-ba-morgh)**: barberry rice with saffron chicken. The wedding-and-celebration plate.
3. **[Baghali Polo ba Mahiche](/recipes/cuisine/persian/baghali-polo-ba-mahiche)**: dill-and-broad-bean rice with slow-braised lamb shank.
4. **[Khoresh Bademjan](/recipes/cuisine/persian/khoresh-bademjan)**: aubergine-and-lamb stew with tomato and dried lime.
5. **[Khoresh Karafs](/recipes/cuisine/persian/khoresh-karafs)**: celery-and-lamb stew with parsley and mint.
6. **[Ash Reshteh](/recipes/cuisine/persian/ash-reshteh)**: thick herb-and-noodle soup with beans, lentils and kashk. The Nowruz (New Year) bowl.
7. **[Kotlet](/recipes/cuisine/persian/kotlet)**: minced beef and potato patties with turmeric. The everyday Persian lunch.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with doogh (savoury yoghurt drink with mint and salt) or chai with crystal-sugar nabat. For a proper Persian table: build around a chelo (rice) base, two khoreshes, a side of sabzi khordan (fresh herb plate), a yoghurt salad and a sweet finish.
