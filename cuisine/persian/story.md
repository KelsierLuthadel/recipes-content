# Persian (Iranian)

*The cuisine of slow rice, sour fruits and the long stewed khoresh. Built on saffron, dried lime and dried sour cherry, with a kitchen tradition that's been refining itself for 2,500 years.*

## A short history

Persian cooking is the longest continuously codified cuisine in the Middle East. The Achaemenid court (550–330 BCE) was already eating recognisable Persian food — slow-cooked stews thickened with nuts, fragrant rice with herbs, lamb spit-roasted with sumac. Cookbooks from the Safavid period (1500s) record recipes essentially unchanged today.

The cuisine's signature is the balance of sweet-and-sour (dried fruit + dried lime, pomegranate molasses + walnut) and the technique of long slow stewing (khoresh — the "spoon-food" family that anchors most meals). The rice tradition is unparalleled in the region: a Persian polo is steam-cooked twice, with the bottom layer (tahdig) cultivated into a crispy golden crust that's the most-prized part of the dish.

The 1979 Revolution and the subsequent isolation kept Persian food largely in the diaspora kitchen for a generation. The diaspora-led recodification (Yotam Ottolenghi's New York Times pieces, Najmieh Batmanglij's cookbooks) brought Persian cooking back into the global conversation. The internal scene is reopening slowly.

## Regional variations

- **Tehran and the centre**: the codified mainstream. Khoresh-led, saffron-rice tradition.
- **North (Caspian Coast)**: fish-heavy, more sour (sumac, vinegar), the Gilan and Mazandaran kitchens.
- **South (Bushehr, Hormozgan)**: Gulf-influenced, more chilli, more seafood, Indian curry influences.
- **Tabriz and Azerbaijan**: distinct dolma tradition, sour-leaning khoreshes.

## Signature ingredients

- **Saffron**: the most-loved spice. Bloomed in hot water, poured over rice.
- **Dried lime (limu omani)**: in stews. Dropped whole into khoresh and pierced.
- **Barberries (zereshk)**: small dried red berries, sour. The garnish on royal rice dishes.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry. The table salt.
- **Rose water and orange-blossom water**: in sweets, in drinks, in some savoury dishes.
- **Advieh**: warm spice blend (cinnamon, cardamom, cumin, rose petals).
- **Pomegranate molasses**: in fesenjan (walnut-pomegranate chicken) and as a finishing flourish.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Chelo Kabab Koobideh](/recipes/cuisine/persian/chelo-kabab-koobideh)**: minced lamb hand-mixed with onion, pressed onto flat skewers, charcoal-grilled. Served with saffron rice and a grilled tomato. The national dish.
2. **[Zereshk Polo ba Morgh](/recipes/cuisine/persian/zereshk-polo-ba-morgh)**: barberry rice with saffron chicken. The wedding-and-celebration plate.
3. **[Baghali Polo ba Mahiche](/recipes/cuisine/persian/baghali-polo-ba-mahiche)**: dill-and-broad-bean rice with slow-braised lamb shank.
4. **[Khoresh Bademjan](/recipes/cuisine/persian/khoresh-bademjan)**: aubergine-and-lamb stew with tomato and dried lime.
5. **[Khoresh Karafs](/recipes/cuisine/persian/khoresh-karafs)**: celery-and-lamb stew with parsley and mint.
6. **[Ash Reshteh](/recipes/cuisine/persian/ash-reshteh)**: thick herb-and-noodle soup with beans, lentils and kashk. The Nowruz (New Year) bowl.
7. **[Kotlet](/recipes/cuisine/persian/kotlet)**: minced beef and potato patties with turmeric. The everyday Persian lunch.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with doogh (savoury yoghurt drink with mint and salt) or chai with crystal-sugar nabat. For a proper Persian table: build around a chelo (rice) base, two khoreshes, a side of sabzi khordan (fresh herb plate), a yoghurt salad and a sweet finish.
