# Estonian

*The kitchen of the northernmost Baltic state. Black bread, salt-cured herring, the barley-and-potato porridge (mulgipuder) of the southern uplands, blood sausage at Christmas, and the cold-soup tradition that drifts in from Russia.*

## A short history

Estonian cooking sits between four neighbours: Finland across the Gulf, Latvia to the south, Russia to the east, and the Baltic-German Lutheran nobility who ran the upper class for 700 years. The deepest roots are Finno-Ugric: the Estonians arrived in the eastern Baltic by around 3000 BCE, settling the forests and lake country with a kitchen built on rye, barley, pork, freshwater fish from the lakes, smoked sprats from the Gulf, mushrooms and berries gathered out of the woods, and the long-keeping sour-rye bread that anchored every household. Pagan Estonia ran on that shelf for thousands of years before Christianity arrived.

The German Northern Crusades crashed into the country in the early 13th century. The Teutonic and Livonian Orders conquered Estonia between 1208 and 1227, killed off the indigenous chieftains, and installed a Baltic-German aristocracy that would run the manors for the next 700 years through Danish, Swedish and Russian sovereignty. The country folk stayed Estonian-speaking and stayed on the rye-and-pork shelf; the manor kitchens ran on the heavier German tradition (sauerkraut, sausage, dumplings, beer) and slowly the two layered into each other. Verivorst (blood sausage) and sült (jellied head cheese) are Baltic-German techniques that became Estonian Christmas standards.

The Swedish era (1561 to 1721) brought literacy and the Lutheran Reformation, which embedded the rye bread and herring tradition at the heart of the working diet (Lent was strictly observed, fish was the protein on most Wednesdays and Fridays). Peter the Great's conquest in 1721 put Estonia inside the Russian Empire for two centuries. The potato arrived in the late 18th century from St Petersburg-bound German agronomists and took over fast: by 1850 it was the second staple after rye. Russian influence brought sour cream (smetana, here called hapukoor) onto every plate, the pickled-everything tradition, and the cold-soup habit that drifted in from the east.

Independence in 1918 lasted just 22 years before Soviet annexation in 1940. The Soviet period (with a brief Nazi occupation 1941-1944) was hungry and grey: kolkhoz farming, food shortages, the disappearance of pre-war Tallinn's German-Estonian cafe culture, deportations to Siberia that emptied whole villages of their cooks. What survived was the home kitchen: black bread baked at home, mulgipuder (the barley-and-potato porridge of the southern uplands) cooked once a week, herring and sprats from the Baltic, mushrooms and lingonberries from the woods.

Independence returned in 1991. The 1990s and 2000s reconnected Estonia to its Nordic neighbours (Finland in particular), and from the 2010s onwards the Tallinn restaurant scene started drawing serious attention. Restaurants like Noa, Rataskaevu 16 and Tchaikovsky built on the country's deep foraging tradition (the law of every-man's-right gives every Estonian access to the forest's mushrooms and berries) and turned out some of the most exciting cooking in Northern Europe. The home kitchen still reads as it did in 1900: black bread, herring, pork, potato, sour cream, dill.

## Regional variations

- **Tallinn and the north**: the codified mainstream. Verivorst, rosolje.
- **Southern (Tartu, Mulgi)**: mulgipuder country. The barley-and-potato porridge belongs here.
- **Western islands (Saaremaa, Hiiumaa)**: fish-heavy. Smoked sprats and herring run the kitchen.
- **Eastern (Setomaa)**: closer to Russian. More fermented cabbage, more cold soup.

## Signature ingredients

- **Black bread (rukkileib)**: dense, sour rye. The cultural icon.
- **Herring**: pickled, salt-cured, smoked.
- **Potato**: in nearly every dish.
- **Pork**: cured, smoked, fresh. Sült (head cheese) and verivorst (blood sausage) at Christmas.
- **Buckwheat (tatar)**: porridge, dumplings.
- **Sour cream (hapukoor)**: dresses everything.
- **Dill, caraway**: the herb pair.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Verivorst](/recipes/cuisine/estonian/verivorst)**: blood sausage with barley, marjoram and onion, baked till the casing crisps. The Christmas Eve standard, served with lingonberry jam and sauerkraut.
2. **[Mulgipuder](/recipes/cuisine/estonian/mulgipuder)**: pearl-barley-and-mashed-potato porridge with crispy bacon and onion. The southern Estonian (Mulgi region) signature.
3. **[Rosolje](/recipes/cuisine/estonian/rosolje)**: pink potato-and-beetroot salad with herring, gherkin, apple, hard-boiled egg and sour cream. The Christmas table classic.
4. **[Sült](/recipes/cuisine/estonian/sult)**: jellied pork head cheese with bay leaf, peppercorns and vinegar.
5. **[Hapukapsasupp](/recipes/cuisine/estonian/hapukapsasupp)**: sour-cabbage soup with pork ribs and barley. The winter weeknight bowl.

## Where to go next

Pour a Saku or A. Le Coq beer (the two oldest Estonian breweries) or a Vana Tallinn (sweet rum-based liqueur, vanilla and citrus). Finish with kama: mixed grain flour stirred into kefir or yoghurt with berries, the traditional Estonian breakfast and dessert in one bowl.
