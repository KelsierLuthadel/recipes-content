# Char Kway Teow

![Char Kway Teow](resources/char-kway-teow.jpg)

*Singapore-Penang stir-fried flat rice noodles: wide rice noodles tossed in a screaming-hot wok with prawns, Chinese sausage, beansprouts and egg, finished with dark soy and chilli. The wok-hei classic, eaten standing up at the hawker stall.*

**Serves:** 2 generously, 4 as a snack

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes

**Cook Time:** 12 minutes

## Overview
Char kway teow ("stir-fried flat noodles") shares its name with Penang's most famous dish but the Singapore version has its own character - a darker sauce (more dark soy), heavier on prawns and Chinese sausage (lap cheong), and finished with bean sprouts that should still be crisp. Wok-hei is the dish: the smoky char that comes from a wok screaming hot enough to vaporise the soy sauce as it hits the pan. The noodles take on a slight crispness at the edges; the egg coats the strands; the prawns are pink and juicy. Eaten quickly with chopsticks (or a fork at the hawker stall) while it's still hot enough to burn the lip.

## Ingredients
- 400 g fresh flat rice noodles (kway teow / ho fun) - or 200 g dried, soaked
- 200 g raw prawns, peeled
- 100 g Chinese sausage (lap cheong), sliced diagonally 5 mm thick
- 100 g squid, cleaned and sliced into rings (optional)
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 4 large eggs, lightly beaten
- 250 g bean sprouts
- 2 spring onions, cut into 4 cm lengths
- 2 tbsp dark soy sauce (the sweet, thick kind)
- 1.5 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp fish sauce
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine (or dry sherry)
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp sambal oelek (or pounded fresh chilli) - optional
- 4 tbsp vegetable oil (or lard, the traditional choice)
- White pepper to taste
- Chinese chives (optional, traditional garnish)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prep
1. Have everything cut, mixed and ready at the wok station. This dish moves fast.
2. In a small bowl, combine both soy sauces, fish sauce, Shaoxing wine, sugar and sambal. Stir to combine.
3. If using fresh kway teow, loosen the noodles gently with hands so they don't clump.
4. If using dried, soak in hot water 10 minutes; drain.

### Stage 2 - Sear the proteins
1. Heat the wok over the highest possible heat until smoking.
2. Add 2 tbsp oil; swirl.
3. Add Chinese sausage; stir-fry 1 minute until edges crisp.
4. Add the garlic; stir 20 seconds.
5. Add prawns and squid; stir-fry 1.5 minutes until prawns turn pink.
6. Lift everything onto a plate.

### Stage 3 - Cook the noodles
1. Return the wok to highest heat; add the remaining 2 tbsp oil.
2. Tip in the rice noodles; toss in the hot oil 30 seconds to start the charring.
3. Push the noodles to one side. Pour the eggs into the empty space.
4. Let the eggs set briefly (15 seconds), then break up and mix with the noodles.
5. Pour over the sauce mixture; toss vigorously.

### Stage 4 - Finish
1. Return the prawns, sausage and squid to the wok. Toss to combine.
2. Add the bean sprouts and spring onions in the final minute - they should stay crisp.
3. Toss for 30 seconds more. Season with white pepper.

### Stage 5 - Serve
1. Tip onto wide plates immediately.
2. Eat at once, while the wok-hei is still on the noodles.

## Notes
- **Wok-hei is everything:** The wok must smoke when oil hits it. If your stovetop can't get that hot, this dish loses 60% of its character. A blowtorch over a cast iron pan is a desperate workaround.
- **Don't overcrowd:** Cook for 1-2 people at a time in a home wok. Bigger batches steam instead of stir-frying.
- **Bean sprouts at the end:** They should be wilted but still crunchy. Adding them earlier makes them soggy.

## Serving
Serve hot on wide plates. A small dish of sambal alongside for those who want extra heat. Sliced cucumber and a lime wedge complete the plate.

## Storage
- Best the same hour. Char kway teow reheated is a sad dish.
- Refrigerate leftovers 1 day; reheat in a very hot pan.
