# Singaporean

*The cuisine of the world's most concentrated food city. Chinese, Malay, Indian and Peranakan kitchens stacked on a single island, served from hawker centres that won UNESCO heritage status.*

## A short history

Singapore's food story starts long before the city did, with the Orang Laut (sea people) who fished the Singapore Strait and the small Malay villages that sat at the southern tip of the peninsula. The early kitchen was Malay: rice cooked in coconut milk (nasi lemak in embryo), the spice-paste rempah pounded fresh in a stone mortar, the slow-cooked rendang and the chilli-and-shrimp-paste sambal that still anchors every meal. The island sat at the choke point of the Strait of Malacca, the busiest sea lane in the medieval world, and even before Stamford Raffles arrived, traders from China, India, Arabia and Java were stopping here to take on water and trade pepper, cloves, nutmeg and tin.

The Peranakan (Straits Chinese) community took shape from the 15th century onward, when Hokkien Chinese traders settled in Malacca, Penang and what would become Singapore and married local Malay women. Their children, called Baba (men) and Nyonya (women), built a fusion cuisine that's now culturally heritage-protected and unmistakably its own: laksa (rice-noodle soup in a rich coconut-curry broth), ayam buah keluak (chicken stewed with the black, almost-truffle-flavoured nut of the keluak tree), ngoh hiang (five-spice pork rolls in beancurd skin) and the rainbow shelf of kueh (steamed and grilled rice-flour cakes coloured with pandan and butterfly-pea flower). The Peranakan kitchen is the original Singapore fusion, three hundred years before the word existed.

Raffles founded modern Singapore in 1819 as a British free port, and the population exploded. The Chinese majority (now 74% of the population, mostly Hokkien, Teochew, Cantonese and Hainanese) brought stir-fry, noodle soups and roast meats; the Tamil and Punjabi Indian diaspora brought banana-leaf rice, chapati, the south-Indian breakfast and the curry tradition; British colonial cooks codified Hainanese chicken rice when Hainanese men, working as cooks for British families, scaled up a Hainan-island village dish into the everyday plate. The kopi tiam (coffee-shop) culture, where you order Hainanese-style coffee with condensed milk, kaya toast and a soft-boiled egg, dates from this early-20th-century period.

The Japanese Occupation (1942-1945) starved the island and broke many of the older food traditions; the cooks who survived rebuilt the kitchen in the post-war years on whatever the new republic's import economy could deliver. Independence in 1965 under Lee Kuan Yew brought massive public-housing reconstruction and the relocation of street hawkers (who had been cooking on charcoal carts in alleys for a century) into government-organised open-air food courts. The hawker centre, a system where individual stallholders pay subsidised rent to cook one dish to perfection, took shape in the 1970s and made the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2020. It's how most Singaporeans eat: fast, world-class, democratically accessible.

The national debate about which dish is "really Singaporean" versus Malaysian is permanent. Chilli crab, Hainanese chicken rice, laksa, bak kut teh: all claimed by both countries with passionate defenders on each side. The contemporary scene splits between hawker stalls that have been cooking the same plate for three generations (Tian Tian for chicken rice, 328 Katong for laksa) and a new wave of Singapore-Chinese-fine-dining chefs (Tetsuya Wakuda, Julien Royer, the Burnt Ends crew) who treat the hawker repertoire as a starting point. The four-way crossroads of Chinese, Malay, Indian and Peranakan kitchens is the cuisine; everything else is variation.

## Regional variations

Singapore is a single city-state, so "regional" mostly means by ethnic community kitchen:

- **Chinese hawker stalls**: Hainanese chicken rice, char kway teow, bak kut teh, Hokkien mee.
- **Malay stalls**: nasi lemak, mee rebus, satay, lontong.
- **Indian stalls (Little India)**: banana-leaf rice, roti prata, fish-head curry.
- **Peranakan kitchen**: laksa, ayam buah keluak, ngoh hiang, kueh.

## Signature ingredients

- **Soy sauce**: light, dark, sweet, kicap manis (a Malay/Indonesian sweet thick soy).
- **Sambal**: the chilli paste base; sambal belacan (shrimp paste), sambal oelek.
- **Coconut milk and pandan**: in laksa, in nasi lemak, in kueh.
- **Belacan (shrimp paste)**: the umami base under many Malay and Peranakan dishes.
- **Hae bee (dried shrimp)**: chopped into nasi goreng, into laksa pastes.
- **Curry leaves and lemongrass**: in the spice pastes.
- **Hainanese ginger-spring-onion sauce**: the signature condiment on chicken rice.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Hainanese Chicken Rice](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/hainanese-chicken-rice)**: poached chicken with rice cooked in the chicken broth and fat, served with chilli-garlic sauce, ginger-spring-onion sauce and dark soy. Singapore's unofficial national dish.
2. **[Chilli Crab](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/chilli-crab)**: mud crab in tomato-chilli-egg sauce, eaten with the hands and mantou buns. The icon dish.
3. **[Laksa](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/laksa)**: rich coconut-curry noodle soup with prawn, fishcake and beansprouts. The Peranakan classic.
4. **[Bak Kut Teh](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/bak-kut-teh)**: pork rib soup with garlic, white pepper and herbs (or, in the Teochew style, just garlic and pepper, no herbs).
5. **[Hokkien Mee](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/hokkien-mee)**: stir-fried yellow noodles with rice noodles, prawn, squid, pork and a rich prawn-stock-egg sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with kopi (kopi-o is local coffee with sugar; kopi peng is iced) or a Tiger beer. Spend a day eating across hawker centres: laksa for breakfast at Katong, chicken rice for lunch at Maxwell, chilli crab for dinner anywhere, kaya toast for the late-night fix.
