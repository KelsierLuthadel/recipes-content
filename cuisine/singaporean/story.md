# Singaporean

*The cuisine of the world's most concentrated food city. Chinese, Malay, Indian and Peranakan kitchens stacked on a single island, served from hawker centres that won UNESCO heritage status.*

## A short history

Singaporean cooking is the four-way crossroads of Southeast Asia. The Chinese majority (74% of the population, mostly Hokkien, Teochew, Cantonese and Hainanese) brought stir-fry, noodle soups and roast meats. The Malay community contributed the spice paste tradition, coconut milk, and the chilli-base rempah. The Tamil and Punjabi Indian diaspora brought banana-leaf rice, chapati, the south-Indian breakfast and the curry tradition. The Peranakan (Straits Chinese) — descendants of Chinese traders who married local Malay women from the 15th century — created a fusion shelf (laksa, ayam buah keluak, kueh) that's now culturally heritage-protected.

The hawker centre — government-organised open-air food court where individual stallholders cook one dish to perfection — was codified in the 1970s and recognised by UNESCO in 2020 as Intangible Cultural Heritage. It's how most Singaporeans eat: cheap, fast, world-class, and democratically accessible.

The national debate about which dish is "really Singaporean" (vs Malaysian) is permanent. Chilli crab, Hainanese chicken rice, laksa, bak kut teh — all claimed by both countries with passionate defenders on each side.

## Regional variations

Singapore is a single city-state so "regional" mostly means by ethnic community kitchen:

- **Chinese hawker stalls**: Hainanese chicken rice, char kway teow, bak kut teh, Hokkien mee.
- **Malay stalls**: nasi lemak, mee rebus, satay, lontong.
- **Indian stalls (Little India)**: banana-leaf rice, roti prata, fish-head curry.
- **Peranakan kitchen**: laksa, ayam buah keluak, ngoh hiang, kueh.

## Signature ingredients

- **Soy sauce**: light, dark, sweet, kicap manis (a Malay/Indonesian sweet thick soy).
- **Sambal**: chilli paste base — sambal belacan (shrimp paste), sambal oelek.
- **Coconut milk and pandan**: in laksa, in nasi lemak, in kueh.
- **Belacan (shrimp paste)**: the umami base of many Malay and Peranakan dishes.
- **Hae bee (dried shrimp)**: chopped into nasi goreng, laksa pastes.
- **Curry leaves and lemongrass**: in the spice pastes.
- **Hainanese ginger-spring-onion sauce**: the signature condiment on chicken rice.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Hainanese Chicken Rice](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/hainanese-chicken-rice)**: poached chicken with rice cooked in the chicken broth and fat, served with chilli-garlic sauce, ginger-spring-onion sauce, and dark soy. Singapore's unofficial national dish.
2. **[Chilli Crab](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/chilli-crab)**: mud crab in tomato-chilli-egg sauce, eaten with the hands and mantou buns. The icon dish.
3. **[Laksa](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/laksa)**: rich coconut-curry noodle soup with prawn, fishcake, beansprouts. The Peranakan classic.
4. **[Bak Kut Teh](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/bak-kut-teh)**: pork rib soup with garlic, white pepper and herbs (or in the Teochew style, just garlic and pepper, no herbs).
5. **[Hokkien Mee](/recipes/cuisine/singaporean/hokkien-mee)**: stir-fried yellow noodles with rice noodles, prawn, squid, pork and a rich prawn-stock-egg sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with kopi (kopi-o is local coffee with sugar; kopi peng is iced) or with a Tiger beer. Spend a day eating across hawker centres: laksa for breakfast at Katong, chicken rice for lunch at Maxwell, chilli crab for dinner anywhere, kaya toast for the late-night fix.
