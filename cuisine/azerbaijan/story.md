# Azerbaijani

*The Caucasian kitchen at the meeting of Persian and Turkic worlds. Plov in dozens of regional variants, the long dolma tradition, sharp sour-plum and pomegranate flavours, and the lyulya kebab on the open-coal grill.*

## A short history

Azerbaijani cooking sits at a deep crossroads, on the western shore of the Caspian where the Caucasus mountains meet the Iranian plateau. The earliest kitchens belonged to the Caucasian Albanians and earlier indigenous peoples, who farmed wheat and barley on the river plains, herded sheep and goats on the foothills, and netted sturgeon, kutum and bream out of the Caspian for the salt-cure and the open fire. The Caspian roe (today the world's most famous caviar tradition) goes back to those Iron Age salt-curers; the country sat on the Silk Road for two and a half millennia, and saffron, cumin, dried fruit and the long-grain rice that defines the modern plate came in on those caravans.

The medieval centuries layered Persian rule onto Turkic settlement. The Sassanian Persians ruled the territory from the 3rd to the 7th centuries and brought the rice-and-saffron pilaff technique, the slow stew built on fruit and meat, and the sour-tart palate (pomegranate molasses, sumac, sour plum) that still defines the cuisine. Arab conquest in the 7th century brought Islam, the spice routes south to Yemen, and the sweets shelf based on syrup and nut. From the 11th century, Oghuz Turkic tribes moved in, settled the highlands and the steppes, and gave Azerbaijan its language and the open-fire kebab tradition: lyulya (minced lamb on a flat skewer), tika (cubed lamb), and the lavash flatbread cooked on a tandir oven sunk into the ground.

The Safavid Persian empire from 1501 made what is now Azerbaijan part of the Persian cultural world for three centuries. The court kitchens at Tabriz (today in Iranian Azerbaijan, where the largest population of ethnic Azerbaijanis still lives) refined the plov tradition into the form it holds today: rice cooked separately and layered with meat, dried fruit, chestnut and saffron, the bottom crust (gazmag) cracked at the table. Russian conquest in the early 1800s split the Azerbaijani people in two along the Aras river, with the larger southern half staying in Persian Iran and the northern half becoming Russian, then Soviet, then independent.

The result is a kitchen that layers Turkic kebabs and bread onto Persian rice and stew. Plov carries more regional variations than any other Azerbaijani dish, with more than 40 documented versions covering shah-plov sealed in a lavash crust, plov with chestnut and dried apricot, plov with herbs (sabzi), plov with fish from the Caspian. Dolma (stuffed vegetables and vine leaves) is an art form, and the country defends its claim against Greek and Turkish counter-claims; UNESCO recognised the Azerbaijani dolma tradition on its Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2017.

The Soviet period (1920 to 1991) layered Russian influences on top: borshch on canteen menus, the bottled mayonnaise salad, the obligatory cognac. The everyday food didn't change much, because the family kitchen kept cooking what it had cooked for generations. Baku's oil-boom wealth from the 1870s onwards produced a bourgeois restaurant scene that codified urban Azerbaijani dining; the 1990s independence and the second oil boom of the 2000s rebuilt that tradition. The cuisine today reads layered, generous, fruit-sweet and lamb-fragrant, and the family table still ends with black tea poured from a samovar into pear-shaped armudu glasses.

## Regional variations

- **Baku and Apsheron Peninsula**: codified mainstream. Plov, dolma, the urban dining tradition.
- **Ganja and the west**: closer to Georgian. More herbs, more khinkali-style dumplings.
- **Lankaran (south, Caspian)**: more fish, Persian influence strongest.
- **Sheki (north-west)**: the famous sweets tradition (Sheki halva, baklava).

## Signature ingredients

- **Basmati rice**: for plov.
- **Saffron**: in plov, in pilafs.
- **Lamb and beef**: the meats.
- **Sumac and pomegranate molasses**: the sour-tart finishes.
- **Dried fruit (raisins, dried apricot, prunes)**: in plov.
- **Fresh herbs (dill, coriander, basil, tarragon)**: huge variety on the table.
- **Yoghurt and qatiq (similar to kefir)**: drinks and dressings.

## Learn it in three recipes

1. **[Plov](/recipes/cuisine/azerbaijan/plov)**: basmati rice with lamb, saffron, dried fruit (raisins, dried apricots), chestnuts and caramelised onion. The national dish, though there are 40+ regional variants.
2. **[Dolma (Azerbaijani)](/recipes/cuisine/azerbaijan/dolma-azerbaijani)**: vine leaves or vegetables stuffed with rice and minced lamb, simmered with mint and lemon. Inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list as Azerbaijani.
3. **[Lyulya Kebab](/recipes/cuisine/azerbaijan/lyulya-kebab)**: minced lamb on flat skewers, charcoal-grilled. Served with grilled tomatoes, sumac onion and lavash bread.

## Where to go next

Drink black tea (chay) from pear-shaped armudu glasses, often with rose-petal jam (gulqand) on the side. Finish with Sheki halva or paxlava (the Azerbaijani baklava). For Novruz (Persian New Year, 20 March), build a seven-dish symbolic table with shekerbura and pakhlava.
