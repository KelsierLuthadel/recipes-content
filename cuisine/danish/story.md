# Danish

*The kitchen that gave the world smørrebrød and then, in the New Nordic 2000s, the world's most-influential restaurant. Rye bread, salt-cured fish, pork, butter, the dill-and-mustard finish.*

## A short history

Danish cooking begins on a flat, windy peninsula and a cluster of cold-sea islands. The Bronze Age and Iron Age farmers who settled Jutland kept pigs in oak forests, grew rye and barley on the sandy soils, churned butter from their cattle, and salted herring out of the surrounding seas. Those four pillars (pork, rye, dairy, herring) have not shifted in three thousand years. The Vikings of the 8th to 11th centuries refined the salt-cure and the smokehouse, traded amber and dried fish across the North Sea and the Baltic, and carried the rye loaf with them on every longship.

Medieval Denmark sat at the hinge of Northern European trade. Through the 13th to 16th centuries the Hanseatic League made Copenhagen, Bergen and Lübeck partners in the salt-cured herring boom, and at the height of the trade Danish boats supplied Catholic Europe with the fasting fish that ran the church calendar. The Lutheran Reformation in 1536 ended the fasting market overnight but the smokehouses kept running, and rugbrød (dense, sour-leavened rye) became the everyday bread it still is. The royal kitchens at Christian IV's court introduced French and German service techniques in the 17th century, but the country kitchen stayed plain: pork, kale, peas, potatoes once they arrived in the 1700s.

The 19th century codified the defining piece of Danish food culture, smørrebrød, the open-faced rye sandwich with carefully layered toppings. A workman's lunch format turned into a national obsession with garnish and arrangement. The same century saw Danish dairy industrialise: cooperative butter creameries spread through Jutland from the 1880s, and Danish butter and bacon became the country's two biggest exports to Victorian Britain. The wienerbrød (the "Danish" pastry is actually a Danish adaptation of an Austrian technique that arrived with striking bakers in 1850) joined the canon in the same era.

The 20th century added Danish-style hot dogs (with the works: fried onions, pickle, remoulade, mustard, ketchup), tarteletter (puff pastry shells with creamy fillings) and the boller i karry (meatballs in mild curry sauce) that hinted at a country opening up to the spice trade. The German occupation from 1940 to 1945 stripped the larder, but the post-war years brought a quiet prosperity and the codification of the Danish lunch culture (smørrebrød at noon, beer and akvavit on the side) that still anchors the working week.

The New Nordic moment (2003 onwards, Noma's manifesto, Rene Redzepi and Claus Meyer setting the terms) recodified Danish food internationally. Foraging, fermentation and the obsessive use of regional Nordic produce moved from country kitchen to white tablecloth in a single decade. The pre-Noma everyday Danish kitchen carries on unchanged: smørrebrød at lunch, frikadeller (pork meatballs) for dinner, kanelsnegle (cinnamon buns) with afternoon coffee.

## Regional variations

- **Copenhagen and Zealand**: the codified mainstream. Smørrebrød, the modern Danish restaurant scene.
- **Jutland (mainland west)**: heartier, more pork. Stegt flæsk (crispy pork) is essentially the unofficial Jutland anthem.
- **Funen (central island)**: more dairy, the apple-cake tradition.
- **Bornholm (Baltic island)**: smoked herring, the kipper-and-egg lunch.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rye bread (rugbrød)**: dense, dark, sour-leavened. The base of smørrebrød.
- **Pork**: every part. Roasted, fried, ground into frikadeller, cured into bacon.
- **Herring**: pickled in dozens of varieties (curry, mustard, dill, sherry).
- **Butter**: the cooking fat and the table butter. Used heavily.
- **Dill**: fresh, in sauces and pickles.
- **Remoulade**: mayo-based sauce with curry, capers, mustard. The condiment.
- **Cardamom and cinnamon**: in pastries.

## Learn it in six recipes

1. **[Smørrebrød](/recipes/cuisine/danish/smorrebrod)**: open-faced rye sandwich with carefully arranged toppings (herring, liver pâté, roast beef with remoulade, egg-and-shrimp). The lunch standard.
2. **[Frikadeller](/recipes/cuisine/danish/frikadeller)**: pork-and-veal meatballs pan-fried in butter, served with potato and brown sauce.
3. **[Stegt Flæsk](/recipes/cuisine/danish/stegt-flaesk)**: crispy pork slices with parsley sauce and boiled potato. Voted Denmark's national dish (2014).
4. **[Boller i Karry](/recipes/cuisine/danish/boller-i-karry)**: pork meatballs in mild curry sauce with rice. The mid-century Danish answer to spice.
5. **[Hot Dog Med Det Hele](/recipes/cuisine/danish/hot-dog-med-det-hele)**: hot dog with everything. Fried onions, raw onion, pickle, mustard, ketchup, remoulade.
6. **[Tarteletter](/recipes/cuisine/danish/tarteletter)**: puff pastry shells with creamy chicken-and-asparagus filling. The classic dinner party starter.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with akvavit (caraway spirit, served chilled) or with a cold beer. For a proper smørrebrød lunch, serve 3-5 different open sandwiches in order: fish first, meat second, cheese last. Eat with knife and fork (never hands).
