# Chilean

*The cuisine of a country shaped like a coastline. A Spanish-indigenous kitchen built on the Pacific catch, the Andean potato, German baking from the south, and the most generous use of bread per capita in South America.*

## A short history

Chilean cooking begins on the long Pacific shelf and in the Andean valleys, where indigenous people farmed and fished for thousands of years before any European arrived. The Mapuche of the south domesticated the potato (Chile shares parentage of the modern spud with Peru and Bolivia: many of the wild potato varieties came from Chiloé island, where farmers still grow more than 200 native types), grew quinoa, beans and corn, and gathered piñones (the giant pine nuts of the araucaria tree) as a winter staple. The Aymara and Atacameño of the dry north herded llama, dried meat into charqui (the word jumped via Spanish into English as "jerky"), and traded salt and seafood up and down the Andean valleys. The curanto pit-bake of Chiloé, with hot stones layered between nalca leaves over shellfish, fish, meat and potato dumplings, is older than the country and still cooked today.

Spanish conquest in the 1540s arrived later than in Mexico or Peru and met fierce Mapuche resistance that kept the Bío-Bío frontier in motion for three centuries. Pedro de Valdivia founded Santiago in 1541, and along with the soldiers came wheat, beef, dairy, pork, citrus, olive oil and the wine vine. The Spanish empanada (itself a descendant of Moorish savoury pastries) became the empanada de pino, stuffed with beef, onion, olive and a wedge of hard-boiled egg. Sofrito became the base of cazuela. Catholic feast days laid the calendar of empanadas at September 18 (the national day) and pan de Pascua at Christmas. Wheat displaced indigenous grains across the central valley, and Chile became one of the great bread cultures of the Spanish empire.

The 18th and 19th centuries opened the country to other influences. The Columbian Exchange ran both ways: Chile sent its potato, its strawberry (Chile's wild beach strawberry, Fragaria chiloensis, crossed with North American varieties to produce the modern garden strawberry), and its lessons in altitude farming back to Europe, while wheat, sugar, citrus and the vine took deep root in the central valley. After independence in 1818, the Chilean government recruited German farmers in the 1850s to settle the temperate southern Lake District around Valdivia, Osorno and Puerto Montt. They came with rye bread, kuchen pastries, sausage, beer and pork-curing traditions, and the south now reads more Bavarian than Iberian on the plate. Chilean wine took off in the same period as French oenologists arrived to escape the phylloxera that devastated European vineyards, planting cabernet, carménère and the syrah varieties that put Maipo and Colchagua on the world wine map.

The 20th century industrialised the kitchen. The nitrate boom and the copper mines pulled workers into the cities, and the cazuela, the bean shelf and the bread habit travelled with them. The 4,300km strip squeezed between the Andes and the Pacific produced a regional cuisine more linear than the rest of South America, but with sharp climatic shifts (northern desert, central Mediterranean, southern temperate, Patagonian sub-polar). The Pinochet years (1973 to 1990) kept tourism low, and the food story stayed local. The cuisine stayed relatively unknown outside Chile until the 21st century.

The contemporary scene took shape after the return to democracy. Boragó in Santiago put Chilean food on the international map, joining the world's 50 best since 2017 with foraging from across the country and a deliberate Mapuche-influenced ethos. Today's kitchen sits between the everyday cazuela tradition, the weekly Sunday asado (open-fire barbecue), and the emerging endemic-ingredient fine dining scene. The bread habit deserves a separate note: Chileans eat the most bread per capita in South America (second-most in the world after Germany), and bakeries appear on every block. Hallulla and marraqueta, the two most-eaten breads, sit on most tables three times a day.

## Regional variations

- **North (Atacama, Arica)**: Andean influence. Quinoa, llama meat, charqui (dried meat), pisco (the grape brandy that triggered a decades-long dispute with Peru about origins).
- **Central (Santiago, Valparaíso, wine country)**: the codified national cuisine. Pastel de choclo, cazuela, empanadas, the asado tradition, the wine valleys (Maipo, Casablanca, Colchagua).
- **South (Concepción, Temuco, Valdivia)**: Mapuche-German fusion. Curanto (the seafood-meat-potato pit-bake), kuchen (German pastries), German sausage, the dairy farms.
- **Chiloé and Patagonia**: the cold-water kitchen. Curanto on Chiloé, lamb on Patagonia (cordero al palo, roasted whole on a stake), the centolla (king crab) and salmon farms.

## Signature ingredients

- **Merkén**: smoked dried chilli powder with toasted coriander, made by the Mapuche. The signature Chilean condiment.
- **Choclo**: large-kernel corn. Used in pastel de choclo, humitas, and as a side.
- **Beans**: Chileans eat porotos (beans) several times a week. Porotos granados, porotos con riendas (with noodles), porotos con mote (with hominy).
- **Mote**: hulled, cooked wheat or hominy. The base of mote con huesillo (the national summer drink, with peaches).
- **Pebre**: chopped tomato-onion-coriander-ají salsa on the table at every meal.
- **Hallulla and marraqueta**: the two everyday breads. Flat round (hallulla) and four-roll cluster (marraqueta).
- **Pisco**: the grape brandy. The base of pisco sour, the national cocktail.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the soup pot, the corn pie, the asado tradition and the everyday plate. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Pastel de Choclo](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/pastel-de-choclo)**: ground beef, chicken, egg, olives and raisins under a sweet corn-puree crust, baked till the corn caramelises on top. The signature Chilean dish and the country's most recognisable plate.
2. **[Cazuela de Vacuno](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/cazuela-de-vacuno)**: beef shank in broth with potato, pumpkin, corn, green bean and rice, a meal in a bowl. The everyday Chilean soup and the most-eaten dish in winter.
3. **[Empanadas de Pino](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/empanadas-de-pino)**: baked turnovers filled with seasoned beef, onion, olive, raisin and a slice of hard-boiled egg. The Sunday empanada, the September 18 national-day empanada, the empanada.
4. **[Curanto](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/curanto)**: shellfish, fish, pork, chicken, sausage and potato dumplings (chapaleles, milcaos) cooked under hot stones in a pit, layered between nalca leaves. Chiloé's ancestral feast and the country's most spectacular cooking method.
5. **[Charquicán](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/charquican)**: pumpkin, potato and beef hash topped with a fried egg. The most reliable Chilean weeknight plate.
6. **[Caldillo de Congrio](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/caldillo-de-congrio)**: conger eel stew with potato and tomato, immortalised in Neruda's *Ode to Caldillo de Congrio*. The Pacific coast's signature soup.
7. **[Chorrillana](/recipes/cuisine/chilean/chorrillana)**: chips topped with beef, onions and a fried egg, shared straight from the platter. The Valparaíso bar plate, eaten with friends and pisco.

Cook the pastel and the cazuela and you have the corn pie and the soup. The empanada and the charquicán are the everyday cooking. The curanto is the ancestral feast; the caldillo is the coast; the chorrillana is the bar.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the asado tradition (the open-fire barbecue with chorizo, beef, lamb), the merkén-heavy Mapuche shelf, the German south (kuchen, raclette, sausage), and the holiday plates (cola de mono, mote con huesillo, sopaipillas pasadas). Chilean food rewards patience and the merkén jar.
