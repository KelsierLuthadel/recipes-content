# Irish

*The kitchen of the green island. The Sunday lamb roast, beef-and-Guinness stew, shepherd's pie, the boxty potato cake, and the world-famous Guinness-and-oysters tradition. A kitchen recovering from the 19th-century famine into a quietly excellent modern restaurant scene.*

## A short history

Irish cooking sits on a deep dairy-and-potato base, and the dairy half is the older part by some four thousand years. Neolithic farmers reached the island around 4000 BCE bringing cattle, and the lush wet pasture of the Atlantic west turned Ireland into a great cattle country. The early-medieval Gaelic kingdoms ran a cattle-and-dairy economy in which a person's wealth was measured in cows, and the law tracts of the 7th and 8th centuries describe a kitchen of butter, curds, cheese, oatcakes and slow-cooked stew. The Celtic monastic tradition added grains and beekeeping; mead, ale and butter buried in bog as a preservative all date from this period.

The Norse founded Dublin, Waterford and Limerick in the 9th and 10th centuries and added the salt-cod and herring trades. The Anglo-Norman invasion of 1169 brought wheat farming, the manor-house kitchen and the rabbit. Medieval Irish cooking ran on dairy, oats, barley, salt pork, salt fish and the occasional roast for a feast day. The "white meats" (curds, cheese, butter, milk) were the everyday food; meat itself was for celebrations.

Potatoes arrived from the Americas in the late 16th century, brought by Walter Raleigh according to legend (he had estates near Youghal in Cork), and the wet Irish climate made them the perfect crop. By 1800 the rural Irish poor were eating ten pounds of potatoes per person per day, supplemented with buttermilk and the occasional herring. The Great Famine (1845-1852), the *Phytophthora infestans* potato blight that killed a million Irish people and forced two million more to emigrate, flattened the cuisine for generations. British grain shipments continued to leave the country during the famine, a fact that still shapes Irish political memory. The diaspora kitchens in the US, the UK and Australia carried Irish cooking abroad while at home the food stayed austere for the next century.

The 19th and early 20th centuries kept the kitchen plain. The poor ate potatoes, oatmeal, bacon, cabbage, salt herring and soda bread (a quick bread made with buttermilk and bicarbonate of soda after the 1830s, since traditional yeast bread needs wheat that the wet climate barely grew). The Anglo-Irish gentry kept a separate, French-influenced kitchen at their big-house tables. Guinness, brewed at St James's Gate in Dublin since 1759, became the national drink and the national cooking ingredient. Independence in 1922 didn't immediately change the food; rationing during the Emergency (Ireland's wartime period) and economic stagnation through the 1950s kept the rural diet narrow.

The modern recovery began in the 1960s and accelerated through the 1990s Celtic Tiger years. Darina Allen's Ballymaloe Cookery School (Cork, founded 1983) led the rebuilding of contemporary Irish food, drawing on the country's exceptional produce: grass-fed beef and lamb, Atlantic seafood (Galway oysters, Carlingford mussels, Donegal salmon), dairy (Kerrygold is the world's most-exported butter brand) and the soda bread tradition. The Guinness-and-stew pairing is the cultural icon. Dublin coddle, the working-class pork-and-sausage one-pot, was Joyce's and Sean O'Casey's favourite dish. Irish stew (mutton, potato, onion, simple) is the everyday national plate.

## Regional variations

- **Dublin and the east**: the mainstream. Dublin coddle, the city pub tradition.
- **Cork and the south**: the food capital. Ballymaloe, the seafood at Kinsale, the English market.
- **Connaught (Galway, Mayo)**: Atlantic seafood, the boxty tradition, the cured-meat traditions.
- **Northern Ireland (Belfast, Derry)**: shared cuisine with the south plus distinctive dishes (Ulster fry, soda farl).

## Signature ingredients

- **Potatoes**: in boxty, champ, colcannon, stews, every form.
- **Cabbage**: with bacon (the diaspora "corned beef and cabbage" is an Irish-American invention; in Ireland it would be bacon, not corned beef).
- **Salmon**: smoked Donegal salmon is the export benchmark.
- **Oysters (Galway and Carlingford)**: the world's most-loved oysters.
- **Butter (Kerrygold)**: grass-fed, golden.
- **Guinness**: cooking ingredient (in stews) and the table drink.
- **Whiskey (Jameson, Bushmills, Redbreast)**: in coffee, in sauces.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Irish Stew](/recipes/cuisine/irish/irish-stew)**: mutton (or lamb) slow-cooked with potato, onion and carrot in a clear broth. Traditionally simple: no thickener, no Guinness.
2. **[Beef and Guinness Stew](/recipes/cuisine/irish/beef-and-guinness-stew)**: beef chuck slow-braised in Guinness with onions, carrot, mushroom and bay. The pub-classic Sunday lunch.
3. **[Shepherds Pie](/recipes/cuisine/irish/shepherds-pie)**: minced lamb with carrot and onion under a mashed potato crust. Swap beef for lamb and it becomes cottage pie.
4. **[Colcannon](/recipes/cuisine/irish/colcannon)**: mashed potato with kale or cabbage, butter and spring onion. The Halloween dish; a ring hidden inside foretold marriage for the finder.
5. **[Champ](/recipes/cuisine/irish/champ)**: mashed potato with spring onions and a well of melted butter in the centre. The Ulster classic.
6. **[Boxty](/recipes/cuisine/irish/boxty)**: a half-grated, half-mashed potato pancake. "Boxty on the griddle, boxty in the pan, if you can't make boxty you'll never get a man."
7. **[Dublin Coddle](/recipes/cuisine/irish/dublin-coddle)**: pork sausages, bacon, potato and onion slow-braised in stock. The Dublin working-class one-pot, Joyce's favourite.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Guinness (the proper Dublin pour, with the right surge-and-settle technique, takes about 119.5 seconds; the bartender will judge you if you rush them) or a Jameson Black Barrel whiskey. Finish with brown bread and butter (the Irish soda-bread tradition), or a slice of barmbrack (the Halloween fruit loaf) and a strong black tea.
