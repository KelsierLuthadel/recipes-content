# Irish

*The kitchen of the green island. The Sunday lamb roast, beef-and-Guinness stew, shepherd's pie, the boxty potato cake, and the world-famous Guinness-and-oysters tradition. A kitchen recovering from the 19th-century famine into a quietly excellent modern restaurant scene.*

## A short history

Irish cooking sits on a deep dairy-and-potato base. Cattle have been on the island for thousands of years; potatoes arrived from the Americas in the 16th century and became the staple. The Great Famine (1845-1852) — the potato blight that killed a million Irish people and forced two million more to emigrate — flattened the cuisine for generations; the diaspora kitchens (in the US, the UK, Australia) carried Irish cooking abroad while at home the food was austere for the next century.

The modern recovery began in the 1960s and accelerated through the 1990s Celtic Tiger era. Chef Darina Allen's Ballymaloe Cookery School (Cork, founded 1983) led the codification of contemporary Irish food drawing on the country's exceptional produce — grass-fed beef and lamb, the Atlantic seafood (Galway oysters, Carlingford mussels, Donegal salmon), the dairy (Kerrygold butter is the world's most-exported butter brand), and the soda bread tradition.

The Guinness-and-stew pairing is the cultural icon. Dublin coddle (the working-class pork-and-sausage one-pot) was Joyce's and Sean O'Casey's favourite dish. Irish stew (mutton, potato, onion, simple) is the everyday national dish.

## Regional variations

- **Dublin and the east**: codified mainstream. Dublin coddle, the city pub tradition.
- **Cork and the south**: the food capital. Ballymaloe, the seafood at Kinsale, the English market.
- **Connaught (Galway, Mayo)**: Atlantic seafood, the boxty tradition, the cured-meat traditions.
- **Northern Ireland (Belfast, Derry)**: shared cuisine with the south plus distinctive dishes (Ulster fry, soda farl).

## Signature ingredients

- **Potatoes**: in boxty, champ, colcannon, stews, every form.
- **Cabbage**: with bacon (the diaspora "corned beef and cabbage" — an Irish-American invention; in Ireland it would be bacon, not corned beef).
- **Salmon**: smoked Donegal salmon is the export benchmark.
- **Oysters (Galway and Carlingford)**: the world's most-loved oysters.
- **Butter (Kerrygold)**: grass-fed, golden.
- **Guinness**: cooking ingredient (in stews) and the table drink.
- **Whiskey (Jameson, Bushmills, Redbreast)**: in coffee, in sauces.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Irish Stew](/recipes/cuisine/irish/irish-stew)**: mutton (or lamb) slow-cooked with potato, onion and carrot in a clear broth. Traditionally simple — no thickener, no Guinness.
2. **[Beef and Guinness Stew](/recipes/cuisine/irish/beef-and-guinness-stew)**: beef chuck slow-braised in Guinness with onions, carrot, mushroom and bay. The pub-classic Sunday lunch.
3. **[Shepherds Pie](/recipes/cuisine/irish/shepherds-pie)**: minced lamb with carrot and onion under a mashed potato crust. With beef instead of lamb it becomes cottage pie.
4. **[Colcannon](/recipes/cuisine/irish/colcannon)**: mashed potato with kale or cabbage, butter and spring onion. The Halloween dish — a ring hidden inside foretold marriage for the finder.
5. **[Champ](/recipes/cuisine/irish/champ)**: mashed potato with spring onions and a well of melted butter in the centre. The Ulster classic.
6. **[Boxty](/recipes/cuisine/irish/boxty)**: a half-grated, half-mashed potato pancake. "Boxty on the griddle, boxty in the pan, if you can't make boxty you'll never get a man."
7. **[Dublin Coddle](/recipes/cuisine/irish/dublin-coddle)**: pork sausages, bacon, potato and onion slow-braised in stock. The Dublin working-class one-pot, Joyce's favourite.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Guinness (the proper Dublin pour with the right surge-and-settle technique takes about 119.5 seconds; the bartender will judge you if you rush them), or with a Jameson Black Barrel whiskey. End with brown bread and butter (the soda bread Irish tradition), or with a slice of barmbrack (the Halloween fruit loaf) and strong black tea.
