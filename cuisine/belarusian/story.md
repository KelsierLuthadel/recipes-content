# Belarusian

*The kitchen of the eastern Slavic forest. Potatoes in twenty forms (the country is sometimes called bul'bashy — "potato people" — only half jokingly), wild mushrooms from the forests, sour-cream finishing everything, and the cold beetroot soup that turns summer lunch pink.*

## A short history

Belarusian cooking sits between Polish, Russian, Lithuanian and Ukrainian traditions, with its own deep peasant agriculture base. The basic shelf — rye and barley bread, potatoes (introduced from the 18th century onwards and absorbed completely), pork, cabbage, beetroot, dairy and forest mushrooms — has been the working kitchen for centuries.

The country's forests are world-class. Bialowieza Forest (which Belarus shares with Poland) is the last primeval lowland forest in Europe; the cuisine reflects this with deep mushroom traditions, wild berry preserves, and a hunting tradition that puts wild boar and venison on celebration tables.

The Soviet period (1922–1991) reduced the cuisine to its hardiest staples — draniki (potato pancakes) became the national dish during the bulk-feeding decades. The post-1991 recovery has been slow but the home kitchen continues. The diaspora kitchens in Poland, Lithuania, the US and Israel preserve the codified tradition.

## Regional variations

- **Minsk and central**: codified mainstream. Draniki, machanka.
- **Vitebsk and east**: closer to Russian cooking. More cabbage, more pickling.
- **Brest and west**: closer to Polish-Lithuanian. Pierogi-style dumplings (kalduny), kugelis-style potato dishes.
- **Polesie (south, marshlands)**: distinct fish tradition. Pike, perch, the marshland one-pots.

## Signature ingredients

- **Potatoes**: grated, mashed, in pancakes, in dumplings.
- **Pork**: every part. Salo (cured fat), kielbasa-style sausages, bacon.
- **Sour cream (smetana)**: dressing everything.
- **Wild mushrooms (boletes, chanterelles)**: fresh in summer, dried in winter.
- **Beetroot**: in cold soup (holodnik) and hot (borscht-style).
- **Buckwheat (grechka)**: porridge, in dumplings.
- **Dill, caraway, garlic**: the herb trinity.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Draniki](/recipes/cuisine/belarusian/draniki)**: grated-potato pancakes pan-fried in oil, served with sour cream. The national dish.
2. **[Holodnik](/recipes/cuisine/belarusian/holodnik)**: cold pink beetroot soup with kefir, cucumber, dill and hard-boiled egg. The summer classic, related to Lithuanian šaltibarščiai.
3. **[Machanka](/recipes/cuisine/belarusian/machanka)**: pork stew with cream and mushrooms, served with draniki or pancakes for dipping.
4. **[Kalduny](/recipes/cuisine/belarusian/kalduny)**: small meat-stuffed dumplings (the Belarusian version of pierogi/varenyky), usually in broth.
5. **[Vereshchaka](/recipes/cuisine/belarusian/vereshchaka)**: pork ribs slow-braised in a rich brown gravy with onion, garlic and bay. The Sunday family plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with kvas (fermented rye-bread drink, slightly alcoholic) or with samogon (homemade vodka — better than the bottled stuff in many country kitchens). For Christmas, kutya (sweet wheat-and-poppy-seed porridge) closes the meal.
