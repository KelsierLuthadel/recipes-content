# Holodnik (Belarusian Cold Beet Soup)

![Holodnik](resources/holodnik.jpg)

*Belarus's hot-summer bowl: grated cooked beetroot whisked into cold kefir with grated cucumber, radish, dill and chopped hard-boiled egg; eaten ice-cold with a hot boiled potato dropped into the centre to steam.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 25 minutes (plus 1 hour chill)

**Cook Time:** 35 minutes (beet + potatoes)

## Overview
Holodnik (from the Slavic "holod", meaning cold) is the Belarusian summer answer to the country's heavy winter stews. The construction is unusual: cooked beetroot is grated coarsely, combined with cold kefir (Belarus's national dairy ferment), thinned with cold water, then loaded with grated cucumber, chopped radish, slivered spring onion, masses of fresh dill and chopped hard-boiled egg. The whole bowl chills two hours, then gets served with a single hot freshly-boiled potato dropped into the middle of each portion. The cold-meets-hot contrast is the dish: the cold soup steams faintly around the hot potato as it sinks in, and the cook then breaks the potato with a spoon to mash some of it through the soup as they eat. The bright pink colour and the dill-cucumber-kefir freshness make holodnik one of the great cold soups of Europe.

## Ingredients

### Beet base
- 4 medium beetroots (about 600 g; raw, scrubbed)
- 1 tablespoon white wine vinegar
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt

### Soup
- 700 ml cold kefir (Belarusian-style if possible; or substitute thinned natural yoghurt 600 ml yoghurt + 100 ml cold water)
- 200 ml cold water (plus more to thin if needed)
- 1 large cucumber (peeled, deseeded, grated coarsely)
- 6 small radishes (finely sliced)
- 4 spring onions (finely sliced, white and green parts)
- 1 small bunch fresh dill (finely chopped; reserve some for garnish)
- 4 hard-boiled eggs (peeled, chopped roughly)
- 1 1/2 teaspoons fine sea salt
- 1/2 teaspoon coarsely cracked black pepper
- 1 small pinch caster sugar
- 1 tablespoon fresh lemon juice

### Hot potato (essential)
- 4 medium floury potatoes (Maris Piper or similar; peeled, halved if large)
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt (for boiling)
- 1 small knob butter (per potato, to dot)

### To serve
- A small bowl of sour cream
- Extra dill sprigs
- A wedge of dark rye bread per person
- Optional: chopped cooked beef ham or smoked salmon (the meat variant)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Cook the beetroot
1. Trim the tops of the beetroots, leaving 1 cm of stalk (prevents bleeding).
2. Place in a saucepan; cover with cold water; add the vinegar and salt.
3. Bring to a boil; reduce; simmer 35-45 minutes till tender (a knife should slide through easily).
4. Drain; cool under cold running water; peel (the skins slip off).
5. Grate the cooled beets coarsely (a box grater is right).

### Stage 2 - Boil the potatoes
1. Place the peeled potatoes in a saucepan; cover with cold salted water.
2. Bring to a boil; cook 18-22 minutes till tender to a knife tip.
3. Drain; keep warm under a clean tea towel (they should still be hot when the soup is served).

### Stage 3 - Build the soup
1. In a large mixing bowl, combine the cold kefir and cold water.
2. Whisk to loosen.
3. Add the grated cooked beetroot.
4. Stir in the grated cucumber, sliced radishes, spring onions, and chopped dill (reserve a small handful of dill for the garnish).
5. Fold in the chopped hard-boiled egg.
6. Season with salt, pepper, sugar and lemon juice.
7. Stir well; taste; adjust seasoning.

### Stage 4 - Chill
1. Cover the bowl; refrigerate at least 1 hour (longer is better; the flavours marry overnight).
2. The soup should be very cold when served.

### Stage 5 - Serve
1. Ladle the cold soup into 4 wide chilled bowls.
2. Drop one hot freshly-boiled potato into the centre of each bowl (this is essential to the dish).
3. Dot the potato with a small knob of butter; it melts immediately.
4. Spoon a tablespoon of sour cream on top of each portion.
5. Scatter the reserved dill over.
6. Serve with a wedge of dark rye bread.

## Notes
- **Cold soup, hot potato:** the contrast is the whole dish. Don't serve the potato cold.
- **Real kefir:** Belarusian kefir has the right balance of acid and viscosity. Substituting yoghurt requires thinning with water and a squeeze more lemon.
- **Coarse-grate the beets:** fine-grated turns the soup to mush; coarse keeps the texture pleasant.
- **Don't skip the dill:** dill is the dish; without it holodnik tastes flat.
- **Chill at least 1 hour:** the longer it sits, the better. Overnight is the Belarusian grandmother's standard.

## Variations
- **Holodnik with kefir + sour cream:** swap 200 ml of the kefir for 200 ml sour cream for a thicker, richer soup.
- **With beef ham:** add 100 g diced cooked salt-cured beef ham (the Belarusian meaty version, a festival dish).
- **With smoked salmon:** add 100 g flaked smoked salmon at the bowl stage (the modern Minsk-restaurant version).
- **Vegan holodnik:** use coconut yoghurt or oat-milk kefir; skip the egg; replace the dairy garnish with vegan sour cream.
- **Tart holodnik:** add 2 tablespoons more lemon juice and 1 tablespoon brine from a jar of pickles; sharper, more refreshing.

## Serving
- At a Belarusian summer table (the traditional setting) · alongside dark rye bread and a glass of cold kvass · at a name-day lunch when it's too hot for borshch · as the first course before machanka or vereshchaka · at a Minsk dacha lunch · packed cold in a Thermos for a forest-walk picnic.

## Storage
- Refrigerates 2 days in a sealed jug; the colour stays bright.
- The egg and cucumber soften over time; freshly add for day-2 servings if possible.
- Don't freeze (the kefir separates).
- Boil the hot potato fresh for each serving; the leftover potatoes don't reheat well into the cold soup.
