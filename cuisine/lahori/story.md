# Lahori

*The kitchen of Punjab's old capital. A grill-and-karahi tradition that turns lamb, fresh ginger and green chilli into the most distinctive cooking in Pakistan. If Karachi codified Pakistani food, Lahore wrote it.*

## A short history

Lahore's kitchen begins in the fertile alluvial plain between the Ravi and the Chenab, one of the oldest continuously farmed landscapes on earth. The Harappan civilisation that flourished here from 2,600 BCE onwards grew wheat, barley, mustard, sesame and the earliest cultivated cottons; they kept cattle, sheep and goats, and they almost certainly ground grain into the flatbread that is still the daily bread of Punjab. The clay tandoor itself, sunk into the ground and fired with wood or dung, is recognisable in Harappan archaeological remains. When historic Lahore emerges in the records (the city is mentioned by name in 7th-century Chinese travellers' accounts) the agricultural base of the kitchen is already the one we still recognise: wheat, dairy, mustard greens, lentils, river fish and a deep meat-eating tradition.

The Ghaznavid and Ghorid sultanates from the 11th and 12th centuries onwards brought Persian and Central Asian cooks into the city, and with them the pilau tradition, the kebab and the codified use of saffron, dried fruit and nuts in savoury cooking. Lahore became one of the great cities of the Delhi Sultanate (1206 to 1526). Then, under Akbar in the late 16th century, the Mughals made it their second capital, and the kitchen took its modern shape. The Mughal-era court cuisine left Lahore a strong inheritance of slow-cooked rice dishes (biryani, pulao) and grilled meats (kebab, tikka). The bawarchis (master cooks) of the Mughal court layered Central Asian, Persian and Indian techniques into the dum (slow-steam) tradition, the tandoor-roasted nihari that the city still serves at dawn, and the rich nut-and-cream Mughlai gravies.

The Columbian Exchange reached Lahore via the Portuguese on the Malabar Coast in the 16th and 17th centuries. Chillies, tomatoes and potatoes worked their way north and rewrote the kitchen completely. The fresh green chilli matchsticks scattered across every modern karahi are a Mughal-era acquisition that the older recipes would not have recognised. The Sikh Empire under Ranjit Singh (1799 to 1849) ran Lahore as its capital and added a strong Punjabi village layer to the courtly tradition: makki di roti (cornmeal flatbread, from Columbian Exchange maize), sarson da saag (mustard greens), the dairy-heavy lassi and yoghurt traditions. The British Raj (1849 to 1947) added tea, the bakery shelf and the railway stations that pushed Lahori food across the subcontinent.

Partition in 1947 froze the cuisine into a particular configuration: Lahore lost its Hindu and Sikh population and gained Muslim refugees from East Punjab, Delhi and Lucknow. The Delhi and Lucknowi cooks brought their dum biryani and qorma traditions; the Lahori native cooking absorbed them. The city's cooking is a particular slice of the broader Punjabi tradition: heavier on lamb than chicken, more karahi-fried than slow-braised, with fresh ginger, green chilli and coriander as the signature finish on most dishes. The karahi (wok-style pan) gave its name to the city's signature cooking method.

Modern Lahore is one of the great food cities of South Asia, with street food districts (Gawalmandi, Anarkali, MM Alam Road) that pull food tourists from across Pakistan and the diaspora. The Food Street tradition, where restaurants spill onto the pavement and cooking happens in the open, codified Lahori cuisine into a recognisable shelf separate from broader Pakistani food. The Lahori diaspora in Birmingham, Bradford, Toronto and Dubai keeps the karahi and the chargha tradition sharp; back home, the city's old breakfast trade (paya at four in the morning, nihari at six, halwa puri at eight) carries on as it has for a century.

## Regional variations

Lahori cooking is itself a regional cuisine within Pakistan, but within the city there are sub-traditions:

- **Old City (Walled City)**: the most traditional shelf. Phajja paya, Taj Mahal nihari, the breakfast trade that runs from dawn till mid-morning.
- **Gawalmandi**: the karahi capital. The Lahori mutton karahi was codified here.
- **Anarkali**: the chargha (whole roasted chicken) belt.
- **Food Street MM Alam**: the modern, more refined Lahori dining scene.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb (mutton)**: more than chicken. The default Lahori meat.
- **Fresh ginger and green chilli**: in matchsticks, on every karahi at the finish.
- **Yoghurt and dahi**: marinade, sauce thickener, raita.
- **Whole spices**: cinnamon, cardamom, clove, peppercorn, added whole rather than ground.
- **Mint and coriander**: fresh, by the handful, as a finishing herb on every plate.
- **Imli (tamarind)**: the souring agent in chutneys and a few sauces.
- **Desi ghee**: clarified butter, the fat of choice for biryani, pulao and the celebration plates.

## Learn it in seven recipes

The catalogue's Lahori shelf concentrates on the signature grill-and-karahi tradition. Here are the standouts.

1. **[Lahori Mutton Karahi](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-mutton-karahi)**: mutton (goat or lamb) slow-cooked karahi-style with tomato, ginger and green chilli, finished with a heavy hand of fresh coriander. The signature Lahori dish.
2. **[Lahori Chargha](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-chargha)**: whole chicken marinated with yoghurt, ginger-garlic and spices, steamed and then deep-fried whole. The Anarkali street-food classic.
3. **[Lahori Chapli Kebab](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-chapli-kebab)**: flat, large kebabs of minced lamb with pomegranate seeds, coriander and green chilli, shallow-fried on a hot tawa. The Pashtun-influenced flat kebab.
4. **[Lahori Beef Boti Kebab](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-beef-boti-kebab)**: cubed beef marinated in yoghurt and spices, charcoal-grilled on skewers. The boti (cube) kebab tradition.
5. **[Lahori Fried Fish](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-fried-fish)**: river fish marinated with chickpea flour, ginger-garlic and ajwain, deep-fried till crisp. The winter speciality, sold from every Food Street stall once the cold weather sets in.
6. **[Lahori Mutton Biryani](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/rice/lahori-mutton-biryani)**: layered rice and mutton with the Lahori spice profile, milder than Sindhi, more aromatic. The Sunday family plate.
7. **[Lahori Chana Pulao](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/rice/lahori-chana-pulao)**: chickpea pulao with cumin, ginger and a light hand on the spice. The everyday rice when biryani is too much.

Cook the karahi and the chargha and you have the Lahori signature. The chapli and the boti are the grill. The fish is the winter; the biryani and chana pulao are the rice shelf.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the Lahori side dishes (mint chutney, imli chutney, onion salad, tarka dal) and the bread tradition shared with broader Pakistani cuisine (naan, roti). Pair everything with a Lahori lassi or kashmiri chai. The Lahori shelf is small but tight. Every dish is a benchmark.
