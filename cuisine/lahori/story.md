# Lahori

*The kitchen of Punjab's old capital. A grill-and-karahi tradition that turns lamb, fresh ginger and green chilli into the most distinctive cooking in Pakistan. If Karachi codified Pakistani food, Lahore wrote it.*

## A short history

Lahore was the second capital of the Mughal Empire and remains the cultural heart of Pakistan. The city's cooking is a particular slice of the broader Punjabi tradition — heavier on lamb than chicken, more karahi-fried than slow-braised, with fresh ginger, green chilli and coriander as the signature finish on most dishes. The tandoor (clay oven) is central; the karahi (wok-style pan) gave its name to the city's signature cooking method.

The Mughal-era court cuisine left Lahore a strong inheritance of slow-cooked rice dishes (biryani, pulao) and grilled meats (kebab, tikka). The Punjabi village tradition contributed the bread shelf (naan, roti, paratha), the dal varieties, and the everyday vegetable curries. Partition in 1947 froze the cuisine into a particular configuration — Lahore lost its Hindu and Sikh population and gained Muslim refugees from East Punjab; the food adjusted but the broad outlines stayed.

Modern Lahore is one of the great food cities of South Asia, with street food districts (Gawalmandi, Anarkali, MM Alam Road) that draw food tourists from across Pakistan and the diaspora. The Food Street tradition — where restaurants spill into the street and cooking happens on the pavement — codified Lahori cuisine into a recognisable shelf separate from broader Pakistani food.

## Regional variations

Lahori cooking is itself a regional cuisine within Pakistan, but within the city there are sub-traditions:

- **Old City (Walled City)**: the most traditional shelf. Phajja paya, Taj Mahal nihari, the breakfast trade that runs from dawn till mid-morning.
- **Gawalmandi**: the karahi capital. The Lahori mutton karahi was codified here.
- **Anarkali**: the chargha (whole roasted chicken) belt.
- **Food Street MM Alam**: the modern, more refined Lahori dining scene.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb (mutton)**: more than chicken. The default Lahori meat.
- **Fresh ginger and green chilli**: in matchsticks, on every karahi at the finish.
- **Yoghurt and dahi**: marinade, sauce thickener, raita.
- **Whole spices**: cinnamon, cardamom, clove, peppercorn — added whole rather than ground.
- **Mint and coriander**: fresh, by the handful, as a finishing herb on every plate.
- **Imli (tamarind)**: the souring agent in chutneys and a few sauces.
- **Desi ghee**: clarified butter, the fat of choice for biryani, pulao and the celebration plates.

## Learn it in seven recipes

The catalogue's Lahori shelf is concentrated on the signature grill-and-karahi tradition; here are the standouts.

1. **[Lahori Mutton Karahi](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-mutton-karahi)**: mutton (goat or lamb) slow-cooked karahi-style with tomato, ginger and green chilli, finished with a heavy hand of fresh coriander. The signature Lahori dish.
2. **[Lahori Chargha](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-chargha)**: whole chicken marinated with yoghurt, ginger-garlic and spices, steamed and then deep-fried whole. The Anarkali street-food classic.
3. **[Lahori Chapli Kebab](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-chapli-kebab)**: flat, large kebabs of minced lamb with pomegranate seeds, coriander and green chilli, shallow-fried on a hot tawa. The Pashtun-influenced flat kebab.
4. **[Lahori Beef Boti Kebab](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-beef-boti-kebab)**: cubed beef marinated in yoghurt and spices, charcoal-grilled on skewers. The boti (cube) kebab tradition.
5. **[Lahori Fried Fish](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/lahori-fried-fish)**: river fish marinated with chickpea flour, ginger-garlic and ajwain, deep-fried till crisp. The winter speciality, sold from every Food Street stall once the cold weather sets in.
6. **[Lahori Mutton Biryani](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/rice/lahori-mutton-biryani)**: layered rice and mutton with the Lahori spice profile — milder than Sindhi, more aromatic. The Sunday family plate.
7. **[Lahori Chana Pulao](/recipes/cuisine/lahori/rice/lahori-chana-pulao)**: chickpea pulao with cumin, ginger and a light hand on the spice. The everyday rice when biryani is too much.

Cook the karahi and the chargha and you have the Lahori signature. The chapli and the boti are the grill. The fish is the winter; the biryani and chana pulao are the rice shelf.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries the Lahori side dishes (mint chutney, imli chutney, onion salad, tarka dal) and the bread tradition shared with broader Pakistani cuisine (naan, roti). Pair everything with a Lahori lassi or kashmiri chai. The Lahori shelf is small but tight — every dish is a benchmark.
