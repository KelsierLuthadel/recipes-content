# Native North American (Indigenous)

*The cooking traditions of the First Nations, Métis and Indigenous peoples of North America. The three sisters (corn, beans, squash), the cedar-plank salmon of the Pacific Northwest, wild rice of the Great Lakes, the bison and venison of the plains, and the fry-bread that became unifying diaspora food.*

## A short history

Native North American cuisines were the original cuisines of the continent: hundreds of distinct traditions across hundreds of nations and language groups, broadly grouped by region. The earliest stories go back at least 15,000 years, to the peoples who followed game across the Bering land bridge and spread south through every landscape the continent offered. By around 7,000 years ago, the squash-growing kitchens of central Mexico had pushed north, and the bean and corn varieties followed over the next several millennia. The three sisters companion-planting system (corn, beans and squash grown in the same mound) became the foundation of farming across what is now the eastern US and southern Canada by roughly 1000 CE.

Regional traditions matured around what each landscape gave. The Pacific Northwest peoples (Tlingit, Haida, Coast Salish, Kwakwaka'wakw) built one of the densest pre-contact populations in the Americas on Pacific salmon runs, with salmon smoked over alder, baked on cedar planks and rendered into oolichan grease that travelled inland on "grease trails." Plains peoples (Lakota, Cheyenne, Crow, Blackfoot) followed enormous bison herds and pounded the dried meat with fat and saskatoon berries to make pemmican, the original travel food. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy in the northeastern woodlands ran sophisticated three-sisters agriculture; the Anishinaabe gathered manoomin (wild rice) from canoes on Great Lakes waters; the Pueblo, Diné and Hopi farmed blue and red maize at altitude in the southwest, irrigating the dry mesa land with techniques that still work today.

European contact from 1492 onward devastated these food systems. Disease epidemics killed up to 90% of some populations within a century. The Columbian Exchange ran both ways: Native cooks gave the world tomatoes, potatoes, corn, beans, squash, chillies, vanilla, chocolate and turkeys, while colonisers brought wheat, cattle, pigs, sugar and the alcohol that would prove ruinous. Tomatoes, chillies and turkeys returned to Native kitchens transformed by their global tour. The bison were nearly exterminated by 1880 by deliberate US Army policy aimed at starving the Plains nations into reservation life.

The 19th-century reservation era and the residential school system in Canada and the US (which forcibly separated Indigenous children from their cultures, languages and food traditions, running into the 1990s) created a near-total break with traditional cooking. Fry-bread (flour-and-baking-powder dough deep-fried into a flat disc) is the most-eaten Native American food today, but it was born of that rupture. Diné (Navajo) cooks developed it from the government-issue flour, sugar and lard during the forced relocation to Bosque Redondo (the Long Walk, 1864). The dish is contested: beloved as comfort food and unifying diaspora cooking, but also a reminder of forced assimilation. The Navajo taco (fry-bread topped with chili, beans, lettuce, tomato, cheese) is the modern signature.

Since the 1990s, an Indigenous food sovereignty movement has worked to recover pre-contact ingredients and techniques. Chefs like Sean Sherman (Oglala Lakota, The Sioux Chef and Owamni in Minneapolis), Loretta Barrett Oden (Citizen Potawatomi) and Inez Cook (Nuxalk, Salmon n' Bannock in Vancouver) lead the contemporary work. Tribal-led salmon recovery on the Klamath, bison restoration on the Plains, manoomin protection in the Great Lakes and seed-keeping projects across the southwest are slowly rebuilding the larder. The cuisine is not a museum piece; it is a living tradition coming back into its own kitchens.

## Regional variations

- **Pacific Northwest (Tlingit, Haida, Coast Salish)**: cedar-planked salmon, halibut, oolichan fish oil, the salmon-and-berry tradition.
- **Plains (Lakota, Cheyenne, Crow, Blackfoot)**: bison, pemmican (dried meat pounded with fat and berries), wojapi (berry sauce).
- **Southwest (Diné/Navajo, Hopi, Pueblo)**: corn (blue, white, red), beans, squash, posole, fry-bread.
- **Eastern Woodlands (Haudenosaunee, Anishinaabe)**: three sisters, wild rice, maple syrup.
- **Subarctic and Arctic (Cree, Dene, Inuit)**: caribou, muskox, Arctic char, bannock.

## Signature ingredients

- **Three sisters (corn, beans, squash)**: the foundational crop trio.
- **Wild rice (manoomin)**: from the Great Lakes; actually a grass, not rice.
- **Salmon (Pacific NW)**: cedar-planked, smoked, dried.
- **Bison**: the plains' protein.
- **Venison**: across much of the eastern and central US.
- **Maple syrup**: from the eastern woodlands.
- **Berries**: saskatoon, juniper, sumac, cranberry, blueberry.
- **Bannock / fry-bread**: the modern diaspora bread.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Three Sisters Stew](/recipes/cuisine/native-north-american/three-sisters-stew)**: corn, beans and squash slow-cooked together, the foundational dish of the eastern woodlands and the Iroquois agricultural philosophy.
2. **[Three Sisters Succotash](/recipes/cuisine/native-north-american/three-sisters-succotash)**: the side-dish version. Corn, beans and squash sautéed together with herbs.
3. **[Cedar-Planked Salmon](/recipes/cuisine/native-north-american/cedar-planked-salmon)**: Pacific Northwest salmon grilled on a soaked cedar board over open coals. Smoke and cedar oils infuse the fish.
4. **[Frybread with Wojapi](/recipes/cuisine/native-north-american/frybread-with-wojapi)**: deep-fried flour-and-baking-powder bread topped with wojapi (a thick berry sauce of saskatoons, chokecherries or blueberries).
5. **[Wild Rice with Venison](/recipes/cuisine/native-north-american/wild-rice-with-venison)**: hand-harvested manoomin (wild rice) with venison, mushrooms and dried berries. The Anishinaabe Great Lakes classic.

## Where to go next

Indigenous-led restaurants are doing the most exciting work in North American food right now. Owamni (Sean Sherman, Minneapolis; James Beard Best New Restaurant 2022), Salmon n' Bannock (Vancouver), Pow Wow Café (London) and others are reclaiming pre-contact ingredients and techniques. The work is ongoing; the cuisine is rebuilding.
