# Korean

*The cuisine of fermentation. Built on the gochujang-doenjang-soy trinity, the kimchi jar, and the tabletop grill; a kitchen that turned salt-and-time into the most distinct flavour profile in East Asia.*

## A short history

Korean cooking sits on a 5,000-year fermentation tradition. The Mumun-era farmers of the second millennium BCE were already growing soybean and barley on the peninsula, and the brutal continental winters (Seoul drops to twenty below in January) and short growing seasons forced the kitchen to preserve everything: cabbage, radish, fish, soybean. Bronze Age clay jars buried up to their necks in cold earth, the ancestor of the modern onggi crock, gave the cook a temperature-stable vessel for the slow microbial work that defines Korean flavour to this day. Kimchi (the fermented vegetable spectrum) runs to hundreds of regional varieties; jang (fermented soybean pastes) comes in three families: ganjang (soy sauce), doenjang (savoury paste), gochujang (chilli paste).

The Three Kingdoms period (roughly 57 BCE to 668 CE) and the Goryeo dynasty (918 to 1392) layered Buddhist temple cooking on top of the older animist shelf, with monks codifying a sophisticated vegetarian tradition (sanchae namul, wild-mountain-vegetable side dishes) that still defines the temple cuisine of Korea today. Beef-eating waned under Buddhist influence and surged back under the Mongol Yuan domination of the late 13th century, when the invaders reintroduced cattle culture and the seonggakwon butcher tradition. Bulgogi's grilled-marinated-meat ancestor, maekjeok, dates to this Mongol-touched period.

The Joseon dynasty (1392 to 1897) codified court cuisine into a Confucian-influenced system with strict rules about colour balance (five colours, five elements), the side-dish (banchan) format, and the tabletop spread. The real revolution arrived in the late 16th century: chillies came in via Portuguese traders in Japan and crossed to Korea around the time of the Imjin War (1592 to 1598). Pre-chilli kimchi was just salt-pickled cabbage, white and salty rather than red and pungent; within a century, gochugaru transformed the jar. The same exchange brought sweet potato, pumpkin, corn and tomato, all of which the peninsula's farmers worked into the rotation. Napa cabbage, the big-leaved variety central to modern kimchi, only arrived from China in the 19th century, putting the now-iconic baechu kimchi in roughly its final shape barely 150 years ago.

The Japanese occupation (1910 to 1945) hollowed out the food culture, banning Korean language and forcing rice exports while Koreans ate barley and millet. The Korean War (1950 to 1953) flattened it further; budae jjigae (army-base stew, spam and ramyeon and kimchi simmered together) is a direct relic of the wartime kitchen, when GIs' surplus tinned meat got folded into the country pot. Post-war South Korea ran on government-issued wheat flour, which is why noodle dishes (kalguksu, jajangmyeon) became as central as rice in the 1960s and 70s. The modern Korean kitchen took shape in these decades: the gas-grill table, the dolsot stone bowl, the late-night pojangmacha tents.

From the 1990s onward, the Hallyu (Korean Wave) carried Korean food worldwide on the back of K-pop, K-drama and a deliberate cultural-export push. Today's Korean food is in a global moment. The K-BBQ restaurant is now in every world capital; kimchi appears in supermarkets that wouldn't have known what it was twenty years ago. At home, the cuisine sits between the codified ban-sang (tray meal) tradition, the modern Korean fine dining scene (Mingles, Jungsik), and the explosive street food culture led by Seoul.

## Regional variations

- **Seoul and Gyeonggi**: the court-cuisine inheritance. Lighter seasoning, ornate banchan, the bulgogi and galbi tradition.
- **Jeolla (south-west)**: the cuisine country. Most banchan, most regional pride, more sweetness in the gochujang.
- **Gyeongsang (south-east, Busan)**: saltier, spicier, more seafood. Milmyeon (cold wheat noodles), the Busan seafood tradition.
- **Jeju Island**: black pig pork (heuk dwaeji), abalone, sea urchin, the volcanic-soil vegetable shelf.
- **North Korea (Pyongyang, Hamgyong)**: cold-noodle country (Pyongyang naengmyeon) and the dumpling tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Gochujang**: fermented red chilli paste with rice and soybean. The signature Korean condiment.
- **Doenjang**: fermented soybean paste, savoury and earthy. The miso of Korea.
- **Ganjang (soy sauce)**: Korean soy sauce, sharper than Japanese.
- **Gochugaru**: coarse red pepper flakes. The dry chilli base in kimchi.
- **Sesame oil and seeds**: toasted, the finishing flourish on everything.
- **Garlic, ginger, spring onion**: the aromatic trinity used heavily.
- **Kimchi**: not an ingredient, a category. Baechu (cabbage) is the most common, but there are hundreds.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven cover the BBQ grill, the stew pot, the rice-bowl tradition and the fried-chicken phenomenon. Cook them and the cuisine reveals itself.

1. **[Bulgogi](/recipes/cuisine/korean/bulgogi)**: thin-sliced beef marinated in soy, sugar, sesame, pear and garlic, grilled fast. The benchmark Korean BBQ dish and the most-loved Korean food internationally.
2. **[Bibimbap](/recipes/cuisine/korean/bibimbap)**: rice topped with seasoned vegetables, beef, a fried egg and gochujang, mixed at the table. The most photogenic Korean dish and the gateway to banchan.
3. **[Kimchi Jjigae](/recipes/cuisine/korean/kimchi-jjigae)**: aged kimchi stewed with pork, tofu and stock. The everyday Korean comfort food, especially with old, sour kimchi past its prime.
4. **[Doenjang Jjigae](/recipes/cuisine/korean/doenjang-jjigae)**: bean-paste stew with tofu, courgette, mushroom and clam (or shrimp). The other stew, with the umami of fermented soybean instead of fermented cabbage.
5. **[Korean Fried Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/korean/korean-fried-chicken)**: chicken fried twice, glazed with gochujang-soy-honey. KFC's better-loved Asian cousin, and the late-night beer-pairing food.
6. **[Japchae](/recipes/cuisine/korean/japchae)**: sweet-potato glass noodles stir-fried with beef, mushroom, carrot, spinach, sesame oil. The celebration noodle dish, served at every birthday and holiday.
7. **[Tteokbokki](/recipes/cuisine/korean/tteokbokki)**: chewy rice cakes simmered in gochujang sauce with fish cake and spring onion. The Seoul street-food standard.

Cook the bulgogi and the bibimbap and you have the grill and the bowl. The kimchi and doenjang jjigae are the stew pair. The fried chicken and tteokbokki are the street; the japchae is the celebration.

## Where to go next

Beyond these seven, the catalogue carries more BBQ (samgyeopsal pork belly, galbi short ribs), the noodle shelf (naengmyeon cold noodles, jajangmyeon), the dak galbi spicy chicken stir-fry, and the banchan canon (pickled radish, seasoned spinach, kongnamul bean sprouts). Build a full ban-sang: rice, soup, three banchan, a main, kimchi. That's the proper Korean meal.
