# Guatemalan

*The kitchen of the Mayan heartland. Pepián (the country's national chilli-and-pumpkin-seed stew), the corn-and-bean spine inherited from the Mayan civilisations, kak'ik (turkey stew), and tamales colorados as the Sunday family lunch.*

## A short history

Guatemalan cooking is the most deeply Mayan-influenced kitchen in Central America. The Maya domesticated corn (maize) in the highlands south of here roughly 9,000 years ago, and the corn-and-bean-and-squash trio (the Three Sisters) has been the spine of the kitchen ever since. The classic Maya civilisation (roughly 250-900 CE) built city-states across what's now Guatemala (Tikal, Uaxactún, Quiriguá) and developed the technique that still defines every tortilla and tamal on the modern table: nixtamalisation, soaking dried corn in slaked lime before grinding, which unlocks the niacin in the kernel and prevents pellagra. Without nixtamal, a corn-based diet starves you. With it, you can build a civilisation. The pre-Columbian Maya also gave the kitchen turkey (the only large domesticated animal in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica), cocoa (drunk unsweetened with chilli and corn), chillies of a dozen varieties, the pumpkin seed (pepita) as a thickener, avocado, vanilla and the tamale itself.

The country holds the largest indigenous population share in the region, and 22-plus Mayan languages are still spoken every day. You taste it on the plate: corn is the star, pumpkin seeds form the base of pepián in a way that's distinctly pre-Columbian, and the slow stew thickened with corn or seed is an ancient technique. The post-Classic period (900-1500 CE) saw the highland K'iche', Kaqchikel and Mam Maya kingdoms develop their own distinct regional kitchens; the slow turkey-and-achiote stew that became kak'ik traces directly to ceremonial cooking from this period in the Verapaz highlands.

Spanish colonisation arrived in 1524 under Pedro de Alvarado and turned the country upside down. The conquistadors brought beef, pork, chicken, dairy, rice, wheat, sugar, citrus, garlic, onion and the slow Iberian stew. The Spanish also brought the Catholic feast calendar, which fused with the older Mayan ceremonial calendar to produce the holiday food tradition that still rules the home kitchen: fiambre on 1 November for All Saints' Day (an enormous cold pickled-meat-and-vegetable platter eaten only on that day, with hundreds of regional variations), tamales colorados at Christmas, ponche de frutas in Advent. The colonial encomienda system bound Mayan farmers to Spanish landowners for three centuries, and the resulting cuisine is deeply indigenous in its seed-and-chilli sauces and tamale culture, and Catholic-mestizo in its holiday calendar.

The 19th century, the independence period (Guatemala broke from Spain in 1821 and from the Federal Republic of Central America in 1838), and the coffee-and-banana republic decades that followed kept the country agriculturally focused but politically unstable. Coffee became Guatemala's signature export from the 1860s onwards, and Antigua's coffee farms still grow some of the world's best beans. Wheat bread arrived properly through the German immigrants who settled the Verapaz coffee region in the 1860s-1870s; pan dulce and the German-Guatemalan baking tradition come from there. Chinese immigration in the late 19th and early 20th centuries layered a chow mein and sweet-and-sour shelf onto the urban menu.

The 20th century brought the 36-year civil war (1960-1996), enormous indigenous displacement and a diaspora that took Guatemalan food to Los Angeles, Houston, Chicago and Washington DC. Through all of it the home kitchen stayed close to the inherited shape. Pepián, a slow-roasted-spice-and-pumpkin-seed sauce served over chicken or beef, is the national dish, with regional twists across the country. Kak'ik, the Mayan turkey stew with achiote and chiltepe peppers, made UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2023. The contemporary Antigua and Guatemala City restaurant scene has built a quietly excellent modern Guatemalan kitchen on top of the Mayan and Spanish inheritance, with chefs cooking heirloom corn, indigenous chillies and pre-Columbian techniques for international diners.

## Regional variations

- **Antigua and central**: the mainstream. Pepián, tamales colorados.
- **Quetzaltenango (Xela) and the highlands (Mayan)**: more distinctly indigenous. Jocon (green stew), kak'ik (turkey stew).
- **Verapaz (north-central Mayan highlands)**: the kak'ik heartland.
- **Pacific coast**: more seafood. The Spanish-mestizo seafood tradition.
- **Petén (north jungle)**: distinct dishes shared with Belize and southern Mexico.

## Signature ingredients

- **Corn**: nixtamalised for tortillas, tamales, atol. The defining grain.
- **Pumpkin seeds (pepitoria)**: ground for sauces.
- **Sesame seeds**: in pepián.
- **Tomatillo and tomato**: in green and red sauces.
- **Dried chillies (guaque, pasa, zambo, chiltepe)**: in the sauce bases.
- **Annatto (achiote)**: the red colourant.
- **Hierba buena (mint), cilantro, ruda**: the herb trio.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Pepián](/recipes/cuisine/guatemalan/pepian)**: chicken (or beef) in a thick sauce of roasted pumpkin seeds, sesame, tomato, tomatillo, dried chillies and spices. The national dish.
2. **[Jocon](/recipes/cuisine/guatemalan/jocon)**: chicken in green sauce (tomatillo, fresh herbs, pumpkin seeds, sesame). The Kaqchikel Mayan classic.
3. **[Kak'ik](/recipes/cuisine/guatemalan/kakik)**: turkey stewed with achiote, chiltepe peppers, mint, ruda and tomato. The Mayan ceremonial dish from Verapaz, on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list.
4. **[Hilachas](/recipes/cuisine/guatemalan/hilachas)**: "shredded". Beef slow-cooked with tomato, peppers and onion until the meat pulls apart in strands. Eat with rice and tortillas.
5. **[Tamales Colorados](/recipes/cuisine/guatemalan/tamales-colorados)**: corn-dough tamales filled with chicken or pork in a red recado sauce, wrapped in banana leaves and steamed. The Christmas and Saturday-night family plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with horchata (rice-and-cinnamon drink) or a Gallo lager. Finish with rellenitos (sweet plantain dumplings filled with refried beans) or mole de plátano, the Antigua dessert of sweet plantain in chocolate-cinnamon sauce.
