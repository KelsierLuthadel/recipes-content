# Canadian

*The kitchen of a vast country with regional larders that look like different cuisines. Poutine, Montreal smoked meat, the maple-syrup tradition, BC salmon, Atlantic lobster, held together by a low-key insistence on whatever's local.*

## A short history

Canadian cooking is hard to summarise because the country covers six time zones and three founding food traditions. The First Nations, Métis and Inuit who held the land for thousands of years before contact built the foundations. They tapped the maple in late winter when the sap rose, dried and pounded buffalo and venison into pemmican mixed with berries and rendered fat (the original travel food, light, calorie-dense, indestructible), smoked salmon on cedar racks along the Pacific, harvested wild rice from the lakes of what is now Ontario and Manitoba, and cooked under hot stones in pit ovens up and down the coasts. Bannock, the flat pan-bread that now reads as indigenous food, actually arrived with Scottish fur traders and got absorbed into First Nations and Métis kitchens so completely it became theirs.

The French arrived in the early 1600s, building Quebec in 1608 and Acadia along the Bay of Fundy. They brought the pork-and-pea-soup country cooking of northern France and adapted it to a brutal continental winter. The tourtière (spiced minced-pork pie), the cretons (potted pork spread), the pea soup with salt pork: all date to the seigneurial farms of New France. The fur trade pushed French voyageurs deep into the interior, and they took the pemmican habit, the dried-meat shelf and the wild-game cooking back to the river-mouth posts. The British conquest of 1763 layered Anglo-Scottish cooking on top: the meat pie, the boiled dinner, the oatcake, the rolled roast.

The 19th century rewrote the country's food map. The Highland Clearances and the Irish Famine pushed Scottish and Irish farmers onto the Atlantic coast and into Ontario; the Loyalist exodus from the American Revolution seeded Nova Scotia and southern Ontario with New England cooking. Maple sugar (cheaper than imported cane through most of the century) sweetened everything. The Hudson's Bay Company and the railway opened the prairies, and Ukrainian, Polish, Mennonite and German farmers arrived between the 1890s and 1914 with perogies, holubtsi (cabbage rolls), kielbasa, rye bread and the dill-and-sour-cream shelf that still reads as prairie food today. The Atlantic fisheries codified salt cod, lobster suppers, the Newfoundland Jiggs dinner of salt beef with root vegetables.

The 20th century gave the country its most distinctive single dish: poutine (chips, cheese curds, gravy), invented in rural Quebec in the 1950s and now a national identity food. Montreal gave smoked meat, courtesy of the city's Jewish-Lithuanian deli tradition, with Schwartz's running since 1928. The Maritimes gave lobster suppers, scallops and the Atlantic salt-cod tradition. Italian immigration after the Second World War turned Toronto and Montreal pasta-fluent (Toronto's College Street still has the old Little Italy bakeries) and Vancouver started its long Pacific Rim arc with post-war Chinese, Japanese and later Hong Kong waves.

From the 1970s onwards a new round of immigration reshaped the city kitchens completely. Vancouver became one of the great Cantonese and Hong Kong food cities outside Asia, with dim sum culture as serious as anywhere on the Pacific. Toronto's diaspora communities (South Asian along the 401, Caribbean in the west end, Portuguese on Dundas, Ethiopian and Somali in the east) built a food scene that the city's tourist board now leans on harder than the bagels and back bacon. The contemporary Canadian table is a regional patchwork held together by maple syrup, a serious coffee shop culture (Tim Hortons since 1964) and a low-key consensus that the cooking should taste of where it came from. Maple syrup is the closest thing to a national ingredient. Canada produces about 70% of the world's supply, mostly from Quebec.

## Regional variations

- **Quebec**: poutine, tourtière, pâté chinois, the cabane à sucre (sugar shack) tradition.
- **Maritimes (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, PEI, Newfoundland)**: lobster, scallops, fish-and-chips, Atlantic salt cod, the Newfoundland Jiggs dinner.
- **Ontario**: the cosmopolitan kitchen. Toronto's food scene is among the most diverse in the world.
- **Prairies (Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta)**: beef cattle, perogies (Ukrainian-Canadian), Saskatoon berry, Alberta bison.
- **British Columbia**: Pacific salmon, the cedar-plank tradition, Asian fusion (especially in Vancouver), the food-truck scene.
- **North (Yukon, NWT, Nunavut)**: Inuit and First Nations traditions. Caribou, muskox, Arctic char, bannock bread.

## Signature ingredients

- **Maple syrup**: in cooking, on pancakes, baked into salmon and pork.
- **Atlantic salmon and BC salmon**: smoked, grilled, planked on cedar.
- **Cheddar**: especially Quebec cheddar (Black River Cheese Co. and Avonlea are world-class).
- **Cheese curds**: fresh, squeaky; essential for proper poutine.
- **Beef**: Alberta beef is the western benchmark; PEI ribeye on the east.
- **Wild berries**: blueberry, saskatoon, cloudberry, partridgeberry.
- **Bannock**: the indigenous-inflected bread tradition.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Poutine](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/poutine)**: hot chips topped with fresh cheese curds and beef gravy. The Quebec invention that became the national dish.
2. **[Tourtière](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/tourtiere)**: spiced minced-pork pie. The Quebec Christmas Eve (Réveillon) centrepiece.
3. **[Montreal Smoked Meat Sandwich](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/montreal-smoked-meat-sandwich)**: brisket cured for 10+ days, smoked, steamed, sliced thick on rye with yellow mustard. The Schwartz's signature.
4. **[Montreal All-Dressed Hot Dog](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/montreal-all-dressed-dog)**: hot dog "all-dressed" with mustard, chopped onion, cabbage relish.
5. **[Cedar Plank Salmon](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/cedar-plank-salmon)**: BC salmon grilled on a soaked cedar board so the smoke runs through the fish. The Pacific Northwest indigenous-inspired technique.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a Canadian Club whisky, a Molson Canadian, or a Yukon Gold beer. Finish with butter tarts or Nanaimo bars. For real Canadian variety: poutine in Montreal, lobster in Halifax, perogies in Edmonton, salmon in Vancouver, and dim sum in Toronto, all in the same week if you can manage it.
