# Canadian

*The kitchen of a vast country with regional larders that look like different cuisines. Poutine, Montreal smoked meat, the maple-syrup tradition, BC salmon, Atlantic lobster — held together by a low-key insistence on whatever's local.*

## A short history

Canadian cooking is hard to summarise because the country covers six time zones and three founding food traditions: French (Quebec, Acadia), British (everywhere else), and Indigenous (across the country, especially in the north and on the Pacific coast — First Nations, Métis, Inuit). The 19th-20th century waves of European immigration (Ukrainian, Italian, Polish, Chinese, South Asian) added regional layers; the post-1970s waves of Asian immigration created the Pacific Rim food culture of Vancouver and the diverse food scene of Toronto.

Quebec gave the country its most distinctive single dish — poutine (chips, cheese curds, gravy), invented in the 1950s and now a national identity food. Montreal gave smoked meat (the city's Jewish-Lithuanian deli tradition — Schwartz's since 1928). The Maritimes gave lobster suppers, scallops and the Atlantic salt-cod tradition.

Maple syrup is the closest thing to a national ingredient — Canada produces about 70% of the world's supply, mostly from Quebec.

## Regional variations

- **Quebec**: poutine, tourtière, pâté chinois, the cabane à sucre (sugar shack) tradition.
- **Maritimes (Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, PEI, Newfoundland)**: lobster, scallops, fish-and-chips, Atlantic salt cod, the Newfoundland Jiggs dinner.
- **Ontario**: the cosmopolitan kitchen. Toronto's food scene is among the most diverse in the world.
- **Prairies (Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta)**: beef cattle, perogies (Ukrainian-Canadian), Saskatoon berry, Alberta bison.
- **British Columbia**: Pacific salmon, the cedar-plank tradition, Asian-fusion (especially in Vancouver), the food-truck scene.
- **North (Yukon, NWT, Nunavut)**: Inuit and First Nations traditions. Caribou, muskox, Arctic char, bannock bread.

## Signature ingredients

- **Maple syrup**: in cooking, on pancakes, baked into salmon and pork.
- **Atlantic salmon and BC salmon**: smoked, grilled, planked on cedar.
- **Cheddar**: especially Quebec cheddar (the world's best from the Black River Cheese Co. and Avonlea).
- **Cheese curds**: fresh, squeaky; essential for proper poutine.
- **Beef**: Alberta beef is the western benchmark; PEI ribeye on the east.
- **Wild berries**: blueberry, saskatoon, cloudberry, partridgeberry.
- **Bannock**: the indigenous-inflected bread tradition.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Poutine](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/poutine)**: hot chips topped with fresh cheese curds and beef gravy. The Quebec invention that became the national dish.
2. **[Tourtière](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/tourtiere)**: spiced minced-pork pie. The Quebec Christmas Eve (Réveillon) centrepiece.
3. **[Montreal Smoked Meat Sandwich](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/montreal-smoked-meat-sandwich)**: brisket cured for 10+ days, smoked, steamed, sliced thick on rye with yellow mustard. The Schwartz's signature.
4. **[Montreal All-Dressed Hot Dog](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/montreal-all-dressed-dog)**: hot dog "all-dressed" with mustard, chopped onion, cabbage relish.
5. **[Cedar Plank Salmon](/recipes/cuisine/canadian/cedar-plank-salmon)**: BC salmon grilled on a soaked cedar board, infusing smoke through the fish. The Pacific Northwest indigenous-inspired technique.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with a Canadian Club whisky, a Molson Canadian, or a Yukon Gold beer. End with butter tarts or Nanaimo bars. For real Canadian variety, eat poutine in Montreal, lobster in Halifax, perogies in Edmonton, salmon in Vancouver, and dim sum in Toronto — all in the same week if you can manage it.
