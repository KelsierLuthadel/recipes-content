# Costa Rican

*The kitchen of pura vida. Gallo pinto for breakfast, casado for lunch, the slow-stewed olla de carne for Sunday family. Modest, fresh, anchored in beans and rice and the country's exceptional produce.*

## A short history

Costa Rican cooking sits between the Mexican and South American traditions but keeps its own quiet character. The land between the two American continents was a long-running trade corridor in the pre-Columbian centuries, and the indigenous Boruca, Cabécar, Bribri and Chorotega peoples drew on traditions from both directions. The Chorotega of the northern Pacific (Guanacaste) had the strongest Mesoamerican imprint: maize as a daily staple, the nixtamal lime-treatment of corn for tortillas, beans, squash and chillies. The Bribri and Cabécar of the wet Caribbean foothills leaned south, growing cassava, sweet potato, plantain (introduced post-contact but absorbed completely), cacao for ceremonial drink, and tropical tubers like ñampí and yuca. The Chibchan peoples processed corn into a sour fermented drink called chicha that still appears in indigenous communities today.

Spanish conquest reached the territory in the 1560s, but Costa Rica turned out to be the poor relation of the empire. There was no silver, no large indigenous population to enslave, no plantation economy worth running. The Spanish settled in modest farms in the Central Valley around Cartago (the colonial capital, founded 1563) and raised cattle, pigs, chickens, dairy and wheat for their own table. They brought rice, the citrus shelf, garlic, onion, lard and the rice-and-beans pairing that became gallo pinto (literally "spotted rooster", for the speckled look of rice mixed with black or red beans). The casado plate (one meat, rice, beans, fried plantain, cabbage salad) emerged from this small-farmer kitchen and remains the universal lunch at every soda (the small family restaurants you find on every street). The country never had a large indigenous empire and never ran plantation slavery on the Mexican-Caribbean scale, so the cuisine feels more rural-bourgeois than aristocratic-court.

The Caribbean coast tells a different story. Britain dominated the Mosquito Coast and the wider Caribbean trade through the 18th and 19th centuries, and English-speaking Afro-Caribbean workers from Jamaica arrived in Limón in the 1870s to build the railway and work the United Fruit Company banana plantations. They brought Jamaican Patois, the Anglican church, rice and beans cooked in coconut milk (distinct from the Spanish-Pacific gallo pinto), rondón (slow-stewed fish and root vegetables in coconut), pati (the Jamaican patty pastry), ginger, allspice and the Scotch bonnet chilli. The Limón kitchen still feels closer to Kingston or Bluefields than to San José.

Coffee built modern Costa Rica. The first commercial planting went in around the Central Valley in the 1820s, and by the late 19th century coffee was the dominant export, building a small, relatively prosperous middle class and funding the country's famously well-resourced public schools and hospitals. Coffee culture (the cloth-filter chorreador brew, the morning gallo pinto with sour cream and fried plantain) became part of the national identity. Bananas followed in the 20th century, and the United Fruit Company's grip on the country gave Costa Rica its complicated banana history (the country was the original "banana republic"). Mid-century saw Chinese, Italian and Lebanese immigration, mostly to San José, and the chop suey-Italian-Levantine threads on the city's restaurant scene date to this period.

The 21st-century pura vida tourism boom hasn't really changed the everyday food. The eco-tourist visiting Manuel Antonio or Monteverde eats the same gallo pinto, casado and olla de carne the locals do, often at the same soda. The slow contemporary revival of indigenous and Afro-Caribbean cooking (Limón is finally getting the credit it deserves on the country's food map) is the one shift worth watching.

## Regional variations

- **Central Valley (San José, Cartago)**: the codified mainstream. Gallo pinto, casado.
- **Pacific coast (Guanacaste)**: more Mexican-influenced. Tamales, more chilli.
- **Caribbean (Limón)**: Afro-Caribbean. Rice-and-beans with coconut milk, rondón (slow-stewed fish), Jamaican Patois-speaking communities.
- **Highlands (Talamanca)**: indigenous influence. Cassava, taro, the chocolate tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Black beans**: the everyday legume. With rice they make gallo pinto.
- **Rice**: long-grain, served with every meal.
- **Lizano salsa**: the national bottled sauce. Sweet, slightly tangy, on every breakfast.
- **Plantain**: fried as patacones (twice-fried) or maduros (sweet ripe).
- **Coriander (culantro)**: fresh, in everything.
- **Achiote (annatto)**: in tamales and Caribbean dishes.
- **Coconut milk**: on the Caribbean coast.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Gallo Pinto](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/gallo-pinto)**: black beans and rice cooked together with onion, bell pepper, coriander and Lizano salsa. The national breakfast, served with scrambled eggs, fried plantain, sour cream and fresh tortilla.
2. **[Casado](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/casado)**: the "married" plate. Rice, beans, meat (beef, chicken or fish), fried plantain, cabbage salad, tortilla. The everyday lunch at every soda.
3. **[Olla de Carne](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/olla-de-carne)**: beef and root-vegetable soup with corn, plantain, chayote, yuca and ñampí. The Sunday family meal.
4. **[Arroz con Pollo](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/arroz-con-pollo)**: chicken-and-rice cooked together with bell pepper, peas and coriander. The birthday-party staple.
5. **[Sopa Negra](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/sopa-negra)**: black bean soup with hard-boiled egg, plantain and coriander. The hangover cure.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with café chorreado (drip coffee made through a cloth filter, the traditional Costa Rican brew) or with Imperial beer. The Central Valley grows some of the world's best coffee; brewing it traditionally takes longer than the meal.
