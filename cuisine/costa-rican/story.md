# Costa Rican

*The kitchen of pura vida. Gallo pinto for breakfast, casado for lunch, the slow-stewed olla de carne for Sunday family. Modest, fresh, anchored in beans and rice and the country's exceptional produce.*

## A short history

Costa Rican cooking sits between Mexican and South American traditions but has its own distinct, understated character. The indigenous Boruca, Cabécar and Chorotega peoples contributed maize, beans, cassava, squash and chillies. The Spanish colonisers brought beef, dairy, rice and the rice-and-beans format that became gallo pinto (literally "spotted rooster" — the speckled appearance of rice mixed with black or red beans).

Unlike its larger neighbours, Costa Rica had no large indigenous empire and no plantation slavery on the Mexican-Caribbean scale; the result is a cuisine more bourgeois-rural than aristocratic-court, with the casado plate (one meat, rice, beans, fried plantain, cabbage salad) as the universal lunch at every soda (small family restaurant).

The 21st-century pura vida tourism boom has not significantly changed the everyday food. The eco-tourist visiting Manuel Antonio or Monteverde eats the same gallo pinto, casado, and olla de carne the locals do, often at the same soda.

## Regional variations

- **Central Valley (San José, Cartago)**: the codified mainstream. Gallo pinto, casado.
- **Pacific coast (Guanacaste)**: more Mexican-influenced. Tamales, more chilli.
- **Caribbean (Limón)**: Afro-Caribbean. Rice-and-beans with coconut milk, rondón (slow-stewed fish), Jamaican Patois-speaking communities.
- **Highlands (Talamanca)**: indigenous influence. Cassava, taro, the chocolate tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Black beans**: the everyday legume. With rice they make gallo pinto.
- **Rice**: long-grain, served with every meal.
- **Lizano salsa**: the national bottled sauce — sweet, slightly tangy, on every breakfast.
- **Plantain**: fried as patacones (twice-fried) or maduros (sweet ripe).
- **Coriander (culantro)**: fresh, in everything.
- **Achiote (annatto)**: in tamales and Caribbean dishes.
- **Coconut milk**: on the Caribbean coast.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Gallo Pinto](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/gallo-pinto)**: black beans and rice cooked together with onion, bell pepper, coriander and Lizano salsa. The national breakfast — served with scrambled eggs, fried plantain, sour cream and fresh tortilla.
2. **[Casado](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/casado)**: "married" plate — rice, beans, meat (beef, chicken or fish), fried plantain, cabbage salad, tortilla. The everyday lunch at every soda.
3. **[Olla de Carne](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/olla-de-carne)**: beef and root-vegetable soup with corn, plantain, chayote, yuca and ñampí. The Sunday family meal.
4. **[Arroz con Pollo](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/arroz-con-pollo)**: chicken-and-rice cooked together with bell pepper, peas and coriander. The birthday-party staple.
5. **[Sopa Negra](/recipes/cuisine/costa-rican/sopa-negra)**: black bean soup with hard-boiled egg, plantain and coriander. The hangover cure.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with café chorreado (drip coffee made through a cloth filter — the traditional Costa Rican brewing method) or with Imperial beer. Costa Rica produces some of the world's best coffee from the Central Valley; brewing it traditionally takes longer than the meal.
