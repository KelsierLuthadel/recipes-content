# New Zealand

*The kitchen of the Pacific's most southerly country. British colonial roots crossed with Māori traditions, the world's best lamb, Pacific seafood, the kūmara (sweet potato) and feijoa, the pavlova rivalry with Australia, and a deep dedication to the outdoor barbecue.*

## A short history

New Zealand (Aotearoa) was the last major landmass on earth that humans reached. The indigenous Māori arrived from East Polynesia in canoes around 1280 to 1320 CE, navigating thousands of kilometres of open Pacific by stars and ocean swells. They brought a careful seed bag from the tropical homelands: kūmara (sweet potato), taro, yam, the paper mulberry. Most struggled in the colder southern climate, but kūmara took hold and became the staple farm crop. The Māori built a forest-and-coast foraging tradition around what the new land offered: enormous flightless moa birds (which they hunted to extinction within about two centuries), kererū (wood pigeon), tuna (freshwater eel), pāua (abalone), kina (sea urchin), pikopiko fern shoots, and the long Pacific shellfish shelf. The hāngī (an underground steam-pit lined with hot stones, where root vegetables and meats cook slowly under earth and leaves) became the gathering meal, and still anchors every major Māori occasion.

Abel Tasman sighted the islands in 1642 but didn't land. James Cook charted the coast in 1769 and the sealing and whaling crews followed, building rough trading posts up the coast through the early 1800s. The Treaty of Waitangi in 1840 brought formal British colonisation and a new agricultural reality. British settlers introduced sheep, cattle, wheat, potatoes, tea, the formal Sunday roast, and a deep suspicion of garlic that lasted nearly 150 years. The sheep took to the South Island high country and the rolling North Island hills like nowhere else on earth; New Zealand now has more sheep than people (the ratio peaked at 22:1 in 1982 and still sits around 5:1), and the country's lamb is the world's benchmark spring meat.

The colonial kitchen ran on roast lamb, mutton stew, scones, pikelets, Anzac biscuits (the oat-coconut-golden-syrup biscuit posted to soldiers in WWI) and the wood-fired colonial oven that anchored every farmhouse. The pavlova (a meringue base topped with whipped cream and fresh fruit) emerged somewhere in the 1920s or 30s; New Zealand and Australia have argued ever since over which side of the Tasman invented it. The case is genuinely disputed and the rivalry is permanent.

The post-WWII decades defined New Zealand through agricultural exports. Anchor butter, Mainland cheddar and New Zealand lamb landed in every British supermarket, and the country built its modern economy on feeding the United Kingdom. When Britain joined the European Economic Community in 1973, the export deal collapsed and New Zealand had to find new markets fast, mostly in Asia and the Middle East. The country also turned outward at home. The 1980s and 90s opened the country to Asian (Vietnamese, Chinese, Japanese, Thai, Korean) and Pacific (Tongan, Samoan, Niuean, Fijian) immigration, particularly in Auckland, and the resulting kitchen is the most diverse in the South Pacific. The Sauvignon Blanc revolution out of Marlborough from the 1980s put New Zealand wine on the world map.

The 21st century brought a Pacific-and-Māori culinary recovery. Chefs like Monique Fiso (Hiakai, Wellington) and Ben Bayly lead a reclamation of indigenous ingredients (pikopiko fern, horopito the bushman's pepper, miro and kawakawa leaves, manuka-smoked everything) on a contemporary fine-dining stage. The everyday kitchen still runs on lamb, the BBQ, fish-and-chips at the beach and a serious flat white at the corner cafe (the flat white is an Australasian invention, and the Kiwis claim it; the Australians dispute that one too).

## Regional variations

- **North Island (Auckland, Wellington)**: the cosmopolitan kitchen. Asian-fusion, the Pasifika influence (Tongan, Samoan, Niuean diaspora cooking).
- **South Island (Christchurch, Queenstown)**: more British. Sunday roast, the lamb-and-cheese-and-wine tradition.
- **Coromandel, Bay of Plenty (north-east)**: the green-lipped mussel and oyster farming country.
- **Otago and Central**: stone fruit (Central Otago peaches), the southern pinot noir wine region.

## Signature ingredients

- **Lamb**: the world's most-celebrated lamb. Spring lamb is the seasonal benchmark.
- **Green-lipped mussels**: endemic to NZ waters; the export-grade shellfish.
- **Kūmara (sweet potato)**: orange, red and golden varieties; the Māori-era staple.
- **Manuka honey**: from the manuka bush; the world's most-prized medicinal honey.
- **Pavlova (the meringue dessert)**: claimed by both NZ and Australia; the rivalry is permanent.
- **Hāngī**: not an ingredient but a technique. Root vegetables, lamb, chicken and pork cooked together in an underground stone pit.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[NZ Lamb Roast](/recipes/cuisine/new-zealand/new-zealand-lamb-roast)**: spring lamb roasted with rosemary, garlic and olive oil, served with roast potatoes, kūmara and mint sauce. The Sunday classic.
2. **[Green-Lipped Mussels](/recipes/cuisine/new-zealand/green-lipped-mussels)**: steamed in white wine with garlic and parsley, served on the half-shell. The Coromandel coastal classic.
3. **[Fish and Chips](/recipes/cuisine/new-zealand/fish-and-chips)**: NZ-style with snapper, gurnard or terakihi. The local fish-and-chip shop tradition, eaten out of paper at the beach.
4. **[BBQ Steak and Salad](/recipes/cuisine/new-zealand/bbq-steak-and-salad)**: the universal summer dinner. Sirloin or scotch fillet grilled on the BBQ, salad of tomato, cucumber and feijoa.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Marlborough sauvignon blanc (the country's most famous wine) or a Speight's beer. Finish with a pavlova (meringue base topped with whipped cream and fresh fruit: kiwifruit, passion fruit, strawberry, the New Zealand version traditionally) or hokey pokey ice cream (vanilla with honeycomb pieces, the unofficial national flavour).
