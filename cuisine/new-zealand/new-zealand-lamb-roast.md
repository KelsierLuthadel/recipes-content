# New Zealand Lamb Roast

![New Zealand Lamb Roast](resources/new-zealand-lamb-roast.jpg)

*A leg of New Zealand lamb roasted with garlic, rosemary and mint sauce: the Sunday lunch that defines Kiwi home cooking. Pink in the middle, dark and crusted outside, served with roast potatoes, kūmara and gravy.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 20 minutes

**Cook Time:** 1 hour 30 minutes (plus 20 minutes rest)

## Overview
Lamb is what New Zealand farming is famous for: grass-fed, raised on the rolling green hills of the South Island and Hawke's Bay, with a sweeter and milder flavour than older European lamb traditions. A leg roasted on the bone is the Sunday lunch centrepiece - studded with garlic and rosemary, basted with its own fat, cooked to pink-medium (not the well-done grey of British nostalgia), then rested while the gravy is made from the pan juices. The classic accompaniments are roast potatoes, roast kūmara (the orange Māori sweet potato, sweeter and softer than supermarket varieties), mint sauce sharp with vinegar and sugar, and a green vegetable - peas, green beans, or silver beet (chard).

## Ingredients

### Lamb
- 1 leg of New Zealand lamb on the bone, 2-2.5 kg
- 4 cloves garlic, sliced into thin slivers
- 4 sprigs fresh rosemary, leaves stripped
- 2 tbsp olive oil
- Sea salt and freshly ground black pepper
- 2 tbsp Dijon mustard

### Pan vegetables
- 1 large onion, quartered
- 2 carrots, cut into chunks
- 4 cloves garlic, smashed
- 1 cup chicken or beef stock
- 1 cup dry red wine (or more stock)

### Roast vegetables (cook alongside)
- 1 kg floury potatoes (Agria, Maris Piper, King Edward), peeled, cut into chunks
- 800 g kūmara (orange sweet potato), peeled, cut into chunks
- 3 tbsp olive oil or duck fat
- Salt

### Mint sauce
- A large handful of fresh mint leaves
- 2 tbsp boiling water
- 2 tsp caster sugar
- 4 tbsp white wine vinegar
- A pinch of salt

### Gravy
- 2 tbsp plain flour
- 500 ml chicken or beef stock (extra if needed)
- A splash of red wine (optional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prep the lamb
1. Take the lamb out of the fridge 1 hour before cooking - it should be at room temperature.
2. Score the fat in a diamond pattern with a sharp knife (cut through the fat but not into the meat).
3. With the tip of a knife, make small slits all over the lamb (about 20).
4. Push a sliver of garlic and a few rosemary leaves into each slit.
5. Rub the lamb all over with olive oil; season generously with salt and pepper.
6. Brush the top with Dijon mustard.

### Stage 2 - Set up the roasting tin
1. Preheat the oven to 220°C.
2. Place the onion, carrot and smashed garlic in a large roasting tin to make a rack for the lamb.
3. Lay the lamb on top.
4. Pour the stock and wine into the tin around the vegetables (not over the lamb).

### Stage 3 - First blast then steady roast
1. Roast at 220°C for 20 minutes (high heat to brown the surface).
2. Reduce to 180°C; continue roasting for about 1 hour for medium (the meat thermometer should read 60°C in the thickest part for medium-pink; 55°C for rare; 65°C for well-done).
3. Total cooking time: about 18-20 minutes per 500 g for medium.

### Stage 4 - Roast potatoes and kūmara (parallel)
1. While the lamb roasts (about 45 minutes in), bring a pot of salted water to a boil.
2. Add the potatoes; boil 8 minutes; add the kūmara; boil a further 5 minutes; drain.
3. Shake the colander to rough up the edges.
4. Tip onto a separate roasting tray; toss with the oil/duck fat and salt.
5. Roast at 180°C for 40-45 minutes (alongside the lamb), turning once, until deeply golden and crisp on the outside, soft inside.

### Stage 5 - Mint sauce
1. Finely chop the mint leaves.
2. Combine in a small bowl with the boiling water, sugar, vinegar and salt.
3. Stir to dissolve; rest 15 minutes; the flavours marry.

### Stage 6 - Rest the lamb
1. When the lamb is done, lift to a board; cover loosely with foil and a tea towel.
2. Rest 20 minutes (essential - this is where the juices redistribute).
3. Keep the roasting tin with juices on the side for the gravy.

### Stage 7 - Gravy
1. Pour off most of the fat from the roasting tin, leaving the dark juices and the vegetables.
2. Place the tin over medium heat on the hob (over 2 burners if it's large).
3. Sprinkle in the flour; stir into a paste with the juices and fat residue.
4. Cook 1 minute, scraping the brown bits off the bottom.
5. Pour in the stock gradually, stirring continuously, until smooth.
6. Bring to a simmer; cook 5 minutes until thickened.
7. Strain through a sieve into a gravy boat, pressing the vegetables to extract their juices; discard the spent vegetables.
8. Taste for salt and pepper.

### Stage 8 - Carve and serve
1. Carve the lamb against the grain into 5 mm thick slices.
2. Plate with the roast potatoes, roast kūmara, mint sauce, gravy, and a green vegetable.
3. Save the bone for soup the next day.

## Notes
- **Lamb pinkness:** The 1950s "lamb cooked grey" tradition is over. Medium (60°C internal) gives juicy, pink meat. Use a meat thermometer; visual estimates lie.
- **Rest properly:** 20 minutes under foil. The juices need time to settle back into the muscle fibres; cut too soon and they all run out onto the board.
- **Kūmara is sweet potato but better:** New Zealand kūmara is denser and less sweet than American sweet potato, with a faintly nutty flavour. If you can't get it, orange sweet potato is the closest substitute.

## Serving
- The traditional New Zealand Sunday roast: lamb in the middle of the table, potatoes and kūmara in serving bowls, gravy in a jug, mint sauce in a small bowl. A green vegetable. A red wine (Hawke's Bay Syrah, Marlborough Pinot Noir). Eat in good company.

## Storage
- Leftover lamb refrigerates 4 days; eat cold in sandwiches with mint sauce, or reheat gently in gravy.
- Leftover bone makes lamb stock or barley soup the next day.
- Gravy refrigerates 3 days; reheats well; freezes 2 months.
