# Lithuanian

*The kitchen of potato and pork. Zeppelin-shaped cepelinai (the national dumpling), cold beetroot soup served bright pink in summer, kugelis (potato pudding) on the Sunday table, and a forager's love of mushrooms.*

## A short history

Lithuanian cooking is the food of a Baltic country with deep country-agricultural roots and a strong forest-foraging habit. The Balts who settled the eastern shore of the Baltic Sea in the second millennium BCE were the last pagans in Europe (Lithuania did not formally Christianise until 1387), and their pre-Christian shelf shows through in the cooking even today: rye and barley porridges, fermented oat-and-rye kissel, mead, the heavy use of poppy seed at midwinter, ceremonial breads baked for the solstices. The forests of Aukštaitija and Dzūkija are world-class mushroom and berry grounds, and the foraging habit (boletus, chanterelle, cranberry, lingonberry, juniper) goes straight back to the Iron Age.

The medieval Grand Duchy of Lithuania, which at its 15th-century peak ran from the Baltic to the Black Sea, brought the cooking into contact with Belarusian, Ukrainian, Polish and Tatar traditions. The Karaite Jews invited from Crimea to Trakai by Vytautas the Great in 1397 brought kibinai (their crescent-shaped meat pastries, still made in Trakai today), and other Tatar communities left a koldūnai dumpling shelf. The 16th-century Union of Lublin merged Lithuania with Poland into the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, and the kitchen took on a shared accent: pierogi/koldūnai overlap, sauerkraut traditions, the rich pork-and-cabbage farmhouse cooking that runs through both countries.

The Columbian Exchange transformed everything in the 18th century. Potato arrived through Prussia and Poland and within fifty years had completely displaced turnip as the working starch. Lithuania took to the potato with more enthusiasm than almost any other country in Europe: by the 19th century, potato featured at every meal, in every form, and the modern Lithuanian potato canon (cepelinai, kugelis, vėdarai, žemaitiškas blynas, potato cheese, potato sausage) was largely established. Maize and tomato arrived more slowly and never reached the same dominance, but did slot into the side-dish shelf.

Tsarist Russian rule from 1795 to 1918 brought the samovar, tea-drinking and certain Russian dishes (selyodka pickled herring, the kvass habit, the cabbage-soup tradition). The first Lithuanian independence (1918 to 1940) saw the first national codification of the cuisine under cookbook authors like Liudvika Didžiulienė-Žmona. The Lithuanian-Jewish community, around 200,000 strong before the war and one of the great centres of Yiddish culture in eastern Europe, gave the wider cuisine kugel (the baked-grain or potato pudding that became Lithuanian kugelis), bagels, herring traditions and the slow-cooked Sabbath bean-and-potato stew that influenced the cholent-cepelinai overlap.

The Holocaust eliminated almost the entire Lithuanian-Jewish population. The Soviet annexation that followed in 1940 to 1990 pushed potato to the absolute centre of the diet; cepelinai became the national dish in those years, partly because pork and potato were the two ingredients most reliably available. Today Lithuanian food is in a quiet renaissance. Diaspora kitchens in Chicago (the largest Lithuanian diaspora population in the world) and London preserve the tradition; the home country's restaurant scene is rediscovering pre-Soviet recipes alongside the modern Baltic foraging movement led by chefs at Sweet Root and Nineteen18 in Vilnius. The kitchen reads as it has for two centuries: potato in every form, rye bread alongside, pork and dairy, a heavy hand of dill, and a foraged mushroom or berry to finish.

## Regional variations

- **Vilnius and the south-east (Aukštaitija)**: the codified mainstream. Cepelinai, kibinai (Karaite pastries from Trakai).
- **Žemaitija (west)**: heavier dairy, more cured pork. The Žemaitiškas blynas (potato pancake) is the regional take.
- **Suvalkija (south)**: closer to Polish cooking. More pierogi-style dumplings.
- **Coast (Klaipėda)**: smoked fish, eel, the German-Prussian historical layer.

## Signature ingredients

- **Potatoes**: the spine. Grated, mashed, pancaked, dumplinged, baked into kugelis.
- **Pork**: cured (rūkyti), fresh, ground. Pork-cracklings (spirgučiai) end up on everything.
- **Sour cream (grietinė)**: dressing potato dishes, cold soups, dumplings.
- **Mushrooms (grybai)**: dried wild varieties from the forests. Stuffed in koldūnai, in sauces.
- **Rye bread (juoda duona)**: dense, sour-leavened.
- **Dill and caraway**: the herb pair.
- **Beetroot**: in shaltibarsciai (cold pink summer soup) and barszczas.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Cepelinai](/recipes/cuisine/lithuanian/cepelinai)**: large potato dumplings (the name means "zeppelins") filled with spiced minced pork, boiled, served with pork-cracklings and sour cream. The national dish.
2. **[Šaltibarščiai](/recipes/cuisine/lithuanian/saltibarsciai)**: cold beetroot soup with kefir, cucumber, dill and a hard-boiled egg, served with hot boiled potatoes on the side. The summer Sunday lunch, bright pink and properly chilled.
3. **[Kugelis](/recipes/cuisine/lithuanian/kugelis)**: baked grated-potato pudding with bacon and onion, served with sour cream and apple sauce. The Sunday side.
4. **[Kepta Duona](/recipes/cuisine/lithuanian/kepta-duona)**: fried rye-bread sticks with garlic, served with cheese sauce. The classic pub starter.
5. **[Vėdarai](/recipes/cuisine/lithuanian/vedarai)**: potato sausage (mashed potato stuffed into pork intestine), baked till crisp. The harvest-festival dish.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with starka (rye-grain spirit) or with gira (a fermented rye-bread drink, sweet-sour). For Christmas Eve (Kūčios), twelve courses of meatless food including kūčiukai (small wheat biscuits soaked in poppyseed milk).
