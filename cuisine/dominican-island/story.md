# Dominican (Commonwealth of Dominica)

*The kitchen of the Caribbean's nature island. The "mountain chicken" (a giant frog, the national protein — now critically endangered), crab back, callaloo soup, and the Creole-French-British synthesis on a small mountainous island between Martinique and Guadeloupe.*

## A short history

The Commonwealth of Dominica (not to be confused with the Dominican Republic — separate country, separate cuisine folder) is a small mountainous English-speaking Caribbean island that ping-ponged between French and British colonial control for two centuries before British rule was confirmed in 1763 and independence followed in 1978. The cuisine reflects both — French-Creole patois is widely spoken alongside English; Creole stew traditions sit alongside British colonial roasts.

The island's nickname is "the Nature Island of the Caribbean" — mountainous rainforest, hot springs, the second-largest boiling lake in the world. The cuisine draws on this — wild herbs from the forest, freshwater shrimp from the rivers, the famous "mountain chicken" (Leptodactylus fallax, a giant frog endemic to Dominica that was historically a delicacy, now critically endangered and largely protected from harvest).

The cuisine is closely related to neighbouring Saint Lucian, Martiniquais and Guadeloupean traditions — green fig (unripe banana), callaloo, the use of bay leaf and thyme, the Creole one-pot tradition.

## Regional variations

The island is small and the cuisine is broadly consistent. Sub-traditions are by community:

- **Roseau (capital, west)**: codified mainstream.
- **Carib Territory (Kalinago Reserve, east)**: indigenous Kalinago cooking. Cassava bread, the traditional fish-and-cassava plate.
- **Coastal villages**: more fish, conch, crab.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cassava (bread and starch)**: the Kalinago staple.
- **Green fig (unripe banana)**: in stews.
- **Callaloo**: dasheen leaves, in soup.
- **Crab (land crab)**: in crab back.
- **Mountain chicken (frog legs)**: historically; mostly off-limits now.
- **Bay leaf and thyme**: the herb pair.
- **Coconut milk**: in some dishes.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Mountain Chicken Style](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/mountain-chicken-style)**: frog legs (historically) or chicken (the modern conservation-friendly substitute) prepared in the traditional mountain-chicken seasoning — herbs, garlic, Scotch bonnet, lime.
2. **[Crab Back](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/crab-back)**: land crab meat seasoned with herbs, bread crumbs and butter, stuffed back into the cleaned shell and baked.
3. **[Callaloo (Dominica)](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/callaloo-dominica)**: dasheen leaves cooked into a thick stew with okra, salt meat or crab, coconut milk and Scotch bonnet.
4. **[Sancoche (Dominica)](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/sancoche-dominica)**: thick stew with provisions (yam, cassava, sweet potato, dasheen), salt meat, dumplings and coconut milk. The Sunday family lunch.
5. **[Pelau (Dominica)](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/pelau-dominica)**: rice cooked with brown-sugar-caramelised chicken, pigeon peas, coconut milk. The everyday Caribbean rice classic, Dominican variant.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Kubuli (the national lager) or with bois bandé (the tree-bark-infused herbal drink, marketed locally as an aphrodisiac). End with passion fruit or guava — Dominica's wild fruit is exceptional.
