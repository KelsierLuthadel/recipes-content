# Dominican (Commonwealth of Dominica)

*The kitchen of the Caribbean's nature island. The "mountain chicken" (a giant frog, the national protein, now critically endangered), crab back, callaloo soup, and the Creole-French-British synthesis on a small mountainous island between Martinique and Guadeloupe.*

## A short history

The Commonwealth of Dominica (not to be confused with the Dominican Republic; separate country, separate cuisine folder) is a small mountainous English-speaking Caribbean island whose food story begins with the Kalinago. These Carib-speaking people arrived from the South American mainland around 1400 CE, displacing the earlier Arawak settlers, and they brought the techniques that still anchor the rural kitchen: cassava grated and pressed dry then baked on a flat clay griddle into farine and cassava bread, fish smoked over green wood in palm-frond shelters, the pepperpot stew kept alive on the hearth for weeks. The Kalinago Territory on the east coast is the only surviving pre-Columbian indigenous reserve in the Caribbean, and the cassava-and-fish plate cooked there today reads almost exactly as Columbus's chroniclers described it in 1493.

Columbus sighted the island on a Sunday in 1493 and named it Dominica. The Spanish never settled it (the Kalinago resistance was fierce, the terrain impossible) and for two centuries the island was a refuge for runaways, pirates and escaped slaves from the surrounding sugar islands. Africans escaping from neighbouring colonies brought yam, dasheen, okra, plantain, the ground-provisions tradition and the slow stew. The French claimed the island formally in 1635 and ran sugar and coffee plantations with enslaved African labour; British control was confirmed in 1763 after the Seven Years War, and English took over from French as the official language. The patois (Kweyol) survived, the African-Creole one-pot survived, and the kitchen kept layering.

The 19th century brought emancipation in 1834 and a slow reorientation away from sugar towards small-holder farming on the mountainous interior. The island's nickname is "the Nature Island of the Caribbean": rainforest from the coast to the volcanic peaks, hot springs, the second-largest boiling lake in the world. Smallholders grew dasheen, yam, plantain, breadfruit (the Bligh expedition brought it to the West Indies in 1793) and citrus, kept goats and pigs, and fished the rivers for freshwater shrimp and crayfish. The "mountain chicken" (Leptodactylus fallax, a giant frog endemic to Dominica that was historically a delicacy, now critically endangered and largely protected from harvest) became the celebration plate.

Independence in 1978 brought a national kitchen identity into clearer focus. The cuisine sits close to the neighbouring Saint Lucian, Martiniquais and Guadeloupean traditions, which makes sense given the island sits between Martinique and Guadeloupe and the patois is mutually intelligible across all three. Green fig (unripe banana), callaloo, bay leaf and thyme, the Creole one-pot. Today's Dominica kitchen leans hard on what the rainforest and the rivers still give freely: wild herbs, freshwater shrimp, plantain, dasheen and the rich black soil that grows almost anything you push into it.

## Regional variations

The island is small and the cuisine is broadly consistent. Sub-traditions are by community:

- **Roseau (capital, west)**: codified mainstream.
- **Carib Territory (Kalinago Reserve, east)**: indigenous Kalinago cooking. Cassava bread, the traditional fish-and-cassava plate.
- **Coastal villages**: more fish, conch, crab.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cassava (bread and starch)**: the Kalinago staple.
- **Green fig (unripe banana)**: in stews.
- **Callaloo**: dasheen leaves, in soup.
- **Crab (land crab)**: in crab back.
- **Mountain chicken (frog legs)**: historically; mostly off-limits now.
- **Bay leaf and thyme**: the herb pair.
- **Coconut milk**: in some dishes.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Mountain Chicken Style](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/mountain-chicken-style)**: frog legs (historically) or chicken (the modern conservation-friendly substitute) prepared in the traditional mountain-chicken seasoning: herbs, garlic, Scotch bonnet, lime.
2. **[Crab Back](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/crab-back)**: land crab meat seasoned with herbs, bread crumbs and butter, stuffed back into the cleaned shell and baked.
3. **[Callaloo (Dominica)](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/callaloo-dominica)**: dasheen leaves cooked into a thick stew with okra, salt meat or crab, coconut milk and Scotch bonnet.
4. **[Sancoche (Dominica)](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/sancoche-dominica)**: thick stew with provisions (yam, cassava, sweet potato, dasheen), salt meat, dumplings and coconut milk. The Sunday family lunch.
5. **[Pelau (Dominica)](/recipes/cuisine/dominican-island/pelau-dominica)**: rice cooked with brown-sugar-caramelised chicken, pigeon peas, coconut milk. The everyday Caribbean rice classic, Dominican variant.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Kubuli (the national lager) or with bois bandé (the tree-bark-infused herbal drink, marketed locally as an aphrodisiac). End with passion fruit or guava. Dominica's wild fruit is exceptional.
