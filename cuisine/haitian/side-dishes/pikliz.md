# Pikliz

![Pikliz](resources/pikliz.jpg)

*Haiti's pickled cabbage relish: shredded cabbage, carrot, bell pepper, onion and Scotch bonnet salted overnight, then drowned in vinegar with lime, peppercorns and cloves. The fierce sharp counter that appears on every Haitian table.*

**Serves:** Makes 1 large jar (about 1 litre)

**Prep Time:** 25 minutes (plus overnight pickling)

**Cook Time:** 0 minutes

## Overview
Pikliz is Haiti's table relish, the fierce sharp pickled cabbage condiment that appears alongside almost every Haitian protein and starch dish from griot to legume to rice and beans: shredded green cabbage, julienned carrot, sliced bell pepper, sliced red onion and (crucially) chopped or whole Scotch bonnet chillies, salted briefly to draw moisture, then drowned in white vinegar with lime juice, peppercorns and cloves. The mixture sits at room temperature for at least 24 hours (the proper Haitian household keeps a jar going at all times, topping up the vinegar as the pikliz gets eaten down), turning from raw cabbage into a properly tangy fierce-hot condiment that cuts through the richness of any Haitian meal. Two things make the pikliz proper. First, Scotch bonnet chillies; jalapeño or other mild chillies don't give the right flavour or heat. A standard jar uses 3-4 Scotch bonnets sliced through, seeds and all; for serious eaters, even more. Second, white vinegar (not cider, not malt; the clean sharp character of plain white distilled vinegar is what gives pikliz its signature bite). Some recipes call for adding a small amount of bitter orange juice (jus orange amer) or sour orange juice; in classic Haitian pikliz this adds the floral citrus note that distinguishes the recipe from generic spicy slaw. The technique is short. Finely shred the vegetables (a mandoline gives the best uniform thickness, but a sharp knife works), salt and rest 30 minutes to draw moisture, drain briefly, pack into a jar with the spices and chillies, cover with vinegar and lime, seal and rest at room temperature for 24-48 hours. After that the pikliz goes into the fridge where it keeps for weeks; the flavour deepens and the heat intensifies over the first week, then stabilises.

## Ingredients

### Vegetables
- 600 g green cabbage (very finely shredded; about ½ a small head)
- 2 medium carrots (peeled and julienned or coarsely grated)
- 1 small red onion (very finely sliced)
- 1 red bell pepper (deseeded and finely sliced)
- 4 spring onions (white and pale green parts; finely sliced)
- 4 garlic cloves (very finely sliced)
- 1 piece (3 cm) fresh ginger (peeled and finely sliced into thin sticks)

### Heat
- 3-5 fresh Scotch bonnet chillies (stems removed; sliced through or chopped, depending on preference); use fewer for milder, more for serious heat

### Salting
- 1 tablespoon fine sea salt

### Brine
- 750 ml white distilled vinegar (or white wine vinegar at a push; never cider or malt)
- 2 tablespoons fresh lime juice
- 2 tablespoons sour orange juice (or bitter orange marmalade dissolved in hot water; optional but traditional)
- 1 teaspoon black peppercorns (whole)
- 6 whole cloves
- 1 teaspoon dried thyme (or 4 fresh thyme sprigs)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the vegetables
1. Use a mandoline or a sharp knife to shred the cabbage into very fine ribbons (1-2 mm wide). The shredding fineness matters; chunky cabbage makes a less flavourful pikliz because the surface area for the brine to penetrate is smaller.
2. Peel and julienne the carrots, or grate coarsely on a box grater.
3. Slice the onion as thin as you can.
4. Slice the bell pepper and spring onions thinly.
5. Slice the garlic cloves into thin slivers.
6. Cut the ginger into thin sticks (julienne).
7. Slice or chop the Scotch bonnets. Use gloves; the capsaicin from Scotch bonnet stings for hours on bare hands and you don't want to rub your eyes after handling them.

### Stage 2 - Salt and draw moisture
1. Tip all the shredded vegetables into a wide bowl.
2. Sprinkle the salt over the top.
3. Toss thoroughly with your hands (still wearing gloves if the chillies are in there) to distribute the salt evenly.
4. Let stand at room temperature for 30 minutes. The salt draws moisture from the vegetables; you'll see liquid collecting in the bottom of the bowl.

### Stage 3 - Drain (don't rinse)
1. Tip the vegetables into a colander.
2. Press lightly with the back of a spoon to drain off the excess liquid. Don't rinse; you want some salt remaining for flavour.
3. Don't dry the vegetables either; some moisture is fine.

### Stage 4 - Pack and brine
1. Pack the vegetables into a clean 1-litre jar (or a wide non-reactive container), pressing down to compact the mixture. The jar should be full but not crammed; there should be space for the brine.
2. Tuck the Scotch bonnets, peppercorns, cloves and thyme down into the vegetables as you pack.
3. Pour the vinegar over the packed vegetables till they're completely submerged.
4. Pour in the lime juice and sour orange juice (if using).
5. Top up with more vinegar if needed so the vegetables are fully covered by the brine.
6. Seal the jar tightly.

### Stage 5 - Ferment at room temperature
1. Leave the jar at cool room temperature for 24-48 hours. The vegetables will absorb the brine and start to soften slightly; the brine takes on a yellow-pink colour from the chillies and bell pepper.

### Stage 6 - Refrigerate and use
1. After 24-48 hours, transfer the jar to the refrigerator.
2. The pikliz is ready to use after the initial pickle but improves significantly over the first week as the flavours and heat deepen.
3. Use a clean spoon every time you take some out (don't let any food cross-contaminate the jar; that shortens the shelf life).

## Notes
- **Scotch bonnet is the chilli:** the fruity-floral heat of Scotch bonnet is what defines Haitian heat. Jalapeño tastes vegetal and the heat profile is wrong; habanero is the closest substitute if you genuinely can't find Scotch bonnet (almost identical heat and flavour but slightly less aromatic). 3-4 chillies is a moderate-spicy pikliz; for the proper Haitian table version, 5-6.
- **Gloves when handling Scotch bonnet:** the oil from Scotch bonnet stings hands for hours and is genuinely painful if you rub your eyes afterwards. Wear thin disposable gloves while slicing, and wash hands with soap (and a little vegetable oil) after.
- **White distilled vinegar specifically:** the clean sharp acidity of white vinegar is what makes pikliz proper. Cider vinegar adds a fruity note that's wrong. Malt vinegar is far too dark and earthy. White wine vinegar works in a pinch but is more expensive and slightly less sharp.
- **Salt-and-drain not salt-and-rinse:** the salt draws moisture out, but you want to keep some seasoning. Drain off the excess liquid but don't rinse the vegetables (which would wash off the flavouring salt).
- **24-48 hours at room temperature:** the room-temperature rest is what kicks off the proper pickling fermentation. Going straight to the fridge gives you a vinegared raw slaw rather than proper pikliz; the 24 hours warm pickles the cabbage and develops the flavour.
- **Keeps for weeks in the fridge:** properly made pikliz keeps 3-4 weeks refrigerated, getting steadily hotter and more flavourful. The traditional household keeps a jar always going and tops up the brine as it gets eaten.

## Variations
**Pikliz nan zoranj (with bitter orange):** double the sour orange juice for a more pronounced citrus character; common in the Cap-Haïtien region.
**Pikliz with cauliflower:** add 200 g of small cauliflower florets blanched 1 minute in salted water and refreshed in cold water; an old-style variant.
**Sweet pikliz:** add 2 tablespoons of cane sugar to the brine for a sweet-sour pickled finish; less traditional but works for those who find the standard pikliz too sharp.
**Pikliz vert (with green vegetables only):** swap red bell pepper for green, and use only green cabbage and pale carrots; gives the pikliz a cooler colour palette, more refined-looking.

## Serving
A spoonful or two on the side of any plate of Haitian food: griot, tasso, poulet créole, legume, rice and beans. The sharp pickled vegetables cut through the rich slow-cooked proteins and the carb-heavy starches. Pikliz on a fried-fish sandwich (poisson en sauce) is another classic. The brine is also a powerful marinade for chicken or fish; use the liquid after the vegetables are eaten.

## Storage
- Keeps 3-4 weeks refrigerated in a sealed jar.
- The vegetables soften and the brine deepens over time; pikliz at 1 week is at peak heat, at 2 weeks it's mellowed slightly, at 3 weeks the vegetables have lost their crunch but the brine itself is excellent.
- Don't freeze; the cabbage texture goes to mush on thawing.
- Top up with fresh vinegar if the brine evaporates and the vegetables get exposed.
