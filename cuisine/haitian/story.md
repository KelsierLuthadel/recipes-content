# Haitian

*The kitchen of the Caribbean's first independent Black republic. Pikliz (the fierce cabbage-and-chilli pickle on every table), griot (fried marinated pork shoulder), the pumpkin soup (soup joumou) that became a national symbol of liberation.*

## A short history

Haitian cooking sits on a base of Taíno indigenous foods (cassava, sweet potato, corn, beans, the use of allspice), Spanish (1492–1697) and French (1697–1804) colonisation, and the dominant West African influence brought by the enslaved African population who became Haiti's majority. The 1804 Haitian Revolution made Haiti the first independent Black republic in the world and the second independent nation in the Americas after the US.

Soup joumou (giblet-and-pumpkin soup) is the national symbol. Under the French colonial system, enslaved Haitians were forbidden to eat pumpkin soup — it was reserved for the white slaveholders. On 1 January 1804, when Haiti declared independence, Haitian women cooked soup joumou and the country ate it for the first time. Every Haitian New Year since then, the country eats soup joumou. In 2021, UNESCO inscribed it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list.

The diaspora kitchens (Miami's Little Haiti, New York, Montreal, Paris) preserve the codified tradition. Internally the food remains close to its 19th-century shape.

## Regional variations

- **Port-au-Prince and the south**: codified mainstream. Griot, diri ak djon-djon (rice with black mushrooms).
- **Cap-Haïtien (north)**: more French-Creole influence in some dishes.
- **Artibonite (rice valley, central)**: more rice-and-bean tradition.
- **Diaspora (Miami, Boston, Brooklyn)**: vibrant Haitian-restaurant scenes carrying the tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Epis**: green Haitian seasoning paste (parsley, scallion, garlic, thyme, peppers, lime). The marinade base.
- **Pikliz**: spicy cabbage-and-Scotch-bonnet pickle, on every plate.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.
- **Djon-djon mushrooms**: black wild mushrooms unique to Haiti.
- **Cassava (manyok)**: in flour, in bread.
- **Plantain**: green for bannann peze (twice-fried), ripe for sweet sides.
- **Marinated pork shoulder**: for griot.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Soup Joumou](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/soup-joumou)**: pumpkin (giblet squash), beef, root vegetables, cabbage, pasta, lime and epis. The 1 January New Year freedom soup.
2. **[Griot](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/griot)**: pork shoulder marinated in epis-and-citrus, slow-braised then deep-fried till the outside crisps. The Sunday celebration plate, served with pikliz.
3. **[Diri ak Djon-Djon](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/diri-ak-djon-djon)**: black rice cooked with djon-djon mushroom stock. Often served with grilled meat or fish. The Cap-Haïtien speciality.
4. **[Poul ak Nwa](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/poul-ak-nwa)**: chicken in a cashew-based sauce with epis and Scotch bonnet.
5. **[Poulet Creole](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/poulet-creole)**: chicken stewed in epis, tomato, onion and pepper sauce.
6. **[Legume](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/legume)**: thick vegetable stew with eggplant, cabbage, watercress, beef and Scotch bonnet.
7. **[Tasso](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/tasso)**: dried goat marinated in epis, citrus and bitter orange, fried crisp.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Prestige beer (the national lager) or with Barbancourt rum (the rhum agricole made from sugar-cane juice rather than molasses — Haiti's national spirit). Pikliz is on every table; expect it on every plate.
