# Haitian

*The kitchen of the Caribbean's first independent Black republic. Pikliz (the fierce cabbage-and-chilli pickle on every table), griot (fried marinated pork shoulder), the pumpkin soup (soup joumou) that became a national symbol of liberation.*

## A short history

Haitian cooking begins with the Taíno, the Arawakan-speaking people who farmed Hispaniola long before Columbus stepped onto its shore in December 1492 and called the island Española. The Taíno gave the world a long list of words that English still uses (barbecue, hammock, canoe, hurricane, tobacco) and the Haitian pantry an even longer list of ingredients that still anchor the kitchen: cassava (manyok), sweet potato, corn, peanuts, beans, allspice, the chilli pepper, papaya, guava, the cooking method of slow-roasting meat over green wood (the original barbacoa). They grated bitter cassava into flour, pressed out the toxic juice, and baked it on a flat clay disc into the cassava bread that's still made on the Haitian-Dominican border today. Spanish colonisation from 1492 wiped out most of the Taíno population through disease and forced labour within a single generation, but their ingredients and techniques survived in the kitchen that grew up around them.

Spanish Hispaniola (1492-1697) brought cattle, pigs, sugar cane, citrus and the Iberian one-pot stew. The Spanish lost the western third of the island to France in 1697 under the Treaty of Ryswick, and French Saint-Domingue became the most profitable colony in the Americas, producing more sugar and coffee than anywhere else in the world. The French built the plantation economy on the backs of enslaved Africans, mostly from West Africa (Fon, Yoruba, Akan, Igbo) and Central Africa (Kongo, Mbundu). By 1789 over half a million enslaved people lived in Saint-Domingue, vastly outnumbering the European and free-coloured populations. The Haitian kitchen we know today is overwhelmingly West and Central African in its bones: the rice and beans, the okra stew, the slow-fried marinated meat, the use of leafy greens, the rhythm of pounding in the mortar, the layering of allspice and citrus and chilli into long marinades.

The Haitian Revolution (1791-1804) led by Toussaint Louverture and then Jean-Jacques Dessalines made Haiti the first independent Black republic in the world and the second independent nation in the Americas after the US, the only country ever founded by a successful slave revolt. Soup joumou (giblet-and-pumpkin soup) is the food memorial of that revolution. Under the French, enslaved Haitians were barred from eating pumpkin soup; the slaveholders reserved it for themselves. On 1 January 1804, the day Haiti declared independence, Haitian women cooked soup joumou and the whole country ate it for the first time. Every Haitian New Year since, the country eats it again. UNESCO put it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2021.

The 19th century was hard. France demanded a 150-million-franc "indemnity" for lost property (including the people it had enslaved) and Haiti spent decades paying it off, draining the new republic of capital before it could industrialise. The home kitchen consolidated around what the land and sea would give without much investment: rice, beans, plantain, root vegetables, pork, goat, chicken, fish from the coast. The Levantine traders who arrived from Lebanon and Syria in the late 1800s opened bakeries and dry-goods stores and brought hummus, kibbeh and pita-style flatbreads into the urban shelf. Mid-century US occupation (1915-1934) added a thin layer of American military rations but didn't displace the home food.

The 20th century carried Haitian food through dictatorships (the Duvalier years, 1957-1986), boat-migration crises and earthquakes. The diaspora kitchens in Miami's Little Haiti, New York's Brooklyn, Montreal and Paris keep the tradition codified, and Haitian-American chefs in the 2010s and 2020s have put griot and pikliz onto upscale menus across North America. Back home, the food has stayed close to its 19th-century shape. The Twi, Ga, Ewe and Dagomba ethnic kitchens each contribute their own dishes to the broader Ghanaian table; in Haiti, the Fon and Yoruba and Kongo lineages each shaped what's on the family table. Epis (the green herb-and-pepper paste) marinates every meat. Pikliz sits on every table. Rice and beans (diri ak pwa) is the everyday lunch.

## Regional variations

- **Port-au-Prince and the south**: the mainstream. Griot, diri ak djon-djon (rice with black mushrooms).
- **Cap-Haïtien (north)**: more French-Creole influence in some dishes.
- **Artibonite (rice valley, central)**: more rice-and-bean tradition.
- **Diaspora (Miami, Boston, Brooklyn)**: vibrant Haitian-restaurant scenes carrying the tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Epis**: green Haitian seasoning paste (parsley, scallion, garlic, thyme, peppers, lime). The marinade base.
- **Pikliz**: spicy cabbage-and-Scotch-bonnet pickle, on every plate.
- **Scotch bonnet**: the heat.
- **Djon-djon mushrooms**: black wild mushrooms unique to Haiti.
- **Cassava (manyok)**: in flour, in bread.
- **Plantain**: green for bannann peze (twice-fried), ripe for sweet sides.
- **Marinated pork shoulder**: for griot.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Soup Joumou](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/soup-joumou)**: pumpkin (giblet squash), beef, root vegetables, cabbage, pasta, lime and epis. The 1 January New Year freedom soup.
2. **[Griot](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/griot)**: pork shoulder marinated in epis and citrus, slow-braised then deep-fried until the outside crisps. The Sunday celebration plate, served with pikliz.
3. **[Diri ak Djon-Djon](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/diri-ak-djon-djon)**: black rice cooked with djon-djon mushroom stock. Eat with grilled meat or fish. The Cap-Haïtien speciality.
4. **[Poul ak Nwa](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/poul-ak-nwa)**: chicken in a cashew-based sauce with epis and Scotch bonnet.
5. **[Poulet Creole](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/poulet-creole)**: chicken stewed in epis, tomato, onion and pepper sauce.
6. **[Legume](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/legume)**: thick vegetable stew with eggplant, cabbage, watercress, beef and Scotch bonnet.
7. **[Tasso](/recipes/cuisine/haitian/tasso)**: dried goat marinated in epis, citrus and bitter orange, fried crisp.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Prestige beer (the national lager) or Barbancourt rum, the rhum agricole made from sugar-cane juice rather than molasses and Haiti's national spirit. Pikliz sits on every table; expect it on every plate.
