# Israeli

*The pan-Middle-Eastern cuisine of a young country. Mizrahi (Iraqi, Yemeni, Persian), Sephardic (Spanish and North African Jewish), Ashkenazi (Eastern European Jewish), all layered onto a deep Levantine Arab foundation of hummus, pita and herbs.*

## A short history

Israeli cuisine, in its current shape, is barely older than the state itself. When the country was founded in 1948, waves of Jewish immigrants arrived from radically different food cultures, and each wave brought a kitchen.

From Iraq came rice as a daily starch, the long-marinated kebab, and the fried-egg-and-aubergine sandwich now known as sabich. From Yemen came jachnun, a slow-baked Sabbath bread that cooks overnight, the buttery pull-apart loaf called kubaneh, and the fierce green chilli-and-coriander hot sauce schug (or zhug) that ended up on every table in the country. From Persia came rice cooked with a deliberate golden crust at the bottom of the pot, the tahdig, and a tradition of herb-thick stews. From Morocco and across North Africa came tagines, couscous, preserved lemon, and the chilli paste harissa. From Eastern Europe came chicken soup with kreplach, brisket, schnitzel borrowed via Austria, the braided egg loaf challah, and potato latkes.

All of this settled onto a much older Levantine and Palestinian foundation that was already there: hummus, falafel, mezze tables of small plates, pita baked daily, the spice-and-herb blend za'atar, the tart red sumac dust, the cool yoghurt cheese labneh. Out of the overlap came the institution of the Israeli breakfast (eggs, salad, cheese, bread, olives, all at once) and, in Tel Aviv especially, a modern restaurant culture that cheerfully mixes everything. The anchor principle is straightforward: an immigrant cuisine still working itself out, with chickpeas, olive oil, lemon, tahini, fresh herbs and the open grill as the shared backbone.

## Regional variations

Less about geography than about which community is cooking. The country is small. The kitchens inside it are not.

- **Mizrahi (Middle-Eastern Jewish)**: rice-heavy, long slow stews, the warm-spice blend baharat, herb-stuffed vegetables, charcoal-grilled kebab.
- **Sephardic (Spanish and Maghrebi roots)**: tagines, couscous, preserved lemon, North African chillies, fish on Friday for Shabbat.
- **Ashkenazi (Eastern European)**: chicken soup with matzo balls or kreplach, brisket, schnitzel, challah, latkes, kugel.
- **Yemeni**: jachnun (the overnight Sabbath bread), kubaneh, the green chilli sauce schug. Spice-forward and bread-led.
- **Palestinian and wider Levantine Arab**: hummus, falafel, the open flatbread manakish, the lamb-and-yoghurt feast mansaf, the cheese pastry knafeh. The oldest layer of the table.
- **Modern Tel Aviv**: a cross-everything new-Israeli dining scene that pulls from all of the above and from European fine-dining technique.

A hummus shop in Jaffa and a knafeh stall in Nazareth and a Yemeni breakfast cafe in south Tel Aviv and an Ashkenazi grandmother's chicken soup are all, at the same time, this cuisine.

## Signature ingredients

- **Chickpeas**: the base of hummus, falafel and a dozen stews. Cooked from dried for any serious result.
- **Tahini**: sesame paste loosened with lemon, garlic and water into a sauce that goes on almost everything.
- **Olive oil**: the everyday fat. Good oil, used generously.
- **Lemon**: a finishing acid on hummus, on grilled fish, on salads, on tahini.
- **[Za'atar](../../base-ingredients/spices/za-atar.md)**: the wild-thyme, sumac and sesame blend, scattered on labneh, oil-and-pita, eggs, roast vegetables.
- **Sumac**: dried ground berry, tart and red, dusted over onions, salads and grilled meat.
- **[Baharat](../../base-ingredients/spices/baharat.md)**: the warm all-spice blend (cumin, coriander, black pepper, cinnamon, cloves) used through Mizrahi cooking.
- **Schug (Yemeni green hot sauce)**: coriander, parsley, green chilli, garlic, cardamom, olive oil. Fierce and fresh.
- **Pomegranate**: seeds for crunch and sparkle, molasses for sour-sweet depth.
- **Parsley, mint, coriander**: the herb wall. Used by the bunch, not the sprig.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. [Hummus](side-dishes/hummus.md). Start where every Israeli meal starts. Cooked chickpeas, raw tahini, lemon, garlic, ice water, blended long and patiently. Teaches the chickpea-and-tahini ratio and the trick of whipping with cold water to get the texture light, not pasty.
2. [Falafel](side-dishes/falafel.md). The other chickpea cornerstone. Soaked (never cooked) chickpeas, parsley, coriander, cumin, ground to a coarse paste, shaped, fried crisp. Teaches the soaked-not-cooked rule that separates real falafel from the wet, dense versions.
3. [Sabich](snacks/sabich.md). The Iraqi-Jewish pita sandwich that became Israeli street food. Fried aubergine, hard-boiled egg, hummus, tahini, salad, amba (pickled-mango sauce). Teaches how a sandwich becomes a meal when every layer is its own little dish.
4. [Chicken schnitzel](schnitzel.md). The Ashkenazi wave's most enduring contribution. Pounded thin, breaded, shallow-fried. Teaches the breading-stations method and why thin-and-fast beats thick-and-slow with poultry.
5. [Jerusalem mixed grill (me'orav yerushalmi)](me-orav-yerushalmi.md). The signature street-grill plate: chicken hearts, livers, spleen and small bits of lamb, hot-griddled with onion, cumin, turmeric, baharat, stuffed into pita. Teaches the high-heat plancha-style grill technique that defines Israeli street meat.
6. [Kibbeh](kibbeh.md). The Levantine bulgur-and-meat dumpling, common across the Mizrahi and Palestinian-Levantine kitchens. Teaches how bulgur and meat bind into a workable shell, and the patience needed to shape it properly.
7. [Malabi](desserts/malabi.md). The cool, milk-based set pudding scented with rose or orange-blossom water, topped with red syrup and chopped pistachio. Teaches the cornflour-set milk-pudding technique that runs through Persian, Turkish and Levantine sweets.

After those seven you have the chickpea-and-tahini base, the spice-and-herb wall, the grill technique, the Ashkenazi cutlet method, and one dessert that explains a whole family of others.
