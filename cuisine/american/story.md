# American

*Many cuisines under one flag. American food is what immigrants brought, what enslaved and indigenous cooks built, and what regions kept on cooking after the rest moved on. Read it regional, never national.*

## A short history

The foundation is indigenous, and it goes back at least nine thousand years. Corn, beans, squash, tomato, chillies, turkey, cranberry, pecan, sunflower, maple: every one of these is a New World plant or animal that Native American cooks worked with for thousands of years before any colonist arrived. The three-sisters planting (corn, beans and squash grown together) ran from southern Mexico up into the Iroquois Confederacy of the Great Lakes. Hominy (corn treated with lye to release its nutrients), succotash, cornbread, the smoke-drying of meat into pemmican, the clambake on the New England shore, the salmon-and-cedar plank of the Pacific Northwest, the desert tepary bean of the Sonoran Tohono O'odham. The bones of American cooking are native, and the Columbian Exchange that followed (Old World grains, livestock and dairy meeting New World plants) reshaped the entire planet's diet, not just America's.

Colonial English, Dutch and German settlers from the 17th century onwards layered the next stratum: pot roast, pies (savoury then sweet), pickling, sausage-making, rye bread, dairy. Enslaved West and Central African cooks, kidnapped from the Senegambia, Kongo and Bight of Benin coasts and forced onto plantations from the 1620s, brought the rest of the spine: okra, black-eyed peas, watermelon, sesame (benne), deep-frying technique, slow-smoked pork, gumbo's roux-and-onion base, and the rice-growing knowledge that built South Carolina's Lowcountry. Most of what the world now recognises as Southern food was built in plantation kitchens by Black hands, often working from memory of West African dishes against the new larder of the American South.

The 19th century opened the gates. Italian, Irish, Polish, Jewish, Scandinavian and Chinese arrivals each rewrote the menu in their adopted cities. Pizza (Lombardi's, New York, 1905), pastrami (Romanian Jewish butchers on the Lower East Side, 1880s), bagels (Polish Jewish bakers, same neighbourhood), kielbasa (Polish meatpackers in Chicago and Milwaukee), chop suey (Cantonese cooks in Gold Rush California), lutefisk (Norwegian and Swedish farmers across Minnesota and the Dakotas), the meatball sub, the Reuben. Neighbourhoods became cuisines: Little Italy, Chinatown, the Lower East Side delis, the Polish enclaves of Chicago, the German breweries of St Louis and Milwaukee. The transcontinental railroad (1869) moved beef from Texas to Chicago and Chicago to everywhere; the refrigerated rail car (1880s) made the New York steakhouse possible.

The 20th century kept going. Mexican and Central American cooks moved north and made Tex-Mex, Cal-Mex and a hundred regional hybrids, then Vietnamese, Korean, Thai, Indian, Ethiopian and Filipino arrivals built the next layer. The two world wars rationed sugar, meat and butter and embedded canned, frozen and convenience foods (Spam was invented in 1937 and became a Pacific Theater staple that's still a Hawaii icon). Fast food was invented here (White Castle in 1921, McDonald's in 1940, Taco Bell in 1962, Burger King in 1953), and so was the farm-to-table movement that pushed back against it, led by Alice Waters at Chez Panisse from 1971. The American supper club, the diner, the drive-in, the food truck, the strip-mall taqueria and the Michelin-starred tasting menu all coexist on the same map.

Contemporary American food is wider and more honest with itself than it has ever been. The 21st century brought a long-overdue reckoning with whose hands actually built the cuisine: Black-led restaurants and writers (Edna Lewis, Toni Tipton-Martin, Mashama Bailey, Michael Twitty) have pulled the credit back where it belongs, indigenous chefs (Sean Sherman, the Sioux Chef) are rebuilding pre-colonial menus, and Mexican-American cooks have moved from neighbourhood institution to James Beard centre stage. The anchor principle is this: there is no single American cuisine. There is an immigration history with regional dialects, and the cooking is most honest when it remembers that. A bagel is American because New York is American. A taco al pastor is American for the same reason Los Angeles is. Gumbo is American because Louisiana is, and Louisiana is American because African, French, Spanish and Choctaw cooks all left their hands in the pot.

## Regional variations

The country is too big and too varied for one menu. Every region keeps its own:

- **New England**: clam chowder, lobster roll, clam bake, Boston baked beans, maple syrup
- **Mid-Atlantic and NYC**: pastrami on rye, bagel and lox, pizza by the slice, Philly cheesesteak, soft pretzel
- **The South**: buttermilk fried chicken, biscuits and gravy, collards, pulled pork, grits, pecan pie
- **Southwest and Tex-Mex border**: chili con carne, fajitas, breakfast burrito, queso, brisket
- **California**: farm-to-table, avocado on everything, fish taco, sourdough, fusion sushi
- **Pacific Northwest**: wild salmon, oyster, dungeness crab, foraging culture, coffee
- **Midwest**: casseroles ("hotdish"), bratwurst, deep-dish pizza, sweet corn, supper-club steaks
- **Hawaii and the Pacific**: poke, kalua pig, Spam musubi, loco moco, shave ice

A national menu would lie. A regional one tells the truth.

## Signature ingredients

- **Corn**: the indigenous staple, eaten fresh on the cob in summer, ground into cornmeal for bread and grits, fermented into bourbon, popped at the cinema. Touches every region.
- **Beef**: the steak culture is real. Ribeye, strip, brisket, ground chuck for the burger. Midwest feedlots, Texas ranches, dry-aged steakhouse cuts.
- **Butter**: more than oil, more than lard, butter is the default fat. Biscuits, pie crust, pancakes, sauteing greens, finishing steak.
- **Bourbon**: corn-based whiskey aged in charred new oak. Kentucky is the heart. Goes in glazes, marinades, pecan pies and, frankly, glasses.
- **Hot sauce**: vinegar and chilli, kept on the table in the South, Louisiana especially. Tabasco, Crystal, Frank's. The fried-chicken finisher.
- **Maple syrup**: tapped from sugar maples in Vermont, New York, Quebec. Pancakes are the obvious use, but it glazes ham, sweetens beans, deepens bourbon cocktails.
- **Peanut butter**: a George Washington Carver invention, now a national obsession. PB&J, peanut butter cookies, satay-adjacent sauces, stirred into chocolate.
- **Blue cheese**: Maytag from Iowa, Point Reyes from California. Crumbled on burgers, dressed onto wedge salads, the obligatory partner to a Buffalo wing.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Smashburger](smashburger.md)**: the burger done right. Loose-pack the ground beef, smash it hard onto a screaming-hot griddle, let the crust form. American cheese, soft bun, a thin sauce. The Maillard reaction is the whole point.
2. **[New England clam chowder](new-england-clam-chowder.md)**: the white, dairy-rich version (red is Manhattan's). Salt pork, onion, potato, fresh clams, cream. Eat it from a bread bowl on the coast of Maine in October.
3. **[Buttermilk fried chicken](buttermilk-fried-chicken.md)**: the South's gift to the world. Overnight buttermilk brine, seasoned flour dredge, fried in lard or peanut oil until the crust shatters. Hot sauce on top.
4. **[Mac and cheese](side-dishes/mac-and-cheese.md)**: a Mornay sauce, sharp cheddar, elbow macaroni, breadcrumb top. Thomas Jefferson popularised it after a trip to Italy, Black home cooks made it Sunday-table food, the box version made it universal.
5. **[Buttermilk pancakes](buttermilk-pancakes.md)**: the American breakfast plate. Tangy buttermilk, baking soda for lift, butter and maple syrup at the table. Stack them tall.
6. **[Apple pie](desserts/apple-pie.md)**: lattice-topped, cinnamon-spiced, served warm with vanilla ice cream. The phrase "as American as apple pie" is technically a lie (the apples and the pie are both European), but the affection is real.
7. **[Philly cheesesteak](philly-cheesesteak.md)**: thin-sliced ribeye, griddled onions, melted cheese (Whiz, provolone or American, the debate is eternal), packed into a soft hoagie roll. Pat's or Geno's, pick a side.

Seven dishes, seven regions, one country. Cook your way through them and you have a working map of the place.
