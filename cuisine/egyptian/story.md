# Egyptian

*The Nile-fed cuisine of the oldest civilisation on the African continent. Bread, beans, leafy greens, cracked-wheat dishes, and a love of pickling that goes back to the Pharaohs.*

## A short history

Egyptian cooking sits on a 7,000-year continuous food tradition, which is something almost no other living cuisine can claim. The Nile flood gave the country bread wheat (emmer) by 5000 BCE, barley for beer, flax and the lentil. Pharaonic-era texts and tomb paintings record the same ingredients still on the everyday Egyptian table: bread (the most ancient form, ancestor to all leavened loaves; the Old Kingdom Egyptians figured out fermentation by accident and never looked back), beans (the cooks were almost certainly making ful medames when the pyramids were built around 2500 BCE), beer, onions, garlic, lettuce, fish from the Nile, and roast goose. The bakers' guilds of Memphis and Thebes ran ovens identical in shape to the clay aish baladi ovens still in use today.

The Greco-Roman centuries (Alexander founded Alexandria in 332 BCE, Rome annexed Egypt in 30 BCE) brought olive oil, the codified wine trade, and the cosmopolitan port cooking that turned Alexandria into the Mediterranean's intellectual and culinary capital. Coptic Christianity, taking hold from the 1st century CE, layered in long fasting periods that built the country's enduring vegetarian shelf: bessara, ful, the lentil-and-rice dishes, the chickpea preparations that became koshary's distant ancestors.

The Arab Islamic conquest in 642 CE was the next inflection. Arab merchants embedded the full spice cabinet (cumin, coriander, cinnamon, cardamom), the kebab tradition, the rice that displaced wheat as the celebration grain, the citrus that arrived from further east, and the sugar refinement that the Mamluks (1250 to 1517) industrialised. Cairo under the Mamluks was the richest food city in the Mediterranean: confectioners' streets ran for blocks, and the modern Egyptian sweet shop (with its rosewater syrup, semolina cakes and milk puddings) descends directly from Mamluk recipe collections.

The Ottoman era (1517 to 1798) brought the meze table, the dolma tradition (stuffed vine leaves, stuffed cabbage, stuffed everything) and the slow-stew technique that built fattah and mahshi. Mehmet Ali's modernising 19th century opened Egypt to European cooks and the Khedival court adopted French service, but the street and home kitchens kept cooking the Mamluk-Ottoman shelf. The mid-20th century, after the 1952 revolution that ended the monarchy, codified street food (koshary at Abou Tarek, ful at every corner cart) as a national identity. Koshary itself is a 19th-century improvisation: rice, lentils, macaroni, chickpeas, fried onions and tomato-vinegar sauce, all on one plate, the cheapest filling lunch in the Arab world.

Today Egyptian food lives between the everyday street kitchen (ful cart at dawn, koshary at lunch, kebab at dinner), the home cooking that has barely changed in centuries, and a slowly emerging modern Cairo restaurant scene drawing on the country's deep larder. The Nile still runs the kitchen, just as it did when the pyramid builders ate their morning beans.

## Regional variations

- **Cairo and Lower Egypt (north)**: the codified mainstream. Koshary, ful, ta'ameya (Egyptian falafel made with broad beans, not chickpeas).
- **Alexandria**: coastal. Grilled fish, sayadiyeh (fish-and-rice), Greek influences from the city's old cosmopolitan era.
- **Upper Egypt (south)**: more conservative, more lamb, fattah-led celebrations.
- **Sinai and Red Sea**: Bedouin cooking. Tea over open fire, simple slow-cooked lamb.

## Signature ingredients

- **Ful medames**: slow-cooked fava beans, eaten daily for breakfast across the country.
- **Molokhia**: jute mallow leaves, mucilaginous green soup. The signature stew base.
- **Dukkah**: a dry mix of ground nuts, sesame, coriander, cumin and salt. The dipping spice for bread and oil.
- **Aish baladi**: Egyptian pita, baked in clay ovens, the universal bread.
- **Bessara**: dried fava bean dip with cumin and chilli.
- **Hawawshi**: spiced meat pita; a takeaway staple.
- **Mahalabia**: rosewater-and-orange-blossom milk pudding.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Ful Medames](/recipes/cuisine/egyptian/ful-medames)**: slow-cooked fava beans with cumin, lemon, olive oil and garlic. The national breakfast.
2. **[Molokhia with Meat](/recipes/cuisine/egyptian/molokhia-with-meat)**: jute-mallow soup with rabbit, chicken or lamb. The signature Egyptian dish.
3. **[Fattah bil Lahma](/recipes/cuisine/egyptian/fattah-bil-lahma)**: layered toasted bread, rice and lamb in garlic-vinegar broth. The Eid al-Adha celebration plate.
4. **[Hawawshi](/recipes/cuisine/egyptian/hawawshi)**: spiced minced lamb pressed into pita and baked till crispy. The Cairo street food.
5. **[Kofta bil Roz](/recipes/cuisine/egyptian/kofta-bil-roz)**: spiced minced lamb meatballs in tomato sauce over rice.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with shai (sweet black tea) or karkadeh (hibiscus tea). For a proper Egyptian feast, start with ful and ta'ameya for breakfast, koshary for lunch, molokhia for dinner, and mahalabia after.
