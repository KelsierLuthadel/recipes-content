# Algerian

*The largest country in Africa eats from a kitchen that runs from the Mediterranean to the Sahara. Berber, Arab, Andalusian, Ottoman and French layers, all built around couscous, the slow tagine and the bread that comes with every meal.*

## A short history

Algerian cooking starts on the high plateau and the Atlas slopes with the Imazighen (Berbers), the indigenous people of North Africa whose kitchens have been working the same wheat-and-lamb-and-olive shelf for at least four thousand years. Neolithic rock paintings in the Tassili n'Ajjer show herds of cattle, sheep and goats, and the slow communal stewing of meat with grain. The Imazighen invented couscous, hand-rolled semolina pearls steamed in a clay-and-reed pot over the simmering stew, and the dish is still the universal Friday lunch from Marrakech to Tripoli. They also brought the date palm shelf, the barley flatbread baked on a hot stone, and the slow-cooked tagine in its conical clay pot.

Phoenician traders out of Carthage layered the Mediterranean shelf on top from the 8th century BCE: olives, olive oil, wine, the fig and the almond. Rome held Algeria as one of its grain provinces for six centuries and embedded the wheat-and-bread tradition that still anchors every meal. The Vandal and Byzantine centuries kept the everyday kitchen running on the same Berber-Mediterranean spine.

The 7th-century Arab conquest is the next great inflection. Arab caravans carried the Eastern spice rack across North Africa: cumin, coriander, cinnamon, ginger, saffron, the ras el hanout blend, and the long lamb braise scented with rosewater and dried fruit. Islamic dietary law (no pork, ritual slaughter) reshaped the meat shelf. Andalusian refugees fleeing Christian Spain after 1492 brought the sophisticated sweet-pastry tradition (almond-and-honey, orange-blossom water, the layered makroud), the green olive in chicken tagine, and the technique of slow-baking lamb in a sealed clay tagine. Constantine and Tlemcen became the great Andalusian cities of the new shore, and their kitchens still carry that refinement.

The Ottoman period (Algiers became a regency of the Sultan in 1517) ran for three centuries and embedded another layer. Stuffed vegetables (mahchi, dolma in everything from courgettes to vine leaves), the skewered kebab, the boureka pastries, the meze tradition of small mixed appetisers, and the strong sweet coffee. Algiers's casbah kitchens cooked an Ottoman-Berber-Andalusian hybrid that's still the codified mainstream today.

French colonisation from 1830 to independence in 1962 was the last great rewrite. The French planted vines (Algeria was briefly the world's biggest wine exporter before independence), built bakeries, and pushed coffee culture. Baguettes, croissants, the bistro chair on a hot pavement, and the merguez sausage (an Algerian-Tunisian-French hybrid that ended up in every Paris market) all date from this period. The independence war (1954 to 1962) and the 1990s civil war kept tourism low for decades, which is one reason Algerian food gets less international attention than Moroccan or Tunisian. Diaspora kitchens in Marseille, Lyon and Paris preserved and codified the cuisine for a global audience. Inside the country, everyday food remains generous, slow, and unmistakably Maghrebi.

## Regional variations

- **Algiers (north coast)**: the codified mainstream. Couscous on Fridays, the meze-style appetisers borrowed from Ottoman Constantinople.
- **Constantine (east)**: the most refined Andalusian-influenced shelf. Trida (small square pasta), chakhchoukha (torn flatbread in spicy sauce).
- **Oran (west)**: closer to Moroccan. More mechoui (whole roasted lamb), more saffron.
- **Sahara (south)**: Touareg cooking. Bread baked in sand, simple slow-cooked goat, date-and-millet staples.

## Signature ingredients

- **Couscous**: tiny semolina pearls steamed three times in a couscoussier. The national starch.
- **Ras el hanout**: warm spice blend (cinnamon, allspice, cumin, ginger, coriander, cardamom).
- **Harissa**: chilli paste with garlic, caraway, coriander. The table condiment.
- **Preserved lemon**: salt-cured lemon, used in tagines.
- **Olive oil**: the fat of choice, often heavy and green.
- **Saffron**: in celebration dishes, especially the rice tradition.
- **Mint**: fresh, in tea and as a finishing herb.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Couscous Algérien](/recipes/cuisine/algerian/couscous-algerien)**: the Friday couscous with lamb, chickpeas, courgette, carrot, turnip, served on steamed semolina. The national meal.
2. **[Chorba Frik](/recipes/cuisine/algerian/chorba-frik)**: lamb soup with cracked green wheat (frik), chickpeas, tomato and coriander. The Ramadan iftar soup.
3. **[Tajine Zitoune](/recipes/cuisine/algerian/tajine-zitoune)**: chicken tagine with green olives, mushrooms and preserved lemon.
4. **[Méchoui](/recipes/cuisine/algerian/mechoui)**: whole roasted lamb, the celebration plate, traditionally cooked over a pit.
5. **[Rechta](/recipes/cuisine/algerian/rechta)**: hand-cut fresh pasta with chicken, chickpeas and a delicate cinnamon broth. The Constantine specialty.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with mint tea (poured from height for the foam) or with strong black coffee. For a proper Algerian table: a stuffed-vegetable appetiser (mahchi), the couscous as a main, a side of harissa, and a sweet pastry (makroud, baklava) with mint tea after.
