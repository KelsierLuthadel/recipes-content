# Palestinian

*The cuisine of olive country. Olive oil-led, sumac-and-za'atar-spiced, with the most distinct stuffed-vegetable tradition in the Levant and an upside-down rice dish (maklouba) that defies imitation.*

## A short history

Palestinian cooking is part of the broader Levantine tradition, sharing roots with Syrian, Lebanese and Jordanian kitchens, but with its own distinct signatures. The olive oil is heavier, the za'atar mix is local (with hyssop, sumac and sesame), and several dishes — musakhan, maklouba, samkeh harra — are unmistakably Palestinian in their codified forms.

The cuisine sits on an ancient agricultural base: olives (some of the trees in the West Bank are 1,000+ years old), wheat, sumac (gathered wild), za'atar (the hyssop herb the spice mix is named for), figs, almonds, dates. The Ottoman period (1516–1917) layered on the meze table and the kebab. The post-1948 displacement created multiple kitchen traditions — the West Bank village kitchen, the Gazan coastal kitchen, the diaspora kitchens of Jordan, Lebanon and the Gulf, and the broader Palestinian-diaspora kitchens worldwide.

The cuisine is increasingly recognised internationally through cookbooks (Yasmin Khan's *Zaitoun*, Sami Tamimi's *Falastin*) and a growing diaspora restaurant scene. Internally the village kitchen continues largely as it has for generations.

## Regional variations

- **West Bank (Hebron, Nablus, Ramallah)**: olive country, the codified mainstream. Musakhan, maklouba, hand-rolled freekeh.
- **Gaza**: spicier, chilli-leaning, more seafood. The most distinct regional palate.
- **Galilee (north)**: closer to Lebanese cooking. Lighter sauces, more meze.
- **Diaspora (Jordan, Lebanon, Gulf, US)**: codified Palestinian-restaurant tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Olive oil**: heavy use. Palestinian olives are the world's most-loved Levantine olive.
- **Za'atar**: hyssop + sumac + sesame seeds + salt. Local to Palestine; on every breakfast.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry, the signature seasoning on fattoush and grilled meats.
- **Tahini**: ground sesame paste. The base of hummus, dressing for fish.
- **Freekeh**: green wheat, roasted while still moist. Used in pilafs.
- **Pine nuts**: toasted, scattered on rice and meat.
- **Lemon and pomegranate molasses**: the two acids.

## Learn it in eight recipes

1. **[Musakhan](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/musakhan)**: sumac-roasted chicken over flatbread with caramelised onions and pine nuts. The Palestinian national dish, eaten with the hands.
2. **[Maklouba](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/maklouba)**: "upside-down" — layered rice, vegetables (aubergine, cauliflower, potato) and chicken or lamb, cooked in one pot then inverted onto a platter.
3. **[Kibbeh bil Saneeyeh](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/kibbeh-bil-saneeyeh)**: tray-baked kibbeh layered with spiced lamb in the middle.
4. **[Mlukhieh Palestinian](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/mlukhieh-palestinian)**: jute-mallow soup with chicken, garlic and ground coriander.
5. **[Lahem bi Ajeen](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/lahem-bi-ajeen)**: spiced lamb flatbread, the Levantine answer to pizza.
6. **[Fatayer Sabanikh](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/fatayer-sabanikh)**: triangular spinach-sumac-onion pies.
7. **[Samkeh Harra](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/samkeh-harra)**: whole baked fish with tahini-chilli-coriander sauce. The Gazan signature.
8. **[Shish Barak](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/shish-barak)**: small meat dumplings in yoghurt sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with sage tea (maramiyya) or with arak. For a proper Palestinian table: start with hummus, baba ghanoush and labneh; main is musakhan or maklouba; finish with knafeh and Arabic coffee.
