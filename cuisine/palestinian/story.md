# Palestinian

*The cuisine of olive country. Olive oil-led, sumac-and-za'atar-spiced, with the most distinct stuffed-vegetable tradition in the Levant and an upside-down rice dish (maklouba) that defies imitation.*

## A short history

Palestinian cooking sits inside the broader Levantine tradition, sharing roots with Syrian, Lebanese and Jordanian kitchens, but it carries its own signatures. The olive oil is heavier, the za'atar mix is local (hyssop, sumac and sesame), and several dishes (musakhan, maklouba, samkeh harra) are unmistakably Palestinian in their settled forms.

The cuisine rests on an ancient agricultural base laid down in the Canaanite hills thousands of years ago. Olive cultivation in the West Bank goes back at least 5,000 years, and some trees around Bethlehem and Jenin are still bearing fruit after a thousand winters. The Bronze Age villagers ground wheat and barley on basalt querns, baked flatbread on a clay saj, salted fish from the Mediterranean, and pressed olives for the lamps and the kitchen alike. Wild sumac came down off the hillsides, hyssop (the za'atar plant the spice mix is named for) grew between the stones, and figs, almonds, pomegranates and grapes filled out the orchard. The shape of a Palestinian breakfast (olive oil, za'atar, bread, olives, white cheese) reads almost identically to what an Iron Age hillside farmer would have eaten.

The medieval centuries layered conquest on top of that base. The Umayyad and Abbasid caliphates (7th to 13th centuries) ran trade routes through Palestine that brought rice from Persia, sugar cane from India and a sophisticated spice shelf from across the Islamic world. Sugar refineries operated around Jericho in the 11th century. The Crusader century left almost nothing on the plate; the Mamluk period that followed (1260 to 1517) is the one that codified much of the Levantine kitchen, including the sweet pastries (knafeh out of Nablus, baklava across the region) and the spice-led meat cookery.

The Ottoman period (1516 to 1917) brought the meze table, the charcoal kebab, the coffeehouse and the long tradition of stuffed vegetables (mahshi). Istanbul's palace kitchens sent recipes outward and pulled regional dishes inward; the kibbeh family, the pilaf tradition and the mezze culture all settled into roughly their present shape in these four centuries. Nablus became famous across the empire for its olive-oil soap and its knafeh; Jerusalem developed its own pastry tradition; the Galilee villages refined the freekeh harvest into the smoky green-wheat pilafs still eaten today.

The post-1948 displacement (the Nakba) scattered the kitchen across several geographies at once. The West Bank village kitchen kept the olive oil and the slow-cooked maklouba; Gazan cooking, hemmed against the coast, intensified its love of chilli, dill and seafood into the most distinct regional Palestinian palate. Refugee camp kitchens in Jordan, Lebanon and Syria preserved the dishes of lost villages; diaspora kitchens in the Gulf, in Chile (which holds the largest Palestinian community outside the Middle East) and across Europe and North America carried the recipes further still. Food became memory, and a cookbook became an archive.

The cuisine is travelling internationally now through cookbooks (Yasmin Khan's *Zaitoun*, Sami Tamimi's *Falastin*, Reem Kassis's *The Palestinian Table*) and a growing diaspora restaurant scene. Inside Palestine the village kitchen continues largely as it has for generations, anchored to the olive harvest in October and November when extended families come back to the groves to pick together.

## Regional variations

- **West Bank (Hebron, Nablus, Ramallah)**: olive country, the mainstream. Musakhan, maklouba, hand-rolled freekeh.
- **Gaza**: spicier, chilli-leaning, more seafood. The most distinct regional palate.
- **Galilee (north)**: closer to Lebanese cooking. Lighter sauces, more meze.
- **Diaspora (Jordan, Lebanon, Gulf, US)**: the settled Palestinian-restaurant tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Olive oil**: heavy use. Palestinian olives are the most-loved Levantine olive.
- **Za'atar**: hyssop, sumac, sesame seeds, salt. Local to Palestine; on every breakfast.
- **Sumac**: ground sour-red berry, the signature seasoning on fattoush and grilled meats.
- **Tahini**: ground sesame paste. The base of hummus, the dressing for fish.
- **Freekeh**: green wheat, roasted while still moist. Used in pilafs.
- **Pine nuts**: toasted, scattered on rice and meat.
- **Lemon and pomegranate molasses**: the two acids.

## Learn it in eight recipes

1. **[Musakhan](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/musakhan)**: sumac-roasted chicken over flatbread with caramelised onions and pine nuts. The Palestinian national dish, eaten with the hands.
2. **[Maklouba](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/maklouba)**: "upside-down". Layered rice, vegetables (aubergine, cauliflower, potato) and chicken or lamb, cooked in one pot then flipped onto a platter.
3. **[Kibbeh bil Saneeyeh](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/kibbeh-bil-saneeyeh)**: tray-baked kibbeh layered with spiced lamb in the middle.
4. **[Mlukhieh Palestinian](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/mlukhieh-palestinian)**: jute-mallow soup with chicken, garlic and ground coriander.
5. **[Lahem bi Ajeen](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/lahem-bi-ajeen)**: spiced lamb flatbread, the Levantine answer to pizza.
6. **[Fatayer Sabanikh](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/fatayer-sabanikh)**: triangular spinach-sumac-onion pies.
7. **[Samkeh Harra](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/samkeh-harra)**: whole baked fish with tahini-chilli-coriander sauce. The Gazan signature.
8. **[Shish Barak](/recipes/cuisine/palestinian/shish-barak)**: small meat dumplings in yoghurt sauce.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with sage tea (maramiyya) or arak. For a proper Palestinian table: start with hummus, baba ghanoush and labneh; the main is musakhan or maklouba; finish with knafeh and Arabic coffee.
