# Mandioca

![Mandioca](resources/mandioca-paraguay.jpg)

*Boiled cassava, peeled and split into wedges, served hot beside every Paraguayan meat plate. A starchy, fluffy, gently sweet root that fills the same place on the table that potato does elsewhere.*

**Serves:** 6

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

**Cook Time:** 30 minutes

## Overview
Mandioca (cassava, also called yuca or manioc) is the Guaraní staple of Paraguay, eaten daily at lunch and dinner. The preparation is so simple that calling it a recipe is generous: thick lengths of fresh cassava are peeled, simmered in salted water until tender, drained, and served alongside grilled or roasted meat. The texture inside is fluffy and starchy, somewhere between a baking potato and a chestnut; the flavour is faintly sweet. Every asado paraguayo includes a platter of boiled mandioca for diners to spear with a fork beside their beef. The key is fresh cassava (frozen works passably, dried does not) and pulling it from the water at the right moment, when a knife slides in easily and the wedges start to crack along the central fibre.

## Ingredients

- 1.2 kg fresh cassava (mandioca), or 1 kg frozen peeled cassava
- 2 litres water
- 2 tbsp salt
- Optional: 1 tbsp lard for richness

## Method

### Stage 1 - Prepare the cassava
1. If using fresh cassava: cut into lengths of about 10 cm.
2. Stand each length on end and slice off the thick brown skin with a sharp knife, working downwards. The waxy pink layer underneath the bark goes too: keep cutting until you reach the pure white flesh.
3. Split each length in half lengthways and pull out the woody central fibre. It comes out cleanly.
4. Rinse the peeled cassava in cold water.

### Stage 2 - Boil
1. Place the cassava in a large pot. Cover generously with cold water; add the salt and the lard if using.
2. Bring to a boil. Lower to a steady simmer.
3. Cook 20-30 minutes (longer for thicker pieces) until a knife slides in with no resistance and the cassava starts to crack along the central fibre.
4. Drain.

### Stage 3 - Serve
1. Pile the hot cassava onto a serving platter.
2. Sprinkle with a little more salt.
3. Serve immediately, hot. The platter goes in the middle of the table for diners to help themselves.

## Notes
- **Test for doneness:** cassava is unforgiving when undercooked (the flesh stays glassy and the centre fibre stays tough). Boil until visibly cracking open and a knife meets no resistance.
- **Frozen cassava:** works well; skip the peeling step. Cook from frozen in salted water for 20 minutes.
- **The central fibre:** the thin woody string down the middle of each length is inedible. Pull it out before or after cooking.
- **Hot is the only way:** mandioca turns rubbery as it cools. Reheat thoroughly or fry leftovers.

## Variations
- **Mandioca frita:** boil first, then fry the cooked wedges in hot oil until crisp. The classic Paraguayan chip.
- **Mandioca con queso:** boiled cassava topped with crumbled fresh queso paraguay and a drizzle of melted butter.
- **Mandioca al ajo:** boiled, then tossed in butter, minced garlic and parsley.
- **Mandioca al horno:** parboil for 15 minutes, drain, then roast in lard at 200 C for 25 minutes until golden and crisp at the edges.

## Serving
Always beside grilled meat · with asado paraguayo, beef ribs or pollo asado · with chorizo paraguayo · with so'o yosopy or vori vori as an extra carbohydrate · with a tomato-and-onion salad.

## Storage
- Cooked mandioca keeps 3 days refrigerated
- Reheat by frying in lard or oil (the best leftover use)
- Microwave reheating turns it gummy
- Freezes well as raw peeled wedges; cook from frozen
