# Paraguayan

*The kitchen of South America's land-locked Guarani-speaking country. Sopa paraguaya (a famously misnamed "soup" that's actually a corn-bread cake), chipa cheese bread by the basket, the Italian-immigrant pasta tradition, and the slow asado that anchors the Sunday table.*

## A short history

Paraguayan cooking sits on a deep indigenous Guarani base. Long before the Spanish arrived in 1537, the Guarani peoples were farming the fertile land between the Paraná and Paraguay rivers, growing cassava (mandioca) as their everyday starch, corn in several varieties, peanuts, sweet potato and pumpkin, and brewing yerba mate from the wild ilex tree that grew through the eastern forests. The Guarani word for food (tembi'u) and most of the staples on a Paraguayan table today (mandioca, chipa, mbeju, jopara) still go by their original Guarani names. Few other South American countries kept the pre-colonial language and the pre-colonial pantry as intact as Paraguay did.

The Spanish colonial layer settled in differently here than elsewhere in the Americas. The Jesuit missions (1609 to 1767) ran a network of reducciones across what is now Paraguay, southern Brazil and northern Argentina, teaching the Guarani to raise European cattle, sheep and wheat alongside their traditional crops. The Jesuits also organised the yerba mate trade, turning the wild leaf into the colonial drink that bound the region together. When Spain expelled the order in 1767 the missions collapsed, but the beef-and-mate culture they had built stayed. Cattle ranching spread across the country, and the slow asado over a wood fire became the Sunday tradition it still is.

Sopa paraguaya, the country's most-loved dish, is famously not a soup. It's a dense corn-bread baked in a heavy pan from cornmeal, cheese, milk and eggs. The name comes from a 19th-century kitchen mishap: a cook serving President Carlos Antonio López let the soup over-thicken in the oven. The president, surprised, called it "sopa paraguaya" anyway. The name stuck. The story is true to a deeper pattern: Paraguayan cooking holds onto the Guarani names for everything, even when the dish has changed beyond recognition.

The Triple Alliance War (1864 to 1870) cut the male population in half and left the country devastated for a generation. The European immigration waves that followed (Italian from the 1880s, German from the 1890s, Slavic and Ukrainian into the 1920s) repopulated the countryside and reshaped the kitchen. The Italian wave was so heavy that pasta turns up on most Paraguayan home tables weekly, and so'o yosopy (beef-and-noodle soup) is essentially a Paraguayan-Italian creation. The Mennonite communities who settled the harsh Chaco from the 1920s onwards brought dairy farming and German-Russian baking, and produce most of Paraguay's cheese, milk and butter today.

The long Stroessner dictatorship (1954 to 1989) kept Paraguay relatively closed and rural, and the kitchen stayed local. Chipa (cheese-and-cassava-starch bread) remained the universal snack sold from baskets on every corner; tereré (cold yerba mate, the universal afternoon drink shared between friends from one gourd) continued to anchor daily life. The post-Stroessner recovery is finally bringing Paraguayan cooking into a wider conversation, but the home table looks much as it has for generations: asado on Sunday, chipa in the basket, tereré in the gourd, and sopa paraguaya on the side of nearly everything.

## Regional variations

- **Asunción and central**: the mainstream. Sopa paraguaya, chipa.
- **Eastern (Itapúa, Misiones)**: more German and Slavic immigrant traditions.
- **Chaco (west)**: Mennonite-German dairy country. Distinct cheese and bread.
- **South (Encarnación)**: closer to Argentine cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cornmeal (harina de maíz)**: in sopa paraguaya, in chipa guazú.
- **Cassava starch (almidón de mandioca)**: in chipa.
- **Cassava (mandioca)**: boiled, the universal starch.
- **Yerba mate**: in tereré (cold mate) and mate (hot). The national daily ritual.
- **Beef**: in asado and so'o yosopy.
- **Cheese (Paraguay)**: typically queso Paraguay, fresh and slightly salty.
- **Mbatá (locust bean)**: the local Vigna-bean for stews.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Sopa Paraguaya](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/sopa-paraguaya)**: the "Paraguayan soup" that isn't. A savoury corn-bread cake with onion, cheese and milk. A side at every barbecue.
2. **[Chipa Guazú](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/chipa-guazu)**: cousin to sopa paraguaya but built around whole corn kernels, denser and sweeter.
3. **[So'o Yosopy](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/soo-yosopy)**: beef-and-noodle soup with garlic and onion. The Paraguayan-Italian fusion classic.
4. **[Bori Bori](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/bori-bori)**: chicken soup with small cornmeal-and-cheese dumplings.
5. **[Vori Vori](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/vori-vori)**: close to bori bori but with beef or chicken; small cornmeal dumplings in a rich broth.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with tereré (cold yerba mate with herbs, the universal Paraguayan drink, shared from one gourd between friends) or with mate (the hot version). Finish with mbeju (cassava-and-cheese flatbread cooked on a griddle) or kivevé (sweet pumpkin pudding).
