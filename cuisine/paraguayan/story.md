# Paraguayan

*The kitchen of South America's land-locked guarani-speaking country. Sopa paraguaya (a famously misnamed "soup" that's actually a corn-bread cake), chipa cheese bread by the basket, the Italian-immigrant pasta tradition, and the slow asado that anchors the Sunday table.*

## A short history

Paraguayan cooking sits on a deep indigenous Guarani base (cassava, corn, peanuts, the use of yerba mate — Paraguay is the world's largest yerba mate consumer per capita) and a Spanish colonial layer (beef, dairy, wheat). The 19th-century European immigration (Italian, German, Slavic) added pasta and dumpling traditions; the Mennonite communities in the Chaco region brought dairy farming and German-Russian baking traditions.

Sopa paraguaya — Paraguay's most-loved dish — is famously not a soup. It's a dense corn-bread baked in a heavy pan, made from cornmeal, cheese, milk and eggs. The name comes from a likely 19th-century kitchen mistake: a cook serving President Carlos Antonio López accidentally let the soup over-thicken in the oven. The president, surprised, called it "sopa paraguaya" anyway. The name stuck.

The Italian immigration was so significant that pasta is on most Paraguayan home tables weekly, and the so'o yosopy (beef-and-noodle soup) is essentially a Paraguayan-Italian creation. Chipa (cheese-and-cassava-starch bread) is the universal snack.

## Regional variations

- **Asunción and central**: codified mainstream. Sopa paraguaya, chipa.
- **Eastern (Itapúa, Misiones)**: more German and Slavic immigrant traditions.
- **Chaco (west)**: Mennonite-German dairy farming. Distinct cheese and bread traditions.
- **South (Encarnación)**: closer to Argentine cooking.

## Signature ingredients

- **Cornmeal (harina de maíz)**: in sopa paraguaya, in chipa guazú.
- **Cassava starch (almidón de mandioca)**: in chipa.
- **Cassava (mandioca)**: boiled, the universal starch.
- **Yerba mate**: in tereré (cold mate) and mate (hot). The national daily ritual.
- **Beef**: in asado and so'o yosopy.
- **Cheese (Paraguay)**: typically queso Paraguay — fresh and slightly salty.
- **Mbatá (locust bean)**: the local Vigna-bean used in stews.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Sopa Paraguaya](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/sopa-paraguaya)**: the "Paraguayan soup" that isn't — a savoury corn-bread cake with onion, cheese and milk. Eaten as a side at every barbecue.
2. **[Chipa Guazú](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/chipa-guazu)**: similar to sopa paraguaya but with whole corn kernels, denser and sweeter.
3. **[So'o Yosopy](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/soo-yosopy)**: beef-and-noodle soup with garlic and onion. The Paraguayan-Italian fusion classic.
4. **[Bori Bori](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/bori-bori)**: chicken soup with small cornmeal-and-cheese dumplings.
5. **[Vori Vori](/recipes/cuisine/paraguayan/vori-vori)**: similar to bori bori but with beef or chicken — small cornmeal dumplings in a rich broth.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with tereré (cold yerba mate with herbs — the universal Paraguayan daily drink, shared from a single gourd between friends) or with mate (the hot version). End with mbeju (cassava-and-cheese flatbread cooked on a griddle) or with kivevé (sweet pumpkin pudding).
