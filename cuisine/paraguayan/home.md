# Paraguayan Cuisine

![Paraguayan Cuisine](resources/hero.jpg)

Paraguay's kitchen runs on two pillars the Guaraní gave the country long before the Spanish arrived: mandioca (cassava) and maíz (corn). Almost every traditional plate leans on one or both. Sopa paraguaya, the national dish, is a moist baked cornbread with onion and fresh cheese that confusingly goes by the name "soup". Chipa guazú is its summer twin, made with fresh corn kernels. The Jesuit reductions of the 17th and 18th centuries brought cattle and dairy, which is why almost everything carries fresh queso paraguay (a soft cow's-milk cheese close to a young feta). Chipa cassava-cheese rings turn up at every long-distance bus stop. Vori vori cornmeal dumplings simmer in beef broth on rural stoves. So'o yosopy beef-and-rice soup feeds families on cold winter mornings. Outside the kitchen, the whole country drinks tereré, the cold mate infusion poured over ice and herbs, passed hand to hand on shaded patios from morning until sundown.
