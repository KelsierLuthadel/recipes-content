# Angolan

*The kitchen of Lusophone Africa. Portuguese-Bantu fusion with palm oil, the slow-cooked muamba (chicken in palm oil), the calulu (dried fish stew), and a love of cassava and matabicho (the morning bread-and-coffee tradition).*

## A short history

Angolan cooking rests on a deep Bantu-African base that goes back two thousand years. The Bantu migration out of the Niger-Congo region (starting around 1000 BCE and reaching central Angola by the early first millennium CE) brought the iron-age farming complex south: millet and sorghum as the original staples, palm oil pressed from the African oil palm Elaeis guineensis, leafy greens (sweet potato leaves, cassava leaves) cooked into the universal one-pot, dried and smoked fish off the long Atlantic coast, and the slow-stewed muamba technique that's still the country's signature. Cassava (yuca) arrived later from Brazil via Portuguese ships in the 16th century and rapidly displaced sorghum as the everyday starch, ground into the funje porridge that anchors every meal.

The medieval Kingdom of Kongo, which covered most of northern Angola from the 14th century onwards, had a sophisticated court cuisine. Royal cooks at Mbanza Kongo (later São Salvador) worked with millet bread, palm wine, smoked game, plantain, the tropical fruit shelf and the slow-cooked palm-oil stew. Trade routes ran inland to the great central African markets and along the coast to the salt pans, and the long-distance trade in dried fish, palm oil and copper ran centuries before Europeans arrived. Further south, the Ndongo and Matamba kingdoms (where Queen Nzinga famously held off Portuguese expansion in the 17th century) cooked their own variations on the same Bantu shelf.

Five centuries of Portuguese influence sit on top. Portuguese ships under Diogo Cão reached the mouth of the Congo River in 1483; Luanda was founded in 1575. The colonial centuries were defined by the transatlantic slave trade, which sent more enslaved Africans to Brazil from Angola than from anywhere else on earth. The brutal exchange ran in both directions across the kitchen: Angolan cooks travelling in chains to Brazil carried muamba, calulu, palm oil and okra into what became Bahian cooking (vatapá, caruru, moqueca all trace back to Angola), while Brazilian crops brought back by returning ships (cassava, maize, peanuts, sweet potato, chilli) reshaped the Angolan home table. Portuguese inputs (wheat bread, wine, salt cod, pork, the morning espresso) embedded in coastal cities. The peri-piri chilli, originally a New World capsicum that Portuguese traders carried from Brazil to Angola and then on to Mozambique, got refined into the chilli-and-garlic marinade that the world now knows as Portuguese peri-peri, but the technique is Angolan.

The result is one of the most distinctive African cuisines, and the most distinctive of the Lusophone-African ones. Angolan dishes use palm oil more heavily than most West African neighbours; the muamba uses it as both the cooking medium and the finishing colour. The bread-and-coffee morning tradition (matabicho, literally "kill the worm") is unmistakably Portuguese. The piri-piri sauce is the everyday table condiment. The slow Sunday lunch around a single big pot is unmistakably Bantu.

Independence in 1975 was followed by a 27-year civil war that ran till 2002 and kept international visibility low. Diaspora kitchens in Portugal (especially the Lisbon and Setúbal restaurant scene), Brazil, France and South Africa preserved the codified tradition. The Luanda restaurant scene is recovering, oil money has built a small but real high-end restaurant culture, and Angolan chefs are starting to claim their place on the global African food stage alongside Senegalese, Nigerian and South African peers.

## Regional variations

- **Luanda and coastal (north-west)**: codified mainstream. Muamba de galinha, calulu, the seafood traditions.
- **Cabinda (the exclave north of the river)**: distinct dishes, more French/Congolese influence.
- **Bie and Huambo (highlands)**: more cattle, more maize.
- **Cunene (south)**: closer to Namibian cooking. Pap (maize porridge), more goat.

## Signature ingredients

- **Palm oil (dendê)**: in nearly every traditional dish.
- **Cassava (mandioca)**: in flour (funje), in chips, boiled.
- **Smoked fish (peixe defumado)**: in calulu, in stews.
- **Beans (feijão)**: in feijoada-style stews, in soup.
- **Sweet potato (batata-doce)**: roasted, in stews.
- **Peri-piri**: the African bird's-eye chilli, used in marinades.
- **Coconut (on the coast)**: in some northern dishes.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Muamba de Galinha](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/muamba-de-galinha)**: chicken slow-cooked in palm oil with okra, garlic and pumpkin. The national dish.
2. **[Calulu](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/calulu)**: dried-and-smoked fish (or beef) with sweet potato leaves, palm oil, garlic and onion. The Sunday family plate.
3. **[Frango Piri-Piri Angolano](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/frango-piri-piri-angolano)**: chicken marinated in peri-piri (African bird's-eye chilli, garlic, lemon, oil), grilled. The Angolan origin of what most of the world now calls Portuguese peri-peri.
4. **[Mufete](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/mufete)**: grilled fish (typically corvina) served with boiled cassava, sweet potato, plantain, palm-oil beans and a fierce peri-piri sauce. The weekend beachside plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with cuca beer (the national lager) or with Angolan red wine (Portuguese-style wine production has returned to the country). End with cocada amarela (yellow coconut sweet) or doce de jinguba (peanut sweet).
