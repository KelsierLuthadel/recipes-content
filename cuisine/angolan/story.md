# Angolan

*The kitchen of Lusophone Africa. Portuguese-Bantu fusion with palm oil, the slow-cooked muamba (chicken in palm oil), the calulu (dried fish stew), and a love of cassava and matabicho (the morning bread-and-coffee tradition).*

## A short history

Angolan cooking sits on a Bantu-African base (cassava, palm oil, leafy greens, the slow-stewed one-pot, dried and smoked fish from the long Atlantic coast) layered with five centuries of Portuguese influence. Portuguese colonisation began in 1483 and lasted until 1975; the country brought back to Portugal much of what we now call Portuguese cuisine (peri-piri chillies — originally African, refined in Angola; the palm-oil cooking technique) while leaving Portuguese inputs (bread, wine, salt cod, pork) in Angolan kitchens.

The result is one of the most distinctive African cuisines and the most distinctive Lusophone-African one. Angolan dishes use palm oil more heavily than most West African neighbours, and the bread-and-coffee morning tradition (matabicho — "kill the worm") is unmistakably Portuguese.

The post-1975 civil war (which lasted till 2002) limited international visibility. The diaspora kitchens in Portugal, Brazil, France and South Africa preserve the codified tradition. The current Luanda restaurant scene is recovering.

## Regional variations

- **Luanda and coastal (north-west)**: codified mainstream. Muamba de galinha, calulu, the seafood traditions.
- **Cabinda (the exclave north of the river)**: distinct dishes, more French/Congolese influence.
- **Bie and Huambo (highlands)**: more cattle, more maize.
- **Cunene (south)**: closer to Namibian cooking. Pap (maize porridge), more goat.

## Signature ingredients

- **Palm oil (dendê)**: in nearly every traditional dish.
- **Cassava (mandioca)**: in flour (funje), in chips, boiled.
- **Smoked fish (peixe defumado)**: in calulu, in stews.
- **Beans (feijão)**: in feijoada-style stews, in soup.
- **Sweet potato (batata-doce)**: roasted, in stews.
- **Peri-piri**: the African bird's-eye chilli, used in marinades.
- **Coconut (on the coast)**: in some northern dishes.

## Learn it in four recipes

1. **[Muamba de Galinha](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/muamba-de-galinha)**: chicken slow-cooked in palm oil with okra, garlic and pumpkin. The national dish.
2. **[Calulu](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/calulu)**: dried-and-smoked fish (or beef) with sweet potato leaves, palm oil, garlic and onion. The Sunday family plate.
3. **[Frango Piri-Piri Angolano](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/frango-piri-piri-angolano)**: chicken marinated in peri-piri (African bird's-eye chilli, garlic, lemon, oil), grilled. The Angolan origin of what most of the world now knows as Portuguese peri-peri.
4. **[Mufete](/recipes/cuisine/angolan/mufete)**: grilled fish (typically corvina) served with boiled cassava, sweet potato, plantain, palm-oil beans and a fierce peri-piri sauce. The weekend beachside plate.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with cuca beer (the national lager) or with Angolan red wine (Portuguese-style wine production has returned to the country). End with cocada amarela (yellow coconut sweet) or doce de jinguba (peanut sweet).
