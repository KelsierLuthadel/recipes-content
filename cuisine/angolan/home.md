# Angolan Cuisine

![Angolan Cuisine](resources/hero.jpg)

Angola's table sits at the meeting point of Portuguese colonial cooking and Bantu African tradition. The everyday foundations are dendê (red palm oil), mandioca (cassava, eaten as flour, leaf and root) and dried fish, with the Atlantic coast supplying fresh prawns, grouper and corvina to the Luanda kitchen while the inland country runs on slow-stewed greens, beans and bushmeat. Muamba de galinha, the national dish, layers chicken and palm oil with okra and garlic. Funje, the stiff cassava porridge, is the daily carb under every stew. The Lusophone-Atlantic exchange shows up in shared dishes with Brazil and Mozambique (pastéis de bacalhau, rissóis, caipirinha) and in the Portuguese love of dried cod, while later Cuban and Soviet trade links left their own marks on the modern Luanda kitchen.
