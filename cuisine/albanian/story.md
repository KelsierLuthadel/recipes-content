# Albanian

*The kitchen at the meeting of Adriatic, Mediterranean and Ottoman. Byrek (the phyllo pastry pie), the slow-roasted lamb, the yoghurt-baked rice (tavë kosi) that turns Sunday lunch into a celebration.*

## A short history

Albanian cooking starts with the Illyrians, the Indo-European people who held the western Balkans from the Bronze Age into the Roman conquest of 168 BCE. Their everyday plate ran on barley, lamb, goat's cheese, wild greens off the mountainsides and honey from the wooden hives the hill villages still keep today. The Illyrian shepherd's habit of curing meat by salt-and-smoke (pastërma is the local descendant) and the cooking of milk into thickened yoghurt and brined cheese both pre-date Rome. The slow lamb roast and the bread-and-cheese-and-olive breakfast are still recognisably the same plate.

Rome, then Byzantium, layered the Mediterranean shelf on top. Greek and Roman colonists along the Adriatic coast brought the olive grove, the grapevine and the wheat-based bread tradition; Byzantine Christianity gave Albania its fasting cuisine of bean-and-grain pottages and pickled vegetables for the long Orthodox lents. The medieval centuries saw the rise of Albanian principalities under figures like Skanderbeg, the 15th-century warrior who held off the Ottomans for twenty-five years and who's still cited as a national symbol. Trade with Venice and Ragusa brought saffron, almond and the first sweet-pastry techniques.

The long Ottoman period (1385 to 1912) is the deepest layer. Five centuries of Turkish rule embedded the byrek (cousin to the Turkish börek), the kebab, the stuffed vegetables (mahchi, dolma), the yoghurt-finished sauces, the kaymak (clotted cream), the Turkish coffee and the baklava. Greek, Turkish and Levantine recipes moved freely across the Ottoman empire, and the Albanian table picked from all of them. The 18th-century Ali Pasha of Ioannina ran his Albanian-Greek court on a kitchen that read essentially the same as Istanbul's, and many of today's signature dishes (tavë kosi, the yoghurt-baked lamb-and-rice, especially) trace back to that period.

The 20th century broke the kitchen in two. The brief inter-war monarchy under King Zog kept Italian influence flowing (pasta, espresso, the focaccia-like petulla). The Communist period under Enver Hoxha (1944 to 1992) then sealed Albania off from the rest of the world more comprehensively than any other Eastern Bloc country, and the cuisine got preserved rather than evolved. Food was rationed; restaurants were state-run; the home kitchen ran on what you grew or traded. The upside is that traditional techniques (the saç (lidded clay pan over coals), the wood-fired bread oven, the curing of byrek by hand-pulled phyllo) survived intact when other Balkan kitchens industrialised away from them.

The post-1992 opening brought modernisation and a diaspora-led recodification, with Albanian restaurants opening in New York, Toronto, London and especially across northern Italy and Greece where the diaspora settled. The Albanian-Italian community in Puglia, Calabria and Sicily (the Arbëreshë, descended from 15th-century refugees) had kept their own version of the cooking alive for five hundred years, and the modern diaspora connected with that older parallel tradition.

The home kitchen stays traditional. Byrek for breakfast or a snack, lamb on Sunday, slow-cooked stews, and the yoghurt-finished sauces that are Albania's signature.

## Regional variations

- **Tirana and central Albania**: the codified mainstream. Tavë kosi, byrek, fërgesë.
- **South (Gjirokastër, Vlorë)**: closer to Greek cooking. More olive oil, more feta-style cheese, qifqi (rice balls).
- **North (Shkodër, Kukës)**: closer to Kosovo and Macedonia. Meat-heavy, the flia (multi-layer crepe stack) tradition.
- **Coast (Durrës, Vlorë)**: seafood. Adriatic fish, the saç-baking tradition.

## Signature ingredients

- **Yoghurt (kos)**: in tavë kosi, as a marinade, dressed onto soups.
- **Phyllo pastry**: in byrek, in baklava.
- **Lamb**: the celebration meat.
- **Olive oil**: from the southern groves.
- **Feta-style cheese (djathë i bardhë)**: in byrek, in salads.
- **Peppers (speca)**: stuffed, roasted, pickled.
- **Mint and oregano**: the herb pair.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Tavë Kosi](/recipes/cuisine/albanian/tave-kosi)**: lamb baked in yoghurt-and-egg custard with rice. The national dish, the Sunday family centrepiece.
2. **[Albanian Byrek](/recipes/cuisine/albanian/albanian-byrek)**: layered phyllo pie filled with spinach-and-cheese or meat. The everyday breakfast and snack.
3. **[Fërgesë](/recipes/cuisine/albanian/fergese)**: cottage cheese, peppers and tomatoes baked together in a clay pot.
4. **[Speca të Mbushur](/recipes/cuisine/albanian/speca-te-mbushur)**: bell peppers stuffed with rice and minced meat, baked in tomato sauce.
5. **[Albanian Çorbë](/recipes/cuisine/albanian/albanian-corba)**: yoghurt-and-lemon soup with chicken or lamb, finished with mint. The everyday Albanian bowl.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with rakia (the grape or plum brandy, close to Italian grappa or Balkan rakija) or with strong Turkish-style coffee (kafe turke). End with revani (semolina syrup cake) or baklava. The Albanian sweet-pastry tradition is largely shared with Greek and Turkish, and each country claims priority.
