# Senegalese

*The kitchen of West Africa's Atlantic gateway. Thieboudienne (fish-and-rice, the national dish) cooked in one giant communal pot, mafé (peanut stew), the yassa (onion-lemon-Dijon marinade), and one of the most refined African cuisines going.*

## A short history

Senegalese cooking sits where West African Wolof, Mandinka, Serer and Fulani traditions meet centuries of Atlantic and Saharan trade. The earliest kitchens of the Senegambia ran on what the river valleys grew: pearl millet, sorghum, fonio (the tiny ancient grain still served at celebrations), Bambara groundnuts, baobab leaves and the river fish out of the Senegal and Casamance basins. Pastoralist Fulani moved cattle and milk across the savanna; coastal Wolof and Serer worked the Atlantic shore; Mandinka traders ran the inland routes to the Niger bend. The slow-stewed one-pot, the dried-and-pounded fish for flavouring, the millet couscous and the fermented sour-milk lakh all come straight from this pre-colonial foundation.

The medieval centuries pulled North African and Islamic influences down the trans-Saharan routes. The Ghana, Mali and Songhai empires that ran the caravan trade brought salt, dates, dried fruits, the coffee-like kinkeliba bush tea and the deep love of cardamom-and-clove sweet drinks that still defines the Senegalese mid-morning. Conversion to Islam (peaceful and gradual, mostly between the 11th and 17th centuries) banished pork from the kitchen and made lamb, mutton and chicken the celebration meats. The mosque-and-market geography of cities like Touba and Tivaouane shaped how food moved, with the Murid and Tijaniyya brotherhoods running their own feeding economies for travellers and pilgrims.

The Portuguese arrived on the coast in 1444; the French built Saint-Louis in 1659 at the mouth of the Senegal River and made it the first European colonial city in West Africa. The Atlantic slave trade ran through Gorée and the river ports for three centuries, and the kitchen reflected it: Senegalese cooks were carried west to the rice plantations of Carolina and Louisiana, taking thieboudienne with them in the form that became jambalaya and Charleston red rice. The Columbian Exchange brought what now anchors the local kitchen: chilli peppers, tomatoes, cassava, peanuts and maize, all American crops naturalised so completely that you can't picture Senegalese food without them.

French colonisation (1659-1960) reshaped the larder. The country grows rice in the Casamance and Senegal River valleys; the Atlantic coast supplies fish (sea bass, grouper, captain fish); peanuts (groundnuts) became the main cash crop in the colonial era and now get ground into the peanut sauce (mafé tigadégué) that's a regional standard. The colonial economy turned the country into one giant groundnut plantation, but the home kitchen quietly absorbed French technique: the long-simmered onion base, the mustard in yassa marinades, the careful builds of layered broth in thieboudienne. Saint-Louis cooks, working in the colonial households, codified what we now think of as classical Senegalese food: French method, African soul.

Thieboudienne (or ceebu jën in Wolof, "fish and rice") is the national dish, and the recipe most cookbooks attribute to a 19th-century Saint-Louis cook called Penda Mbaye. UNESCO put it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2021. The cook builds it in a single pot: fry the fish stuffed with rof (a parsley-garlic-pepper paste), simmer the vegetables in tomato sauce, cook the rice in the broth till it soaks up everything. The pot comes to the table whole; people gather around with spoons and eat from the communal vessel.

Independence in 1960 made Senegal one of West Africa's cultural capitals under the philosopher-president Léopold Senghor. Dakar's restaurant scene is among the best in West Africa, and the diaspora kitchens of Paris, Brooklyn and Brussels have made yassa and mafé internationally recognisable. The country's reputation as a regional culinary capital is well earned. The home cooking has stayed close to its centuries-old shape: a Sunday thieboudienne, served from one pot, shared with everyone.

## Regional variations

- **Dakar and the coast**: the mainstream. Thieboudienne, yassa, the fish traditions.
- **Casamance (south)**: more rice, more palm oil. Dishes shared with Guinea-Bissau.
- **Tambacounda and the interior**: more peanut, more couscous-millet.
- **Northern (Saint-Louis)**: closer to Mauritanian influence. More dates, more couscous.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rice (broken rice, "brisure")**: in thieboudienne. Broken-grain rice gives the right texture.
- **Fish (especially captain fish, sea bass, grouper)**: in thieboudienne and yassa.
- **Peanuts (groundnut paste, tigadégué)**: in mafé.
- **Lemon (lime in practice)**: in yassa marinades.
- **Onion**: huge quantities, slow-cooked, in yassa.
- **Scotch bonnet and Cayenne pepper**: the heat.
- **Bissap (hibiscus)**: in the country's most-loved drink.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Thieboudienne](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/thieboudienne)**: rice-and-fish (often grouper or red snapper) with cassava, sweet potato, cabbage, eggplant, carrot and tamarind, all cooked together in one tomato-and-pepper broth. The national dish.
2. **[Mafé](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/mafe)**: peanut stew with beef or lamb, sweet potato, carrot and cabbage. Served with rice. The everyday lunch.
3. **[Yassa Grilled Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/yassa-grilled-chicken)**: chicken marinated in lemon, onion, Dijon mustard and Cayenne, grilled then simmered with huge quantities of onion.
4. **[Thiof Yassa](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/thiof-yassa)**: grouper or sea bass in the yassa onion-lemon sauce. The fish version.
5. **[Domoda](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/domoda)**: a close cousin of mafé, peanut stew with meat or fish.
6. **[Caldou](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/caldou)**: clear fish soup with rice, lime and chilli. The Casamance speciality.
7. **[Supu Kanja](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/supu-kanja)**: thick okra-and-fish (often smoked) stew with palm oil.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with bissap (hibiscus drink, sweetened, served cold), bouye (baobab-fruit drink) or a Gazelle beer. For a proper thieboudienne meal: everyone gathers around the single pot, eats with the right hand from the communal vessel, and the cook keeps adding rice from the bottom until everyone has had their fill.
