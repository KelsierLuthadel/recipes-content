# Senegalese

*The kitchen of West Africa's Atlantic gateway. Thieboudienne (fish-and-rice — the national dish) cooked in one giant communal pot, mafé (peanut stew), the yassa (onion-lemon-Dijon marinade), and one of the most refined African cuisines.*

## A short history

Senegalese cooking sits at the meeting of West African Wolof, Mandinka and Fulani traditions with French colonial influence and Islamic North African layers via the trans-Saharan trade. The country grows rice in the Casamance and Senegal River valleys; the Atlantic coast supplies fish (sea bass, grouper, captain fish); peanuts (groundnuts) became the country's main cash crop in the colonial era and are now ground into the peanut sauce (mafé tigadégué) that's a regional standard.

Thieboudienne (or ceebu jën in Wolof — "fish and rice") is the national dish. UNESCO inscribed it on the Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2021. The dish is built in a single pot: fish stuffed with rof (parsley-garlic-pepper paste) is fried; vegetables are simmered in tomato sauce; rice is cooked in the broth till it absorbs everything. The pot is brought to the table whole; people gather around with spoons and eat from the communal vessel.

Senegal's reputation as a regional culinary capital is well-established. Dakar's restaurant scene is among the best in West Africa.

## Regional variations

- **Dakar and the coast**: codified mainstream. Thieboudienne, yassa, the fish traditions.
- **Casamance (south)**: more rice, more palm oil. Distinct dishes shared with Guinea-Bissau.
- **Tambacounda and the interior**: more peanut, more couscous-millet.
- **Northern (Saint-Louis)**: closer to Mauritanian influence. More dates, more couscous.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rice (broken rice — "brisure")**: in thieboudienne. Broken-grain rice is preferred for the texture.
- **Fish (especially captain fish, sea bass, grouper)**: in thieboudienne and yassa.
- **Peanuts (groundnut paste — tigadégué)**: in mafé.
- **Lemon (lime in practice)**: in yassa marinades.
- **Onion**: huge quantities, slow-cooked, in yassa.
- **Scotch bonnet and Cayenne pepper**: the heat.
- **Bissap (hibiscus)**: in the country's most-loved drink.

## Learn it in seven recipes

1. **[Thieboudienne](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/thieboudienne)**: rice-and-fish (often grouper or red snapper) with cassava, sweet potato, cabbage, eggplant, carrot, tamarind, all cooked together in one tomato-and-pepper broth. The national dish.
2. **[Mafé](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/mafe)**: peanut stew with beef or lamb, sweet potato, carrot, cabbage. Served with rice. The everyday lunch.
3. **[Yassa Grilled Chicken](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/yassa-grilled-chicken)**: chicken marinated in lemon, onion, Dijon mustard and Cayenne, grilled then simmered with huge quantities of onion.
4. **[Thiof Yassa](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/thiof-yassa)**: grouper or sea bass in the yassa onion-lemon sauce. The fish version.
5. **[Domoda](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/domoda)**: similar to mafé — peanut stew with meat or fish.
6. **[Caldou](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/caldou)**: clear fish soup with rice, lime and chilli. The Casamance speciality.
7. **[Supu Kanja](/recipes/cuisine/senegalese/supu-kanja)**: thick okra-and-fish (often smoked) stew with palm oil.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with bissap (hibiscus drink — sweetened, served cold) or with bouye (baobab-fruit drink) or with Gazelle beer. For a proper thieboudienne meal: everyone gathers around the single pot, eats with the right hand from the communal vessel, the cook keeps adding rice from the bottom until everyone has had their fill.
