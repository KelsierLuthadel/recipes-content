# Hollandse Nieuwe Haring (Dutch New Herring)

![Hollandse Nieuwe Haring](resources/hollandse-nieuwe-haring.jpg)

*The Netherlands' most identity-defining street snack: a single soused North Sea herring (the canonical "Hollandse Nieuwe" arrives in late May / June and is the year's first catch, prized for its fat content), filleted, brined, and served on a small paper plate with raw white onion and tiny chopped sweet pickles. The Dutch eat it standing up at the haringkar (herring stall), holding the tail and tipping the head back to drop the whole fish into the mouth - this is the canonical Dutch street-food posture. Sold from temporary stalls when the new-catch season opens (around 17 May, "Vlaggetjesdag" - Flag Day); year-round at every Dutch market.*

**Serves:** 4

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes

**Cook Time:** None (the fish is already filleted and brined by the fishmonger)

## Overview
Hollandse Nieuwe Haring (Dutch "new herring", also called maatjesharing - "maiden herring" because it's from the first catch of the season before the herring has spawned) is the Netherlands' most distinctive street food. The canonical herring is North Sea herring caught between late May and early July, when the fish has reached optimal fat content but hasn't yet spawned - the flesh is tender, sweet and fatty. Three things distinguish the Dutch preparation. First, the cure: the herring is gibbed (the gills and most of the guts are removed, but the pancreas is left in - the enzymes in the pancreas play a crucial role in the curing process), then brined in a 17% salt brine for around 5 days, then ice-stored at minus 27°C for 48 hours (this freezing step is legally required to kill any parasites). The fillet is then served raw but cured - similar in concept to ceviche or gravadlax but distinctly Dutch in execution. Second, the trim: the head, backbone and pin bones are removed; what remains is a long, tender, light-pink fillet sometimes still showing the tail. Third, the canonical Dutch service: the herring is placed on a small paper plate; a generous scoop of FINELY chopped raw white onion goes alongside; sometimes a small spoonful of chopped sweet pickles. No bread on the plate (the bread comes separately if at all). The eating posture is canonical: hold the tail; tilt your head back; lower the fish from above into the mouth from the tail end; bite from the head end. The Dutch will photograph tourists attempting this - it's a national rite of passage. Three details: USE GENUINE HOLLANDSE NIEUWE (look for the "Hollandse Nieuwe" or "maatjesharing" label; not generic pickled herring), SERVE WITH ONLY ONION AND OPTIONAL PICKLE (a Dutch herring with extra dressings is a tourist version; the purist keeps it minimal), and EAT STANDING UP HOLDING THE TAIL (the canonical posture is part of the cultural identity).

## Ingredients

### Per serving (multiply by 4)
- 1 whole Hollandse Nieuwe Haring fillet (filleted, brined, ready-to-eat - bought from a Dutch fishmonger, herring stall, or specialty shop)
- The herring should be a butterfly fillet (the two sides still joined at the back), pink-fleshed and shiny

### The garnish (canonical)
- 1 small white onion, very finely diced (the Dutch chop it down to 2 mm pieces - finer than you'd think)
- 4 tablespoons total

### Optional garnishes
- 1 tablespoon chopped sweet pickles (gerkjes / cornichons) per serving
- 1 lemon wedge per serving (the modern variant; purists skip)

### To serve
- 4 small white paper plates (the canonical Dutch market presentation)
- 4 small wooden cocktail forks (some Dutch diners refuse forks; eating with the hands is the canonical way)
- A glass of cold Dutch lager (Heineken, Amstel) OR a small glass of jenever
- 4 slices of dense Dutch dark rye bread (roggebrood) on the side (optional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Source the herring
1. Buy from a reputable Dutch fishmonger, herring stall, or specialty fish shop.
2. Look for "Hollandse Nieuwe" or "maatjesharing" on the label.
3. The fish should be pre-filleted, brined, and ready to eat.
4. The flesh should be pink-orange, shiny, slightly translucent, with no fishy smell (just a faint sea-brine).
5. Keep cold until serving.

### Stage 2 - Prepare the garnish
1. Peel a small white onion.
2. Chop it very finely - aim for 2 mm dice (much finer than you might naturally do).
3. Place in a small bowl.
4. (Optional) Chop a small amount of sweet pickles (gerkjes) the same fineness.

### Stage 3 - Plate (canonical Dutch market style)
1. Place 1 herring fillet on each small paper plate.
2. Spoon a generous tablespoon of finely chopped onion alongside the herring.
3. (Optional) Add a small spoonful of chopped pickles.
4. (Optional) Place a wedge of lemon - though Dutch purists skip this.

### Stage 4 - The eating posture (the canonical Dutch experience)
1. Hand each plate to a diner.
2. Each diner picks the herring up by the tail.
3. Holds it above their mouth.
4. Tilts head back.
5. Lowers the herring vertically from above; bites from the tail-end first.
6. Eats in 3-4 large bites, picking up some chopped onion between bites.
7. (Tourists are allowed to cheat - use a fork, eat smaller pieces, take photos.)

### Stage 5 - Serve with the canonical accompaniments
1. A glass of cold Heineken or Amstel.
2. A small plate of dense dark rye bread on the side (for the non-vertical-eating purist).
3. Eat standing up - this is street food.

## Notes
- **Source matters:** a real Hollandse Nieuwe is dramatically different from supermarket pickled herring (rollmops or matjes). Find a Dutch fishmonger, a herring stall, or a specialty fish shop.
- **The season:** the canonical Hollandse Nieuwe season opens around 17 May ("Vlaggetjesdag" - Flag Day in Scheveningen) and runs through June and July. Outside the season, "maatjesharing" is available year-round (deep-frozen and brined; legally required to be frozen).
- **The pancreas:** gibbed herring (Dutch-style) has the pancreas left in. The enzymes are what give the cured flesh its tender, slightly sweet character. Don't try to fully gut a fresh herring at home unless you really know the Dutch technique.
- **Onion fineness:** 2 mm dice. The fine chop is part of the canonical presentation.
- **The eating posture:** holding the tail and tilting back is the cultural marker. Tourists are encouraged to try; locals appreciate the effort.
- **No bread on the plate:** the bread is a separate item, eaten between herring or alongside. The herring itself is the protein course.

## Variations
**Hollandse Nieuwe op brood:** for those who can't manage the standing-up technique, the herring is placed on a slice of dense dark Dutch rye bread with chopped onion - the polite restaurant version.
**Maatjesharing met aardappels (potato variant):** alongside warm new potatoes - the home-cook Sunday-lunch version.
**Salaad van haring (herring salad):** chopped herring + chopped beetroot + chopped apple + sliced gherkin + mayo - the Dutch deli salad.
**Modern Amsterdam herring (with extras):** add finely chopped chives, capers, and a small dollop of crème fraîche - the modernised restaurant version. Purists object; tourists enjoy.
**Herring on toast (the open-sandwich variant):** a slice of dark rye, butter, herring, chopped onion, capers - the Scandinavian-influenced variant.
**Smoked herring (gerookte haring):** smoked rather than soused; different texture and flavour, also Dutch but a different dish.
**Pickled herring rolls (rolmops):** the herring rolled around a pickled onion and gherkin, fixed with a cocktail stick - the German-influenced variant.
**Maatjesharing with a glass of jenever:** the canonical pairing - a small shot of jenever beside the herring is the traditional way.

## Serving
At a Dutch haringkar (herring stall; portable street carts found at every Dutch market) - the canonical setting · at the annual Vlaggetjesdag herring festival in Scheveningen (around 17 May) · at a Dutch fish market (Volendam, Marken) · at an Amsterdam canal-side market · at any Dutch town's market square on market day · at home with a glass of cold lager and a slice of dark rye bread · at a Dutch wedding reception canapé table · as the canonical Dutch "I'm a real local" moment for visitors.

## Storage
- Hollandse Nieuwe is sold ready to eat. Eat the same day for best flavour and freshness.
- Refrigerates 3-4 days in the original packaging or wrapped tight.
- Don't freeze the prepared product - the texture suffers irreversibly.
- The raw whole herring (before brining) is a specialist project; don't attempt at home unless you know the gibbing technique.
- Out of season: maatjesharing from a Dutch fishmonger is available year-round (deep-frozen and brined); the flavour is slightly less vivid than the fresh-catch May herring.
- Open packages should be refrigerated and eaten within 24-48 hours.
