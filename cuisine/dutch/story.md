# Dutch

*The kitchen of the low countries. Stamppot (mashed potato with vegetables), erwtensoep (the famously thick pea soup), the bitterballen at the brown café. A cuisine that's modest at home and explosive in its diaspora: the Indonesian rijsttafel, the Surinamese roti, the Antillean kitchen.*

## A short history

Dutch cooking begins on land the Dutch made themselves. From the 11th century onwards Frisian and Hollander farmers diked, drained and reclaimed the wet North Sea coast into pasture, and the resulting polders became the dairy heartland of Europe. The medieval kitchen ran on butter, buttermilk, hard cheese (Edam and Gouda were already being shipped through the Hanseatic League by the 14th century), pickled and salt-cured herring out of the North Sea, peas and beans, rye bread and beer. Willem Beukelszoon's 14th-century method for gutting and salting herring at sea (the haring kaken) turned the Dutch fishing fleet into the protein supplier for half of Northern Europe and made the herring barrel the foundation of the Dutch Golden Age economy.

The 17th-century Golden Age opened the larder dramatically. The Dutch East India Company (VOC, founded 1602) and its Caribbean sister the West India Company brought pepper, cloves, nutmeg, mace, cinnamon and sugar onto the Amsterdam quay in industrial quantities. The merchant townhouses of Amsterdam and Delft ate richly: spiced beef, sugared fruit, the elaborate spiced honey-cake (taai-taai and ontbijtkoek), the speculaas biscuit. The home kitchen stayed plainer: hutspot (mashed potato with carrot and onion), said to date from the 1574 siege of Leiden when the Spanish abandoned their cooking pots; erwtensoep so thick the spoon stood up; stamppot in every regional variation. The potato arrived in the 18th century and was absorbed in a generation.

The 19th and early 20th centuries kept the home kitchen frugal. A culture that prizes "doe maar gewoon, dan doe je al gek genoeg" (just act normal, that's crazy enough) was never going to develop the show-off court cuisine of France or Italy. The Dutch ate plainly at home and saved the elaboration for the colonies. The Indonesian (formerly Dutch East Indies) influence runs so deep that nasi goreng and bami goreng are now staple Dutch home-cook dishes. The rijsttafel, the Dutch codification of Indonesian feast eating into twelve to twenty small dishes laid out on a long table, is as much a Dutch invention as an Indonesian one. It emerged in the 1880s in the colonial planters' clubs of Java and came back to the Hague when the planters retired.

Indonesian independence in 1949 brought 300,000 Dutch-Indonesians back to the Netherlands with the recipes intact, and the Indonesian restaurant became a Dutch institution. The Surinamese-Caribbean kitchen, from the former Dutch colonies of Suriname, Curacao and Aruba, is the second great diaspora addition. Surinamese independence in 1975 sent half the country to the Netherlands, and Suriname's roti culture (East Indian, African and Javanese all in one wrap) is now an Amsterdam staple. The Antillean cuisine (Caribbean with Dutch overlays) has been integrating since the 1950s.

At home the Dutch still eat plainly. Stamppot for winter weeknights, herring (raw, with onion, in a roll; the famous "Hollandse nieuwe" eaten by tilting the head back and lowering the fish in tail-first) at the fish stalls, bitterballen with mustard at the brown café (bruine kroeg), pannenkoeken for the kids. The country's most influential modern cooking happens at the borrowed colonial table.

## Regional variations

- **North Holland (Amsterdam)**: codified mainstream. Stamppot, the brown café tradition.
- **Friesland (north)**: distinct identity. Sugar bread, dairy-heavy.
- **Brabant and Limburg (south)**: Burgundian-leaning. Asparagus, more pork, vlaai (the regional fruit tart).
- **Zeeland (south-west coast)**: shellfish. Mussels from the Oosterschelde.

## Signature ingredients

- **Potatoes**: boiled, mashed into stamppot.
- **Cabbage, kale, sauerkraut, endive**: the vegetables that get mashed into stamppot.
- **Rookworst**: smoked sausage, the universal stamppot accessory.
- **Herring (haring)**: salt-cured, eaten raw with onion.
- **Stroop (treacle/syrup)**: on stamppot, on poffertjes.
- **Cheese**: Gouda, Edam, Leerdammer; the world's most-traded cheeses.
- **Mustard (mosterd)**: especially Zaanse, with bitterballen.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Stamppot Boerenkool](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/stamppot-boerenkool)**: mashed potato with kale, served with rookworst, mustard and a well of melted butter. The winter weeknight standard.
2. **[Hutspot](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/hutspot)**: mashed potato with carrot and onion. Said to date from the 1574 siege of Leiden, when the Spanish abandoned their cooking pots.
3. **[Erwtensoep (Snert)](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/erwtensoep)**: pea soup so thick a spoon stands up in it; with smoked sausage, bacon and celery. The winter classic; "snert" only refers to it when the spoon stands up.
4. **[Hachee](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/hachee)**: beef-and-onion stew slow-cooked with vinegar, cloves and bay leaf. Served over potato and red cabbage.
5. **[Amsterdam Pizza Dog](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/amsterdam-pizza-dog)**: a uniquely Amsterdam late-night fusion. Hot dog with pizza-style toppings on a bun, sold at the city's broodjes carts.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Heineken (or Amstel, or a Belgian beer; the brown-café list goes long), or with jenever (Dutch genever, the world's original gin, often drunk as a chaser with beer in the "kopstoot", "headbutt"). End with stroopwafels (caramel-filled syrup waffles) or with poffertjes (small pancakes with butter and icing sugar).
