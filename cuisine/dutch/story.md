# Dutch

*The kitchen of the low countries. Stamppot (mashed potato with vegetables), erwtensoep (the famously thick pea soup), the bitterballen at the brown café — a cuisine that's modest at home and explosive in its diaspora: the Indonesian rijsttafel, the Surinamese roti, the Antillean kitchen.*

## A short history

Dutch cooking is the food of a small Northern European country that has eaten plainly at home for centuries — boiled potato, vegetables, simple meat preparations, dairy in abundance — and absorbed entire cuisines through its colonial empire. The Indonesian (formerly Dutch East Indies) influence is so deep that nasi goreng and bami goreng are now staple Dutch home-cook dishes; the rijsttafel (the Dutch codification of Indonesian feast eating) is a Dutch invention as much as an Indonesian one.

The Surinamese-Caribbean kitchen (from the former Dutch colonies of Suriname, Curaçao, Aruba) is the second great diaspora addition. Suriname's roti culture is now an Amsterdam staple. The Antillean cuisine — Caribbean with Dutch overlays — has been integrating since the 1950s.

At home the Dutch eat plainly. Stamppot for winter weeknights, herring (raw, with onion, in a roll, the famous "Hollandse nieuwe" eaten by tilting the head back and lowering the fish in tail-first) at the fish stalls, bitterballen with mustard at the brown café (bruine kroeg), pannenkoeken for the kids.

## Regional variations

- **North Holland (Amsterdam)**: codified mainstream. Stamppot, the brown café tradition.
- **Friesland (north)**: distinct identity. Sugar bread, dairy-heavy.
- **Brabant and Limburg (south)**: Burgundian-leaning. Asparagus, more pork, vlaai (the regional fruit tart).
- **Zeeland (south-west coast)**: shellfish. Mussels from the Oosterschelde.

## Signature ingredients

- **Potatoes**: boiled, mashed into stamppot.
- **Cabbage, kale, sauerkraut, endive**: the vegetables that get mashed into stamppot.
- **Rookworst**: smoked sausage, the universal stamppot accessory.
- **Herring (haring)**: salt-cured, eaten raw with onion.
- **Stroop (treacle/syrup)**: on stamppot, on poffertjes.
- **Cheese**: Gouda, Edam, Leerdammer — the world's most-traded cheeses.
- **Mustard (mosterd)**: especially Zaanse, with bitterballen.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Stamppot Boerenkool](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/stamppot-boerenkool)**: mashed potato with kale, served with rookworst, mustard and a well of melted butter. The winter weeknight standard.
2. **[Hutspot](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/hutspot)**: mashed potato with carrot and onion. Said to date from the 1574 siege of Leiden when the Spanish abandoned their cooking pots.
3. **[Erwtensoep (Snert)](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/erwtensoep)**: pea soup so thick a spoon stands up in it; with smoked sausage, bacon and celery. The winter classic — "snert" only refers to it when the spoon stands up.
4. **[Hachee](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/hachee)**: beef-and-onion stew slow-cooked with vinegar, cloves and bay leaf. Served over potato and red cabbage.
5. **[Amsterdam Pizza Dog](/recipes/cuisine/dutch/amsterdam-pizza-dog)**: a uniquely Amsterdam late-night fusion — hot dog with pizza-style toppings on a bun, sold at the city's broodjes carts.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Heineken (or Amstel, or a Belgian beer — the brown-café list goes long), or with jenever (Dutch genever — the world's original gin, often drunk as a chaser with beer in the "kopstoot" — "headbutt"). End with stroopwafels (caramel-filled syrup waffles) or with poffertjes (small pancakes with butter and icing sugar).
