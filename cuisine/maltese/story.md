# Maltese

*The kitchen of the Mediterranean's smallest island nation. Rabbit stew (the national dish), the lampuki (dorado) pie, the timpana pastry-pasta bake, and a cuisine that synthesises Italian, North African, Spanish, French and British across millennia.*

## A short history

Maltese cooking sits on a small but strategically located archipelago that has been ruled by Phoenicians, Carthaginians, Romans, Arabs, Normans, Spanish, French Knights Hospitaller, French Napoleon, and the British. Each ruler left a culinary mark; the result is a Mediterranean kitchen with strong Sicilian-Italian base and significant North African, Spanish, French and British layers.

Rabbit (fenek) is the most-loved meat and the national meat. Fenkata (rabbit feast) is a Maltese social ritual — friends gather for a long evening of multiple rabbit courses with red wine and crusty bread. The lampuki (dorado fish) pie is the autumn seasonal dish; the timpana (pastry-wrapped baked pasta with bolognese and bechamel and eggs) is the celebration plate; pastizzi (cheese or pea-filled pastry parcels) is the universal breakfast and snack.

The Phoenician-era olive oil tradition, the Arab-era kunserva (sun-dried tomato paste — a remarkable staple of Maltese kitchen), the Norman-era ricotta-and-pasta tradition, and the British-era tea-and-Maltese-loaf are all visible on a modern Maltese table.

## Regional variations

- **Valletta and central**: codified mainstream. Stuffat tal-fenek, timpana.
- **Gozo (smaller island)**: the cheese tradition (gbejna — small ricotta-like cheeses), the Gozo ftira (the Maltese pizza-like flatbread).
- **Mdina and the south**: more traditional, more rabbit.
- **Sliema and the coast**: more international, the urban dining scene.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rabbit (fenek)**: the national meat.
- **Tomato (and tomato paste — kunserva)**: in nearly every sauce.
- **Olive oil**: heavy use.
- **Capers and olives**: wild Maltese capers are exceptional.
- **Garlic**: heavy hand, especially with rabbit.
- **Lampuki**: dorado/dolphinfish, the prized autumn fish.
- **Sheep's milk cheese (gbejna)**: fresh, dried or peppered.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Stuffat tal-Fenek](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/stuffat-tal-fenek)**: rabbit slow-braised in red wine, garlic, tomato and bay leaf. The national dish.
2. **[Lampuki Pie](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/lampuki-pie)**: dorado pie with capers, olives, raisins, spinach and tomato in a buttery crust. The autumn (August-November lampuki season) plate.
3. **[Timpana](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/timpana)**: short pasta with bolognese, béchamel, eggs and chicken liver, all baked in a thick puff-pastry crust. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Bragioli](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/bragioli)**: thin beef rolls stuffed with eggs, bacon, breadcrumbs, parsley and garlic, braised in tomato sauce. The Maltese braciole.
5. **[Aljotta](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/aljotta)**: garlicky fish soup with tomato, rice and marjoram. The summer-evening bowl.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Cisk lager (the national beer) or with Maltese wine (the local Marsovin and Meridiana). End with kannoli (Sicilian-Maltese hybrid filled with ricotta and candied peel) or with imqaret (date-stuffed pastry diamonds).
