# Maltese

*The kitchen of the Mediterranean's smallest island nation. Rabbit stew (the national dish), the lampuki (dorado) pie, the timpana pastry-pasta bake, and a cuisine that pulls Italian, North African, Spanish, French and British strands together across millennia.*

## A short history

Maltese cooking sits on a small but strategically placed archipelago that has hosted Phoenicians, Carthaginians, Romans, Arabs, Normans, Spanish, French Knights Hospitaller, French Napoleon, and the British. Each ruler left a mark; the result is a Mediterranean kitchen with a strong Sicilian-Italian base and significant North African, Spanish, French and British layers.

The deepest roots are pre-classical. The Ġgantija temples on Gozo, built around 3600 BCE, are older than the Egyptian pyramids, and the people who raised them grew barley, kept sheep, harvested wild capers from the cliffs and salt from the limestone pans that still produce sea salt for the modern kitchen. Phoenician traders arrived around 800 BCE and brought olive cultivation, the wine press, and the early instinct for sun-drying and salting fish that still defines the island's preserved-food shelf. Roman occupation from 218 BCE folded Malta into the wider Mediterranean economy and laid down the wheat-and-olive-oil base on which everything since has been built. The Romans also planted the fig, the almond and the carob (St John's bread, used in carob syrup and biscuits to this day) that still grow wild across the islands.

The Arab period from 870 to 1091 was decisive. North African Aghlabid rule introduced the citrus tree (orange, lemon, bitter orange), sugar cane, rice, cotton, and the irrigation techniques that still water the small terraced gardens of the interior. The Maltese language itself is a Semitic tongue descended from medieval Arabic, with Italian and English overlays; you can hear the Arab past in the words for many ingredients (qara' for pumpkin, basal for onion, hut for fish). The pastizzi pastry technique, the love of cumin and coriander seed, the way preserved fish gets used as a flavouring rather than a centrepiece, all trace to that era. Norman conquest in 1091 tied the island culturally to Sicily and put Latin Christianity, ricotta-and-pasta cooking and the medieval European spice trade on top of the Arab base.

The Knights Hospitaller arrived in 1530 after Charles V granted them the island, and ruled for 268 years until Napoleon turned up in 1798. The Knights, a multinational chivalric order with langues (national divisions) from France, Italy, Spain, Germany and Provence, ran what amounted to an early Europe-wide kitchen. French pastry technique, Spanish saffron and almond traditions, and Italian pasta cooking all settled into the Maltese repertoire. The Columbian Exchange came in during this same period: tomatoes, potatoes, peppers, beans, maize and turkey all arrived from the Americas through Spanish and Italian trade. The Maltese took to the tomato with such enthusiasm that kunserva (the deeply concentrated sun-dried tomato paste) became a defining ingredient by the 18th century, the bittersweet umami backbone of half the country's stews.

The British seized Malta in 1800 and held it until independence in 1964. Naval garrisons stationed thousands of British sailors on the island for nearly two centuries, and the kitchen quietly absorbed tea-time, baked beans on toast (called *fażola fil-bażwa*), Worcestershire sauce in stews, and the Maltese loaf (a slightly sweet enriched bread that sits between English and Mediterranean traditions). The Second World War nearly broke the country: the Siege of Malta from 1940 to 1942 saw the islanders awarded the George Cross for civilian endurance, and rationing pushed the kitchen back to its most rural roots. Post-war recovery brought a tourism boom from the 1960s on, and independence in 1964 freed Malta to build a national cuisine on the inherited layers. Today's table holds the timpana on Sunday, the fenkata on a long evening with friends, pastizzi at the kiosk on the way to work, and the lampuki pie in autumn, all underpinned by olive oil from trees the Phoenicians might have planted.

## Regional variations

- **Valletta and central**: codified mainstream. Stuffat tal-fenek, timpana.
- **Gozo (smaller island)**: the cheese tradition (gbejna, small ricotta-like cheeses), the Gozo ftira (the Maltese pizza-like flatbread).
- **Mdina and the south**: more traditional, more rabbit.
- **Sliema and the coast**: more international, the urban dining scene.

## Signature ingredients

- **Rabbit (fenek)**: the national meat.
- **Tomato (and tomato paste, kunserva)**: in nearly every sauce.
- **Olive oil**: heavy use.
- **Capers and olives**: wild Maltese capers are exceptional.
- **Garlic**: heavy hand, especially with rabbit.
- **Lampuki**: dorado/dolphinfish, the prized autumn fish.
- **Sheep's milk cheese (gbejna)**: fresh, dried or peppered.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Stuffat tal-Fenek](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/stuffat-tal-fenek)**: rabbit slow-braised in red wine, garlic, tomato and bay leaf. The national dish.
2. **[Lampuki Pie](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/lampuki-pie)**: dorado pie with capers, olives, raisins, spinach and tomato in a buttery crust. The autumn (August to November lampuki season) plate.
3. **[Timpana](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/timpana)**: short pasta with bolognese, béchamel, eggs and chicken liver, all baked in a thick puff-pastry crust. The Sunday family dish.
4. **[Bragioli](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/bragioli)**: thin beef rolls stuffed with eggs, bacon, breadcrumbs, parsley and garlic, braised in tomato sauce. The Maltese braciole.
5. **[Aljotta](/recipes/cuisine/maltese/aljotta)**: garlicky fish soup with tomato, rice and marjoram. The summer-evening bowl.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Cisk lager (the national beer) or with Maltese wine (the local Marsovin and Meridiana). End with kannoli (Sicilian-Maltese hybrid filled with ricotta and candied peel) or with imqaret (date-stuffed pastry diamonds).
