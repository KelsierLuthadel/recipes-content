# Turkish

*The cuisine of an empire: Ottoman court kitchens taking from Persia, Arabia, the Balkans, the Caucasus and Greece, then refining the lot into the world's grilled-meat-and-yoghurt benchmark.*

## A short history

Turkish food starts on horseback. The Turkic peoples of Central Asia were nomads who herded sheep and goats across the steppe, and the foundations of the modern table are already visible in that period: yoghurt (a Turkic word that has travelled into nearly every language), skewered meat cooked over open flame, and the small filled dumpling that would become manti.

The westward migration changed everything. Passing through Persia from the 11th century onward, the Seljuks absorbed rice technique, the pilaf principle, the meatball, and a far broader spice cabinet. Arriving in Anatolia they inherited a Byzantine larder of olives, sea fish, leafy greens, eggplant and pulses, the eastern Mediterranean shopping list more or less intact.

The synthesis was completed at Topkapi. From 1465 to 1839 the Ottoman palace kitchens fed thousands daily, employing specialist cooks for soups, kebabs, breads, sherbets and the sugar-and-syrup desserts that became baklava. The court codified dolma, the meze table, the long slow stews known as sulu yemek, the elaborate rice pilaf, and the coffee ceremony that gave the world a new social ritual. Cooks moved between palace and city, and the recipes leaked outward through the empire to the Balkans, the Levant, North Africa and beyond.

Mass migration in the 19th and 20th centuries carried the cuisine further: Turkish workers in Germany and the Low Countries built the doner kebab into Europe's defining street food. The anchor principles still hold today. The grill (mangal) and the slow simmer (sulu yemek), built on yoghurt, lemon, olive oil, sumac, pul biber, and a herb-and-onion meze table.

## Regional variations

- **Istanbul and the Marmara**: the imperial cuisine. Olive oil dishes (the family known as zeytinyağlılar), Bosphorus fish, and the most refined version of the meze tradition. The food here is the one most travellers think of as Turkish.
- **Aegean (Izmir region)**: olive-oil heavy and herbaceous. Wild greens, light fish, sweet white onions, and a lighter cooking hand than the rest of the country.
- **Black Sea (Trabzon, Rize)**: corn rather than wheat, anchovy (hamsi) in everything, kale-like greens, more dairy. The wettest, greenest stretch of the coast and the most distinct kitchen.
- **Eastern (Erzurum, Kars)**: the Caucasian edge. Cevizli sucuk (walnut-and-grape-must sweets), more dairy, spiced sausage, charcoal-grilled lamb across the board.
- **South-Eastern (Gaziantep, Urfa, Sanliurfa)**: the kebab capital. Adana, Urfa and sis kebab were codified here. Pistachio baklava is a Gaziantep speciality. The hottest, most Arab-leaning region, and the hottest food.
- **Central Anatolian (Konya)**: bread country. Etli ekmek (long flatbread with minced lamb), simple lamb stews, tarhana soup from fermented yoghurt and wheat.

You can eat Turkish food for a month and never repeat a region.

## Signature ingredients

Eight building blocks define the cuisine.

- **Yoghurt** is the foundation. Served plain alongside almost everything, beaten into garlic and herbs for cacik or haydari, stirred into hot soups, and crucially cooked as a sauce in dishes like manti and Iskender kebab. Tempering yoghurt so it does not split is a defining technique.
- **Pul biber** is the coarse red chilli flake on every table. Warm rather than fierce, slightly oily, sweeter than its Italian cousin.
- **Sumac** is the dried crushed berry of a Mediterranean shrub. Tart, fruity, the colour of dried blood, sprinkled over kebabs, onions and salads.
- **Lemon** carries the acid line. Squeezed at the table over grilled meat, fish, soups and pulses.
- **Olive oil** runs through the western kitchen, the medium for the entire family of cold vegetable dishes that bear its name.
- **Mint, parsley and dill** form the standing herb wall. Bunched at every greengrocer, used by the handful rather than the sprig.
- **Lamb** is the default red meat. Charcoal-grilled, slow-cooked with vegetables, minced for kofte, layered in pide.
- **[Bulgur](/ingredients/bulgur)** is the working grain. Cracked parboiled wheat, used for pilav, in kofte, and as the base of kisir, the Turkish answer to tabbouleh.

## Learn it in seven recipes

These seven dishes between them cover the grill, the stovetop, the oven and the syrup pot. Cook them in order and the cuisine opens up.

1. **[Adana kebab](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/adana-kebab)**: minced lamb hand-mixed with pul biber and pressed onto a flat skewer, grilled hard over charcoal. The defining kebab of the south-east, named for the city that codified it.
2. **[Kofte](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/kofte)**: the everyday Turkish meatball. Lamb or beef bound with onion, parsley and bread, panfried or grilled. The base recipe everyone's grandmother knows, with a thousand regional twists.
3. **[Imam bayildi](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/imam-bayildi)**: eggplant stuffed with onion, tomato and garlic, braised in olive oil and served cold. The most famous of the zeytinyağlılar, and the gateway to the entire olive-oil-vegetable family.
4. **[Pilav](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/side-dishes/pilav)**: long-grain rice toasted with broken vermicelli in butter, then steamed in stock. Served with almost every meal. Master this and Turkish, Persian and Lebanese rice cookery all open up.
5. **[Manti](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/manti)**: tiny lamb-stuffed dumplings boiled and dressed with garlic yoghurt and pul-biber butter. The clearest line back to the Central Asian Turkic roots, and the dish that announces a serious cook.
6. **[Pide](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/pide)**: the boat-shaped flatbread baked with toppings of minced lamb, cheese, egg or spinach. The Turkish answer to pizza, older than its Italian cousin, and a weekday staple across the country.
7. **[Baklava](/recipes/cuisine/turkish/desserts/baklava)**: layered filo pastry with pistachio or walnut, drenched in lemon-spiked sugar syrup the moment it comes out of the oven. The final flourish of the Ottoman court, and still the best argument for sugar that any cuisine has ever made.

Cook the kebab and the kofte and you have the grill. Cook the imam and the pilav and you have the everyday vegetable table. The manti and the pide are the dough work. The baklava is the destination. Together they map the cuisine end to end.
