# Simit

![Simit](resources/simit.jpg)

*Turkey's sesame-encrusted ring bread: a soft wheat-flour dough shaped into rings, dipped briefly in molasses-water for the canonical mahogany top, then pressed into a plate of sesame seeds till every surface is coated, and baked till the outside crisps and the inside stays soft. The Istanbul street bread sold from glass-fronted carts on every corner of the city.*

**Serves:** 8 simit

**Prep Time:** 30 minutes (plus 1.5 hours rising)

**Cook Time:** 22 minutes

## Overview
Simit is Istanbul's signature street bread and one of the most iconic foods of Turkey: a yeasted wheat-flour bread shaped into a ring (about 18 cm in diameter), dipped briefly in a molasses-and-water mixture (pekmez su; the canonical Turkish technique that gives simit its distinctive mahogany top), pressed into a wide plate of sesame seeds till every surface is generously coated, and baked till the outside is crisp and deep-coloured and the inside stays soft, chewy and slightly steamy. Sold from glass-fronted red carts on virtually every street corner of Istanbul (the simitçi cart is part of the visual landscape of the city), simit is what Turkish people eat for breakfast on the way to work (with strong sweet tea and a wedge of feta or cheese), as a mid-morning snack at the office, as a child's after-school treat, and as part of every Turkish breakfast spread (kahvaltı) alongside cheeses, olives, jams, honey, eggs and tomatoes. The dish is closely related to Greek koulouri, Lebanese ka'ak, and the wider regional sesame-ring family; the Istanbul simit is the most iconic of them. Three details define proper simit. First, the molasses dip. Pekmez (grape molasses) or pomegranate molasses, thinned with water, is the canonical Turkish dip; this is what gives the dark colour and faint caramel sweetness. Skipping the dip gives a pale insipid simit. Second, generous sesame coating. The proper Istanbul simit has a thick layer of toasted sesame seeds covering every visible surface. Skimpy sesame is wrong. Third, the ring shape. Two strands of dough twisted together (sometimes), shaped into a ring with the centre hole about 4-5 cm across. The twist gives the visual texture; the ring shape is what makes it a simit.

## Ingredients

### Dough
- 600 g strong bread flour
- 10 g instant dried yeast
- 2 tablespoons caster sugar
- 2 teaspoons fine sea salt
- 3 tablespoons olive oil
- 380 ml warm water

### Molasses dip
- 4 tablespoons grape molasses (pekmez; or substitute with pomegranate molasses; or 2 tablespoons treacle thinned with 2 tablespoons hot water)
- 200 ml warm water

### Sesame coating
- 300 g raw sesame seeds (lightly toasted in a dry pan for 2-3 minutes till slightly golden and fragrant)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Make the dough
1. In a large bowl, whisk together the flour, yeast, sugar and salt.
2. Add the olive oil and warm water; stir to combine.
3. Knead for 8-10 minutes on a lightly floured surface till smooth and elastic.
4. Place in an oiled bowl; cover with a damp cloth; let rise 1.5 hours at room temperature till doubled.

### Stage 2 - Toast the sesame seeds
1. While the dough rises, heat a wide heavy frying pan over medium heat.
2. Add the raw sesame seeds in batches (about 100 g at a time).
3. Toast 2-3 minutes, stirring constantly, till the seeds are lightly golden and fragrant.
4. Don't let them brown deeply; they finish browning in the oven.
5. Tip into a wide shallow plate (large enough to dip a ring into).

### Stage 3 - Prepare the molasses dip
1. Combine the grape molasses (or substitute) with the warm water in a wide shallow bowl.
2. Whisk to combine; the mixture should be a thin reddish-brown liquid.

### Stage 4 - Shape the simit
1. Knock back the risen dough; divide into 8 equal pieces (about 125 g each).
2. Working with one piece at a time, divide it in half.
3. Roll each half into a long thin rope (about 30 cm long and 1.5 cm thick).
4. Place the two ropes side by side; twist them together loosely (about 3 twists).
5. Form into a ring by joining the ends; pinch firmly to seal.
6. The finished ring should be about 18 cm in diameter with a 4-5 cm hole in the centre.
7. Place on a baking sheet lined with parchment.
8. Repeat with the remaining dough pieces.

### Stage 5 - Dip in molasses
1. Take one shaped ring; submerge briefly in the molasses-water (count to 3).
2. Lift out; let excess drip off.

### Stage 6 - Coat in sesame
1. Immediately press the wet ring into the plate of toasted sesame seeds.
2. Turn to coat all sides; press firmly so the seeds stick.
3. Lift carefully; place on a fresh sheet of parchment paper on a baking sheet.
4. Repeat with the remaining rings; you should have 8 simit total.

### Stage 7 - Second prove
1. Cover the coated rings loosely with a damp cloth.
2. Let prove 20 minutes at room temperature till slightly puffed.

### Stage 8 - Bake
1. Preheat the oven to 210°C (410°F).
2. Place the trays in the oven.
3. Bake 18-22 minutes till the simit are deep mahogany-brown all over and sound hollow when tapped on the bottom.
4. If they're browning too fast, cover loosely with foil for the final 5 minutes.

### Stage 9 - Cool slightly and serve
1. Transfer to a wire rack to cool for 5-10 minutes.
2. Serve warm with strong Turkish tea (çay), a wedge of feta cheese, olives and tomato.
3. Or wrap in paper for breakfast on the go.

## Notes
- **Molasses dip gives the mahogany colour:** the canonical Turkish technique uses grape molasses (pekmez) thinned with water. Pomegranate molasses works as substitute; treacle-and-water as a last resort. Skipping the dip gives pale unappetising simit.
- **Toast the sesame first:** raw sesame doesn't bloom properly in the oven; pre-toasting gives the nutty depth that makes simit distinctive.
- **Press the seeds firmly:** simit should have a thick layer of sesame covering everything. Skimpy coating gives a bald-patches simit which isn't right.
- **The twist is canonical:** two ropes twisted together give the distinctive visual texture. You can skip the twist and form from a single rope (simpler), but the result is less iconic.
- **Don't underbake:** simit needs to be properly deep-brown for the right flavour and the proper crispy outside.

## Variations
**Antep-style simit:** thinner and crisper; roll the ropes thinner and bake 2-3 minutes longer. The Gaziantep version.
**Cheese-stuffed simit:** flatten the dough rope, lay a strip of feta down the centre, fold the dough over to enclose, then form the twist and ring. Common modern Turkish café variation.
**Black-cumin simit (çörek otlu simit):** add 1 tablespoon of nigella seeds (çörek otu) to the sesame coating; gives a deeper darker flavour.
**Mini simit:** divide the dough into 16 smaller pieces; shape into smaller rings (about 10 cm diameter); bake 15 minutes. Party snacks.

## Serving
At Turkish breakfast (kahvaltı) with feta or beyaz peynir cheese, black and green olives, sliced tomato and cucumber, jam, honey, butter, scrambled eggs (menemen if you're going proper Turkish), and many small glasses of strong sweet tea. Or as a quick mid-morning snack with a glass of çay. Or stuffed with cheese, tomato and rocket as a Turkish-style sandwich.

## Storage
- Best eaten warm and fresh.
- Keeps in a sealed bag at room temperature 1 day; refrigerated 3 days.
- Reheat in a 180°C / 350°F oven for 5-6 minutes to refresh.
- Don't microwave; the simit goes leathery.
- Freezes 2 months in a sealed bag; defrost at room temperature for 2 hours and warm in the oven before serving.
- Day-old simit makes excellent croutons or breadcrumbs for soups and salads.
