# Turkish Pilav

![Turkish Pilav](resources/pilav.jpg)

*Turkey's pillowy rice pilaf: long-grain rice cooked in butter and chicken stock with toasted vermicelli noodles, finished with toasted pine nuts and currants.*

**Serves:** 4-6

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus 30 minutes rice soaking)

**Cook Time:** 35 minutes

## Overview
Turkish pilav (pirinç pilavı) is one of Turkey's most foundational dishes and the canonical accompaniment to grilled meats, kebabs, stews and dolma. Long-grain rice: baldo is the canonical Turkish choice, but basmati works: cooks in a base of melted butter and toasted broken vermicelli noodles, then simmers in well-seasoned chicken stock till the grains are fluffy and the vermicelli has cooked into the rice giving the pilaf its golden-flecked appearance. The vermicelli is non-negotiable: it's what makes Turkish pilav distinct from Persian or Indian rice, and the broken noodles must turn deep golden in the butter before the rice joins, or you end up with a bland pilaf. The rice itself wants rinsing and a 30-minute warm-water soak so the grains separate after cooking. Lid stays on through the fifteen-minute simmer and a ten-minute rest off the heat: every lid-lift loses steam. Often dressed up for special occasions with toasted pine nuts, currants and a touch of cinnamon (iç pilav, the celebration version, adds diced chicken liver and stuffs a whole roast chicken).

## Ingredients

### Pilav
- 400 g long-grain rice (baldo, basmati, or long-grain American)
- 80 g unsalted butter
- 2 tablespoons olive oil
- 80 g vermicelli noodles (broken into small 1 cm pieces; or shop-bought broken vermicelli; sometimes labelled "şehriye")
- 1 small onion (finely chopped, optional but very Turkish)
- 800 ml hot chicken stock (or vegetable stock)
- 1 ½ teaspoons fine sea salt (taste before adding; the stock is often salty)
- 1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
- ½ teaspoon ground allspice
- ½ teaspoon ground black pepper

### Pine nut and currant garnish
- 40 g pine nuts
- 30 g currants (or sultanas, soaked in warm water for 10 minutes)
- 2 tablespoons butter (for toasting the pine nuts)

### To finish
- 1 tablespoon fresh dill (chopped) - optional
- 1 tablespoon fresh parsley (chopped) - optional

## Method

### Stage 1 - Rinse and soak the rice
1. Place the rice in a sieve or fine colander; rinse under cold running water for 30 seconds, tossing the grains, till the water runs almost clear.
2. Tip the rinsed rice into a wide bowl; cover with warm water (about 40°C / hand-warm) by 3 cm.
3. Let stand 30 minutes; the grains will hydrate and turn slightly opaque.
4. Drain thoroughly.

### Stage 2 - Toast the vermicelli
1. Melt the butter with the olive oil in a wide heavy saucepan (with a tight-fitting lid) over medium heat.
2. Add the broken vermicelli; cook for 3-4 minutes, stirring frequently, till the noodles are deep golden-brown all over.
3. Don't let them burn; the kitchen will smell properly nutty.

### Stage 3 - Add the onion (if using)
1. Add the finely chopped onion to the pan with the golden vermicelli.
2. Cook 4-5 minutes till the onion is soft and translucent.

### Stage 4 - Toast the rice
1. Add the drained rice to the pan.
2. Stir to coat in the buttery noodles for 1-2 minutes.

### Stage 5 - Add stock and simmer
1. Pour in the hot chicken stock.
2. Add the salt (taste first), cinnamon, allspice and pepper.
3. Stir once to distribute.
4. Bring to a gentle simmer.
5. Reduce to lowest heat; cover with the tight-fitting lid.
6. Cook 15 minutes covered (don't lift the lid).

### Stage 6 - Rest off heat
1. Take off the heat; keep the lid on.
2. Let rest 10 minutes; the rice finishes steaming.
3. Don't lift the lid early; the steam needs to penetrate the top layer.

### Stage 7 - Toast the pine nuts and prepare the garnish
1. While the rice rests, melt 2 tablespoons of butter in a small pan over medium heat.
2. Add the pine nuts; toast for 2-3 minutes till golden.
3. Add the drained currants; toss 30 seconds.
4. Set aside.

### Stage 8 - Fluff and garnish
1. Uncover the rice; fluff gently with a fork, lifting from the bottom to release any compressed grains.
2. Scatter the toasted pine nuts and currants over (with the buttery juices).
3. Add the chopped dill and parsley if using.
4. Serve immediately as a side.

## Notes
- **Rinse and soak:** both steps matter. Rinsing removes the surface starch (preventing sticky rice); soaking helps the grains stay long and separate.
- **Toast the vermicelli deep golden:** the proper golden colour is essential for the pilav's character. Under-toasted = bland; over-toasted (burnt) = bitter.
- **Don't lift the lid:** the rice cooks by absorption-and-steam. Every glance loses steam. 15 minutes covered cooking, then 10 minutes covered resting.
- **1:2 ratio:** 400 g rice to 800 ml stock is the proper Turkish ratio (slightly less than the 1:2 ratio works for soaked-and-drained rice; without soaking use 1:2.25).
- **Optional onion:** some Turkish cooks include onion, some don't. With onion gives more aromatic depth; without is cleaner and more pilaf-like.

## Variations
**İç pilav (special-occasion pilaf):** add 200 g of diced chicken liver (sautéed in butter for 3 minutes) to the rice along with the stock; gives the proper Anatolian celebration pilav. Often used as stuffing for whole roasted chicken.
**Saffron pilav (zaferanli pilav):** add a generous pinch of saffron threads infused in 2 tablespoons of warm water to the stock; gives a golden colour and floral note. Festive version.
**Tomato pilav (domates pilavı):** add 100 g of finely chopped tomato to the rice with the stock; gives a slightly orange-tinted pilaf with subtle tomato flavour.
**Bulgur pilav:** swap the rice entirely for medium bulgur wheat; cook the same way but reduce cooking time to 12 minutes (see bulgur-pilav for the full version).

## Serving
On warm plates as a side alongside grilled meats (kuzu şiş, köfte, iskender), stews (hünkar beğendi, etli kuru fasulye), dolma (stuffed peppers, tomatoes, grape leaves), or any Turkish main course. A small bowl of plain yogurt on the side. With pickled vegetables.

## Storage
- Keeps refrigerated 3 days; reheat gently with a splash of stock or water in a covered pan over low heat.
- Don't microwave; the rice goes off-texture.
- Freezes 2 months in portioned containers; defrost in the fridge.
- Day-old pilav fries beautifully into a Turkish pilaf-omelette; pan-fry with eggs for breakfast.
- The pine-nut-and-currant garnish is best added fresh each time.
