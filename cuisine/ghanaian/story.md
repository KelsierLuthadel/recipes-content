# Ghanaian

*The kitchen of West Africa's gold coast. Jollof rice (and the eternal argument with Nigeria over whose is better), waakye (rice-and-beans, the working breakfast), and the groundnut soup that turned peanuts into a national obsession.*

## A short history

Ghanaian cooking sits on the West African coastal-and-forest tradition that runs back thousands of years. The forest belt that became modern Ghana gave its early inhabitants yams, palm oil, kola nuts and the bushmeat-and-snail proteins of the rainforest, while the savannah north grew millet, sorghum and the groundnut. The Akan-speaking peoples who built the great forest kingdoms (Bono, Ashanti, Akwamu, Denkyira) from the 11th century onward developed the pounded-starch-and-soup pattern that is still the universal celebration meal: fufu in the bowl, hot soup poured over, eaten with the right hand. The technique of pounding cooked yam, plantain or cassava in a tall wooden mortar with a long pestle is one of the oldest cooking actions on the continent.

The Atlantic contact from the 1470s rewired the kitchen. The Portuguese built Elmina Castle in 1482 (the first European trading fort in sub-Saharan Africa) and brought New World foods through it: tomatoes, scotch bonnet peppers, maize, cassava and groundnuts all landed on the Gold Coast in the 16th century, and Ghanaian cooks absorbed them so completely that none of them feel imported today. Cassava replaced or joined yam in many fufu kitchens. Maize became the base of kenkey and banku, the fermented corn doughs of the Ga and Fante coast. Peppers and tomatoes became the foundation of every stew. The same centuries carried the horrors of the transatlantic slave trade out of Cape Coast and Elmina; the Ghanaian dishes that travelled the Middle Passage seeded a lot of what became Caribbean and southern US cooking (red rice, peanut soup, the okra stew).

The Ashanti Empire (1701-1901) codified the inland kitchen at the same time as the coastal trading culture was building its own hybrid shelf. Ashanti court protocol made fufu and groundnut soup the food of celebration; the long, low simmer of the soup pot, the smoking of fish and game, the heavy reliance on palm oil pressed fresh from the Bono and Ashanti forests, all consolidated into a recognisably Ghanaian cuisine. The Trans-Saharan trade that connected the northern savannah to the Sahel kept millet porridge (tuo zaafi), grilled meat and dried-spice flavours strong in the Dagomba and Mamprusi kitchens of the north.

British rule over the Gold Coast (1874 to 1957) layered on tea, bread and a curry tradition (mostly carried in by Indian colonial-administration imports). The Lebanese and Syrian traders who arrived in the late 19th and early 20th centuries put kebabs, hummus and flatbreads onto the city menus of Accra and Kumasi. Independence in 1957, under Kwame Nkrumah, put jollof rice at the centre of the national identity, and the Ghanaian version uses jasmine rice, more tomato paste than Nigeria's, and a softer hand on the pepper. The Twi, Ga, Ewe and Dagomba ethnic kitchens each contribute their own dishes to the broader Ghanaian table.

The post-independence decades carried the cuisine into the diaspora (London, New York, Toronto, Hamburg all have established Ghanaian neighbourhoods and restaurants), and the chop bar (the everyday lunch spot) remains the heart of the urban food scene at home. Fufu (pounded cassava-and-plantain, eaten with one of several soups) is the universal celebration food. Waakye breakfast queues still snake around corners in Accra at 7am, jollof still gets argued about with Nigeria on social media every week, and the Ghanaian pepper sauce (shito) sits on every table the way ketchup does elsewhere.

## Regional variations

- **Greater Accra (Ga people)**: kenkey (fermented corn dough), banku, the coastal seafood tradition.
- **Ashanti (Kumasi region)**: fufu and groundnut soup capital. The forest-and-yam kitchen.
- **Northern (Dagomba, Mole-Dagbon)**: tuo zaafi (millet/sorghum porridge), Sahelian-influenced.
- **Volta region (Ewe)**: more leafy greens, abolo (steamed corn cake), Togolese overlap.

## Signature ingredients

- **Palm oil (red)**: in nearly every stew. Distinct from refined palm oil.
- **Scotch bonnet pepper**: the heat. Used heavily.
- **Tomato paste**: the base of jollof and most stews.
- **Groundnuts (peanuts)**: ground into peanut butter for groundnut soup, in chinchinga (suya-style skewers).
- **Cassava and plantain**: pounded into fufu, fried as kelewele or red-red.
- **Smoked fish and dried shrimp**: the umami base.
- **Garden eggs**: small white eggplants. In stews.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Jollof Rice](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/jollof-rice)**: long-grain rice cooked in a tomato-and-pepper base till the bottom catches into a smoky crust. The signature dish; the Ghanaian version uses jasmine rice and milder pepper than Nigerian.
2. **[Groundnut Soup](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/groundnut-soup)**: peanut-and-tomato stew with chicken or goat, served with fufu or rice. The Ashanti signature.
3. **[Fufu with Light Soup](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/fufu-with-light-soup)**: pounded cassava-and-plantain dumpling with a clear tomato-based broth (chicken, goat or fish). The Sunday celebration plate.
4. **[Waakye](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/waakye)**: rice-and-black-eyed-beans cooked with dried sorghum leaves (which give the dish its signature reddish-brown colour), served with shito (fermented fish-chilli paste), gari (toasted cassava granules), boiled egg, fried plantain. The Accra working breakfast.
5. **[Banku with Okra Stew](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/banku-with-okra-stew)**: fermented corn-and-cassava dough served with thick okra stew. The everyday Ga dish.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with kelewele (spiced fried plantain) as a side, and drink palm wine, sobolo (hibiscus drink) or akpeteshie (palm spirit). The Ghana-Nigeria jollof debate is ongoing; pick a side at your peril.
