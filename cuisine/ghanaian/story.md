# Ghanaian

*The kitchen of West Africa's gold coast. Jollof rice (and the eternal argument with Nigeria over whose is better), waakye (rice-and-beans the working breakfast), the groundnut soup that turned peanuts into a national obsession.*

## A short history

Ghanaian cooking sits on the West African coastal-and-forest tradition: cassava, plantain, yam, corn, palm oil, peppers, leafy greens, peanuts (groundnuts in Ghanaian English), the deep-fry-and-stew technique. The 16th-century Portuguese contact brought New World foods — tomatoes, peppers, maize, cassava — that Ghanaian cooks absorbed completely.

The British colonial period (Gold Coast 1874–1957) layered on tea, bread and a curry tradition (mostly via Indian colonial-administration imports). The post-independence era saw the codification of jollof rice as a national identity dish — the Ghanaian version uses jasmine rice, more tomato paste than Nigeria's, and a softer hand on the pepper.

The Twi, Ga, Ewe and Dagomba ethnic kitchens each contribute their own dishes to the broader Ghanaian table. Fufu (pounded cassava-and-plantain, eaten with one of several soups) is the universal celebration food.

## Regional variations

- **Greater Accra (Ga people)**: kenkey (fermented corn dough), banku, the coastal seafood tradition.
- **Ashanti (Kumasi region)**: fufu and groundnut soup capital. The forest-and-yam kitchen.
- **Northern (Dagomba, Mole-Dagbon)**: tuo zaafi (millet/sorghum porridge), Sahelian-influenced.
- **Volta region (Ewe)**: more leafy greens, abolo (steamed corn cake), Togolese overlap.

## Signature ingredients

- **Palm oil (red)**: in nearly every stew. Distinct from refined palm oil.
- **Scotch bonnet pepper**: the heat. Used heavily.
- **Tomato paste**: the base of jollof and most stews.
- **Groundnuts (peanuts)**: ground into peanut butter for groundnut soup, in chinchinga (suya-style skewers).
- **Cassava and plantain**: pounded into fufu, fried as kelewele or red-red.
- **Smoked fish and dried shrimp**: umami base.
- **Garden eggs**: small white eggplants. In stews.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Jollof Rice](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/jollof-rice)**: long-grain rice cooked in a tomato-and-pepper base till the bottom catches into a smoky crust. The signature dish. Ghanaian version uses jasmine rice and milder pepper than Nigerian.
2. **[Groundnut Soup](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/groundnut-soup)**: peanut-and-tomato stew with chicken or goat, served with fufu or rice. The Ashanti signature.
3. **[Fufu with Light Soup](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/fufu-with-light-soup)**: pounded cassava-and-plantain dumpling with clear tomato-based broth (chicken, goat or fish). The Sunday celebration plate.
4. **[Waakye](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/waakye)**: rice-and-black-eyed-beans cooked with dried sorghum leaves (which give the dish its signature reddish-brown colour), served with shito (fermented fish-chilli paste), gari (toasted cassava granules), boiled egg, fried plantain. The Accra working breakfast.
5. **[Banku with Okra Stew](/recipes/cuisine/ghanaian/banku-with-okra-stew)**: fermented corn-and-cassava dough served with thick okra stew. The everyday Ga dish.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with kelewele (spiced fried plantain) as a side and palm wine, sobolo (hibiscus drink) or akpeteshie (palm spirit) to drink. The Ghana-Nigeria jollof debate is ongoing; pick a side at your peril.
