# Bofrot

![Bofrot](resources/bofrot.jpg)

*The Ghanaian round doughnut (the Ga togbei), a slightly-yeasted ball of sweet dough with nutmeg, dropped from a spoon into hot oil and fried until deep gold and crackle-edged.*

**Serves:** Makes about 20 balls

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus 1 hour proof)

**Cook Time:** 25 minutes

## Overview
Bofrot (Twi) or togbei (Ga) is the round doughnut you find at every Ghanaian roadside stand, every funeral, every wedding, every after-church Sunday. The dough is a soft, slightly sweet yeasted batter, scented with nutmeg, dropped in walnut-sized balls into hot oil from a wet hand or a tablespoon. They puff into rough spheres, crackle along the seams as they fry, and come out deep gold with a slightly crisp shell and a soft, slightly chewy crumb. Eat warm out of a paper bag with sugar dusted on top. The trick is the dough hydration (wetter than English doughnut dough) and the oil temperature (lower than you think, around 160 C, so the inside cooks through).

## Ingredients

- 300 g plain flour
- 50 g caster sugar
- 1 tsp instant dried yeast (or 7 g sachet)
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1/2 tsp ground nutmeg
- 1/4 tsp ground cinnamon (optional)
- 280 ml warm water
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil (plus more for frying)
- Vegetable oil for frying (about 1 litre)
- 2 tbsp caster sugar for dusting (optional)

## Method

### Stage 1 - Mix the dough
1. In a large bowl, whisk together the flour, sugar, yeast, salt, nutmeg and cinnamon if using.
2. Make a well in the centre; pour in the warm water and the 1 tbsp oil.
3. Whisk vigorously for 2 minutes until you have a thick, smooth, slightly sticky batter (the texture of a thick pancake batter, not bread dough).

### Stage 2 - Proof
1. Cover the bowl with a damp cloth.
2. Leave in a warm place for 1 hour until doubled and bubbly on top.

### Stage 3 - Fry
1. Heat 5 cm of oil in a deep pan to 160-170 C.
2. Wet one hand with cold water; scoop a small ball of batter (a tablespoon's worth), close your fingers into a fist and let the ball squeeze out of the top of your fist (the traditional method).
3. Drop straight into the oil. Do not overcrowd; fry 5-6 at a time.
4. Fry 4-5 minutes, turning often, until deep gold all over. The bofrot will puff and crack.
5. Lift with a slotted spoon onto kitchen paper.
6. Dust with caster sugar while warm if using.

## Notes
- **Oil temperature is everything:** If it is too hot, the bofrot browns outside while the centre stays doughy. 160-170 C is right. A scrap of dough should bubble steadily and reach the surface in about 8 seconds.
- **The wet-hand trick:** Wetting your hand before scooping stops the dough sticking. A small ice-cream scoop works as a beginner alternative.
- **Do not over-prove:** An hour is right. Over-proofed dough collapses in the oil.

## Variations
- **Banana bofrot:** Mash 1 ripe banana into the batter; reduce water by 50 ml.
- **Coconut bofrot:** Replace 100 ml water with coconut milk.
- **Lemon-glazed bofrot:** Dip the warm balls in a glaze of 100 g icing sugar with 2 tbsp lemon juice.
- **Savoury bofrot:** Skip the sugar, add 1 tsp dried chilli flakes, eat with shito.

## Serving
Eat warm from a paper bag · with a cup of cold sobolo · with hot Milo (chocolate malt drink) · alongside kose for a breakfast snack pair.

## Storage
- Best eaten the day they are made
- Keep 1 day in a covered tin at room temperature; refresh in a 180 C oven for 3 minutes
- Do not refrigerate; they go stale
