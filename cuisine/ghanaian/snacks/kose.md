# Kose (Akara)

![Kose](resources/kose.jpg)

*Black-eyed pea fritters whipped to a soft cloud with onion and chilli, deep-fried in spoonfuls until the outside crackles golden and the inside stays light and pillowy, the breakfast staple of West Africa.*

**Serves:** Makes about 16 fritters

**Prep Time:** 15 minutes (plus overnight soak and 30 minutes for skinning)

**Cook Time:** 20 minutes

## Overview
Kose (Hausa-Ghanaian name) is what Nigerians call akara: black-eyed pea fritters whipped to a soft pale cloud and deep-fried into puffy crackling balls. The trick is the whip, the soaked-and-skinned peas are blended with a little onion, ginger and scotch bonnet, then beaten by hand or whisk until the paste lightens, lifts and holds soft peaks (the air in the paste is what makes the fritters rise in the oil). Dropped in spoonfuls into hot oil, they puff into rough golf-ball shapes, with a crisp shell and a moist, light-yellow inside. Sold every morning across Accra alongside porridge, with shito and a slice of bread, or beside hausa-koko (millet porridge).

## Ingredients

- 300 g dried black-eyed peas
- 1 small onion, finely chopped
- 1 scotch bonnet, deseeded and chopped
- 2 cm ginger, peeled
- 1 garlic clove (optional)
- 1 tsp salt
- 100-150 ml water (as needed)
- Vegetable oil for deep-frying

## Method

### Stage 1 - Soak and skin
1. Soak the black-eyed peas in plenty of cold water overnight.
2. Drain; rub the peas vigorously between your palms in a large bowl of fresh water. The skins will float; pour them off.
3. Repeat 3-4 times until most of the skins are gone (some will remain; that is fine).
4. Drain the skinned peas.

### Stage 2 - Blend
1. In a blender or food processor, combine the peas, onion, scotch bonnet, ginger, garlic if using and salt.
2. Add water a little at a time, just enough to keep the blender moving, until you have a thick smooth paste (a little coarser than hummus).

### Stage 3 - Whip
1. Tip the paste into a large bowl.
2. Beat hard with a wooden spoon or whisk for 3-4 minutes until the paste lightens in colour, lifts in volume and holds soft peaks. The air is what makes the kose rise. A small spoonful dropped into a glass of water should float.

### Stage 4 - Fry
1. Heat 5 cm of oil in a deep pan to 170 C.
2. Drop heaped tablespoons of paste into the oil. Do not overcrowd; fry 5-6 at a time.
3. Fry 3-4 minutes, turning often, until deep gold all over and crackle-edged.
4. Lift onto kitchen paper.

## Notes
- **Skinning is mandatory:** Unskinned peas give grey, heavy kose. Spend the 20 minutes; it is the difference between proper kose and a sad version.
- **The float test:** Properly whipped paste floats in water. If a spoonful sinks, keep whipping.
- **Oil temperature:** 170 C is right. Too cool, oily and dense; too hot, browned outside and raw inside.

## Variations
- **With prawns:** Fold 80 g chopped raw prawns into the whipped paste before frying.
- **Pepper-onion kose:** Add 1/2 finely chopped red pepper to the whipped paste.
- **Vegan and gluten-free as standard:** No flour, no eggs; naturally suitable.
- **Stuffed:** Wrap a small piece of cooked sausage or smoked fish in each spoonful before frying.

## Serving
Eat hot with shito · with hausa-koko (millet porridge) for breakfast · with a slice of buttered bread · alongside bofrot for a snack pair.

## Storage
- Best eaten fresh and hot
- Whipped paste keeps 4 hours in the fridge but loses some lift
- Fried kose keeps 1 day in a covered tin; reheat in a 200 C oven for 5 minutes
