# Arabian (Gulf)

*The kitchen of the Arabian Peninsula. Lamb on rice, the cardamom-and-saffron flavour profile, dates as both ingredient and standalone, and a hospitality tradition that puts more food on the table than anyone can possibly eat.*

## A short history

Arabian (Gulf) cooking — covering Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, Oman, Qatar and parts of Yemen and Kuwait — sits on three historical layers. The Bedouin desert kitchen contributed the slow-cooked whole lamb, the date palm shelf, and the coffee-and-dates hospitality ritual. The Persian-Indian trading routes brought rice, saffron, cardamom, and the basmati pulao tradition. The maritime trade with East Africa, Yemen and India layered on more spices (cinnamon, clove, black lime).

The 20th-century oil wealth transformed the cuisine — the home table got more elaborate, restaurant culture grew, and the Indian and Filipino expat populations layered curry and stir-fry onto local kitchens. But the everyday traditional table — kabsa rice with chicken or lamb, dates and Arabic coffee for guests — has remained almost unchanged for generations.

The hospitality protocol is essentially a culinary rule. Guests sit; coffee with dates is served; multiple courses follow whether you said you were hungry or not. The host gets offended if you don't eat enough.

## Regional variations

- **Saudi Arabia (centre, west)**: kabsa territory. Mandi (pit-cooked spiced rice with lamb) in the south.
- **UAE (Gulf coast)**: stronger Indian and Persian influence. Margoog, harees, fish dishes.
- **Oman**: the most coastal. Shuwa (lamb wrapped in banana leaf and pit-cooked), fish curries with Indian and East African layers.
- **Yemen**: separate cuisine folder. Hotter, more chilli, distinct rice traditions.
- **Bahrain and Qatar**: closer to Saudi-UAE shelf. Machboos rice is the signature.

## Signature ingredients

- **Basmati rice**: aged, long-grain, the spine of every meal.
- **Lamb**: the meat. Whole-roasted at celebrations.
- **Cardamom**: in every coffee pot, in every rice dish.
- **Saffron**: bloomed in rosewater, threads scattered over rice.
- **Black lime (loomi)**: dried whole, dropped into stews and rice.
- **Baharat**: the Gulf spice blend (allspice, black pepper, coriander, cumin, cardamom, cinnamon).
- **Dates**: medjool, khalas, lulu, sukkari — eaten daily, gifted constantly.

## Learn it in five recipes

1. **[Kabsa](/recipes/cuisine/arabian/kabsa)**: chicken or lamb on spiced basmati rice with tomato, dried lime and baharat. The Saudi national dish.
2. **[Madghoot](/recipes/cuisine/arabian/madghoot)**: pressure-cooked lamb and rice. The everyday version of the celebration plate.
3. **[Saleeg](/recipes/cuisine/arabian/saleeg)**: creamy rice porridge with chicken — the Hejazi (Mecca region) speciality, often eaten as breakfast or Ramadan iftar.
4. **[Mathlouta](/recipes/cuisine/arabian/mathloutha)**: layered grain dish with rice, lentils and bulgur, served with stewed meat.
5. **[Mutabbal](/recipes/cuisine/arabian/mutabbal)**: smoky aubergine puree with tahini, lemon and garlic. The meze starter.

## Where to go next

Pair everything with Arabic coffee (qahwa — cardamom-spiced, served in small cups with dates) or with karak chai (the Indian-inspired sweet milky tea now ubiquitous across the Gulf). For a proper Arabian dinner: dates and coffee on arrival, kabsa at the centre of the table, multiple side dishes, ma'amoul (date-stuffed shortbread) to finish.
