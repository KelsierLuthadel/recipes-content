# Chinese

*A cuisine built on contrast and high heat, where a hot wok, a sharp cleaver and a handful of fermented pastes turn three or four humble ingredients into something balanced, glossy and unmistakably alive.*

## A short history

Chinese cooking is one of the world's oldest continuous food traditions, with techniques traceable through more than three thousand years of agricultural records, dynastic court menus and regional folk practice. The Han period codified the importance of grain in the north and rice in the south, and the Tang and Song dynasties opened trade routes that brought tea culture, sugar refining and new spices into everyday kitchens. The Ming and Qing courts then refined banquet cooking into the elaborate multi-course style still echoed in modern restaurants, while ordinary households kept the everyday repertoire of pickles, stir-fries and slow-simmered stocks alive.

The anchoring principle is balance: of yin and yang, of cold and hot ingredients, but more practically of five flavours (salty, sweet, sour, bitter, umami) and of textures within a single bite. A good cook is judged on knife work first, fire control second and seasoning third. The wok is treated as an instrument rather than a pan, prized for its ability to shift food in and out of intense heat in seconds. Fermentation underpins nearly every savoury dish, with soy sauce, doubanjiang, fermented black beans and Shaoxing wine doing the slow flavour work that lets the actual cooking finish in minutes.

## Regional variations

China's cuisine is best understood as eight major regional traditions rather than one national style, each shaped by local climate, crops and trade history.

- **Cantonese (Guangdong)**: restrained seasoning, supreme respect for the freshness of the main ingredient, and a love of steaming and quick stir-frying. Signature dishes include steamed whole fish with ginger and spring onion, char siu and dim sum.
- **Sichuan**: famous for mala, the numbing-and-hot interplay of Sichuan peppercorn and dried chilli, layered over fermented chilli bean paste. Mapo tofu, kung pao chicken and dry pot dishes define the palate.
- **Hunan**: brighter and more sharply chilli-forward than Sichuan, with a stronger sour and smoked dimension. Steamed fish head with chopped chillies and smoked pork with dried beans are the calling cards.
- **Shandong**: northern, wheat-eating, broth-driven. Clear stocks, braised seafood, sweet-and-sour carp and crisp-fried river prawns show off the technical precision the region is known for.
- **Jiangsu and Zhejiang (Huaiyang)**: refined, slightly sweet, knife-work obsessed. Lion's-head meatballs, red-braised pork and West Lake fish in vinegar gravy are the household names.
- **Fujian**: coastal, soup-loving, generous with seafood, peanuts and red yeast rice. Buddha jumps over the wall is the showcase, though everyday cooking leans on light fish stocks and oyster-laced stir-fries.
- **Anhui**: mountain cooking, with wild herbs, bamboo, ham and slow braises. Less famous abroad but central to the inland flavour map.
- **Northern and northwestern styles**: hand-pulled noodles, lamb skewers, cumin and the wheat-based dumpling and bao tradition that defines everyday eating from Beijing through Xi'an.

Across these regions, the same wok, cleaver and steamer do very different work depending on which ferments and aromatics sit in the cook's pantry.

## Signature ingredients

- **Soy sauce**: light soy for seasoning and depth, dark soy for colour and a faint molasses sweetness in braises.
- **Shaoxing rice wine**: amber, slightly nutty fermented wine used to deglaze woks, marinate meat and lift fishiness from seafood.
- **Ginger, garlic and spring onion**: the holy trinity of Chinese aromatics, almost always added to hot oil before anything else.
- **Sichuan peppercorn**: the husks of a citrus-relative berry that produce a tingling numbness rather than heat. Essential to mala cooking.
- **Doubanjiang**: fermented broad-bean and chilli paste from Pixian in Sichuan. Provides the deep red colour and savoury chilli backbone of mapo tofu and twice-cooked pork.
- **Fermented black beans (douchi)**: salted, sun-dried soybeans with a funky, almost coffee-like depth, used in steamed spare ribs and many Cantonese stir-fries.
- **Five-spice powder**: a balanced blend of star anise, fennel, cloves, cinnamon and Sichuan pepper that anchors roasted meats, especially char siu and Shandong-style fried chicken.
- **Sesame oil and toasted sesame seeds**: finishing oils rather than cooking oils, added off the heat for nuttiness and shine.

## Learn it in seven recipes

A short curriculum that, if cooked through, gives you the core technical vocabulary of Chinese home cooking.

1. [Kung Pao Chicken](kung-pao-chicken.md). Diced chicken velveted with cornflour and egg white, then flashed in a hot wok with dried chillies, Sichuan pepper and peanuts. Teaches velveting and the rhythm of wok stir-frying: prep everything, cook in ninety seconds, season at the end.
2. [Mapo Tofu](mapo-tofu.md). Silken tofu simmered in a fierce sauce of doubanjiang, fermented black beans, minced beef and Sichuan peppercorn. Teaches how to bloom fermented pastes in oil and balance mala heat with starch-thickened sauce.
3. [Steamed Fish](steamed-fish.md). A whole fish set over ginger and spring onion, steamed until just opaque, then finished with smoking-hot oil and a soy-based dressing. Teaches Cantonese-style steaming and the sizzle-pour finish that defines so many fish dishes.
4. [Char Siu](char-siu.md). Pork shoulder marinated in hoisin, fermented bean curd, soy and five-spice, then roasted until lacquered and slightly charred at the edges. Teaches sweet-savoury marinade building and oven-as-roasting-oven technique.
5. [Jiaozi](snacks/jiaozi.md). Hand-folded pork and Chinese leaf dumplings, boiled or pan-fried into potstickers. Teaches a basic hot-water wheat dough, pleating by hand and the three-stage fry-steam-fry method that gives potstickers their crisp bottoms.
6. [Chow Mein](chow-mein.md). Parboiled noodles seared in a screaming wok with prawns, bean sprouts and a light soy-oyster sauce. Teaches wok hei, the smoky breath you only get when noodles meet very hot metal and a little oil ignites at the edges.
7. [Fried Rice](fried-rice.md). Day-old rice tossed with egg, spring onion, soy and whatever proteins are at hand. Teaches grain handling, the importance of a dry starting point and the discipline of seasoning lightly so each grain stays separate and glossy.

Cook these in order and you will have practised velveting, paste-blooming, steaming, marinating, dough work, wok hei and the everyday grain technique that ties the rest of the kitchen together.
