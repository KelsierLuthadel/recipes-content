# Chef's Knife

*If you only ever buy one knife, this is it. A 20-22cm chef's knife handles 95% of every cutting task in a home kitchen — slicing, dicing, mincing, chopping, butchering small cuts. Spend more on this than on any other tool.*

## What to buy

**Budget (£20–40)**: Victorinox Fibrox Pro 20cm. Stamped steel, polypropylene handle, dishwasher-safe (don't, but you could). Used in commercial kitchens worldwide because it just works. The benchmark cheap knife.

**Good (£80–150)**: Wüsthof Classic Ikon 20cm or Henckels Pro. Forged steel, full tang, double-bolster. Heavier than the Fibrox, holds an edge longer, feels like a real tool. The everyday knife for serious home cooks.

**Premium (£200–500+)**: Japanese gyutos — Tojiro DP, Misono UX10, Shun Premier, Masamoto, Sakai Takayuki. Lighter, thinner, sharper, harder steel (60+ HRC). Lifetime tools when maintained. Choose handle style (Western or wa/octagonal) by hand fit.

Look for: 20–22cm blade length (8–9 inch), full tang on Western knives, a comfortable handle for your hand size, weight that feels balanced when you pinch-grip the blade. Avoid serrated chef's knives (they can't be sharpened) and anything sold in a knife block as a "set" — sets are loss leaders to sell you 12 mediocre knives for the price of two good ones.

## How to use

Hold it with a **pinch grip**: thumb and forefinger pinched on the blade just forward of the bolster, remaining fingers around the handle. The blade is a tool, not a handle.

The **claw grip** on the guide hand: fingertips curled under, knuckles touching the blade. The knife slides against your knuckles; you cannot cut what you cannot see.

For most slicing: **rock the blade** through the cut, pivoting on the tip. Don't lift the whole blade off the board; the tip stays down. This is faster, safer and more precise than the up-and-down chop most beginners default to.

For mincing herbs and garlic: hold the tip down with one hand, rock the heel of the blade. The closed-tip rocking motion turns a pile of parsley into a fine mince in seconds.

## How to maintain

**Sharpen weekly** with a honing steel (10–15 light passes per side at a 20° angle). The steel doesn't sharpen — it realigns the micro-burrs that bend during use.

**Sharpen properly** every 3–6 months with a whetstone (a 1000-grit/6000-grit combination stone, £30, lasts a lifetime) or get it professionally sharpened. A sharp knife is safer than a dull one because it cuts on intent rather than slipping.

**Never put it in the dishwasher.** The detergent corrodes the steel, the heat warps the handle, and the rattling chips the edge. Wash by hand, dry immediately, store either in a wooden block, on a magnetic strip, or in a knife guard. Don't toss it loose in a drawer.

## Common mistakes

- **Sawing instead of slicing**: a sharp blade should glide through onion in one stroke. If you're sawing, the knife needs sharpening.
- **Cutting on glass or stone**: dulls the edge instantly. Use wood or plastic only. (See cutting board guide.)
- **Bringing the heel down on bones**: chef's knives are for soft tissue. Use a cleaver or kitchen shears for bones.
- **Letting it touch other metal in the sink**: chips the edge. Wash separately.

## What it replaces

A sharp chef's knife replaces: paring knives (90% of the time — pick one up only for peeling apples or coring strawberries), bread knives (a sharp chef's knife slices crusty bread just fine), Santoku knives (the chef's knife does everything Santokus do plus more), and any "vegetable cleaver" sold in catalogues.

You need: chef's knife, paring knife (for fiddly small work), serrated bread knife (genuinely useful for big crusty loaves). That's the kitchen knife rack. Everything else is a luxury.
