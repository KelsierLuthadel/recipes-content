# Digital Scale
*A flat electronic kitchen scale that reads to 1g and switches between grams, ounces and millilitres. The most accurate tool in the kitchen, and the only way to bake bread, pastry or any recipe that depends on hydration ratios.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£10–20)**: any flat digital scale with 1g resolution and a 5kg capacity. Salter, Taylor, Etekcity. Battery-powered (AAA usually), tare button, gram/ounce toggle. The cheap supermarket scale is functionally identical to the £40 branded one.

**Good (£25–50)**: OXO Good Grips Stainless, MyWeigh KD-8000. Built-in pull-out display (lets you read the screen even when a bowl is hiding it), larger platform (40cm+), more durable.

**Premium (£100+)**: Escali R-Series or Acaia Lunar. Coffee-shop quality, 0.1g resolution. Only needed if you bake espresso-precision or weigh small spice quantities regularly.

Look for: **1g resolution** (or 0.1g if you want precision for spices), **5kg capacity** (you'll weigh a chicken on it), **tare function** (the "zero" button — essential), **flat platform** (not a bowl built-in — you want your own bowl on top), **automatic shut-off** longer than 90 seconds (otherwise it switches off mid-recipe).

Avoid: scales with built-in bowls (limits what you can weigh), mechanical/spring scales (less accurate, harder to read), scales without tare (they're not really scales, they're paperweights).

## How to use
**Tare zeroing**: place the empty container on the scale, press tare to zero, add the ingredient. Read the weight. To add a second ingredient, press tare again to zero, add the next ingredient on top. This is how you weigh five different ingredients into the same bowl without having to clear and reset.

**Switch to grams.** Grams are precise (1g is 1g, everywhere). Cups vary by how packed the flour is, by the brand of measuring cup, by whether you scoop or spoon. A pro recipe is in grams; a home recipe in cups is a guess.

**For liquids**: 1g = 1ml for water, milk, stock, vinegar. For oil and honey, 1ml weighs 0.92g and 1.42g respectively. The scale is more accurate than the measuring jug for these as well.

**For meat and produce**: weigh on the scale before cooking. "500g chicken" in a recipe is the raw weight; if your supermarket pack is 480g, the recipe needs slight adjustment.

## How to maintain
**Replace batteries** every 12–18 months (or when the display dims). Most kitchen scales take 2× AAA.

**Wipe clean** with a damp cloth. Don't immerse in water — the electronics will short. Most modern scales have a sealed platform that resists splashes.

**Calibrate occasionally**: place a known weight (e.g. a 500g packet of butter) on the scale. If it reads more than 5g off, the scale is drifting; replace it.

## Common mistakes
- **Using cups for baking**: cup measurements are 10–20% inaccurate depending on how you scoop. For bread and pastry, the hydration matters at the 5% level — the cup is too imprecise.
- **Not zeroing before adding ingredients**: pour the flour, weigh the flour with the bowl, subtract the bowl weight. This is the pre-scale way; the tare button makes it obsolete.
- **Buying a "kitchen scale" that's actually a postal scale**: postal scales tend to top out at 5kg and lack the tare button or the precision.
- **Old-style mechanical scales**: less accurate, harder to read, no tare. The digital scale beats it on every metric.

## What it replaces
A digital scale replaces: every set of measuring cups (cup-and-spoon volume measures are a US convention; the rest of the world uses weight), measuring jugs for dry ingredients (jugs are fine for liquids but lie about dry weight), and the "eyeball it" approach to bread baking that produces a different loaf every time. One scale + one set of measuring spoons (for spices under 5g) = the complete measurement kit.
