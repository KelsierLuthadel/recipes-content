# Sous Vide Circulator
*An immersion circulator that clips to the side of a pot, heats water to a precise temperature (±0.1°C), and circulates it gently around vacuum-sealed food. Cooks the meat to the exact done-temperature, end to end. Eliminates the steak overcook risk entirely.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£60–120)**: Anova Precision Cooker Nano or Inkbird ISV-100W. 800–1000W heating, Wi-Fi or Bluetooth control, clips to a pot. The Anova line is the consumer standard.

**Good (£150–250)**: Anova Precision Cooker Pro, Joule Turbo. 1100W+, faster heating, more reliable in long cooks. Worth the upgrade if you sous vide weekly.

**Premium (£400+)**: PolyScience Creative or Breville Joule (with their tank). Larger volume, faster recovery, professional-kitchen reliability.

For vacuum-sealing: a **chamber vacuum sealer** is the proper tool (£300+), but most home cooks use a **Foodsaver-style external sealer** (£50–100) or even the **water-displacement method** with regular freezer bags (free — push the bag into water to expel air, then seal). The water-displacement method works for the vast majority of home sous-vide cooks.

Look for: **at least 800W**, **±0.1°C accuracy**, **fits in a standard 12L+ pot**, **Wi-Fi or Bluetooth** (set-and-forget — leave the bath running and walk away).

## How to use
**Set temperature, set time, walk away.** Steak at 54°C for 1.5 hours = perfect medium-rare. Chicken breast at 65°C for 1 hour = perfectly cooked, never dry. Salmon at 52°C for 30 minutes = silky, melt-in-mouth.

**Always sear after sous-viding meat.** The bath cooks to temperature; it doesn't brown. Sear the steak in screaming-hot cast iron with neutral oil and butter for 60–90 seconds per side after pulling it from the bag. This is the magic step — bath + sear = restaurant-grade meat at home.

**Use a large enough water bath.** 8–12L for steaks, 15L+ for whole chickens. The water needs room to circulate.

**Cover the pot** with a clip-on lid, plastic wrap or sous-vide balls (foam balls that float on the surface) to reduce evaporation. Long cooks (24+ hours) lose 1–2L of water through evaporation without a cover.

## How to maintain
**Run a descaling cycle** every 3–6 months: bath with diluted citric acid solution (1 tbsp per litre water), run at 60°C for 30 minutes. Removes the limescale that builds up on the heating element.

**Wipe dry** after every use. Don't immerse the control head; only the metal cylinder goes in water.

**Don't run it dry.** The heating element will burn out if there's no water. Most circulators auto-shut-off below a fill line, but cheap ones don't.

## Common mistakes
- **Not searing afterwards**: a sous-vide steak straight from the bag is pale, soft, unappetising. The sear is mandatory.
- **Cooking too long**: sous-vide doesn't ruin meat at the time scales most recipes use, but 12+ hours of cooking turns steak texture mushy. Stick to the recipe.
- **Filling the bag too full**: water can't conduct heat to the centre of a thick bag. Single layer only.
- **Bathwater not deep enough**: the food needs to be fully submerged. A floating bag has air gaps; the air gaps are uncooked spots.

## What it replaces
A sous-vide circulator replaces: the meat thermometer for steak (you set the temperature, you don't need to check), the "rest the meat for 10 minutes" step (sous-vide meat is already at temperature, sear and serve), the precision needed for cooking fish (52°C bath gives perfect salmon every time). Doesn't replace cooking by feel and judgment — but for any recipe where temperature is the variable, it's transformative.
