# Stand Mixer
*A heavy, motorised mixer on a stand that does the kneading, whipping and beating that hands and arms used to. Indispensable for serious bakers, completely unnecessary for everyone else. Buy only if you bake bread or pastry weekly.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£100–180)**: Kenwood kMix, KitchenAid Classic 4.3L (entry-level — sometimes on heavy discount). 250–300W motor, basic 3-attachment set (paddle, whisk, dough hook). Underpowered for bread dough but fine for cake batters and whipped cream.

**Good (£300–450)**: KitchenAid Artisan 4.8L. The iconic stand mixer. 300W tilt-head, 10 speeds, dozens of attachment options (pasta roller, meat grinder, sausage stuffer). The world's most-recognised baking appliance.

**Premium (£500–800)**: KitchenAid Pro 6.9L bowl-lift, Kenwood Chef XL Titanium, Ankarsrum Original. Bowl-lift design (lifts the bowl into the head, rather than the head tilting up — sturdier), 500W+ motors, can handle 1.5kg of bread dough without bogging down.

Look for: **direct-drive motor** (no belts, more reliable), **planetary mixing action** (the head orbits while spinning — covers the whole bowl), **stainless steel bowl** (non-reactive, lasts forever), **at least 300W** for occasional dough work, **500W+** if you bake bread weekly.

Avoid: hand-mixers sold as "stand mixers" with a stand attachment (not the same tool), cheap unbranded stand mixers (motor burns out within a year on the first stiff dough), specialty single-purpose mixers like dedicated bread machines (limited to bread, takes more counter space).

## How to use
**Start slow.** Stand mixers come up to speed fast and splatter ingredients everywhere if you start on high. Always begin at speed 1–2; ramp up gradually.

**Use the right attachment**:
- **Paddle (flat beater)**: for cake batters, creaming butter and sugar, mashing potatoes, mixing meatballs. The everyday attachment.
- **Whisk (balloon whisk)**: for whipping cream, beating egg whites, light meringue. NOT for dough.
- **Dough hook**: for bread dough. Knead on speed 2 for 6–8 minutes; the dough should slap the sides of the bowl rhythmically.

**Don't overload.** Most home stand mixers can handle 1kg of bread dough comfortably; beyond that they bog down and burn the motor. Halve your batch if needed.

**Scrape the bowl.** Even with the right attachment, ingredients hide in the bottom corner. Stop the mixer every minute or two and scrape down with a silicone spatula.

## How to maintain
**Wipe down the head** after every use — flour and butter get into the planetary gears if you don't.

**Wash the bowl and attachments** by hand or dishwasher (top rack). The dough hook and paddle are usually dishwasher-safe; check the model.

**Lubricate the planetary mechanism** every 5 years (or have it serviced). KitchenAid recommends a teaspoon of food-safe mineral oil applied directly to the planetary head gears.

**Don't bury it in a cupboard.** A stand mixer that's not at hand never gets used. Leave it on the counter or buy one in a colour you don't hate looking at.

## Common mistakes
- **Buying without baking regularly**: the most-returned kitchen appliance after the first six months. If you don't bake bread or cakes weekly, you don't need one.
- **Running on high from the start**: ingredients splatter across the kitchen. Always start at speed 1.
- **Overloading**: 1kg of bread dough is the limit for most home machines. The motor will literally smoke if you push it.
- **Buying the cheapest one**: a £80 stand mixer will burn out within months. The price floor for a serviceable machine is around £150.

## What it replaces
A stand mixer replaces: hand-mixers (faster, doesn't tire your arm), bread machines (does everything a bread machine does and more), manual kneading (saves 10 minutes per loaf), the food processor for cake batters (the paddle is better than the dough blade). For a baker, it's transformative. For a non-baker, it's furniture.
