# Microplane & Box Grater
*A long thin rasp-style grater (Microplane) for the fine zesting of citrus, hard cheese, nutmeg, garlic and ginger; a four-sided box grater for the larger work — potato, cheddar, courgette. Two tools, both indispensable, neither replaceable by the other.*

## What to buy
**Microplane, budget (£10–20)**: the Microplane Classic or Premier rasp grater. The brand name is the same as the product; "Microplane" is genuinely the only fine-grater worth owning. Stainless steel, photo-etched blades, sharp enough to take fingerprints.

**Box grater, budget (£8–15)**: any sturdy 4-sided box grater. Joseph Joseph, OXO Good Grips, Cuisinart. Look for: a stable base (rubber feet), a stainless steel body that won't bend, four panels (large shred, small shred, slice, fine zest).

**Premium**: a Microplane Master Series (slightly thicker handle, lifetime warranty) and a Cuisinart 4-sided box grater. £40 total. These are 10-year tools.

Avoid: combo "all in one" graters that try to be both Microplane and box grater (they're both badly), rotary cheese graters (slow, awkward), specialty single-purpose graters ("perfect for ginger!" — the Microplane already does that).

## How to use
**Microplane** for zest (citrus — never grate into the pith), garlic (turns into a paste in seconds), ginger (the fibres get caught and removed automatically), nutmeg (whole nutmeg is 5x better than ground), and hard cheese (parmesan, pecorino, aged Manchego). Hold the grater above the dish; let the zest fall directly in.

**Box grater** for soft cheese (cheddar, mozzarella — too soft for the Microplane, which mushes them), potato (rosti, hash brown), courgette, carrot, beetroot, and anything you want to shred rather than mince.

For garlic on the Microplane: peel, rub the clove back and forth across the rasp. The pulp falls; the dry skin stays in your fingers. For ginger: don't peel, just rub. The fibres stay; the flesh passes through.

## How to maintain
**Microplane**: hand-wash with hot water and a soft brush (the bristles get the cheese out of the holes). Dishwasher-safe but the blades dull faster.

**Box grater**: dishwasher-safe and indestructible. Soak in hot soapy water after cheese-grating to dissolve the stuck-on residue.

Both will eventually dull. The Microplane goes first (after 2–5 years of heavy use). When it stops cutting cleanly (you have to press harder), replace it. The box grater is essentially permanent.

## Common mistakes
- **Using a fine grater (Microplane) for cheddar**: cheddar mushes into a paste. Box grater for soft cheese.
- **Using a box grater for parmesan**: takes forever, produces uneven shreds. Microplane for hard cheese.
- **Grating citrus through the pith**: bitter, ruins the dish. Stop at the coloured layer; the white pith stays on the fruit.
- **Pressing too hard on the Microplane**: clogs the holes and dulls the blades. Light pressure, more strokes.

## What it replaces
A Microplane plus a box grater replaces: every novelty grater (rotary, electric, drum-style), garlic presses (the Microplane does it cleaner), ginger graters (Microplane again), and the temptation to buy ground spices (whole + Microplane on demand is always better). Two graters, ten quid each, the complete grater shelf.
