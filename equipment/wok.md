# Wok
*A round-bottomed, sloped-side cooking pan, traditionally Chinese, that handles stir-fry, deep-fry, steaming (with a bamboo basket), and braising. The high heat plus the sloping sides create the breath-of-the-wok (wok hei) — the smoky char that defines Chinese restaurant cooking.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£20–40)**: a 35cm (14-inch) carbon steel wok with a long wooden handle. Either the round-bottom (needs a ring stand on a Western hob) or the flat-bottom (works directly on electric/induction). Chinese supermarkets sell the same wok the restaurants use for £25.

**Good (£50–100)**: a Japanese Tamagoyaki-grade carbon steel wok or a Joyce Chen-imported Chinese wok. Higher-quality steel, more responsive heat, lifetime tool.

**Premium (£150+)**: a hand-hammered Chinese carbon steel wok from a specialty supplier (Mandarin Stuff or The Wok Shop in San Francisco). The hammered surface adds texture for food to grip when stir-frying. Often comes pre-seasoned.

**Avoid stainless steel woks** (don't season, food sticks badly), **non-stick woks** (can't take the heat needed for proper stir-fry), and **electric "wok cookers"** (don't get hot enough).

For Western kitchens with electric or induction hobs: **flat-bottom carbon steel** is the right choice. The round-bottom only works on gas with a ring stand.

Look for: **carbon steel** (not stainless, not aluminium, not coated), **35cm (14-inch)** diameter (the universal home size — bigger is better for searing but harder to handle), **long wooden handle on one side and a short loop handle on the other** (the long handle for stir-fry motion, the loop for lifting).

## How to use
**Season it before first use** (or re-season if the patina is damaged). Wash the new wok with hot soapy water to remove the factory coating. Dry thoroughly. Heat over high heat until smoking, brush a thin layer of high-smoke-point oil (peanut, grapeseed, vegetable) all over the inside, heat until it smokes again. Repeat 3–4 times. Each cook from then on adds to the seasoning.

**Heat the wok smoking-hot before adding oil.** "Hot wok, cold oil" is the Chinese cooking principle — preheat the dry wok over high heat for 2–3 minutes until you see wisps of smoke, then swirl in the oil.

**Stir-fry in batches.** A 35cm wok handles 300–400g of protein plus 300g of vegetables maximum. Beyond that, the heat drops, the food steams instead of frying, and the wok hei is gone. Cook proteins first, remove, cook vegetables, then combine.

**Keep the food moving.** The constant tossing and pushing against the sloping sides is the technique — it exposes every surface to maximum heat. A wok ladle (a wide, slightly curved spatula with a long handle) is the proper tool; a wooden spoon is the compromise.

**For deep-fry and steaming**: the wok's depth and shape are ideal. 6cm of oil deep-fries spring rolls; a bamboo steamer fits on top for dumplings.

## How to maintain
**Don't wash with soap.** Carbon steel develops a non-stick patina through use; soap strips it. Rinse with hot water, scrub stuck-on bits with a stiff brush or chainmail scrubber, dry on the hob over heat (always dry on the hob, never with a towel — residual moisture causes rust).

**Wipe with a thin layer of oil** after drying. The seasoning lives on this layer.

**If it rusts**: scrub off with steel wool, re-season from scratch (same process as breaking in a new wok). Rust is a sign of moisture left on the surface; fix the drying habit, not the wok.

**The patina is the goal.** A new wok is silver; a 2-year-old wok is jet-black, slightly slick, and slides eggs around like a non-stick. Don't try to scrub it back to silver — embrace the colour.

## Common mistakes
- **Not hot enough**: the difference between home stir-fry and restaurant stir-fry is heat. Crank it hotter than feels comfortable. Smoke from the wok is good; flames briefly licking up the side is okay.
- **Overloading**: a quarter-full wok stir-fries; a half-full one steams. Cook in batches.
- **Using soap or putting it in the dishwasher**: strips the seasoning; the wok rusts within hours.
- **Treating it like a flat pan**: the curved sides are for tossing. A pancake-flipping motion (lift the wok, snap the wrist forward, the food jumps) is the technique.

## What it replaces
A wok replaces: most large frying pans (the wok does what they do, faster and with more flavour), the deep-fryer (6cm of oil in a wok deep-fries cleaner than most appliances), and the steamer pot (bamboo steamer over wok water = perfect dumplings). With a cast iron skillet for searing and a wok for everything else, your large-cookware needs are covered.
