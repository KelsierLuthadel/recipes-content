# Mortar and Pestle
*A heavy stone or hardwood bowl with a matching pounding tool. Crushes whole spices, breaks down spice pastes (Thai red curry, harissa), makes proper pesto (the "pesto" in Italian comes from "pestare" — to pound), and turns hard ingredients into pulpy ones with technique no machine quite replicates.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£15–30)**: a granite mortar, 15–20cm internal diameter, 2kg+ weight. Asian supermarket basement stock — Thai granite is excellent and cheap. The heavier the better; light mortars dance around the counter.

**Good (£40–80)**: a heavy granite or marble mortar from a kitchen specialist. Bigger size (20cm+), polished interior (or unpolished — see below), proper Thai-style with a tall mortar and shorter pestle.

**Premium (£100+)**: a Molcajete (Mexican volcanic basalt mortar — porous surface seasons over time, used for guacamole and salsas), or a Japanese suribachi (ceramic with ribbed interior for grinding sesame and pastes).

Material matters: **granite/basalt** is unbreakable and heavy (best for crushing), **marble** is heavy but stains (avoid for spice work), **ceramic suribachi** has ridges that grip pastes (best for sesame, herbs), **wood** is light and stylish (worst for serious grinding — wood absorbs flavours).

Look for: **unpolished interior** (the rough surface grips ingredients and breaks them down faster; polished is for looks), **at least 1.5kg weight** (lighter than that and it slides on the counter), **at least 15cm diameter** (smaller and you can't fit a whole spice paste batch).

Avoid: small "spice mortars" sold as gifts (5cm diameter, useless for real work), wooden mortars (absorb spice oils, get rancid), plastic mortars (don't crush — they're decorative).

## How to use
**Crush in stages.** Hard ingredients first (whole spices, garlic, salt — the salt acts as an abrasive grit), then softer ingredients (chilli, ginger), finally herbs and liquids.

**Pound, don't grind.** A mortar works by impact, not friction. Lift the pestle a few centimetres above the mortar bowl and strike straight down; let gravity do the work. Wrist movement is for stirring the paste between strikes.

**Use a rotary motion** to clear the sides between strikes. Pound, sweep around the bowl edge, pound again.

**Add liquid at the end.** Lemon juice, oil, vinegar — these go in only after the solids are broken down. Adding liquid early just slips the ingredients around.

For Thai curry paste: start with the toughest things (dried chillies, lemongrass, galangal), pound for 10–15 minutes till they're a fine paste, then add softer ingredients (garlic, shallot, coriander root), finally shrimp paste. A proper Thai curry paste from scratch takes 20 minutes of pounding.

## How to maintain
**Don't use soap on stone mortars.** Soap absorbs into the porous surface; the next pesto tastes like dish-soap. Rinse with hot water, scrub with a hard brush, dry thoroughly.

**Season new stone mortars** by grinding raw rice (a cup at a time) until the rice comes out clean (3–5 cycles). This removes the grit and dust from the unpolished surface.

**Don't dishwasher** — the heat can crack stone, and detergent absorbs into the porous interior.

**For molcajete and similar volcanic stone**: grinds garlic-and-salt as a finishing seasoning treatment every few months. This re-builds the patina.

## Common mistakes
- **Grinding instead of pounding**: the mortar isn't a food processor. Use impact; don't rub.
- **Skipping the salt**: salt acts as the abrasive grit that helps break down soft ingredients (garlic, herbs). A little salt makes the work go much faster.
- **Too small a mortar**: 8cm-diameter "spice mortars" can crush coriander seeds but nothing larger. A real working mortar is 15cm+ diameter.
- **Using soap**: stone mortars absorb soap. Hot water and a brush only.

## What it replaces
A mortar and pestle replaces: spice grinders (better texture, finer control, no electric noise), the blender for thick pastes (a mortar makes a smoother, more aromatic pesto than a blender — Italians will tell you this with religious fervour), and the food processor for small batches (you don't waste 5 minutes cleaning a processor for two cloves of garlic). For Thai cooking specifically, it's not replaceable — Thai curry pastes from a blender taste different.
