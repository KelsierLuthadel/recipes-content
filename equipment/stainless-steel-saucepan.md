# Stainless Steel Saucepan
*The pan that does the real cooking. Stainless steel doesn't react with acid, browns food properly, sears meat, builds fond, can go from hob to oven, lasts a lifetime, and survives every cleaning method including the scourer. The non-stick can have its eggs; this one does everything else.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£30–60)**: a 20cm or 22cm sauté pan with a triple-clad (5-ply) base. Stellar 7000, Lakeland Hard Anodised Stainless, or the cheaper Cuisinart MultiClad lines. Look for: heavy weight, magnetic base (induction-compatible), oven-safe to at least 200°C.

**Good (£100–200)**: All-Clad D3 or Stellar SteelTech (a 24cm sauté pan and a 18–20cm saucepan covers most needs). Full triple-clad (steel-aluminium-steel right up the sides, not just the base), riveted handles, lifetime warranty.

**Premium (£300+)**: Demeyere Atlantis, All-Clad D5/Copper Core, Mauviel Made-In. Five-ply or copper-cored, professional-kitchen quality. Genuinely a lifetime tool.

For a starter kit: one **24cm sauté pan** (the lidded shallow pan with a long handle) and one **18cm saucepan** (the deep pan with a lid) covers ~80% of cooking. Add a **stockpot** (5L+) later if you make stock or pasta regularly.

Avoid: aluminium pans without a steel layer (react with acid, scratch easily), copper pans without a tin or steel lining (need re-tinning every few years, very expensive), pans with rivetless interiors (the seamless inside is a marketing gimmick that makes the construction weaker).

## How to use
**Preheat the dry pan** until a droplet of water dances and evaporates (about 60 seconds on medium-high heat). Then add the oil. Then add the food. Adding food to a cold pan is the most common cause of sticking on stainless steel.

**Cook on medium-high to high heat.** Stainless steel can take the heat. Crank it for searing, lower it for sauces.

**Build the fond.** The brown bits stuck to the bottom after browning meat aren't burnt; they're flavour concentrate. Deglaze with wine, stock or vinegar — scrape the bottom with a wooden spoon and incorporate the fond into your sauce.

**Use any utensil.** Metal, wood, silicone — anything. Stainless steel is the most tolerant cookware surface.

## How to maintain
**Dishwasher-safe**, but hand-washing keeps it shinier. For burnt-on bits: fill with water, bring to a boil, scrape with a wooden spoon. For tough stains: Bar Keepers Friend (oxalic-acid-based, removes the heat-discoloration). For rainbow-colored "heat tint": white vinegar.

If food sticks: it's almost always because the pan wasn't hot enough before food went in. Reheat empty until it sizzles a water droplet, then start over.

## Common mistakes
- **Adding food to a cold pan**: the food bonds with the surface as it heats. Hot pan, cold oil, then food.
- **Adding oil to a cold pan**: the oil sits and burns at the edges before the food goes in. Hot pan first.
- **Lifting food too soon**: when meat is properly seared and ready to flip, it releases cleanly. If you have to scrape it up, it's not ready.
- **Treating it like non-stick**: stainless steel WILL stick if you use it gently. The seared crust is the point.

## What it replaces
A stainless sauté pan and a saucepan replace: copper-bottom "shiny" pans (look pretty, work the same), specialty omelette pans (use the non-stick), and most of the pans in a "set". You want: 1 non-stick (for eggs), 1 stainless sauté pan (for everything else), 1 stainless saucepan (for rice and sauces), 1 cast iron (for searing and high-heat). Four pans = a complete kitchen.
