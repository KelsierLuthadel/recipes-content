# Banneton (Bread Proofing Basket)

*A coiled rattan or wood-pulp basket that supports wet, slack bread dough through its second rise. The spirals leave the signature pattern on the crust, and the porous material wicks moisture away from the surface, giving a dryer skin that bakes into a more crackling crust.*

## What to buy
**Budget (£10–20)**: a 22cm round rattan banneton or an 8" oval. Comes with a linen liner (essential — see below). Cheap rattan is fine; performance is the same as expensive.

**Good (£25–45)**: a Brot & Taylor or San Francisco Baking Institute banneton. Higher-quality rattan, denser coils (better support), removable liners.

**Premium (£60+)**: a wood-pulp (cellulose) banneton, or a hand-woven artisan one. Wood-pulp absorbs more moisture (better crust); hand-woven gives a more dramatic spiral pattern.

Size: **22–23cm round** holds an 800g–1kg dough ball — the standard home-baker loaf size. **Oval (10×6")** holds a batard-shape loaf. Both have their place; round is the better starter.

Look for: **natural rattan or cane** (not coloured or coated), **6–8cm depth**, **spiral coil pattern** (the inside grooves), **machine-washable linen liner** included.

Avoid: lacquered or coated bannetons (defeats the moisture-wicking purpose), plastic "banneton-style" baskets (don't wick moisture; the crust comes out soft).

## How to use
**Flour the banneton heavily before each use.** Rice flour is the standard — it doesn't gluten-up, so it slides off the dough cleanly. Regular wheat flour works but sticks more.

**Place the dough seam-side up** in the banneton. The smooth top of the loaf goes against the spiral grooves (which leave the pattern); the seam side is what becomes the bottom of the baked loaf.

**Proof for 45 minutes to overnight in the fridge** (cold retard). The colder the proof, the slower the rise and the more complex the flavour. Most home sourdoughs benefit from a 12-hour fridge proof.

**Invert onto a piece of parchment** before scoring and baking. The dough should release cleanly with a gentle shake; if it sticks, you need more flour next time.

**Don't bake bread in the banneton.** The rattan burns above 200°C. The banneton is for proofing only; transfer to a Dutch oven or hot pizza stone for the actual bake.

## How to maintain
**Don't wash with water or soap.** Rattan absorbs water and develops mould or warps; soap leaves a film that affects future bakes. Instead: shake out the residual flour after every use, brush with a stiff dry brush, leave to air-dry completely overnight.

**Replace the linen liner** when it gets too stained or fragile. Wash the liner on a normal cycle every few uses.

**If mould appears**: it's almost certainly because the banneton was put away while still damp. Brush off, sun-dry for several days, then continue using. Severe mould — throw it out.

**A well-cared-for banneton lasts 5+ years.** The flour-and-bread residue eventually coats the rattan in a thin patina (called "banneton seasoning") which actually helps dough release.

## Common mistakes
- **Not enough flour in the banneton**: dough sticks, your loaf is destroyed during transfer. Use plenty of rice flour, especially for the first few bakes (until the seasoning builds up).
- **Washing it**: water destroys the rattan structure. Brush only.
- **Storing damp**: mould risk. Bone-dry storage only.
- **Baking in it**: rattan burns at 200°C, releasing nasty fumes. Banneton is a proofing tool only.

## What it replaces
A banneton replaces: improvised proofing vessels (mixing bowls lined with floured tea towels — works but doesn't wick moisture as well), proofing without support (high-hydration dough just spreads out), and the trial-and-error of getting a free-form sourdough that holds its shape. For sourdough specifically, the banneton is the single most useful piece of bread-baking kit.
