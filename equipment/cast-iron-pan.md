# Cast Iron Pan

*A heavy iron skillet, pre-seasoned, that goes from the hob to the oven to the grill. Sears steak harder than any other home pan, finishes pizzas at 250°C+, lasts generations when cared for. Heavier than you'd expect; that's the point.*

## What to buy

**Budget (£20–40)**: Lodge Cast Iron 26cm skillet. American-made, pre-seasoned, indestructible. The benchmark cheap cast iron and genuinely as good as the expensive ones. Bargain of the kitchen world.

**Good (£60–120)**: Smithey Ironware, Field Company, Stargazer. American smiths making smooth-bottom skillets (the Lodge has a slightly rough surface, which is fine but the smooth ones release better). Lighter weight, same performance.

**Premium (£300+)**: Solidteknics quenched-finish (Australian) or Le Creuset enamel-coated (different beast — see below). The price premium gets you smoother surface and slightly lighter weight; the performance is the same.

**Enamel-coated cast iron** (Le Creuset, Staub, £150+) is a different tool — easier to clean, doesn't need seasoning, can do acidic dishes (tomato sauce, wine reductions) without stripping the seasoning. Heavier, more expensive, less robust to dropping. Different niche; both are useful.

Size: **26cm (10 inch)** is the universal home size. Anything larger is hard to lift one-handed when full.

## How to use

**Preheat slowly.** Cast iron has huge thermal mass; it takes 5–10 minutes to heat evenly. Crank it onto medium-high for 5 minutes, test with a few drops of water (they should dance and evaporate in 1–2 seconds).

**The right temperature for searing**: smoking-hot. Cast iron browns better than any home pan because it stays hot when you add cold meat — the temperature drop is small, the crust forms fast.

**Add oil just before food.** Hot pan, hot oil (just-smoking), then food. The oil season-conditions the pan with every cook.

**Don't lift food too soon.** Properly seared meat releases on its own. If it sticks, give it another 30 seconds.

**Goes in the oven.** Hand-protect the handle (it's the same iron and gets searingly hot). Cast iron is the only home skillet that takes 300°C+ heat without warping.

## How to maintain

**Don't wash with soap regularly.** Soap doesn't destroy modern seasoning instantly (the polymerised oil layer is more robust than people think), but builds up over time as a stripping agent. Wipe out with a paper towel, rinse with hot water, scrub with salt and a brush for stuck-on bits.

**Dry immediately and thoroughly.** Wet cast iron rusts in hours. After washing, place on a warm hob for 2–3 minutes till bone dry, then wipe with a thin film of neutral oil before storing.

**Re-season when food sticks.** Light coat of oil (flaxseed, vegetable, or even shortening) on the surface; bake upside down at 230°C for an hour; let cool in the oven. Repeat 2–3 times for a fresh pan or a stripped one.

**Don't dishwasher.** Strips the seasoning, rusts the iron instantly.

## Common mistakes

- **Cooking acidic food in raw cast iron**: tomato, wine, vinegar strip the seasoning. Use enamel-coated cast iron, or just don't cook tomato sauce in your bare cast iron skillet.
- **Not preheating long enough**: cast iron is slow to heat. Give it 5–10 minutes; the cooking is much better.
- **Storing wet**: rusts in hours. Dry on the hob.
- **Treating it like a non-stick**: cast iron sticks if you're not careful. The seasoning helps but doesn't replicate PTFE.

## What it replaces

A cast iron skillet replaces: steak-searing pans, oven-going skillets, "pizza pans", and the entire "perfect for browning" category. With a stainless saucepan, a non-stick, and a cast iron skillet, your three-pan kitchen handles every cooking task — eggs (non-stick), sauces and stocks (stainless), searing and oven work (cast iron).
