# Pressure Cooker / Instant Pot

*A sealed pot that cooks at 1.5–2 atmospheres of pressure, raising the boiling point to around 120°C. Beef cheek that would take 4 hours becomes 50 minutes; dried beans that need overnight soaking become 25 minutes from dry. The most time-shifting tool in the kitchen.*

## What to buy

**Budget (£60–120)**: Instant Pot Duo 5.5L. The electric multi-cooker that launched the genre. 7-in-1 (pressure-cook, slow-cook, rice, sauté, steam, yoghurt, warm). The world's most-used pressure cooker, sells in tens of millions of units globally.

**Good (£140–220)**: Instant Pot Pro or Ninja Foodi. Better sauté function, faster pressure release, sturdier build. Worth the upgrade if you use it daily.

**Premium — stovetop (£100–300)**: Kuhn Rikon Duromatic or Fissler Vitavit. Stovetop pressure cookers (need a hob, no electronics) — faster to come to pressure, more powerful (higher PSI), lifetime tools. The serious cook's choice.

**Premium — electric (£250+)**: Breville Fast Slow Pro. Sensor-driven pressure curve, better cooking control. Edge-case useful.

Look for: **5.5–6L size** (fits a chicken or a 1.5kg pot roast), **stainless steel inner pot** (non-stick wears out, scratched coatings are common in electric ones), **multi-program functionality** in electric models (sauté is essential — lets you brown before pressure cooking).

## How to use

**Always sauté first.** Brown the meat (electric: use sauté mode; stovetop: brown directly), then deglaze with stock or wine, then add the rest of the ingredients and seal. Browning is everything; pressure cooking can't make up for skipping it.

**Liquid minimum**: 250ml for most cookers. Pressure cooking is a closed system — barely any liquid evaporates. If your braise recipe calls for "submerge in stock", use half that volume in a pressure cooker.

**Don't overfill.** Half-full for foaming foods (beans, rice, pasta — they expand), two-thirds for everything else.

**Natural vs quick release**: meat braises want natural release (let pressure drop on its own, 15–20 minutes). Vegetables, fish, rice want quick release (turn the valve, steam shoots out, immediate stop). Mixing these up overcooks the result.

## How to maintain

**Replace the silicone gasket ring** every 12–18 months (or when it stops sealing). The ring absorbs odours — a separate one for sweet (yoghurt, jam) vs savoury (curries, braises) is worth doing.

**Clean the pressure valve** after every use — bits of food block it and can affect pressure release. Soak overnight in hot water if it's gunky.

**Hand-wash the inner pot** with hot soapy water. Dishwasher-safe for the inner pot only — never the electronic base.

## Common mistakes

- **Not browning the meat**: pressure cooking softens but doesn't brown. A pale braise from a pressure cooker is undercooked at the flavour level.
- **Too much liquid**: the sauce comes out watery. Reduce hard after pressure-cooking if your sauce is thin.
- **Lifting the lid too early**: the pressure must drop fully (or be released via the valve) before the lid will open. Forcing it is dangerous.
- **Cooking dairy under pressure**: cream and milk separate and curdle. Add dairy at the end, after pressure-cooking.

## What it replaces

A pressure cooker replaces: slow cookers (does everything a slow cooker does, faster), the stovetop simmer pot for stocks (8-hour stock becomes 90-minute stock), the "soak beans overnight" step (25 minutes from dry, no soak), the rice cooker (most pressure cookers do rice perfectly). One pot, multiple appliances retired.
